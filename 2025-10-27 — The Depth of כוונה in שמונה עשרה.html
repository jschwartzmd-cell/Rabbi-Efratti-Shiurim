Rabbi Ephrati · Sunday Morning שיעור on תפילה

The Depth of כוונה in שמונה עשרה

יהיו לרצון, אלוקי נצור, ונפשי כעפר, the Four למען-Phrases, and the Three Steps Back
Recorded כ״ו תשרי תשפ״ו · 27 October 2025 · Reworked from the original שיעור
לעילוי נשמת ר' חסקל פלדמן ז״ל, whose יארצייט was on Tuesday;
לעילוי נשמת הרבנית ויינגרטן ליבע שמילצר, on her שניים שנים יארצייט;
ולעילוי נשמת יאיר בן בנימין ז״ל.

Last week we discussed the פסוק of יהיו לרצון, אלוקי נצור, and the מנהג to bracket אלוקי נצור with יהיו לרצון on both ends. This week we go deeper into the close of שמונה עשרה — the כוונות hidden inside יהיו לרצון, the personal language of אלוקי נצור and ונפשי כעפר, the four למען-phrases that בני קודש say with broken hearts, and the three steps back at the very end.

I. The עשר תיבות, עשר יודי״ן of יהיו לרצון

The ספר סדר היום

Last week we covered the פסוק of יהיו לרצון that we say at the end of שמונה עשרה, and the פסוקים that begin and end with שמותיו של הקדוש ברוך הוא inside אלוקי נצור. We also discussed the מנהג to say יהיו לרצון twice — once before אלוקי נצור and once after.

An important addition belongs here from ספר סדר היום[1]. The סדר היום was written by ר' משה בן מכיר (or בן מוכיר), who lived in צפת in the time of the האריז״ל and the בית יוסף. He is, incidentally, the author of the famous יגדל אלוקים חי, which was composed only in the 1500s and has no earlier source.

The סדר היום writes that יהיו לרצון is an extraordinarily significant פסוק. It is a סגולה גדולה to have כוונה in it, and one should say it slowly — not mumble through it. This single פסוק contains everything we asked, every kavvanah we had, every word of שמונה עשרה. In one sentence we are asking הקדוש ברוך הוא to accept everything — even the thoughts we could not express.

A Hidden סוד in the פסוק

The סדר היום points out something fascinating. יהיו לרצון אמרי פי והגיון לבי לפניך — a פסוק in תהלים — contains exactly ten תיבות. It begins with a יו״ד and ends with a יו״ד, and the letter יו״ד appears in it ten times. This is a great סוד. Those who know סודות התורה are מכוון in this פסוק, and if they are מכוון in it, הקדוש ברוך הוא answers their תפילה.

II. The History of אלוקי נצור

It Was Not Originally Part of שמונה עשרה

אלוקי נצור is a relatively recent fixture. If you look at the ראשונים, it is clear that they did not say it. There is one early hint among the גאונים — רב עמרם גאון[2] mentions תפילות like אלוקי נצור — but it was far from universal.

Strictly speaking, אלוקי נצור is not part of שמונה עשרה. Yet over the centuries it became standardized into שמונה עשרה. Today it is so embedded that if you have not yet said יהיו לרצון before it, you cannot answer קדיש, קדושה, or ברכו — you must remain silent, because you are still considered to be in the middle of תפילה.

The אבודרהם[3] — one of the great ראשונים on תפילה — testifies that in his day they finished with ברוך אתה ה' עושה השלום, took three steps back, and that was the end. אלוקי נצור entered our regular tefillah only later — through the influence of the printed סידור.

Its Source in the גמרא

The full sugya is in ברכות י״ז[4]. There you find a complete page of גמרא listing what each אמורא would add as his personal תפילה after שמונה עשרה. From perhaps fifteen אמוראים' personal תפילות, the printers — beginning with the first modern printed סידור in the 1500s — chose one: the תפילה of מר בריה דרבינא. They placed it at the end of שמונה עשרה, and over time it became so welded to the עמידה that today it carries הלכתי consequences of its own.

Sensitivity in Tefillah His first יארצייט is approaching: he would — I do not know whether you saw it or whether he told you — sometimes skip אלוקי נצור. If he saw the ציבור was waiting for him because he davened a longer שמונה עשרה, he simply skipped it. "Whoa, the רב didn't say אלוקי נצור!" Relax. It is not the end of the world. We say it, but in such a case you may certainly leave it out — it is not like leaving out a ברכה of שמונה עשרה. It is within the bounds of what חז״ל instituted. The ראשונים we look up to did not say it at all.

III. Why This תפילה of All the אמוראים?

The question is sharp. There were so many beautiful personal תפילות in ברכות י״ז. Why did those who integrated this into our שמונה עשרה specifically choose מר בריה דרבינא's אלוקי נצור לשוני מרע — "guard my tongue from speaking evil"?

We touched on one answer last week. The גמרא teaches that every yid transgresses לשון הרע every day. We think we are such צדיקים. Sometimes we actually speak לשון הרע; אבק לשון הרע — the dust of it, the hint of it — is even harder to avoid. Are you really careful about לשון הרע? Out of all the מצוות and עברות in the תורה, this is what we ask הקדוש ברוך הוא specifically to help us with at the close of every שמונה עשרה: please help me guard myself from לשון הרע today.

Another Reading — שפתי מדבר שקר

But there is another פשט. Listen to how the אחרונים read this. רבינו, מר בריה דרבינא, was saying, in effect: "I stood before You, הקדוש ברוך הוא, for שמונה עשרה — two minutes, three minutes, ten minutes, whatever — I stood there. And like all of us, I talked. I had no idea where I was sometimes. I mumbled the words. I said and said and said. Sometimes I did not even understand what I was saying."

"רבונו של עולם, please — if I said something and my heart was truly not there, then in some sense I was speaking falsely before You. I was talking to the King and not even present. Forgive me. My lips moved and my mouth said words, but my heart was not in them. Help me to be honest with my mouth and with my heart."

In that light, אלוקי נצור is a kind of apology at the close of שמונה עשרה. הקדוש ברוך הוא, we are honest with You in our vulnerability. We understand that You understand we are human. Please guard us. Help me, in the next שמונה עשרה, to do better — to be more focused, more present, less of a stranger in my own תפילה. A beautiful way to end an עמידה.

IV. ונפשי כעפר לכל תהיה

The Plain Reading

We end the תפילה with ונפשי כעפר לכל תהיה. עפר — earth, dust, sand. The ראשונים read it like this: I am about to step out of שחרית into my day. I am going to interact with people. And whenever you interact with people there are conflicts, arguments, misunderstandings — that is the human condition. I do not want to fight. I do not want to speak negatively. I do not want to say לשון הרע or שקר. So I tell הקדוש ברוך הוא: those who curse me, those who provoke me — let my soul be like the dust that everyone tramples on. Whatever happens today, I should not have a wrong reaction. You take care of it; I do not need to be involved.

The וילנא גאון — Reward of the Dust

The וילנא גאון[5] writes (he brings it from the זוהר) something deeper. People want good children. הקדוש ברוך הוא promises the אבות that their children will be like — for אברהם אבינו — ככוכבי השמים, and like עפר הארץ. עפר הארץ. You cannot count the grains of sand any more than you can count the stars.

Says the גר״א: if a yid says, "I want to be like עפר that everyone tramples on" — and despite his ego, despite his urge to fire back — "Are you going to let Yankel humiliate you?! Respond!" — he refuses, and lets himself be like עפר that people walk on, then he merits the reward of dust: that his children will be numerous, like grains of sand, and good. מידה כנגד מידה.

The מהרש״א — Eventually the Dust Wins

The מהרש״א[6] adds a quietly humorous reading. ונפשי כעפר לכל תהיה — make yourself like עפר that everyone tramples on. What is the end of the story? Everyone tramples on dust — but what happens at the very end? The dust tramples on us. We all end up beneath the dust. So if you make yourself עפר now, in humility, eventually you will come out on top. People trample on dust today; the dust outlives them.

The Story of ר' חיים מוואלאזשין Two Jews came to ר' חיים מוואלאזשין with a fight over a piece of real estate. "Rebbi, this land is mine!" "No, it's mine!" No one could make peace between them. ר' חיים said, "Reb Yitzchak, Reb Moshe — let me hear what the land says." He took them outside, bent his ear to the קרקע, listened a moment, and stood up. "The קרקע says that you both belong to it. It does not belong to you. You will both end up in the קרקע. What are you fighting for? One hundred and twenty years — and you belong to the קרקע." This is the very lesson of ונפשי כעפר לכל תהיה: a person who makes himself like עפר ultimately rises above.

V. Two Schools of Anavah — נאוואַרדאָק and סלאַבאָדקא

This concept was taken to an extreme in נאוואַרדאָק. To put it in context: a few weeks ago we discussed ר' ישראל סלנטר, the founder of the מוסר movement. נאוואַרדאָק represented one wing of that movement, led by the Alter — ר' יוסף יוזל הורוויץ — and was very radical. They emphasized putting oneself down.

The other wing was סלאַבאָדקא, which emphasized גדלות האדם — the greatness of man. You are the crown of creation; you should walk with confidence and pride. (This is, by the way, where certain behaviors in the ישיבה world have their source. You may have noticed older בחורים sometimes treating younger בחורים with a certain air — "I am older, I am holding up the world with my תורה." It comes from somewhere. The thinking was that at sixteen, seventeen, eighteen בחורים should feel they are sustaining the world with their learning. It can go both ways. It can also degenerate into what one might call "Litvish גאוה" — I apologize for the phrase, but it is real. The intention was good; the execution requires discipline.)

נאוואַרדאָק went the other extreme. The בחורים used to walk into a pharmacy and ask for a package of nails — knowing perfectly well that pharmacies do not sell nails — specifically to be shouted at, looked at as nothing, treated as foolish. They did this deliberately as a technique to bring themselves to שפלות, true humility. עפר is the paradigm of this avodah.

VI. The Two Pockets — Self-Esteem and Anavah

Our generation is obsessed with self-esteem. Every day I hear: "self-esteem, self-esteem!" Vadai true — our generation is in a matzav where we need elevation. But come on — we are not children. Why shouldn't we also work on humility?

The רבי ר' בונים מפשיסחא[7] said it best. Every yid must have two pockets. In one pocket the words בשבילי נברא העולם — the whole world was created just for me, just for my avodah, just for my תורה, just for my תפילה. אם אין אני לי, מי לי. In the other pocket: ואנכי עפר ואפר — I am but dust and ash. It is a balance. You need an equilibrium, and you have to be a wise person to maintain it. Every person has his own avodah here.

The famous כלי יקר on פרשת ויצא[8] speaks about exactly this. הקדוש ברוך הוא gives the אבות two analogies for their children: כעפר הארץ and ככוכבי השמים. עפר is humble, simple, a grain of sand. A כוכב is an entire universe. From here it looks small; up close it is enormous. A yid has to know simultaneously: I am a star in heaven, my light shines very far — and I am also a grain of sand. Hold both at once.

VII. The Continuation — "Take Care of It Yourself"

The end of אלוקי נצור speaks about others. Whoever is planning to take me down today, whoever is planning to take advantage of me — a business partner, a friend, somebody saying negative things about me — You, הקדוש ברוך הוא, take care of it. Some נוסחאות add an additional passage right before the closing יהי רצון: that nobody should be jealous of me, that I should not be jealous of them, that I should not get angry today, that they should not get angry at me. We add it.

Personal Kavvanah — Where Do You Connect? Let me share a question I love to ask people: which words in שמונה עשרה do you have the most כוונה in? Where do you find yourself really there, talking to הקדוש ברוך הוא with full attention? Each yid has his own. הלוואי we should all have כוונה through the entire שמונה עשרה.

My own — for whatever it is worth: ותחזינה עינינו בשובך לציון. When we ask הקדוש ברוך הוא to bring us back together, to redeem His scattered sheep, I lose myself. Two thousand years we have been asking for גאולה, for the בית המקדש, three times a day. We beg, we cry, we want the צרות to be over. There is a Yiddish saying: ביז מ'קומט צום קרעטשמע, דארף מען אויך אַ טרינק — until you reach the inn, you also need a drink along the way. They used to travel from city to city by horse and buggy, with inns at intervals. Until the גאולה comes, what will be in the meantime? Make Your children happy. A father wants his children to be happy. If they are not happy, he is not happy. We ask הקדוש ברוך הוא to make us happy until we get to the next inn.

VIII. The Four Phrases — למען שמך

If you add according to נוסח ספרד the closing four phrases, this is one of the most powerful pieces of the entire close of שמונה עשרה. The ראשונים say רבי עקיבא used to say this תפילה, and perhaps רבי אליעזר said parts of it as well.

The בית יוסף[9] brings in the name of a מדרש: whoever says these four phrases every day after שמונה עשרה —

עשה למען שמך, עשה למען ימינך, עשה למען תורתך, עשה למען קדושתך.

— is זוכה to be מקבל פני השכינה. The תורה does not often promise that for words said inside שמונה עשרה. These four carry a special weight.

The כוונה

What should one have in mind? The thought is: not for me. I do not deserve all the בקשות I just made. I do not deserve to have them fulfilled. I know my עברות, my thoughts, my failures. So I ask: do it for Your sake. If I have פרנסה, health, good children — it is so that I will be able to serve You. למען שמך, למען ימינך, למען תורתך, למען קדושתך. If you say those words with כוונה, the power is tremendous.

IX. The חתם סופר — קשתי נתתי בענן

The תלמידים of the חתם סופר[10] testified that he said the following vort every single year on פרשת נח. The פסוק says about the rainbow after the מבול: קשתי נתתי בענן[11] — "My bow have I placed in the cloud."

Said the חתם סופר: קשת״י is an acronym for our four phrases:

קדושתך · שמך · תורתך · ימינך.

Breaking Through the Cloud

The גמרא in ברכות ל״ב[12] says — these are difficult words for a yid to hear — מיום שחרב בית המקדש ננעלו שערי תפילה. From the day the בית המקדש was destroyed, the gates of תפילה are closed; no תפילה ascends. But the same גמרא adds: שערי דמעות לא ננעלו — the gates of tears were not closed. You can still beg with tears.

And how do we know the gates of tears are still open? From a פסוק in איכה[13]: סכותה בענן לך מעבור תפילה — He has placed a cloud, and that cloud blocks תפילה from rising.

The חתם סופר said: there is one way to breach that cloud. That is exactly what הקדוש ברוך הוא tells נח. קשתי נתתי בענן — "I have placed קשת״י in that cloud." If you daven not for yourself but say "למען שמך, למען ימינך, למען תורתך, למען קדושתך" — that goes through the cloud. If הקדוש ברוך הוא is blocking our תפילה because of our own merits, but we tell Him "this is for Your sake, for Your Name, for Your תורה, for Your holiness" — He opens it up.

The תלמידים of the חתם סופר said that a yid who has that כוונה in these words, with a broken heart, is guaranteed that his תפילה will be accepted. This is the meaning of the close of שמונה עשרה: I ended my תפילה — and what I said is for Your sake, not for mine. Then קשת״י breaks through the ענן.

X. The Three Steps Back

Source One — The כהן גדול and the Honor of a King

The גמרא in יומא דף נ״ג[14] says that when the כהן גדול left the קדש הקדשים on יום כיפור he did not turn his back — he retreated. He took three steps back. The גמרא extends this: anytime כהנים, לויים, even ישראלים in בית המקדש attended the מעמדות (the rotations of those who came for a week to be present at the קרבנות, discussed in מסכת תענית[15]), they left the מקדש the way one leaves a king. You take three steps back.

This is true even of leaving one's רבי. People do not do this much today, but I remember when I first went to my ישיבה, ישיבת בריסק, to take the farher with רבי משולם הלוי וייטשיק זצ״ל. He was about to become my רבי for the next three years. I already treated him as a רבי even before he had taught me. When I left after the farher — he had not even told me yet whether I was accepted; it is very hard to get accepted there — I walked out of the room. My father had told me beforehand: when you leave a רבי, when you leave a חסיד'יש Rebbe, you take three steps back.

The Stories on דף נ״ג ביומא

The גמרא there brings remarkable examples of תלמידים' respect for their רבנים. רבי אלעזר learned with רבי יוחנן. When רבי אלעזר would leave the שיעור, he walked backwards. If רבי יוחנן left first, רבי אלעזר would stand bent over with his head bowed and wait until רבי יוחנן was out of sight.

The גמרא tells further about רבה, the תלמיד of רב יוסף. רב יוסף was blind. When רבה would leave רב יוסף's presence he would take three steps back — but unlike the standard practice, he would not look behind him. He was so absorbed in humility that he simply stepped back, fell, stumbled, fell again. The גמרא writes that his feet became bloodied from the falls, and the threshold of רב יוסף's house filled with blood. The מפרשים say it was never washed away; the dried blood blackened the threshold.

When they finally told רב יוסף — who was blind and could not see it — that the floor was full of blood, רב יוסף stood up, placed his hands on רבה's head, and said:

יהי רצון מלפני הקדוש ברוך הוא שתהיה ראש לכל ישראל, ראש לעיר הזאת — מחוזא.

This is our history. This is how רבה became the רבי of כלל ישראל. The תלמוד הבבלי is full of his words; on every page רבה speaks. He is one of the fathers of the בבלי. Anytime אביי and רבה disagree — and there are roughly three hundred disputes between them — the הלכה is always like רבה, except for the famous six cases of יע״ל קג״ם[16]. All of this — say the מפרשים — is bound up with the כבוד he was so careful to give his רבי: he never turned his back, only walked backward, even at the cost of falling and bleeding.

Source Two — Nevuchadnezzar's Three Steps

The גמרא[17] says something striking. Before נבוכדנצר achieved greatness, before he became king, he was only a scribe to the king of Bavel — אביל מרודך, or whoever it was. They wrote a peace letter to חזקיהו, מלך יהודה: "שלום to the king, שלום to עם ישראל, and שלום to the great God of the Jews."

When נבוכדנצר returned and saw the letter, he said: "Are you crazy? You wrote G-d last? הקדוש ברוך הוא? You must give כבוד! Put שלום to G-d first, then to the people, then to the king of flesh and blood." They told him: fine, you write it. He rewrote the letter and ran after the messenger to deliver it himself, taking three steps backward in honor of the שכינה after he had recopied השם at the head of the page.

This is נבוכדנצר — the destroyer of the בית המקדש, the head of the wicked, whom every מדרש names with the suffix ש״ט: שחיק טמיא — "may his bones be ground as dust, like his place." How terrible, how evil, how wicked he was. We still suffer today from what he did to us. But — this one thing he got right. He gave כבוד to הקדוש ברוך הוא in that letter and took three steps back.

And the גמרא says — these are difficult words to digest — that in the merit of those three steps back, הקדוש ברוך הוא granted him the privilege of being the one to destroy the בית המקדש. The reverse holds for us: when a yid takes the same three steps back at the close of his עמידה, but for קדושה, we ask עושה שלום במרומיו, הוא יעשה שלום עלינו — and may the בית המקדש be rebuilt speedily in our days. He took three steps back and destroyed; we take three steps back and ask Him to build.

The גמרא in יומא that we cited earlier does not actually mention the number three. It only says that the תלמידים walked backward and did not turn their backs on their רבי. The number "three" comes specifically from the נבוכדנצר story. We do it — but we do it for the side of קדושה.

XI. רבינו יצחק אבוהב — The Hr Sinai Connection

Who He Was

The בית יוסף brings something interesting from one of the great figures in the transition between ראשונים and אחרונים — רבינו יצחק אבוהב, the מהרי״א אבוהב[18]. He was a רב and ראש ישיבה in Toledo, Spain, at the time of the גירוש ספרד of 1492. The expulsion order was lifted in 1493. He was a politically connected leader of כלל ישראל at that time. Together with אברבנאל — דון יצחק אברבנאל, treasurer of Spain and a wealthy man who tried to bribe Ferdinand and Isabella ימ״ש to rescind the decree — there was much diplomacy. They tried; ultimately the gates closed.

רבינו יצחק אבוהב fled to Portugal and met with King João II of Portugal, hoping to win the right of return for the Jews. He never succeeded. He died in Portugal in 1493, the year after the גירוש. To this day there is a בית הכנסת in צפת called בית הכנסת אבוהב — part of the regular tour route. Why is it named after a man who died in 1493? Because the legend goes that the ספר תורה in the ארון הקודש of that shul, still standing in 2025, is the ספר תורה that רבינו יצחק אבוהב himself wrote — he was a sופר. A ספר תורה of five hundred or six hundred years. (I do not believe they take it out anymore; I believe it is kept in a safe.) Every time the בית יוסף brings him he calls him רבינו הגדול. He was apparently the גדול הדור. Many things we do today through the בית יוסף come from him.

His Reason for the Three Steps

רבינו יצחק אבוהב says — and this is what I find difficult to understand — the reason we take three steps back: the גמרא in מסכת שבת ח״ח[19] says that when כלל ישראל stood at הר סיני and heard אנכי from the mouth of הקדוש ברוך הוא — they could not bear it. Our physical body could not contain the divine voice. We all fainted. The גמרא says we all actually died. Our נשמות flew back.

Says the בית יוסף in the name of המהרי״א אבוהב: our נשמות flew back three מילין. To remind ourselves that we are standing before the שכינה — and that we really should retreat before such an awesome Presence — we should ideally retreat three מיל. Three מיל would mean walking all the way to the next town; we have just finished מנחה. So we commemorate it with three steps.

A Difficulty I open the גמרא in שבת ח״ח and it does not say three מיל — it says twelve מיל. רש״י brings it in פרשת יתרו, the מכילתא brings it, the גמרא says it — no one says three מיל. I do not understand for the life of me where three comes from. Possibly it is three פרסאות (each פרסה is four מיל, which equals twelve מיל). I really do not know — but this is what the בית יוסף quotes from המהרי״א אבוהב, and it is the reason given for the three steps back at the end of every עמידה.

XII. כבוד שכינה and the ארון קודש

Some have the practice of leaving a shul facing the ארון קודש. The הלכה is in the שלחן ערוך and is based on the מגן אברהם[20], not directly on the גמרא. Just as one does not turn his back on his רבי, so too one does not turn his back on the שלחן ערוך. The חסידים who went to the Lubavitcher רבי certainly did not turn their backs on him as they left.

On the basis of the same גמרא, the שלחן ערוך writes that when we leave the presence of the מלך מלכי המלכים we do not turn our backs. We look to the sides — first of all because someone may be standing within ד׳ אמות still in שמונה עשרה, and it is forbidden to stand there. (Even with our benches between us today, many פוסקים say one should still not do so.) Then we take three steps back.

סיכום

May we be זוכה to daven and to be answered. May we have כוונה in יהיו לרצון as the סדר היום teaches — slowly, with awareness of the עשר תיבות and the עשר יודי״ן. May our אלוקי נצור be a true apology and a true request. May we hold both pockets — בשבילי נברא העולם and ואנכי עפר ואפר — without losing our balance. May our four למען-phrases break through the ענן with the כח of קשת״י. And may our three steps back, in the שבר־לב of a yid leaving the presence of the מלך מלכי המלכים, bring the building of the בית המקדש במהרה בימינו.

מראה מקומות  ·  Mareh Mekomos

  1. ספר סדר היום, ר' משה בן מכיר (צפת, late 16th century). — ר' משה בן מכיר was a רב in עין זיתים outside צפת in the era of the אריז"ל and the בית יוסף. סדר היום (first printed Venice 1599) is a structured exposition of daily, weekly, and yearly עבודת ה'; the פיוט יגדל אלוקים חי is widely (though not universally) attributed to him. His remark on the ten תיבות and ten יו"דין in יהיו לרצון אמרי פי (תהלים י"ט:ט"ו) appears in his treatment of the close of שמונה עשרה; it is a kabbalistic סוד consonant with the י' ספירות. ↩
  2. סדר רב עמרם גאון (9th century). — רב עמרם בן ששנא, גאון סורא (d. ca. 875), composed the earliest extant ordered סדור. The exact form of אלוקי נצור is not standard in his סדור; the פסקי גאונים reference closing personal תפילות at the end of שמונה עשרה as רשות for יחידים. Verify exact placement against ed. Goldschmidt, Jerusalem 1971. ↩
  3. אבודרהם, on סיום שמונה עשרה. — ר' דוד אבודרהם (Seville, mid-14th century) testifies that in his time the תפילה ended with ברוך אתה ה' עושה השלום and three steps back, with אלוקי נצור reserved for יחידים wishing to add a personal closing תפילה. Universal incorporation into the סדור was the work of the early printers from the late 15th and 16th centuries onward. ↩
  4. ברכות ט"ז ע"ב – י"ז ע"א — אמוראים' personal תפילות after שמונה עשרה. — The גמרא in ברכות records the personal closing תפילות of fifteen or so אמוראים — רבי, רבי אלעזר, רב, רבי יוחנן, רבי זירא, רבי חייא, רב ספרא, רבי אלכסנדרי, רבא, מר בריה דרבינא, and others. The early printers chose מר בריה דרבינא's אלוקי נצור for universal placement at the close of every שמונה עשרה. ↩
  5. ביאור הגר"א; זוהר on ונפשי כעפר לכל תהיה. — The גר"א draws on the זוהר's teaching that one who is כעפר in this world — accepting humiliation without retort — merits the parallel blessing of אברהם אבינו: ושמתי את זרעך כעפר הארץ (בראשית י"ג:ט"ז) and והיה זרעך כעפר הארץ (בראשית כ"ח:י"ד). מידה כנגד מידה: humble like dust today → numerous and good children like dust. Verify exact location in ביאור הגר"א on או"ח קכ"ג or in his פירוש על תהלים. ↩
  6. חידושי אגדות מהרש"א, ברכות י"ז ע"א. — ר' שמואל אליעזר אידלס (1555–1631) on the תפילה of מר בריה דרבינא: ונפשי כעפר לכל תהיה contains an סגולה — the dust outlasts the trampler. Make yourself כעפר in humility now, and you will eventually be over those who tread on you (since all flesh ultimately becomes dust). The מהרש"א's reading is widely cited in מוסר and חסידי sources on the close of שמונה עשרה. ↩
  7. ר' שמחה בונים מפשיסחא (1765–1827) — the "two pockets" teaching. — Founder of חסידות פשיסחא and רבי of קוצק. His celebrated משל: every yid must carry two slips of paper, one in each pocket. In one: בשבילי נברא העולם (סנהדרין ל"ז ע"א) — the world was created for me. In the other: ואנכי עפר ואפר (בראשית י"ח:כ"ז) — I am but dust and ash. The challenge of עבודת ה' is knowing which slip to draw at which moment. Cited throughout חסידי פשיסחא and קוצק literature; reported in סיפורי חסידים (ר' זוין) and elsewhere. ↩
  8. כלי יקר on פרשת ויצא, בראשית כ"ח:י"ד. — ר' שלמה אפרים מלונטשיץ (Lemberg/Prague, 1550–1619), author of the כלי יקר. On והיה זרעך כעפר הארץ he reads the dual analogies of כעפר הארץ and ככוכבי השמים as the dual identity of כלל ישראל: simultaneously a star (each Jew a universe of light) and a grain of sand (the same Jew bowed before הקב"ה). The שיעור connects this to the פשיסחא teaching of the two pockets. ↩
  9. בית יוסף, אורח חיים סימן קכ"ב, citing a מדרש. — The בית יוסף brings a מדרש (associated in some sources with רבי עקיבא and רבי אליעזר) that one who recites the four למען-phrases at the close of שמונה עשרה — עשה למען שמך, עשה למען ימינך, עשה למען תורתך, עשה למען קדושתך — is זוכה to קבל פני שכינה. The four phrases are based on פסוקים in תהלים (e.g., ס':ז', ק"ח:ז') and תהלים ע"ט:ט'. They appear in נוסח ספרד and in many סדורי אשכנז as a רשות at the close of אלוקי נצור. ↩
  10. חתם סופר על התורה, פרשת נח; תורת משה. — ר' משה סופר (Pressburg, 1762–1839), in his חתם סופר על התורה (תורת משה) on פרשת נח: the פסוק קשתי נתתי בענן is read as a רמז that הקב"ה gave us a "bow" — קשת"י = קדושתך, שמך, תורתך, ימינך — to pierce the ענן of סכותה בענן לך מעבור תפילה (איכה ג':מ"ד). His תלמידים reported he repeated this וורט annually on שבת פרשת נח. ↩
  11. בראשית ט':י"ג — את קשתי נתתי בענן. — הקב"ה's sign of the covenant with נח after the מבול: the rainbow placed in the cloud as a sign He will not again destroy the world by water. חז"ל (חגיגה ט"ז ע"א) treat the rainbow as bearing aspects of the שכינה's כבוד, hence the prohibition on staring at it. The חתם סופר's דרשה reads קשת"י as ראשי תיבות of the four למען-phrases. ↩
  12. ברכות ל"ב ע"ב — ננעלו שערי תפילה; שערי דמעות לא ננעלו. — אמר ר' אלעזר: מיום שחרב בית המקדש ננעלו שערי תפילה... ואע"פ שננעלו שערי תפילה, שערי דמעות לא ננעלו, שנאמר 'שמעה תפלתי ה' ושועתי האזינה אל דמעתי אל תחרש' (תהלים ל"ט:י"ג). One of the most cited חז"ל on תפילה after the חורבן: the gates of תפילה are closed, but the gates of tears were never closed. ↩
  13. איכה ג':מ"ד — סכותה בענן לך מעבור תפלה. — "You have screened Yourself with a cloud, that no prayer may pass through." The פסוק in איכה is the רש"י-cited proof-text for the principle in ברכות ל"ב ע"ב that the gates of ordinary תפילה closed at the חורבן. The חתם סופר's דרשה uses this פסוק as the ענן through which the קשת"י of the four למען-phrases breaks. ↩
  14. יומא נ"ג ע"ב. — "לא יחזור פניו אלא יצא אחורנית": the כהן גדול exits the קודש הקודשים on יום הכפורים by walking backward, never turning his back on the שכינה. Same דף: stories of רבי אלעזר and רבי יוחנן, and of רבה and his blind רבי רב יוסף — רבה's feet bloodied on the threshold from walking backward. The גמרא there is the foundational source for the מנהג of three steps back at the close of שמונה עשרה; the precise number "three" derives from the נבוכדנצר source (see fn 17 below). ↩
  15. תענית ב' ע"א – ה' ע"ב and תענית כ"ז — מעמדות. — The משנה in תענית פ"ד describes the system of מעמדות: the rotational delegations of ישראל who came to ירושלים to be present at the קרבן תמיד on behalf of all of כלל ישראל for one week of every משמר. Those present in the מקדש followed the same דין of leaving by walking backward. ↩
  16. יע"ל קג"ם — הלכה כאביי in six places. — A classical mnemonic taught to every תלמיד: in the disputes of אביי ורבא the הלכה follows רבא except in the six cases יע"ל קג"ם — יאוש שלא מדעת (ב"מ כ"א), עד זומם למפרע (ב"ק ע"ב), לחי העומד מאליו (ערובין ט"ו), קדושין שלא נמסרו לביאה (קידושין נ"א), גלוי דעתא בגיטא (גיטין ל"ד), מומר אוכל נבילות להכעיס (סנהדרין כ"ז). Note: the שיעור describes these as אביי ורבה; the standard mnemonic refers to אביי ורבא, the disputes of רבא (not רבה) being the relevant body. Verify the source as desired. ↩
  17. סנהדרין צ"ו ע"א — נבוכדנצר and the three steps for כבוד שמים. — The גמרא: when נבוכדנצר was a scribe to מרודך בלאדן, he discovered that the king's letter to חזקיהו placed the salutation to הקב"ה last; he objected, rewrote it placing הקב"ה first, and ran three steps after the messenger to retrieve and rewrite the letter. הקב"ה said: "אתה רצת אחר כבודי שלוש פסיעות, אני אתן לך שלושה מלכים שיהיו שולטים בעולם" — and as recompense for those three steps he and his line were granted dominion over the world (and the destruction of בית המקדש). The number "three" in the מנהג of three steps at the close of the עמידה is widely traced to this סוגיא. ↩
  18. בית יוסף, אורח חיים סימן קכ"ג, citing the מהרי"א אבוהב. — ר' יצחק אבוהב (the second; Castile/Portugal, 1433–1493), one of the great רבני ספרד at the time of the גירוש ספרד (1492). After the expulsion he led a delegation to פורטוגל to negotiate with King João II for refuge of his community. The בית יוסף on או"ח קכ"ג brings his explanation for three steps back at the close of שמונה עשרה: at מתן תורה the נשמות of כלל ישראל retreated upon hearing אנכי; we commemorate that retreat with three steps. (The שיעור flags the difficulty: the גמרא in שבת פ"ח ע"ב records the retreat as י"ב מיל, not three; possibly three פרסאות = י"ב מיל.) The בית הכנסת אבוהב in צפת bears his name and tradition. ↩
  19. שבת פ"ח ע"ב — מתן תורה and the retreat of the נשמות. — "בכל דיבור ודיבור שיצא מפי הקב"ה יצתה נשמתן של ישראל... אם בדיבור ראשון יצאה נשמתן... חזרה נשמתן בטל". The גמרא describes the retreat of the נשמות as י"ב מיל at each דיבור; רש"י brings the same in פרשת יתרו, שמות י"ט:י"ז; the מכילתא parallel. The מהרי"א אבוהב's reading of "three" remains a recognized קושיא noted by the שיעור — perhaps שלושה פרסאות reckoned (each פרסה = ד' מיל, hence י"ב מיל). ↩
  20. מגן אברהם, אורח חיים סימן קכ"ג; שולחן ערוך, או"ח קכ"ג. — ר' אברהם הלוי גומבינר (1635–1682), in his מגן אברהם on the שולחן ערוך, codifies the practice of leaving בית הכנסת facing the ארון הקודש as derived from the principle of not turning one's back on the שכינה. The שולחן ערוך in או"ח קכ"ג ס"א details the order of the three steps back, the בקשת שלום, and the prohibition of returning to one's place before the חזרת הש"ץ; the משנה ברורה ad loc. extends the discussion. ↩
