Pesukei d’Zimra: From Hodu to Baruch She’amar
Shiur of Rabbi Ephrati — Sunday Morning Series on Tefillah
Recorded 4 August 2024 · Reworked 30 April 2026 · Mareh Mekomos upgraded 5 May 2026

Sponsored by Boruch Ber Menachem l'zecher nishmas his mother, Leah Rivka bas Yochanan HaLevi, on her 45th yahrtzeit. Today is also the yahrtzeit of Rashi, kaf-tes Tammuz — a striking detail since his commentary on Bava Basra ends precisely on Daf Kaf-Tes.

I. The Yahrtzeit of Rashi — and the Note That Fell on Daf 29

Today is kaf-tes Tammuz, the yahrtzeit of רש״י. For those following Daf Yomi, the cycle was on Bava Basra Daf Kaf-Tes only eleven blatt ago. That is no coincidence: most printings of Shas note at that point in Bava Basra the words "כאן מת רש״י" — "Here, Rashi died." From Daf Kaf-Tes to the end of the masechta, the commentary is from his grandson, the Rashbam.

Rashi had daughters who married the gedolei olam of his generation, and from those unions came the Ba'alei HaTosafos. The Rashbam and Rabbeinu Tam were brothers — both grandsons of Rashi, sons of Rabbeinu Meir (Rashi's son-in-law and himself a major Tosafist). The entire Rishon world of Ashkenaz flows from this single mesorah.

She'eilos U'Teshuvos Min HaShamayim

This raises the famous question of whether to put on tefillin like Rashi or like Rabbeinu Tam. One Rishon answered it in a manner unique in our entire literature: Rabbeinu Yaakov of Orleans, a Ba'al HaTosafos and great mekubal, would write down profound she'eilos at night, place them under his pillow, and find written answers there in the morning. He compiled them into a sefer entitled She'eilos u'Teshuvos Min HaShamayim (שאלות ותשובות מן השמים).[1]

One of his answers — perhaps the most famous — was on the Rashi-vs-Rabbeinu-Tam tefillin question. The reply from Heaven was that the Yeshiva Shel Ma'alah rules like Rashi while the malachim rule like Rabbeinu Tam. (What this precisely means we leave to the deeper peirushim.) The premise echoes the Gemara in Berachos[2] that HaKadosh Baruch Hu Himself "wears" tefillin — and the Gemara even asks what is written inside them.

II. Hodu — Returning to the Levi'im at the Aron

Source: Divrei HaYamim, Not Tehillim

Unlike most of Pesukei d'Zimra, Hodu is not from Tehillim — it is from Divrei HaYamim Aleph,[3] the last sefer of Nach that almost no one studies systematically. To understand why we recite it daily, we have to return to its historical moment.

The Forty-Three Years of the Tent

When David HaMelech brought the Aron HaBris with the Shnei Luchos HaBris back to Yerushalayim, he erected a tent — called ohel in Nach — on what would become Har HaBayis. For roughly 43 years, the Aron remained there in this temporary dwelling, until Shlomo HaMelech finally completed the Beis HaMikdash.

This was an unusual liturgical interregnum: the Mishkan Shiloh had been destroyed, the Mishkan had moved from place to place, and there was no Mishkan at Givon. In its place, David HaMelech built a quasi-Mishkan around the tent, where korbanos were offered every day — the tamid shel shachar and the tamid shel bein ha'arbayim.

David HaMelech instituted a takanah that Asaf — head of Shevet Levi and one of the great composers of Tehillim ("Mizmor l'Asaf") — together with his fellow Levi'im would sing a shirah at each daily korban. The shirah they sang was precisely Hodu laHashem kir'u vishmo. When we say Hodu every morning, we are re-enacting that 43-year service of the Aron in the tent.

Where Hodu Originally Ended

If you check Divrei HaYamim directly, the original shirah of the Levi'im ends at "v'amru ha'am amen v'hallel laHashem" — not where our siddur ends. The additional pesukim that follow (verses asking for yeshuah) were appended in the Geonic era and incorporated into the standard nusach from there.

How Hodu Was Originally Said — and Why Only on Shabbos

The siddurim of the Geonim — Rav Amram Gaon, Rav Saadia Gaon — record Hodu only on Shabbos and Yom Tov, not weekdays. The reason is tircha d'tzibbur: in their day, Hodu was said the way the Levi'im sang it in the Beis HaMikdash — responsively, with the chazzan reading a verse and the entire kahal answering. That took five to seven minutes. On Shabbos and Yom Tov there was time; weekdays before work, there was not.

Our minhag today is to say Hodu daily, but everyone together — the call-and-response form is preserved only in our awareness of how it once sounded.

The Sefard / Ashkenaz Placement Debate

This historical context resolves the apparent puzzle of why Nusach Sefard places Hodu before Baruch She'amar, while Nusach Ashkenaz places it after:

  • Nusach Sefard: Since Hodu is the shirah that accompanied the daily korbanos — and tefillah as a whole is k'neged temidin tikkenum — its proper place is with the korbanos, before the formal opening of Pesukei d'Zimra. The Arizal held this way.
  • Nusach Ashkenaz: The Tur[4] rules that since the brachos of Baruch She'amar and Yishtabach are designed to "frame" all the praises, you should place every mizmor inside that frame and not exclude any.

There is a practical halachic nafka mina: from Baruch She'amar through the end of Shemoneh Esrei, there is a strict prohibition against speaking. Nusach Ashkenaz davenners are bound from Baruch She'amar onward — including all the extra mizmorim of Shabbos. Nusach Sefard davenners technically have a small window through Hodu and Mizmor Shir Chanukas HaBayis before Baruch She'amar begins (though shul decorum still applies).

III. Shema in Karbanos — The Tur's Reason

The Tur writes that we recite the first parsha of Shema already in the Korbanos section. Why? Because in earlier centuries, especially on Shabbos and Yom Tov, people would say so many piyutim that davening began late — sometimes too late to be yotzei the mitzvah of kriyas Shema in its proper time (z'man kriyas Shema). The early insertion was a safety net.

The Tur further explains that one says only Shema and Va'ahavta here — not all three parshiyos — because saying all three would be yotzei the full mitzvah and would leave no room to say it later with the birchos kriyas Shema. (The Vilna Gaon follows the Tur; the Mishnah Berurah generally follows this view as well, though there is a machloikes.)

A grandson of Reb Yehudah HaChasid records a different reason — found only in this one Rishon — for the brief pasuk that appears at the end of Hodu: just as one must recite Shema daily, one must also be mazkir yetzias Mitzrayim daily (per Mishnah Berachos, which is why we say parshas Tzitzis). The early insertion within Hodu ensures that even if you arrive late, you have already mentioned yetzias Mitzrayim.[5]

IV. Mizmor Shir Chanukas HaBayis

The Latest Addition to the Standard Siddur

This mizmor is conspicuously absent from the Rishonim, the Geonim, the Rambam, and the Tur Shulchan Aruch. It does not appear in any of the ancient siddurim we have from Ashkenaz or Sefarad. The first siddurim to include it appear in the very late 1400s and early 1500s.

The figure responsible is the Arizal. Born around 1520–1530 and niftar at age 38, he had only a brief lifespan — yet his stamp on our siddur is unparalleled. He instituted Mizmor Shir Chanukas HaBayis as a daily recitation. First the Sephardic siddurim adopted it, then the Ashkenazic ones followed.

The Arizal's Dual Heritage

The Arizal's surname was Luria — an Ashkenazic family. His father came from Ashkenaz to Mitzrayim and married a Sephardic woman from Egypt. The Arizal was raised by his mother's family in Mitzrayim, which is why he absorbed so deeply the Sephardic minhagim. But on certain matters — including Hodu before Baruch She'amar — he sided with the Ashkenazic position. On Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur he davened specifically in Ashkenazic shuls — not because he preferred their nusach, but because the Ashkenazim said more piyutim, and he did not want to lose the kedushah of those piyutim.

The Aruch HaShulchan's Pshat Nigleh

On a nigleh level, the Aruch HaShulchan[6] — Rav Yechiel Michel Epstein, the Rav of Novardok, whose son was the Torah Temimah (Baruch Epstein, author of "My Uncle the Netziv") — explains Mizmor Shir Chanukas HaBayis as the conclusion of the Hodu story. After David HaMelech had brought the Aron to the tent and the Levi'im had sung Hodu for 43 years, finally Shlomo HaMelech completed the Beis HaMikdash. Mizmor Shir Chanukas HaBayis L'David — L'David attributes the inauguration to David, his father, by whose zechus the gates ultimately opened.

Aside: Many poskim hold that when the Aruch HaShulchan and the Mishnah Berurah directly disagree, we follow the Aruch HaShulchan. The reasoning: the Aruch HaShulchan served for years as the practicing rav of a city, with the weight of poskening for an actual tzibbur; the Chofetz Chaim, by contrast, was not formally a rav — he sat in his small home in Radin and learned. Both are gedolim; on disputed points, the working rav's judgment carries weight.

The Gates That Tried to Swallow Shlomo

The Gemara[7] describes the dramatic moment when Shlomo HaMelech tried to bring the Aron into the Kodesh HaKadoshim — and the gates would not open. He davened twenty-four tefillos; nothing. He recited the pasuk:

שְׂאוּ שְׁעָרִים רָאשֵׁיכֶם, וְהִנָּשְׂאוּ פִּתְחֵי עוֹלָם, וְיָבוֹא מֶלֶךְ הַכָּבוֹד.
"Lift up your heads, O gates, and be lifted up, eternal openings, that the King of Glory may enter."

The Gemara's description is startling: the gates uprooted themselves from their place, took on a life of their own, and ran after Shlomo HaMelech to swallow him — because they thought he was claiming to be the Melech HaKavod. He cried out: "I am not the Melech HaKavod! It is the Shechinah, dwelling in the Aron with the Luchos HaBris!"

Even then, the gates would not yield until Shlomo invoked "b'zchus David avdecha" — by the merit of David, Your servant, my father. Only then did the gates open. And it was at that moment that Shlomo composed and sang Mizmor Shir Chanukas HaBayis L'David — thanking HaKadosh Baruch Hu by attributing the chinuch ha'bayis to David's zechus.

The Sephardic Variant — and Its Kabbalistic Hint

Genuine Sephardic Jews (Edot HaMizrach) skip the opening pasuk of this mizmor entirely. They begin: "Aromimcha Hashem ki dilisani". Why? Counting from Aromimcha through the end of the chapter yields exactly 91 words. Ninety-one corresponds to the combined gematria of the Shem Havayah (26) and the Shem Adnus (65) — a combination of central importance in Kabbalah.

The Ben Ish Chai's Vision

The Ben Ish Chai, in his sefer on tefillah,[8] writes something extraordinary: Mizmor Shir Chanukas HaBayis was not only Shlomo's poem at the inauguration of the First Temple — it is destined to be the national anthem of Klal Yisrael when the Beis HaMikdash HaShlishi is rebuilt. Each day we recite it, we are instilling in our hearts the conviction that the Bayis HaShlishi will rise.

V. Hashem Melech, Hashem Malach — The Song of the Malachim

After Mizmor Shir, we rise and proclaim twice:

ה' מֶלֶךְ, ה' מָלָךְ, ה' יִמְלֹךְ לְעוֹלָם וָעֶד.

This sentence is not a single pasuk in Tanach. It is a composite of three separate verses joined by an unknown hand at some point in liturgical history.[9]

The source for reciting it is Maseches Sofrim,[10] one of the masechtos ketanos appended to Maseches Avodah Zarah, which preserves a midrash on the practice of the malachim: every morning, a single malach stands in the emtza ha'rakia and proclaims this verse, and all the malachim and serafim answer in unison. They say it twice — and so do we. We stand when we say it because the malachim are omdim, and we are joining their shirah.

As with Hodu, the early siddurim recorded this only on Shabbos and Yom Tov — because they too sang it call-and-response, lengthening it. Our daily recitation is a later chiddush.

VI. Lamnatzeach B'Neginos — David's Battle Shield

The Menorah of Fire

The 67th mizmor, Lamnatzeach B'Neginos,[11] contains 49 words excluding the opening pasuk. Chazal teach[12] that when Moshe Rabbeinu struggled to grasp the construction of the Menorah — which had to be hammered from a single block — HaKadosh Baruch Hu showed him a menorah shel eish (a menorah of fire), and engraved upon it was this mizmor. The 49 words correspond to the seven branches × seven (or, alternatively, to the gevi'im, kaftorim, and prachim — the ornaments of the Menorah — which total 49). When we recite it, we commemorate the daily hadlakas ha'menorah in the Beis HaMikdash.

David HaMelech's Engraved Shield

There is a deeper layer. Chazal say that David HaMelech, when he went to war, carried a golden plate engraved on his shield with the mizmor of Lamnatzeach arranged in the shape of a Menorah — the mizmor in the seven branches, with the seven verses arrayed as the kanim. Chazal attribute his battlefield victories to that engraved shield. The Bnei Yissaschar[13] records that the Chashmonaim took the same engraved mizmor with them into battle and conquered the Greeks through its merit — which is why this mizmor has a Chanukah connection.

The Klaf in the Shape of a Menorah

The mekubalim teach that reading this mizmor daily from a klaf (parchment) on which it is written in the shape of a Menorah is a tikkun gadol and a segulah for hatzlachah. Many shuls have such a parchment hanging on the wall (or printed in the front of the siddur). The Chida[14] records a minhag of saying it seven times every morning before going to work, as a segulah for the day's hatzlachah. Some Sephardic siddurim incorporate it multiple times into the daily davening.

VII. Baruch She'amar — A Tefillah from Heaven

We now arrive at Baruch She'amar, the brachah that formally inaugurates Pesukei d'Zimra. Its origin is unique in our entire liturgy. It does not appear in Chazal in the standard sense — neither in the Bavli nor the Yerushalmi, neither in the Tannaim nor the standard Amoraim. The Zohar HaKadosh mentions the words Baruch She'amar, but does not present it as a fixed tefillah.

The Note from Heaven

The Or Zarua — not the famous Or Zarua of Rabbeinu Yitzchak of Vienna, but a different one, a grandson of the Ramban — records the source. He writes that one day, a pesek (note) fell from the heavens, and on it was written the text of Baruch She'amar. The Tur[15] brings this as halacha l'ma'aseh; the Taz reaffirms it.

This is, on its face, an extraordinary claim — that an entire central tefillah entered our siddur via a slip of paper from Shamayim. We are ma'aminim bnei ma'aminim, and where the Tur rules and the Acharonim follow, we accept it as emes l'amito. Its very heavenly authorship is, perhaps, why it was deemed worthy to serve as the gate to Pesukei d'Zimra.

A Cautionary Comparison

The Rebbe noted by way of contrast that the language "a note fell from heaven" appears in modern times in another, very different context: the founding "Petek" of the Na Nach movement. Rabbi Yisroel Dov Odesser ("the Saba"), born in Tveria, claimed that as a depressed young bachur sitting alone in a beis medrash, a note fell on him purportedly from Reb Nachman of Breslov, instructing the segulah of "Na Nach Nachma Nachman M'Uman." The Rebbe's gentle suggestion: more probably someone in the ezras nashim, seeing this depressed bachur, dropped the note out of compassion. Whatever happened, this is a recent and contested case.

The Or Zarua's account of Baruch She'amar, by contrast, is brought as halacha by the Tur and the Taz and is part of our normative mesorah — and this is precisely why we are willing to grant it the gravity that we do.
