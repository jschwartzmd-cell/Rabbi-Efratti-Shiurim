A deliberate deviation this week. After finishing מעריב of חול and of שבת the natural next step would have been to open שחרית של שבת — but Rabbi Ephrati paused. The bedtime Shema, he flagged, is something so foundational that skipping past it into Shacharis would be a lost opportunity. "I am not going to ask who goes to sleep like a goy — but if you do, if you fall asleep watching whatever you're watching without saying קריאת שמע על המיטה, that bothers me a lot." The arc: the Gemara in ברכות ה' ע״א — Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's derasha on רִגְזוּ וְאַל תֶּחֱטָאוּ אִמְרוּ בִלְבַבְכֶם עַל מִשְׁכַּבְכֶם וְדֹמּוּ סֶלָה; the מזיקין and the Beis Yisrael's chiddush on why a horizontal Yid is more exposed; the Meiri's re-reading of mazikin as bad thoughts that intrude when the mind quiets down at night (Rabbi Ephrati flagged the sharpness of this for our age of smartphones and Claude); the Ben Ish Chai / mekubalim on killing the mazikim and channeling them to קדושה; the Vilna Gaon on קריאת שמע על המיטה as the single most important thing a Yid says every day; the חרב פיפיות — double-edged sword for both עולם הזה and עולם הבא; the Rabbeinu Tam vs Rashi split in Tosafos on whether the earlier plag-Maariv already discharged the obligation of בשוכבך; the Meiri's isolated chiddush that one should in fact make a brachah of "אשר קדשנו במצוותיו וצוונו לקרוא את שמע" in edge cases; why we don't make a brachah at all — the twin frames of Tosafos in חולין (things done for סכנה) and the Rambam in his teshuvos (rishut, not chovah); the Rambam's biography as physician to the Sultan; the Rekach on the mazikim crowding around the bed; the machloikes on how to say it — al mitosoy literally (Rashi) or samuch l'mita (Meiri, with strong words against saying קריאת שמע in pajamas without kavana); the two-hundred-and-forty-eight-word count and Keil Melech Ne'eman; and the opening frame of הַמַּפִּיל — the after-chatzos machloikes of the Ben Ish Chai and Kaf HaChaim, the hefsek problem, and the tosafos on the ל"שב" בסוכה analogy. I. The Deliberate Detour

Rabbi Ephrati opened the שיעור with an unusual note. Someone from the kehillah — he did not name them — had approached him during the week with a suggestion: to gather the two-and-a-half years of Sunday-morning שיעורים on תפילה into a proper sefer. He flagged the suggestion with quiet gratitude and moved on. But the mention framed the שיעור's opening pivot. Two and a half years of methodical work through the daily tefillos had brought us through שחרית, מנחה, and מעריב of both חול and שבת. The natural next chapter would have been to open שחרית של שבת — פסוקי דזמרה של שבת, אנא בכח (again), and the שחרית עמידה.

Rabbi Ephrati chose to detour first into קריאת שמע על המיטה. His stated reason was pastoral rather than curricular. "Some of the younger generation — maybe the millennials, maybe those a little younger than that — they are on their phones until they fall asleep. That is going to sleep like a בהמה, like a הוא ילך. A Yid goes to sleep with קריאת שמע על המיטה." Before moving to any further sugya, he wanted to place the halachic and hashkafic weight of the bedtime Shema fully in front of the kehillah. What follows is that treatment.

II. The Gemara — ברכות ה' ע״א

The source is ברכות ה' ע״א[1]. רבי יהושע בן לוי — a first-generation Amora, the halachic architect of the Gemara's opening masechta — teaches that even though a person has already recited קריאת שמע at מעריב (with all the surrounding ברכות), fulfilling the daily obligation of בשוכבך, he is still required to recite קריאת שמע again upon lying down to sleep.

The proof text is unusual — not from the standard set of pesukim in שמע or והיה אם שמוע, but from תהלים פרק ד'[2]: רִגְזוּ וְאַל תֶּחֱטָאוּ אִמְרוּ בִלְבַבְכֶם עַל מִשְׁכַּבְכֶם וְדֹמּוּ סֶלָה. The Gemara reads David HaMelech's אִמְרוּ בִלְבַבְכֶם עַל מִשְׁכַּבְכֶם as an instruction: speak in your hearts upon your beds — say קריאת שמע where you lie down. And only then וְדֹמּוּ סֶלָה — silence yourselves, be still. The דומ״ו is understood not merely as external quiet but as the shutting-down of the body and the ascent of the נשמה upward, back to the כסא הכבוד for the night.

III. Why Mazikim Come at Night — The Beis Yisrael's Chiddush

The Gemara continues by explaining why קריאת שמע על המיטה is instituted. האומר קריאת שמע על מיטתו — מזיקין בדילין ממנו. The mazikin — the destructive forces of the night — flee from the one who says it.

Why is night specifically the time of מזיקין? The kehillah has heard this framework repeatedly across the run through מעריב — night is structurally the time when the boundary between worlds is thinner. But Rabbi Ephrati brought a specific chiddush from the בית ישראל[3] — the third Gerrer Rebbe, ר' ישראל אלתר — that sharpens the point in a way particularly relevant to bedtime.

The Beis Yisrael observes that אדם — the human being, the crown of creation — walks vertical. His ראש points upward, connected to למעלה. A בהמה, by contrast, walks on all fours; its head points downward, toward the earth. When a person is horizontal — lying down — his structural posture is closer to that of the בהמה than to that of the standing man. The יצר, the קליפות, and the מזיקין all have easier access to the person's heart and mind in that horizontal position. The bedtime Shema is therefore not just an evening obligation like the earlier מעריב Shema. It is a defensive act at the precise structural moment of maximum exposure — when the Yid's body assumes the posture of a בהמה and the neshama begins its ascent.

IV. The Meiri's Chiddush — Bad Thoughts as the Real Mazikim

The מאירי[4] — ר' מנחם המאירי of Perpignan, thirteenth-century Provence — offers a reading of the mazikin that Rabbi Ephrati flagged as prophetic for our era. The Meiri is not thinking about non-physical demonic agents. He is thinking about the mind.

During the day, a Yid is busy. He is running אחר מחייתו ואחר כל הכל — פרנסה, learning, family, obligations. His mind is engaged, occupied, directed outward. At night, when he lies down, the noise stops. He is trying to fall asleep. And what happens? The mind begins to wander. He starts to think.

The Meiri identifies exactly this drift as the danger the Gemara is addressing. The מזיקין of the Gemara are, on the Meiri's reading, דעות כוזבות — false or destructive thoughts. Bad thoughts. Depressing thoughts. In some cases — the Meiri's own language, though he had no name for what we now call clinical depression — thoughts that tend toward the darkest places a person can go. The person is calm, serene, undistracted for the first time in the day, and precisely that quiet becomes the entry point for destructive mental content. חז״ל's response: at that moment, speak קריאת שמע. Focus the mind on קבלת עול מלכות שמים. Displace the drift with the structural anchor.

A Frame for the Smartphone Era Rabbi Ephrati took a moment to press the Meiri's chiddush against contemporary life. "Back in the good old days, people used to think. Today we don't think anymore — we have our new friend, what's his name…" — the kehillah supplied various tech names — "…Claude. Today Claude is your best friend. AI is better; you don't have to think." The light joke sat inside a serious point. The Meiri's דעות כוזבות attacked the still, unoccupied mind. Contemporary life has replaced that stillness with a different threat entirely: the scrolling phone, on until the moment consciousness fails. The Yid who falls asleep mid-scroll, mid-video, mid-argument on a group chat, is never in the Meiri's exposed still-mind state at all — but he is also never in the קריאת שמע state either. The bedtime Shema, on the Meiri's frame, requires reclaiming the quiet moment from the phone first, and then filling it with תורה rather than with drift. "Put the stupid phone down for three minutes before you fall asleep, and say קריאת שמע. Better yet, say the whole thing lying down. I guarantee you — according to the Vilna Gaon, according to the מקובלים — you will have a better night and a better day."

V. The Ben Ish Chai — Killing and Channeling

The בן איש חי[5] — ר' יוסף חיים of Baghdad — reads the Gemara's מזיקין one step further into the kabbalistic frame. On the Ben Ish Chai's reading, every עבירה a Yid commits during the day creates מלאכי חבלה — negative angels attached to that specific אבירה. When the Yid says קריאת שמע על המיטה at night, he does not merely send them away — he kills them. And more remarkably: he then resurrects them, but re-channels them toward the צד הקדושה. The energy that the אבירה created is not annihilated; it is converted. The same koach that was destructive is now serving purposes of holiness.

The framework is characteristically Ben Ish Chai — kabbalistic, dramatic, focused on the transformative power of the individual's action. Rabbi Ephrati offered it not as normative halacha but as the emotional weight the mekubalim place on this single daily act. Every night, the Yid has the koach to undo and convert the destructive residue of his day.

VI. The Vilna Gaon — The Most Important Thing You Say Every Day

The גר״א[6] is quoted by his תלמידים as having said that קריאת שמע על המיטה may be the single most important thing a Yid says every day. The chiddush is structural. The next day's הצלחה in פרנסה, in learning, in every domain that matters, is determined — on the Gra's framework — by whether the Yid ended the previous night with קריאת שמע. The מזיקין that leave when he says it do not return in the morning. The person wakes up into a day whose structural conditions have already been shaped by that single act at the end of the previous night.

"If you were מזלזל in קריאת שמע על המיטה until now — today is the day to be מקבל on yourself never to fall asleep without it."

VII. The חרב פיפיות — Double-Edged Sword

The Gemara adds a final image. When a Yid says קריאת שמע על המיטה, it is as though he holds a חרב פיפיות in his hand — a double-edged sword. What does the double edge cut? On the simplest level: the מזיקין on his right, the מזיקין on his left. But the Vilna Gaon[7] reads deeper. One edge cuts the מזיקין of עולם הזה — the harms directed at the Yid in this world. The other edge cuts the מזיקין of עולם הבא — the harms that stand between the Yid and his ultimate share. The single act of קריאת שמע at bedtime discharges obligations and vanquishes threats across both worlds simultaneously.

VIII. Tosafos — The Rabbeinu Tam / Rashi Split

Reb Yitz in the kehillah pressed a structural question. The very first Tosafos in Shas — the opening Tosafos of ברכות — addresses exactly this sugya. The Tosafos is famously difficult; it is the source of the shittah of רבינו תם on when night begins and where מעריב fits into the day. Rabbi Ephrati opened the Tosafos to explain how it connects to the bedtime Shema.

A) The Structural Question

In אשכנז — in the days of the ראשונים and continuing into our own kehillos — the practice was widespread to daven מעריב at פלג המנחה, well before שקיעה. In practical terms, on a summer evening one might daven מעריב at 6:40 PM while the sun is still visibly up until roughly 8:30. But קריאת שמע is a mitzvah of ובשכבך — recited "when you lie down," structurally a night-time act. If a Yid davens מעריב including קריאת שמע while it is still light outside, has he been יוצא?

B) The Rabbeinu Tam Shitta

רבינו תם[8] — the great-grandson of Rashi, twelfth-century France — resolves the difficulty through his famous position that from פלג המנחה onward, halachically it is already considered night. On Rabbeinu Tam's frame, the plag-Maariv Yid is יוצא קריאת שמע של ערבית — the earlier zeman is already halachically night. The bedtime Shema is therefore not being said to make up for a missed חיוב דאורייתא. It is being said, per the Gemara in ברכות ה', purely because of the mazikin.

C) The Alternative Shitta

Tosafos also raises an alternative reading — the shitta widely attributed to Rashi in his commentary at the opening of Berachos[9]. On the Rashi-reading, plag-Maariv does not fully discharge the חיוב דאורייתא of קריאת שמע. The bedtime Shema then serves a dual function: it addresses the mazikin (Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's reason), and it also serves as the actual קריאת שמע of ובשכבך for those who davened early.

D) The Practical Payoff

The practical output differs based on which shitta one follows. On the Rabbeinu Tam-line — the mainstream halacha — one need only say the first parasha or two at bedtime, because the חיוב of קריאת שמע is already discharged from מעריב; the bedtime is only for מזיקין. On the Rashi-line, one must say the full קריאת שמע (all three parashos) at bedtime, because it is the operative קריאת שמע of ובשכבך. The kehillah practice, per the משנה ברורה[10], is to say the full קריאת שמע לכתחילה — accommodating the Rashi-position as a חומרא even where the Rabbeinu Tam-position is the נורמטיבית. If saying the full קריאת שמע is impractical (fatigue, spouse-considerations, etc.), the first parasha with Shema Yisrael and V'ahavta suffices בדיעבד.

IX. Why No Brachah? — Two Frameworks

If קריאת שמע at bedtime is a rabbinic mitzvah instituted by חז״ל, why do we not make a brachah before it? During שחרית and מעריב, קריאת שמע is preceded by the ברכות קריאת שמע — יוצר אור / אהבת עולם in the morning, מעריב ערבים / אהבת עולם at night — which function bein hasumin as ברכות המצוה for the recitation. At bedtime there are no such surrounding ברכות. Why not, at least, a simple אשר קדשנו במצוותיו וצוונו לקרוא את שמע?

A) The Tosafos in חולין — The סכנה Framework

תוספות בחולין דף ק״ו[11] establishes a structural principle. Consider מים אחרונים. The Gemara explains that מים אחרונים was instituted because of מלח סדומית — a dangerous salt used in certain foods that, if left on the fingers and then transferred to the eyes, could cause blindness. Mayim Achronim is therefore a mitzvah of avoiding סכנה. And Tosafos rules that we do not make a brachah on a mitzvah instituted for סכנה. The washing is done; no brachah is recited.

The parallel application to קריאת שמע על המיטה is direct. The Gemara's stated reason for the takana is מזיקין בדילין ממנו — protection from a spiritual danger. A takana for סכנה — whether physical (as in מים אחרונים) or spiritual (as in the bedtime Shema) — does not carry a brachah. This is the standard explanation in the ראשונים for why no brachah is recited.

B) The Meiri's Chiddush — A Brachah in Edge Cases

The מאירי[12] — in what Rabbi Ephrati flagged as one of the Meiri's characteristically bold isolated positions — appears to hold that in certain edge cases, one should make a brachah. The Meiri's case: a person had an אונס at מעריב and did not say ברכות קריאת שמע at that time. He arrives home and needs to recite קריאת שמע without the surrounding structural ברכות. On the Meiri's reading, in that gap-case he should recite the brachah of אשר קדשנו במצוותיו וצוונו לקרוא את שמע. No other ראשון takes this position, and the halacha does not follow it. But Rabbi Ephrati preserved the Meiri as a real position within the historical record — a reminder that the no-brachah practice is not obviously settled.

C) The Rambam's Teshuvah — Rishut, Not Chovah

The רמב״ם[13] — in one of the relatively few teshuvos he wrote during his years as physician to the sultan in Cairo — was asked precisely this question by a correspondent: why do we not make a brachah on קריאת שמע על המיטה?

The Rambam's answer diverges from Tosafos in חולין. He does not cite the סכנה frame. Instead he says something structurally new — and Rabbi Ephrati flagged the significance sharply: קריאת שמע על המיטה is רשות, not חובה. It is not an obligation. And we do not make a brachah of "אשר קדשנו במצוותיו וצוונו" on a mitzvah that is not an obligation.

The Rambam's framework has real halachic weight. He is drawing an analogy to תפילת ערבית itself — originally רשות, only later accepted by כלל ישראל as חובה through practice. The bedtime Shema, on the Rambam's reading, remains in that earlier רשות category. Practically it is universal — every Yid should say it — but structurally it never crossed the threshold from רשות to חובה, and the brachah of "וצוונו" cannot be said on something one is not truly commanded to do.

D) A Subtle Second Question — Why Did the Rambam Need This?

Rabbi Ephrati pressed the question that Yossi in the kehillah raised. The Rambam's teshuvah is doing extra work. In הלכות ברכות the Rambam already accepts (per Tosafos in חולין) that we do not make ברכות on things instituted for סכנה. Why did he need to add, in the teshuvah, the further reason of "it is רשות"?

The most compelling answer — which Rabbi Ephrati flagged as speculative — is that the Rambam does not accept the Gemara's מזיקין frame at face value. The Rambam's philosophical framework, laid out in מורה נבוכים and elsewhere, is skeptical of the standard readings of מזיקין, שדים, and similar categories. If the Rambam does not read the Gemara's מזיקין as literal spiritual agents, then the סכנה explanation of Tosafos does not apply to him. He therefore needs a completely different reason for the no-brachah practice — hence the רשות frame.

On the Rambam's own reading, the reason to say קריאת שמע at bedtime might not be to keep מזיקין away at all. It might be to ensure that a Yid ends his day with words of Torah, so that his last conscious thought is a thought of תורה. The chiddush is characteristically Rambam — reframing an aggadic-mystical takana as a rational spiritual practice. And on that reframe, the practice is elevated but not commanded — which is exactly the רשות status the teshuvah names.

The Rambam's Life — A Preserved Detail Rabbi Ephrati took a moment to describe the Rambam's daily life during the years the teshuvos were being written, because the biography frames why the Rambam wrote so few teshuvos compared to other ראשונים of his era. The Rambam served as doctor to the sultan of Egypt. His day: rise before dawn, ride by donkey from his home to the sultan's palace, attend the sultan and his ministers throughout the day. He came home exhausted at night — Rabbi Ephrati preserved the Rambam's own words: "אויסגעמושט, אויסגעמאטערט" — completely worn out. At his home, lines of Yidden and non-Jewish patients would already be waiting outside his door. He would ask, sometimes, for a moment simply to eat a bite before beginning his evening consultations. "רחמנות עלי" — have mercy on me. But he never turned patients away. Rabbi Ephrati closed the aside with the sharpest possible frame: "That is why the Rambam is our Rebbe. Not because he was only a great תלמיד חכם and wrote the יד החזקה. He gave his neshama for Yidden. He never lived a single moment for himself." The Rambam wrote fewer teshuvos than his contemporaries because he had almost no time — which makes the teshuvos he did write, including the one on קריאת שמע על המיטה, all the more weighty.

X. Neshama Leaves the Guf — And R' Akiva

Rabbi Ephrati brought the sugya into its deepest frame: every night, the neshama leaves the guf. The pasuk from מסכת ברכות[14]: בְּיָדְךָ אַפְקִיד רוּחִי — "in Your hand I deposit my spirit." Sleep is not a partial rest of consciousness. Halachically and metaphysically, sleep is אחד מששים במיתה — one-sixtieth of death. The nightly ritual is a nightly rehearsal of the moment of death itself.

The Zohar HaKadosh[15] takes this seriously. Before the neshama returns to הקדוש ברוך הוא at death, the Yid must be מקבל עול מלכות שמים. Some are זוכה to say קריאת שמע as their final words themselves. Some are זוכה that their family will say it around them at the bedside. Some pass without ever hearing it. Rabbi Ephrati flagged this with care — we cannot judge; everyone had the way הקדוש ברוך הוא wanted them to leave this world. But the מיטב, the aspiration, is to arrive at the final moment with the words of קריאת שמע.

The emblem of that aspiration is רבי עקיבא at the end of מסכת ברכות[16]. As the Roman authorities were combing his flesh with iron combs, R' Akiva was reciting קריאת שמע, prolonging the word אחד. His תלמידים pressed him — even now, רבי? — and he answered: "מתי יבוא דבר זה לידי ואקיימנו" — all my life I have wondered when this pasuk of בכל נפשך would come to my hand, and now it has come; how could I not fulfill it? He was נפטר on the word אחד.

The bedtime Shema is the daily practice of that moment. Every night, when the neshama begins its ascent, the Yid says the same words R' Akiva said. It is the אב״ג of Jewish devotion — the practice into which every other spiritual discipline eventually collapses.

XI. Bilaam's Brachah — הן עם כלביא יקום

Rabbi Ephrati brought a striking derasha that Rashi[17] from the מדרש preserves on Bilaam's brachos, which took on unexpected contemporary resonance this week.

Bilaam wanted to curse Israel. הקדוש ברוך הוא, ברוב רחמיו וחסדיו, converted the curses to blessings. Every morning we say Bilaam's מַה טֹּבוּ אֹהָלֶיךָ יַעֲקֹב. Another of his brachos is הֶן עַם כְּלָבִיא יָקוּם וְכַאֲרִי יִתְנַשָּׂא[18] — behold, a people rises like a lioness and lifts itself up like a lion.

Rabbi Ephrati flagged that the Israeli military's most recent operation against Iran, which had begun days earlier, was named "כלביא יקום" — precisely from this pasuk. Rashi's derasha on the pasuk becomes newly urgent. What does it mean for a Yid to "rise like a lion"? Rashi from the מדרש: the Yid does not go to sleep until he consumes the spoils of war from his enemies. Before he can rise like a lion, he must have vanquished the enemies of the night — the מזיקין who tried to consume him — by consuming them first through the words of קריאת שמע at bedtime.

The parallel to the current war Rabbi Ephrati left standing without laboring it. Klal Yisrael is literally rising like a lioness against physical enemies. And every individual Yid, in the smaller theater of his own bedroom, is called to the same act: do not fall asleep before you have vanquished the mazikin who came to consume you. The military campaign and the bedtime Shema, on this reading, are two scales of the same act.

A Sober Note on the Present Moment Rabbi Ephrati preserved the moment openly — "just last night the operation started. We need a lot of רחמי שמים." The kehillah, like all kehillos in America and Eretz Yisrael this week, was carrying the ongoing casualties and the escalating war with Iran during the שיעור itself. Rabbi Ephrati did not push through it. He named it, and let the Bilaam-derasha carry the double meaning without commentary.

XII. The Rokeach — The Mazikim at the Bedside

The רוקח[19] — ר' אלעזר של גרמיזא, twelfth-century חסידי אשכנז — offers the most vivid image of what happens at bedtime. Drawing on a Midrash, the Rokeach says: if we had the eyes to see, we would see the מזיקין crowding around the person's bed at night, trying to destroy him, trying to be מקטרג on the ascending neshama, trying to interfere with the ritual passage from waking to sleep. They are not passive presences. They are actively working.

Rabbi Ephrati preserved the Rokeach's plea in first-person voice: "I am begging you — start tonight. Even if you did not say קריאת שמע על המיטה until now — start tonight. When you say the words, all the מזיקין stop. They say אוי — and they fly the heck out of here. They do not touch the person. That is the koach of קריאת שמע על המיטה."

XIII. How to Say It — על מיטתו vs. סמוך למיטתו

There is a practical machloikes on how to say קריאת שמע at bedtime. The Gemara says על מיטתו — upon his bed. What does this mean?

A) The Standard Reading — Literally On the Bed

The רוב ראשונים read על מיטתו literally. One may recite קריאת שמע while lying in bed itself, on one's side. Rabbi Ephrati acknowledged the practical reality: "I will be honest with you — as a father of four, as a husband, at two o'clock in the morning when I finally go to sleep, sometimes I am not getting up out of bed. I say it lying down." The mainstream halacha permits this לכתחילה, though there is a distinction: one may lie on one's side, but should not recite קריאת שמע flat on one's back with head facing up. (A separate halacha, Rabbi Ephrati noted parenthetically, forbids sleeping flat on one's back for reasons Rabbi Stein of Far Rockaway is currently blogging about; he flagged this as an aside.)

B) The Meiri's Chiddush — סמוך למיטתו

The מאירי[20] reads על מיטתו differently — סמוך למיטתו, close to one's bed. Not lying in it. The Meiri's language is sharp: do not read קריאת שמע על המיטה כדרך הדיוט — the way an ignorant person does it — בגילוי ראש (with the yarmulke off), in pajamas, without אימה, without יראה, without any real קבלה של עול מלכות שמים. The Yid must say it standing next to the bed, dressed, with the yarmulke on, with kavana. Then he may lie down.

The halacha does not follow the Meiri. Most poskim maintain that lying-in-bed recitation is לכתחילה. But Rabbi Ephrati preserved the Meiri's kavana-frame as the ideal — if you can rise for the moment, if you can say the words with real yiras shamayim before lying down, do so. The Meiri's aspiration is right even where his psak is not the mainstream.

XIV. How Much to Say — The 248 Words

How much of קריאת שמע must be said at bedtime? The Gemara does not specify explicitly, and the halacha has settled at multiple levels.

At the minimum, per the mainstream halacha on the Rabbeinu Tam-shitta, the first parasha (שמע ישראל plus ואהבת) suffices for the מזיקין function. This is the widespread practice among busy contemporary Yidden and is halachically defensible. Rabbi Ephrati flagged that saying only the first parasha is a serious step up from saying nothing, and encouraged the kehillah members who were מזלזל until now to be מקבל at least this level tonight.

At the ideal, the משנה ברורה[21] writes that לכתחילה one should say all three parshios plus the קל מלך נאמן preface. Why? Because the רמז is precise: קריאת שמע has 248 words, corresponding to the 248 evarim (limbs) of the human body. Each word of קריאת שמע protects a specific limb of the Yid. Kabbalistic frames on the 248-word alignment are extensive.

A structural detail worth flagging: standard קריאת שמע does not actually have 248 words. The count is short by three. קל מלך נאמן — a three-word phrase inserted at the beginning — completes the count to exactly 248. Rabbi Ephrati flagged this: the original purpose of the "קל מלך נאמן" preface was specifically for קריאת שמע על המיטה. That the siddurim also print it in front of shacharis-קריאת שמע when one is not davening with a tzibbur is a secondary extension of the original bedtime purpose.

XV. Women and קריאת שמע על המיטה

Reb Bernie in the kehillah asked about women's obligation. The question is structurally sharp. קריאת שמע is generally classified as מצות עשה שהזמן גרמא — women are exempt from the standard מעריב and שחרית recitations. Does the bedtime Shema follow the same rule?

Rabbi Ephrati brought the standard analysis. If the reason for קריאת שמע על המיטה were the mainline זמן-based obligation of ובשכבך, then yes, women would be exempt under the standard rule. But — critically — the actual reason for the bedtime takana is מזיקין, not the זמן-obligation. And on that reasoning, the rule is different. Women are as exposed to the מזיקין of night as men are. If anything more so — Rabbi Ephrati flagged the popular עולם quip "women are the מזיקין" — but seriously: whatever the cosmic geography of the night, women are within it.

The משנה ברורה[22] paskens accordingly: women should say קריאת שמע על המיטה every single night. Not necessarily the full three parshios — the first parasha, plus a few of the shemira-pesukim that follow in the siddur, is sufficient. The frame Rabbi Ephrati offered: your entire home is פור, your bedroom is פור, your sleep is a Yiddish shlof — the household's spiritual condition is directly downstream of whether both spouses said קריאת שמע at bedtime.

XVI. Opening the Sugya of הַמַּפִּיל

With about three minutes remaining in the שיעור, Rabbi Ephrati opened the sugya of הַמַּפִּיל — the brachah recited before falling asleep — but explicitly flagged that a full treatment would wait for next week.

A) The Basic Frame

הַמַּפִּיל is a brachah brought in ברכות ס' ע״ב[23] — ברוך אתה ה'... הַמַּפִּיל חבלי שינה על עיני ותנומה על עפעפי. It is a full brachah with שם ומלכות. Some in the kehillah say it every night; some do not say it at all; some say it without שם ומלכות. Rabbi Ephrati openly polled the room and noted his own past ambivalence with the brachah, going back to childhood.

B) After Chatzos — The Ben Ish Chai / Kaf HaChaim Machloikes

There is a striking machloikes on whether one may say הַמַּפִּיל if he goes to sleep after חצות. The בן איש חי[24] and the כף החיים[25] — following the מקובלים — hold that after חצות one should not say הַמַּפִּיל at all. The framework is kabbalistic and beyond the scope of tonight's treatment. Notably, the Kaf HaChaim records that he personally did not say הַמַּפִּיל after חצות for many years, following the mekubalim. Then, upon reviewing the standard poskim, he found no poskim make this distinction. He changed his practice and began saying it after חצות. Rabbi Ephrati flagged this as one of the most honest reversals in the Kaf HaChaim's writings.

C) The הפסק Problem

The deeper structural problem with הַמַּפִּיל — and the reason many contemporary Yidden do not say it — is the הפסק issue. The brachah is a ברכת המצוה before sleep. If the Yid recites the brachah and then cannot fall asleep, he has a serious problem: the brachah is now בטלה, or worse, לבטלה. The Rema[26] famously advises that if you cannot fall asleep after saying הַמַּפִּיל, you should keep saying קריאת שמע over and over until you do — a form of אילן להיתלות to preserve the brachah's status. But many contemporary poskim, and many contemporary Yidden, are choshesh: if I cannot fall asleep quickly, if I need to get up to check on a child, if my wife needs to talk to me, the brachah becomes complicated. The משנה ברורה[27] is explicitly cautious.

D) The ל"שב" בסוכה Analogy

Tosafos[28] anticipates the objection. Why is הַמַּפִּיל different from the brachah of לישב בסוכה that one says before sleeping in the sukkah? The analogy is real. One says the ליישב-brachah before sleeping in the sukkah, even though one may not actually fall asleep once inside. Tosafos accepts the analogy and holds הַמַּפִּיל follows the same structural principle: you make the brachah in good faith intending to sleep. If the sleep does not come, the brachah is not thereby retroactively invalidated.

E) The Bottom Line for This Week

Rabbi Ephrati's practical guidance, pending next week's fuller treatment: if you are the kind of person who reliably falls asleep quickly — say הַמַּפִּיל with שם ומלכות, especially before חצות. If you take sleeping pills, you have no excuse. If you are the kind of person who often lies awake and might need to be הפסק, then the חשש is real; either omit the brachah or say it without שם ומלכות. The full weighing of the sugya — the shitos in the mekubalim, the more recent poskim, the specific edge cases — will resume next week.

The arc of the שיעור in three movements. (i) Establishing why קריאת שמע על המיטה matters — from Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi in ברכות ה' ע״א, through the Beis Yisrael on the horizontal-בהמה vulnerability, through the Meiri's re-reading of mazikin as bad thoughts (with Rabbi Ephrati's frame of the smartphone/Claude era as a distinct-but-parallel danger), the Ben Ish Chai on killing-and-channeling, the Vilna Gaon on it being the most important thing you say every day, and the חרב פיפיות cutting both עולם הזה and עולם הבא. (ii) The halacha — Tosafos on the Rabbeinu Tam / Rashi split on whether plag-Maariv already discharged the חיוב, why no brachah is said (Tosafos in חולין on the סכנה frame; the Meiri's isolated position that one should in edge cases; the Rambam's teshuvah reframing the practice as רשות rather than חובה, with the biographical detail of his life as sultan's physician preserved). The Rokeach on the mazikim crowding around the bed. How to say it — Rashi's mainline "in bed on one's side" vs the Meiri's סמוך למיטתו-with-yarmulke-and-kavana. The 248 words plus קל מלך נאמן. Women per the משנה ברורה. (iii) Opening the sugya of הַמַּפִּיל — the Ben Ish Chai / Kaf HaChaim on after-chatzos (and the Kaf HaChaim's honest reversal), the הפסק problem, the sukkah analogy from Tosafos, and the practical guidance to be continued next week.

Underneath everything: the nightly moment when the neshama leaves the guf and the Yid rehearses death itself. בְּיָדְךָ אַפְקִיד רוּחִי. Rabbi Akiva said אחד as his final word — extending it, holding it, dying on it. The bedtime Shema is that same act, in miniature, every single night. Put the stupid phone down for three minutes, say the words, and give the neshama safely to הקדוש ברוך הוא until morning.

מראה מקומות

  1. גמרא ברכות ה' ע״א — רבי יהושע בן לוי on קריאת שמע על המיטה. — The foundational Gemara: even after one has fulfilled the daily obligation of בשכבך through the earlier מעריב recitation of קריאת שמע, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi teaches that one must recite קריאת שמע again upon lying down to sleep. The proof text is the pasuk from Tehillim 4 (see fn2). The Gemara continues: האומר קריאת שמע על מיטתו — מזיקין בדילין ממנו. The reason is thus explicitly given as protection from the מזיקין of night. ↩
  2. תהלים ד' פסוק ה' — רִגְזוּ וְאַל תֶּחֱטָאוּ אִמְרוּ בִלְבַבְכֶם עַל מִשְׁכַּבְכֶם וְדֹמּוּ סֶלָה. — The pasuk from David HaMelech that the Gemara in Berachos reads as the source-text for the bedtime Shema. The read: speak in your hearts upon your beds = recite קריאת שמע at bedtime. וְדֹמּוּ סֶלָה = only after that, silence the body and let the neshama ascend. The Gemara's reading of דומ״ו as silence-into-death-of-sleep is central to the framework of sleep as one-sixtieth-of-death that the שיעור developed in Section X. ↩
  3. The בית ישראל — ר' ישראל אלתר (Gerrer Rebbe, 1895–1977) — on the horizontal-בהמה frame. — The third Rebbe of Gur, son of the Imrei Emes. His reading: אדם walks vertical (head toward heaven); בהמה walks horizontal (head toward earth). At bedtime, the Yid's structural posture inverts from אדם-vertical to בהמה-horizontal. The מזיקין, the יצר, and the קליפות have easier access to the horizontal posture. קריאת שמע על המיטה is the defensive act at that specific structural moment. The exact citation in the Beis Yisrael's writings was not specified in the שיעור; the chiddush is preserved across the Gerrer chassidish literature. ↩

  4. The מאירי on ברכות ה' ע״א — mazikin as דעות כוזבות. — ר' מנחם המאירי (c. 1249–1315, Perpignan) — one of the great Provençal ראשונים, whose בית הבחירה is a systematic commentary on much of shas. His characteristic mode is philosophical-rationalist within a halachic frame. His reading of the Gemara's מזיקין in the bedtime-Shema sugya is representative: he re-frames the מזיקין as דעות כוזבות — destructive or false thoughts — that intrude into the still, undistracted mind of the pre-sleep person. The takana of קריאת שמע על המיטה is, on the Meiri's reading, a mental-defensive act. Rabbi Ephrati flagged the sharpness of this reading for contemporary life — see the callout in Section IV. ↩
  5. The בן איש חי — killing and channeling the mazikim to קדושה. — ר' יוסף חיים בן אליהו אל-חכם of Baghdad (1834–1909). His reading of the bedtime-Shema sugya is characteristically kabbalistic. The מלאכי חבלה created from the day's אבירות are not merely dispersed by קריאת שמע — they are killed and then resurrected and re-channeled toward the צד הקדושה. The energy is converted, not annihilated. Framework grounded in the Lurianic מסורה that the Ben Ish Chai worked within. Exact reference in the ספרי הבן איש חי was not specified in the שיעור. ↩
  6. The גר״א — קריאת שמע על המיטה as the most important thing said daily. — The Vilna Gaon (ר' אליהו, 1720–1797). His attributed statement — reported in his תלמידים' collections — that קריאת שמע על המיטה may be the single most important thing a Yid says every day. The framework: the next day's הצלחה in פרנסה, in learning, in every domain, is determined by whether the Yid ended the previous night with קריאת שמע. Exact source in the published Gra-mesorah literature not specified in the שיעור. ↩

  7. The גר״א on חרב פיפיות — cutting both עולם הזה and עולם הבא. — A parallel Gra-chiddush on the double edge. One edge of the sword cuts the מזיקין of עולם הזה — the harms directed at the Yid in this world. The other edge cuts the מזיקין of עולם הבא — the harms that stand between the Yid and his ultimate share. The single act discharges obligations and vanquishes threats across both worlds simultaneously. Exact citation not specified in the שיעור. ↩
  8. תוספות בברכות ב' ע״א ד״ה מאימתי — the shittah of רבינו תם. — The very first Tosafos in the entire Talmud Bavli, addressing exactly this sugya. Rabbeinu Tam's position — the mainstream halacha across אשכנז — is that from פלג המנחה onward it is already halachically considered night. On this reading, the plag-Maariv Yid is יוצא the חיוב of קריאת שמע at the earlier zeman. The bedtime Shema is therefore purely for the מזיקין function, not to make up for a missed חיוב. Rabbi Ephrati flagged that this is the first Tosafos in shas — the one the Gemara opens with, before anyone has been distracted by Shabbos or Eruvin — and a Yid should learn it carefully. ↩
  9. Rashi on ברכות ב' ע״א — the alternative reading. — Rashi's shittah (as reflected in his commentary at the opening of Berachos and as understood by the Tosafists) is that plag-Maariv does not fully discharge the חיוב דאורייתא of קריאת שמע. On the Rashi-reading, the bedtime Shema serves a dual function: מזיקין (per Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi) plus the actual קריאת שמע of ובשכבך for those who davened early. Rabbi Ephrati emphasized that Rashi is also at the very opening of the daf, not deep in the commentary — this is the first Rashi in shas. ↩
  10. משנה ברורה על שולחן ערוך אורח חיים סימן רל״ט — the practical psak. — The Mishna Berura's treatment of קריאת שמע על המיטה in סימן רל״ט and the surrounding simanim (רל״ח–רל״ט). לכתחילה: say the full קריאת שמע (all three parshios), accommodating both the Rabbeinu Tam and Rashi shittos. בדיעבד: the first parasha alone suffices for the מזיקין function on the Rabbeinu Tam-line. ↩
  11. תוספות חולין ק"ה ע"א–ק"ו ע"א ד״ה מים ראשונים — no brachah on things instituted for סכנה. — The Tosafos in חולין on the sugya of מים ראשונים and מים אחרונים. The structural principle: mayim achronim was instituted because of מלח סדומית — a dangerous salt that could cause blindness if transferred from the hands to the eyes. Because the takana is for סכנה rather than for a positive-obligation mitzvah, no brachah is recited. The principle extends by analogy: takanos instituted for סכנה — whether physical (mayim achronim) or spiritual (קריאת שמע על המיטה per the מזיקין framing) — do not carry a brachah. The Tosafos is the standard source for this principle across the ראשונים' literature. ↩
  12. The מאירי — chiddush that one should make a brachah in edge cases. — An isolated position of the Meiri, not adopted by other ראשונים. The Meiri's specific case: someone who had an אונס at מעריב and did not say ברכות קריאת שמע at that time. When he arrives home and needs to recite קריאת שמע without the surrounding structural ברכות, the Meiri holds he should recite the brachah of אשר קדשנו במצוותיו וצוונו לקרוא את שמע. Rabbi Ephrati flagged this as a real position preserved in the historical record but not accepted normatively. Exact reference in the מאירי's בית הבחירה was not specified in the שיעור. ↩
    The specific location of this Meiri chiddush — likely in his בית הבחירה on Berachos in the discussion around ברכות ה' — was not specified in the שיעור. The position is well-attested in the historical literature as a "Meiri-style isolated chiddush" but should be verified against the published edition of the Meiri (Mossad HaRav Kook or similar) before citing formally.
  13. תשובות הרמב״ם — קריאת שמע על המיטה is רשות, not חובה. — One of the Rambam's teshuvos, addressing precisely the question of why we do not make a brachah on the bedtime Shema. His answer diverges from the standard Tosafos-in-Chulin סכנה framework. Instead: the practice is רשות, not חובה — analogous to the Rambam's own framework on תפילת ערבית (originally רשות, only accepted later as חובה through practice). One does not make a brachah of "אשר קדשנו במצוותיו וצוונו" on a mitzvah that is not truly commanded. Rabbi Ephrati flagged the structural significance: the Rambam needed his own reason because his rationalist framework does not accept the Gemara's מזיקין at face value, so the סכנה-based reason of Tosafos does not apply to him. Exact citation in the standard editions of the Rambam's teshuvos was not specified in the שיעור. ↩

  14. The pasuk בְּיָדְךָ אַפְקִיד רוּחִי — Tehillim 31:6, quoted in the bedtime siddur. — The pasuk from תהלים פרק ל״א פסוק ו': בְּיָדְךָ אַפְקִיד רוּחִי פָּדִיתָה אוֹתִי ה' אֵל אֱמֶת. The pasuk is central to the bedtime liturgy and expresses the nightly deposit of the neshama with הקדוש ברוך הוא. The Gemara's framing of sleep as אחד מששים במיתה (Berachos 57b) is the conceptual ground on which the pasuk lands: every night rehearses the moment of death, and the deposit of the נשמה is real, not merely metaphorical. ↩
  15. זוהר הקדוש — קריאת שמע as the pre-departure act. — The Zohar's framework that before the neshama returns to הקדוש ברוך הוא at death, the Yid must be מקבל עול מלכות שמים. The nightly practice of the bedtime Shema is the daily rehearsal. The specific Zohar passage was not identified in the שיעור; the framework is developed extensively across the Zohar's treatment of sleep, death, and קבלת עול מלכות שמים. ↩
  16. גמרא ברכות ס״א ע״ב — רבי עקיבא's death, extending the word אחד. — The famous Gemara at the end of Berachos. As the Romans combed R' Akiva's flesh with iron combs, he was reciting קריאת שמע — reaching שמע ישראל ה' אלקינו ה' אחד, he prolonged the word אחד. His תלמידים asked whether even now he was still saying קריאת שמע. He answered מתי יבוא דבר זה לידי ואקיימנו — all his life he had wondered when the pasuk of בכל נפשך (with all your soul) would come to his hand for fulfillment. He was נפטר on the word אחד. The Gemara serves as the emblem of the bedtime Shema as a daily rehearsal of the moment of death. ↩
  17. רש״י על במדבר כ״ג פסוק כ״ד — הן עם כלביא יקום as not going to sleep until vanquishing enemies. — Rashi's commentary on the pasuk from Bilaam's brachos (see fn18). Drawing on the Midrash, Rashi reads the "rising like a lion" as: the Yid does not go to sleep until he consumes the spoils of war from his enemies. Before he can rise like a lion in the morning, he must have vanquished the enemies of the night. On the reading Rabbi Ephrati developed, this transposes precisely to the bedtime Shema: the מזיקין are the enemies of the night, and קריאת שמע על המיטה is the vanquishing. ↩
  18. במדבר כ״ג פסוק כ״ד — הֶן עַם כְּלָבִיא יָקוּם וְכַאֲרִי יִתְנַשָּׂא. — The pasuk from Bilaam's second brachah on Klal Yisrael. The IDF operation against Iran that had begun in early July 2026 was named "כלביא יקום" — directly from this pasuk. Rabbi Ephrati flagged the contemporary resonance and let the double meaning stand: Klal Yisrael rising like a lioness against physical enemies at the macro scale, and every individual Yid rising against the מזיקין at the micro scale of his own bedside. The military operation and the bedtime Shema are, on Rashi's derasha, two scales of the same act. ↩
  19. The רוקח — the mazikim crowding around the bed. — ר' אלעזר של גרמיזא (Worms) — the Rokeach (c. 1176–1238), foundational figure of חסידי אשכנז. His vivid framing of the pre-sleep moment: if we had the eyes to see, we would see the מזיקין actively crowding around the person's bed, trying to destroy him, trying to be מקטרג on the ascending neshama, trying to interfere with the ritual passage from waking to sleep. Rabbi Ephrati preserved the Rokeach's first-person plea: start tonight — when you say קריאת שמע, they say אוי and fly away. Exact citation in ספר הרוקח was not specified in the שיעור. ↩
  20. The מאירי — סמוך למיטתו, with yarmulke and kavana. — The Meiri's chiddush that על מיטתו means סמוך למיטתו — close to the bed, standing beside it, not lying in it. His language is sharp: do not read קריאת שמע בגילוי ראש (yarmulke off), in pajamas, without אימה ויראה — this is the way of הדיוט. Say it standing next to the bed, dressed appropriately, with kavana. The halacha does not follow the Meiri (mainstream permits lying-in-bed recitation), but the aspiration frame stands. Exact citation in the Meiri was not specified in the שיעור. ↩
  21. משנה ברורה סימן רל״ט — the 248-word framework. — The Mishna Berura's לכתחילה guidance: say all three parshios of קריאת שמע plus the preface קל מלך נאמן. The count is precise — קריאת שמע alone has 245 words; the three-word phrase קל מלך נאמן completes the count to 248, corresponding to the 248 evarim of the human body. Each word protects a specific limb. Rabbi Ephrati flagged that this exact count is the original purpose of the "קל מלך נאמן" preface — it was instituted for the bedtime Shema specifically, and only secondarily extended to the shacharis-קריאת שמע when one is not davening with a tzibbur. ↩
  22. משנה ברורה — women's obligation to say קריאת שמע על המיטה. — The Mishna Berura paskens that women should say קריאת שמע על המיטה every single night. The framework: קריאת שמע as a זמן-based obligation would exempt women (מצות עשה שהזמן גרמא), but the bedtime takana is instituted for מזיקין, not for the ובשכבך obligation, and women are as exposed to the מזיקין of night as men. The practical guidance: at minimum the first parasha (שמע plus ואהבת), plus a few of the shemira-pesukim printed in the siddur. Exact siman not specified in the שיעור. ↩
  23. גמרא ברכות ס' ע״ב — the brachah of הַמַּפִּיל. — The Gemara that establishes the brachah recited before falling asleep: ברוך אתה ה' אלקינו מלך העולם המפיל חבלי שינה על עיני ותנומה על עפעפי.... The full brachah is preserved in the siddurim. It carries שם ומלכות and is a full ברכת המצוה per the Gemara's language. The structural questions the שיעור opened around הַמַּפִּיל — after-chatzos, הפסק, ל"שב" בסוכה analogy — will be developed in next week's שיעור. ↩
  24. The בן איש חי — no הַמַּפִּיל after חצות. — The Ben Ish Chai, following the mekubalim, holds that after חצות one should not say הַמַּפִּיל. The framework is kabbalistic — the halachic-mystical calculus of the second half of the night differs from the first half in ways that affect the brachah. The specific reasoning belongs to a later סוגיא in the שיעור sequence. Exact reference in the ספרי הבן איש חי was not specified. ↩
  25. The כף החיים — the honest reversal. — ר' יעקב חיים סופר (Yaakov Chaim Sofer, 1870–1939, Baghdad → ירושלים) — author of the כף החיים on שולחן ערוך. The Kaf HaChaim records his own personal history with הַמַּפִּיל after חצות: for many years, following the mekubalim, he did not say it. Then, upon reviewing the standard poskim, he found no poskim make this distinction. He changed his practice and began saying הַמַּפִּיל after חצות with שם ומלכות. Rabbi Ephrati flagged this as one of the most honest reversals in the Kaf HaChaim's writings — a rare glimpse of a major posek publicly changing a long-held personal practice after re-examining the sources. ↩
    The exact siman in the כף החיים where this reversal is recorded was not specified in the שיעור. Verify against the standard editions of the כף החיים on the relevant siman in שולחן ערוך (likely around סימן רל״ט) before citing.
  26. רמ״א אורח חיים סימן רל״ט סעיף א' — if you can't fall asleep, say קריאת שמע again. — The Rema's practical guidance for the הפסק problem: if a person recites הַמַּפִּיל and cannot fall asleep, he should not simply lie there awake (which would render the brachah problematic). Instead, he should keep saying קריאת שמע over and over until he does fall asleep — a form of "chained recitation" that preserves the brachah's status by keeping the Yid engaged in shema-related dibbur until sleep comes. Rabbi Ephrati flagged this as an underappreciated Rema and encouraged the kehillah to know it. ↩
  27. משנה ברורה סימן רל״ט — caution on the הפסק problem. — The Mishna Berura's cautious framing: because of the הפסק problem, one who does not reliably fall asleep quickly should be שוישש about the brachah. Practical guidance depends on the individual — those who reliably sleep quickly may say הַמַּפִּיל with שם ומלכות; those who often lie awake or need to be הפסק for other reasons face a real חשש and might either omit the brachah or say it without שם ומלכות. The full working-through of the position will be developed in next week's שיעור. ↩
  28. תוספות — the ל"שב" בסוכה analogy. — Tosafos anticipates the objection to הַמַּפִּיל: why is it different from the brachah of לישב בסוכה that one says before sleeping in the sukkah, where one may also not actually fall asleep? Tosafos accepts the analogy: you make the brachah in good faith intending to sleep. If the sleep does not come, the brachah is not retroactively invalidated. The frame legitimizes הַמַּפִּיל under the same principle by which the ליישב-brachah legitimizes sukkah-sleep even when it does not happen. Exact location of the Tosafos (likely in ברכות or in סוכה on the parallel sugya) was not specified in the שיעור. ↩
