יְדִיד נֶפֶשׁ — מְקוֹרוֹ וְהִתְפַּתְּחוּתוֹ שֶׁל הַפִּיּוּט

Yedid Nefesh: Origin and Journey of the Piyut
A Detour from Hilchos Krias Shema — Hei Teves: Yahrtzeit of Rav Shlomo Molcho
Recorded 5 January 2025 (Hei Teves 5785) · Reworked 3 May 2026 · Mareh Mekomos upgraded 5 May 2026

A deviation from the regular seder ha'siddur on the occasion of the yahrtzeit of Rav Shlomo Molcho. The Rabbi traces the origins of Yedid Nefesh through the most extraordinary chapter of post-Expulsion Sephardic historiah: the Anusim Diego Pires becoming Shlomo Molcho; his self-circumcision and rise to talmid chacham; his prophecies before the Pope; his kiddush Hashem on Yud-Gimmel Teves 1532; the Beis Yosef's request to die al kiddush Hashem; and the secret tefillah of Rav Elazar Azkari and his chaverim — preserved as the piyut we now sing daily. The shiur closes with the piyut's journey into our siddurim, beginning with Rav Yaakov Emden.

I. Why a Detour Today?

The Rabbi opened with the announcement: today the luach records Hei Teves as the yahrtzeit of Shlomo Molcho. The olam deserves to be educated about him — and the connection to Yedid Nefesh is direct.

The Question — Why Did You Skip Yedid Nefesh?

Several talmidim — Bernie Nagelberg and others — had asked: between Korbanos and Pesukei DeZimra, in most siddurim today, sits the piyut of Yedid Nefesh. Why does the Rabbi skip it?

The Rabbi's rav, in fact, would not skip it. Aaron once suggested skipping Yedid Nefesh when they were rushing; the rav's answer: "No — we sing Yedid Nefesh at Shalosh Seudos; we don't skip it. Yedid Nefesh is my oneg Shabbos."

Where the Piyut Appears

Yedid Nefesh sits in our siddurim in three places:

  • Morning — between Korbanos and Pesukei DeZimra
  • Erev Shabbos — before Kabbalas Shabbos (especially Dati Le'umi, Yung Yisrael, Chabad, and the German Ashkenaz olam)
  • Shabbos afternoon — at Shalosh Seudos

The Rabbi grew up in his grandfather's shul in Petach Tikvah, where the Dati Le'umi olam sings it together every Friday night, followed by a flowing "na na nai nam..." niggun. "It is beautiful."

II. The Mechaber — Rav Elazar Azkari

The Author of Sefer Charedim

The piyut was composed by Rav Elazar Azkari, famous for the sefer Sefer Charedim. He lived in the days of the Arizal in Tzfas in the 1500s, and served as the shamash — the gabbai — of the Arizal's shul. To the olam he was a bal habayis; many considered him a "simpleton".

The Lag B'Omer Story (Hadras Zekenim)

The legend recorded in Hadras Zekenim on Hilchos Tisha B'Av[1] — one of the nosei keilim on the Shulchan Aruch — tells: the Arizal popularized the minhag of traveling on Lag B'Omer to Rabi Shimon bar Yochai in Meron. (The minhag exists today because of him.) On one Lag B'Omer, in the middle of the dancing in the chatzer by the me'arah of Rashbi, the Arizal suddenly pulled Rav Elazar Azkari into the inner circle and danced with him with intense levas, with great dveikus, for a long time. The talmidim were astonished — what business did this simple gabbai have at the center of the hilula?

Afterward, the Arizal revealed: "Rav Elazar Azkari is a gadol me'od ba'kabbalah. I saw Rabi Shimon dancing with him; I joined them." From that moment, the olam's perception of him changed radically.

Sefer Charedim — Yiras Hashem and Ahavas Hashem

In the middle of Sefer Charedim[2] — perek beis or gimmel — Rav Elazar Azkari writes that yiras Hashem and ahavas Hashem are two mitzvos that go hand in hand, and our generation needs to be mechazek in both. Reishis chochmah yiras Hashem — only from yirah can one arrive at ahavah, as the Chovos HaLevavos[3] and the mussar sefarim establish.

The Source of the Piyut

And then Sefer Charedim goes further: quoting the Chovos HaLevavos, he writes that part of love is expressing it constantly. To express love, one must say. So we say Tehillim; we express love to HaKadosh Baruch Hu. Additionally, says Rav Elazar Azkari, one must write love-songs — shirei ahavah to the Beloved. HaKadosh Baruch Hu is the Beloved. Yedid Nefesh is exactly this: a love-song expressing the love and kisufim of Klal Yisrael for HaKadosh Baruch Hu.

The Roshei Teivos — YHVH

The roshei teivos of the four stanzas spell the Sheim Havayah:

  • ידיד נפש
  • הדור נא
  • ותיקך יהמו רחמיך
  • הגלה נא

Not Originally Liturgy

Crucially: Rav Elazar Azkari never intended Yedid Nefesh as part of davening. He wrote it as piyut — poetry, not liturgy. He records: "I sing this song with a chevra shel chaverim" — the elite mekubalim of Tzfas, the talmidim of the Arizal. He never specified when, never said it should enter the tefillah. He was niftar, and for 130 years Yedid Nefesh appeared in no siddur.

III. Background — The Spanish Expulsion (1492)

To understand how Yedid Nefesh reached our siddurim, the Rabbi backed up to the 1500s.

Don Yitzchak Abarbanel

1492: the expulsion of the Jews from Spain — one of the worst tekufos in our history. The Rabbi pointed to the eyewitness account of Don Yitzchak Abarbanel, treasurer-minister to the king of Spain and one of the leaders of Spanish Jewry, in his hakdamah to Sefer Melachim[4]: hundreds of thousands of Yidden expelled, with nowhere to go; the wealthiest, most learned community in the diaspora — bankers, scholars, royal advisors — destroyed in six to eight years.

The Anusim / Conversos

Those who refused to publicly accept Catholicism were either killed or driven out. Those who could not leave became Anusim — Conversos. (The Rabbi paused on terminology: he refuses the term Marranos — "in Spanish that means 'pigs'" — and uses Anusim.) They lived as Catholics publicly and as Jews in basements; they baked matzah, made sederim, kept what they could. Marcus Lehmann's historical novellas captured this olam beautifully[18] (an old-time author whom "the young guys don't even know").

The Geulah-Yearning of the 1500s

Jews scattered through the Middle East, North Africa, and Greece. In this tzar-soaked moment, any vision of geulah became powerfully appealing. The Rabbi compared this to the post-Holocaust messianic atmosphere of 1948: in Mea Shearim Reb Shalom Shvadron would describe heimishe Yidden in kaftans dancing in the streets when the IDF trucks rolled in — their grandchildren today would deny the photo. "Today we are ultra-Orthodox with a fully formed ideology of Zionism. But you cannot judge the Jews of 1948 — and you cannot judge the Jews of 1500."

IV. Diego Pires — The Marrano in the Royal Court

Born 1500

Shlomo Molcho was born in Lisbon, Portugal (Lisbon) in 1500, eight years after the Expulsion, into a family of Anusim. He was baptized as an infant. He grew up as a Catholic; his parents told him he was Jewish, but no more — no bris milah, no Shabbos, no Yiddishkeit.

The Rise at Court

Extraordinarily bright and charismatic, he attended university and at age 22–23 became Secretariat of the Highest Appeals Court of Portugal under King Manuel III. His Portuguese name: Diego Pires. He worked at the king's side, surrounded by Portuguese politicians — knowing he was Jewish, but practicing nothing.

V. 1523 — David HaReuveni Arrives

The Mysterious Emissary

1523: at the Portuguese court appears David HaReuveni. Dark-skinned, looking Sefardi, speaking mostly Arabic with some Hebrew. To this day historians do not know his origin.

He claimed to come from a faraway desert. His brother — he said — was king of the Ten Lost Tribes, with 300,000 Jewish soldiers ready to fight. (The Gemara in Sanhedrin[5] records a machlokes on whether the Aseres HaShevatim will return; some place them in India, some in China — the Rabbi flagged it as a "Shavuos night" topic.)

The Strategic Vision

His proposed alliance: combine his brother's 300,000 men with the Christian armies of Portugal, Spain, and France — the Holy Roman Empire — against the Ottoman Empire, which then held Eretz Yisrael. The Christians and Jews would jointly conquer Eretz Yisrael and share sovereignty. HaReuveni's charisma was so powerful that the Portuguese king at one point committed to providing eight warships, ammunition, and soldiers (the original letters survive). He later withdrew, judging HaReuveni too "weird" — but provided letters of introduction to other European monarchs.

VI. The Self-Circumcision and the Bris Milah

At court HaReuveni met young Diego Pires. The impact was profound. The 23-year-old approached him and asked: "Make a bris milah for me." HaReuveni refused — to be discovered milah-ing Anusim would alert the king that he was reversing Ferdinand and Isabella's work, undoing all of malchus Catolikus's efforts. It would jeopardize the entire geo'politische mission.

Diego acted alone. He performed his own bris milah. He records this in his sefer Sefer HaMefoar[6] (online today on Hebrewbooks.org). He describes nissim in the healing — a doctor came, and within days the wound healed. He took the name Shlomo Molcho ("Molcho" — malchus; immediately some Jews suspected messianic pretensions) and committed to a life of Torah.

VII. From Marrano to Talmid Chacham

Rav Ovadya Sforno's Encouragement

He learned Torah and mitzvos and met Rav Ovadya Sforno, the great parshan on Chumash, who strengthened and encouraged him. But personal teshuvah was not the end. Molcho was so impacted by HaReuveni's vision that he joined the mission. He writes in Sefer Chayas Kinah[7]: "I am like a servant to his master. My master David HaReuveni leads me; I do his bidding." HaReuveni, who initially distanced himself from the new ger, eventually accepted him as a partner.

The Greatest Rebbi of the Era

In Greece Molcho met Rabi Yosef Taitatzik — the Maharit Tzitzit, frequently quoted by the Beis Yosef. The Rabbi noted: this single rav was the rebbi of:

  • The Alshich HaKadosh
  • Rabi Shlomo Alkabetz, the Mechaber of Lecha Dodi
  • The Beis Yosef

Molcho, the former Marrano, had penetrated the most elite circle of talmidei chachamim, mekubalim, and poskim of his generation — the very olam we follow today as Shulchan Aruch Jews.

The Beis Yosef's Drift Toward Kabbalah

The Beis Yosef — at least 12 years Molcho's senior — was so impacted by him that he began to delve into Torah ha'sigulah and kabbalah. From here flows the chain influencing the Arizal and Rav Chaim Vital. The Rabbi noted that Molcho was deeply involved in calculating the keitz — despite the Gemara in Sanhedrin's prohibition[8] — and had visions and dreams. The Beis Yosef's focus on Mashiach and geulah traces in part to Molcho's influence. The tekufah, says the Rabbi, was "re'uyah la'geulah" — but we were not zocheh.

The Tzitzis of Shlomo Molcho

Most remarkably — and this is where Molcho enters our daily halachah — the Beis Yosef in Hilchos Tzitzis[9] records the way Shlomo Molcho's tzitzis were tied — the specific kshorim — and brings it down l'halachah l'maaseh.

The Magen Avraham, writing some 250 years after the events, testifies that Shlomo Molcho's actual tzitzis still existed in his time. The Tosfos Yom Tov — whose kever is in Krakow near the Rema, the Rabbi noted (he had recently visited) — writes that he traveled to Prague specifically to see them, and confirmed the Beis Yosef's description. The Mishnah Berurah and the Mogen Avrohom both bring this down. Our tzitzis today are tied based in part on the way Shlomo Molcho tied his.

VIII. The Messianic Campaign — 100 Years Before Shabsai Tzvi

HaReuveni and Molcho traveled together from city to city, monarch to monarch. The Rabbi flagged the chronology: this is exactly 100 years before Shabsai Tzvi, the most infamous false mashiach in our history.

Opposition

Not every kehillah welcomed them. Salonika in Greece — a major post-Expulsion Sefardishe center — feared sin'as Yisrael from the political fallout of these two. The trauma of the Expulsion was fresh. Yet the support they garnered was substantial.

IX. The Prophet of Rome

Two Prophecies Fulfilled

In Italy, Molcho made two famous prophecies:

  • The Tiber River flood — he told the authorities a major flood was coming. One month later thousands died in the Tiber's flooding of Rome.
  • The 1532 Portugal earthquake — he prophesied to the Pope. It came to pass — a historic natural disaster on secular record, killing thousands.

Pope Clement

The Cardinals admitted him to an audience with Pope Clement VII. Clement was floored. He considered an alliance with the Jews and HaReuveni against the Ottoman Empire; he provided letters of introduction. There is even a tradition that the Pope saved Molcho from the Inquisition at one point. (The Rabbi: "There is a theory.") Ultimately the politics were too tangled; the alliance never materialized.

At the Gates of Rome — 30 Days

Before approaching the Pope, Molcho had sat for thirty days at the gates of Rome, dressed as a pauper, in torn clothing, fasting. Why? The Gemara in Sanhedrin[10] records: Rabi Yehoshua ben Levi asked Eliyahu HaNavi, "Where is Mashiach?" Eliyahu: "At the gates of Rome." Mashiach sits as a metzora, leper, changing his bandages — but unlike other lepers, one bandage at a time, replacing each before moving to the next. Why? Because at any moment HaKadosh Baruch Hu may command him to redeem Klal Yisrael; he must be ready to spring instantly into action. Rabi Yehoshua ben Levi went, found him, asked: "When are you coming?" Mashiach: "Today." Sunset came; he had not come. Eliyahu's explanation: there is a tnai — "hayom im b'kolo tishma'u" — it depends on teshuvah.

Molcho was following the trajectory. The Jews of Rome were speaking of this mystical Jew whose prophecies came true; with this reputation, he approached the Pope.

X. Yud-Gimmel Teves 1532 — Al Kiddush Hashem

Eventually Molcho was caught with HaReuveni in Mantua, Italy. HaReuveni disappeared into the dungeons; he was killed by the Inquisition in 1538. (One Portuguese national archive entry records that he converted to Christianity before death — but, the Rabbi noted, the Church wrote whatever it wished.)

In 1532, on Yud-Gimmel Teves, Shlomo Molcho faced a Inquisitorial court martial. He was sentenced to be burned alive. The historical records preserve the details:

  • They stuffed his mouth with cotton — so charismatic a speaker that the authorities feared even Christians would be swayed if he spoke before the flames.
  • Before the flames were lit, they removed the cotton and offered him one final chance: return to Catholicism — he had been born and baptized Catholic; he would be saved.

His Last Words

Quoted by the acharonim:

"I regret ever having been born and practicing Christianity. My nefesh is going to return to her Father in Shamayim."

He then screamed:

שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל ה' אֱלֹקֵינוּ ה' אֶחָד!

And on Yud-Gimmel Teves 1532 he was killed al kiddush Hashem.

XI. The Beis Yosef's Request

"I Want to Die Like Shlomo Molcho"

When the Beis Yosef (Rabi Yosef Karo) heard that his hero — Molcho, older than him but deeply admired — had died al kiddush Hashem, the news swept the Jewish world. The Beis Yosef declared: "I want to be zocheh to die al kiddush Hashem like Shlomo Molcho."

The Maggid

The Beis Yosef had a Maggid — an entity from Shamayim that revealed things to him. He recorded these revelations in his sefer Maggid Meisharim[11] — a real sefer, found in many libraries, not "some legend."

The Maggid told the Beis Yosef: "I hear you are davening to die al kiddush Hashem like Shlomo Molcho." The Maggid promised three times in Maggid Meisharim — in Parshas Noach, in Parshas Lech Lecha, and the very last entries of those parshiyos — that the Beis Yosef would be zocheh to die al kiddush Hashem, to be burned by the enemies of the faith — "like Shlomo b'chiri", "like My chosen one Shlomo".

The Problem

The Beis Yosef lived to 87. He was niftar on Yud-Gimmel Nisan, two days before Pesach, around 1573-1574 — more than 40 years after Shlomo Molcho's kiddush Hashem. He died peacefully in his bed. What happened to the Maggid's threefold promise from Shamayim?

XII. The Secret Tefillah of Rav Elazar Azkari

Why the Beis Yosef Could Not Be Allowed to Die Young

Rav Elazar Azkari was a talmid of the Beis Yosef and learned from the Arizal. He was born after Molcho's death, but knew the parshah well — knew the Maggid's promise, knew the Beis Yosef's wish.

Rav Elazar and his chevra reached a terrible realization: they could not allow the Beis Yosef to die young. Why? Klal Yisrael would not have a Shulchan Aruch. He needed to live long enough to compose his magnum opus. We follow Shulchan Aruch today precisely because the Beis Yosef's request was not fulfilled.

So Rav Elazar Azkari and his chevra dedicated time every day to daven that the Beis Yosef's request not be granted, that he survive long enough to write the Shulchan Aruch.

The Composition of Yedid Nefesh

The tefillah they wrote — the piyut they composed — is Yedid Nefesh.

The JTS Manuscript

The Jewish Theological Seminary in New York holds the actual ksav yad of Rav Elazar Azkari's Yedid Nefesh.[19] There are several differences between his original and the version printed in our siddurim today. The most striking: the third stanza of the original reads:

וְחוּסָה נָּא עַל בִּנְךָ יְחִידְךָ.
"Have mercy on Your only son."

"Bincha yechidcha" — Your only son — was a coded reference to the Beis Yosef: the only one who can write Shulchan Aruch for us. Klal Yisrael would need him for generations to come.

The Subsequent Edit

When b'siyata di'shmaya the tefillah was accepted and the Beis Yosef survived, Rav Elazar Azkari himself changed the text in subsequent printings. The danger was past; the remez was no longer needed. He shifted "bincha yechidcha" to:

וְחוּסָה נָּא עַל עַמְּךָ יְחִידְךָ.
"Have mercy on Your only nation."

Generalized — a tefillah for the tzibbur at large. Later printers found the original ksav yad and reverted to "bincha yechidcha". Most siddurim today carry the original text — but most who daven with it do not know that they are reciting a coded tefillah for the survival of the Beis Yosef.

XIII. Kiddush Hashem — Actualized vs. Accepted

The Lubavitcher Rebbe's Sichah

The Lubavitcher Rebbe addressed this parshah in a sichah on Chof Teves Tav-Shin-Lamed-Vav[12]: how gedolei ha'doros grappled with the question of the Beis Yosef's peaceful death versus the Maggid's threefold promise.

Rav Yaakov Chaim Sofer

Rav Yaakov Chaim Sofer, a great posek from Eretz Yisrael who was niftar approximately two years before this shiur, devoted an entire chapter to it[13]. His teretz: there is kiddush Hashem when one actually dies al kiddush Hashem; there is also kiddush Hashem when one accepts and commits to moser nefesh al kiddush Hashem, even if it does not come to pass.

His proof: the Sefer Chasidim[14] records that throughout the generations Jews were caught by hostile authorities, told they would be killed, accepted upon themselves moser nefesh al kiddush Hashem, and then were released. The Sefer Chasidim: it is considered as if they died al kiddush Hashem. The koach of accepting alone equals the koach of doing.

Rabbeinu Bachya — Chananyah, Mishael, and Azaryah

The Rabbi added a derech agav: in last week's parshah, Parshas Mikeitz, the parshah that includes the early midrashim on the Asarah Harugei Malchus: Rabbeinu Bachya[15] brings from Pirkei Heichalos a midrash: when the Gemara in Avodah Zarah[16] describes how Chananyah, Mishael, and Azaryah were killed — and we recite this in our kinos on Tishah B'Av and our avodah on Yom Kippur in Eileh Ezkerah — the midrash says: it never happened. A malach min ha'shamayim substituted someone else — Lupinus Caesar — and the three of them were spared. We don't know what became of them.

Why then do we commemorate them? Because they accepted upon themselves moser nefesh al kiddush Hashem. This is not the accepted view in our liturgy (we generally believe the harigah happened) — but the opinion exists in our mesorah.

XIV. Yedid Nefesh's Journey into the Siddur

Rav Yaakov Emden — 1730s

The first siddur to print Yedid Nefesh was Siddur Beis Yaakov[17] of Rav Yaakov Emden, the Yaavetz, in the 1730s — one of the most accepted siddurim in the Yiddishe olam. (The Rabbi: "All the old-timers know Reb Yaakov Emden's siddur.")

He placed it after Maariv, during or near Krias Shema Al Ha'Mitah. Not by Shalosh Seudos, not by Kabbalas Shabbos, not by Korbanos. His reason: his father, the Chacham Tzvi, said Yedid Nefesh every night before sleep. The Chacham Tzvi said it awakened his cheishek and his ahavah for HaKadosh Baruch Hu. The Yaavetz followed his father.

The Mekubalim of Tzfat — Late 1700s, Early 1800s

Some fifty years later a siddur appeared in Livorno, Italy with Yedid Nefesh. From there, in the late 1700s and early 1800s, the mekubalim of Tzfas began incorporating it more broadly:

  • In the morning, between Korbanos and Pesukei DeZimra — said to be "good to say every morning".
  • Before Kabbalas Shabbos — claimed (with uncertain mesorah) to have been said by the Arizal.
  • At Shalosh Seudos — purely as a mizmor to sing.

A Matter of Minhag

The Rabbi distinguished Shalosh Seudos from tefillah: "Shalosh Seudos doesn't bother me — sing whatever mizmor you want!" But inserting an extra piyut into tefillah proper is more involved. The Rabbi noted that the olam's history of inserting piyutim into the machzor is a long, much-debated parshah; Yedid Nefesh in the morning is no more than 150–200 years old. "It is in the siddur, so I say it. Why not? It is a beautiful thing."

XV. The Paradox — Reuveni and Molcho

The Rabbi closed with a she'eilah: why is David HaReuveni consigned to the "dustbins of history" as a mashiach sheker, while his partner Shlomo Molcho — equally involved in the same messianic movement — is venerated as a talmid chacham whose halachah we follow?

The answer lies in Molcho's transformation into a genuine talmid chacham. Despite the messianic activities, despite calculating the keitz, despite the visions and dreams — his limud, his kabbalah, his halachah, his tzitzis, his death al kiddush Hashem — earned him a place among the gedolim. HaReuveni, lacking limud, is forgotten. Molcho is b'kohol kadosh.

XVI. Closing

The Rabbi closed: "Now you know the origins of Yedid Nefesh. It had nothing to do with tefillah originally. Through the influence of the mekubalim, it made its way into the siddur. Next time you say Yedid Nefesh, you will have a deeper appreciation and feeling for what you are saying — because you are saying a tefillah for the survival of the man who gave us the Shulchan Aruch."

Physical Remnants in Prague[20]

The Altneu (Altneu Shul) — the Maharal's shul in Prague — preserves in its Jewish Museum a flag of Shlomo Molcho, inscribed with pesukim from Tehillim — the messianic standard he carried from court to court.

The Meisel Synagogue in Prague preserves another flag — inscribed with "Maccabi — Mi Kamocha Ba'eilim Hashem" — and his tachrichim, his burial shrouds.

The Rabbi: "One day we will go to Prague."

  11. Beis Yosef (R' Yosef Karo), Maggid Meisharim — entries on Parshas Noach and Parshas Lech Lecha. — The sefer recording the Maggid's revelations to the Beis Yosef. The threefold promise that the Beis Yosef would be zocheh to die al kiddush Hashem "like Shlomo b'chiri" — a promise that, by the plain peshat of his life, was not fulfilled. The unfulfilled promise drives the entire parshah of Yedid Nefesh. ↩
  12. Lubavitcher Rebbe, Sichos Kodesh, Chof Teves 5736 (1976). — Discussion of how gedolei ha'doros grappled with the Beis Yosef's peaceful death versus the Maggid's threefold promise. Frames the she'eilah the next teretz resolves: kiddush Hashem through accepting upon oneself the moser nefesh, even unfulfilled in deed. ↩
  13. Rav Yaakov Chaim Sofer, Kaf HaChaim ha'Chadash / Toras Yaakov / collected chiddushim (general reference; the posek from Eretz Yisrael, niftar ~2 years before this shiur). — Devotes an entire chapter to the she'eilah. Teretz: there is kiddush Hashem when one actually dies al kiddush Hashem, and there is also kiddush Hashem when one accepts upon oneself moser nefesh, even where it does not come to pass. The koach of accepting alone equals the koach of doing. ↩
  14. Sefer Chasidim (R' Yehudah HaChasid). — Examples of Jews caught by hostile authorities, told they would be killed, who accepted upon themselves moser nefesh al kiddush Hashem, and were then released. The Sefer Chasidim's ruling: it is considered as if they died al kiddush Hashem. The textual anchor for Rav Yaakov Chaim Sofer's teretz on the Beis Yosef's peaceful death. ↩
  15. Rabbeinu Bachya al haTorah, Bereishis (Parshas Mikeitz), citing Pirkei Heichalos. — A striking midrash: Chananyah, Mishael, and Azaryah's harigah never happened; a malach min ha'shamayim substituted Lupinus Caesar; the three of them were spared. We commemorate them not for the harigah in deed but for accepting moser nefesh al kiddush Hashem. Not the accepted view in our liturgy (which assumes the harigah happened) but a real opinion in our mesorah. ↩
  16. Avodah Zarah י״ח ע״א (18a). — The classical peshat account of the harigah of Chananyah, Mishael, and Azaryah; the sugya recited in kinos on Tisha B'Av and in Eileh Ezkerah on Yom Kippur. The textual baseline against which Rabbeinu Bachya's midrash from Pirkei Heichalos stands as alternative. ↩
  17. Siddur Beis Yaakov / Siddur Yaavetz (R' Yaakov Emden), 1730s — placement at Krias Shema Al Ha'Mitah in the name of his father the Chacham Tzvi. — The first printed siddur to include Yedid Nefesh. Placed not by Shalosh Seudos, not by Kabbalas Shabbos, not by Korbanos — but at Krias Shema Al Ha'Mitah, on the precedent of the Yaavetz's father the Chacham Tzvi, who said it every night before sleep to awaken his cheishek and ahavah for HaKadosh Baruch Hu. The other placements are 50–100 years later, from the mekubalim of Tzfas and Livorno. ↩
  18. Source-type note (historical-fiction): Rabbi Marcus Lehmann (1831–1890) — Orthodox German rabbi and educator; author of historical novellas dramatizing Jewish life in galus, including extensive treatment of the Anusim of Spain and Portugal. Mentioned by the Rabbi as background olam-knowledge for the Anusim milieu in which Diego Pires grew up. Not a halachic source; cited as illustrative literary-historical reference.

  19. Source-type note (manuscript): The original ksav yad of Rav Elazar Azkari's Yedid Nefesh, held at the Jewish Theological Seminary in New York. The third stanza in the original reads "v'chusah na al bincha yechidcha" — a coded reference to the Beis Yosef as "Your only son", the only one who could write Shulchan Aruch. After the tefillah was accepted and the Beis Yosef survived, Rav Elazar Azkari himself revised it to "al amcha yechidcha" — generalized to the tzibbur. Most printed siddurim today carry the original "bincha yechidcha" (later printers reverted to the ksav yad); most who recite it are unaware of the encoded petition.
    ↩
  20. Source-type note (artifacts): Physical remnants of Shlomo Molcho in Prague:

    • Meisel Synagogue (Prague) — second flag, inscribed "Maccabi — Mi Kamocha Ba'eilim Hashem"; and his tachrichim (burial shrouds).
