Rabbi Ephrati · ערב חנוכה Drasha

The Light That Never Dims

פרשת ויגש, יוסף הצדיק as איש מצליח, and the מנורה in the Tunnels of עזה
Recorded 14 December 2025 · Reworked from the original דרשה

This week's drasha is short and not on our usual sugya of תפילה. As חנוכה approaches I want to share a single message — about what success means in Jewish terminology, and about a connection between פרשת ויגש, a photograph from 1932 in Kiel, and a video from a tunnel in עזה in 2024.

I. A Story of Tears

I want to begin with a deeply moving encounter that transformed my understanding of what חנוכה really means. I once sat together with an eighty-five-year-old Holocaust survivor. He had been a young child during the שואה. As we learned together, I shared a story about מסירות נפש during the שואה — how yidden went up in flames על קידוש השם — and I found myself overcome.

This elderly survivor took my hand and asked, "Why are you crying?" I said, "רבי, this is an amazing story. I have never heard such a thing from a survivor; it is deeply moving."

He looked at me with eyes that had seen unimaginable horrors and said something I will never forget: "You know, I do not cry anymore. When I was a child — I do not remember all the details — there was one time an SS officer asked me a question. I did not speak German well, only Yiddish, and I did not respond properly. He beat me. I was just a ten-year-old child. This was 1943 or 1944. I looked up at that SS officer on that ראש השנה and I realized something. I did not shed a tear. I could not cry."

Then he recited the words we say in הלל: המלאך הגאל אתי מכל רע — the מלאך who redeemed me from all evil.[1] "Every time I say הלל, I think of this. השם, You saved me. You pulled me out of that valley of death. But You also took away my capacity to cry. I have seen so much in my life. I cannot cry anymore."

Then he gripped my hand and said with intensity: "You should cry." And we wept together. Here in Woodmere, this encounter gave me a new understanding of what חנוכה is about.

II. The Measure of Success — יוסף הצדיק

This week's פרשה contains a quiet but stunning detail. The תורה gives one of our great heroes — יוסף — a unique title. Throughout כל התנ״ך, the תורה never calls anyone else an איש מצליח — a successful man. Not אברהם אבינו, not יצחק, not יעקב, not משה, not אהרן, not דוד המלך. Only יוסף.

We all want to know what success is. We want our parenting to be successful, our families, our careers. We are constantly chasing it. Wouldn't it be worthwhile to look in תנ״ך at how the תורה defines the word מצליח? Three times the תורה calls יוסף "מצליח." Three times it says ויהי ה' אתו[2] — and that ה' was with him made him successful. The question is: when? In what context?

The Three Phases of יוסף's Life

When you actually look, the answer is unbelievable. יוסף's life splits into three phases.

Phase one — the years of light. His younger years, basking in the light of his father. Learning by יעקב — imagine learning by the best רבי of all time. Learning תורה, learning the secrets of the universe, sitting with his family, basking in his father's love. He was seventeen, learning with extraordinary חשק, beloved by his father. Surely that is success. A seventeen-year-old learning תורה with the greatest רבי in the world — surely that is "מצליח." The תורה does not use the word.

Phase three — the years of power. From age thirty onward. He interprets פרעה's dreams, predicts the famine, saves the world from starvation. פרעה loves him because he sustains the entire world. He is the most powerful person in מצרים after פרעה himself, in the great superpower of antiquity. Surely that is success. The תורה does not use "איש מצליח."

Phase two — the years of darkness. Look at the middle chapter, between seventeen and thirty. He is thrown into a pit. There is hatred between him and his brothers. He is sold into slavery. He ends up in the house of פוטיפר. אשת פוטיפר fabricates a blood libel against him. He is thrown into a dungeon. Throughout this darkest stretch — the pit, slavery, the prison — the תורה says three times: מצליח, מצליח, מצליח.

If that is success, you might think I do not want any of it. In a pit? In a dungeon? Why does the תורה specifically use this term then?

The Secret

Look at the פסוקים carefully. Always preceding the word "מצליח" is ויהי ה' אתו — Hashem was with him. רש״י, quoting a מדרש,[3] explains: יוסף used to come into his master פוטיפר to serve him — cleaning, feeding the animals, doing slave's work — and as he came in, he was mumbling, whispering something. פוטיפר saw it. מצרים was the country of black magic and sorcery; that was מצרים. So פוטיפר became afraid: "This Jew is coming in here mumbling — abracadabra — who knows what could happen?" He demanded, "What are you saying? Are you doing כישוף on me?"

יוסף answered: "No. שם השם is regularly on my mouth. Thank You, Hashem, for putting me here. Please, ה', help me find favor in my master's eyes. Thank You for the past. Please help me in the future." He was constantly davening, constantly thanking, constantly connecting to הקדוש ברוך הוא.

This is where we yidden get it from. We say "ברוך השם" all day long. Count how many times you say "thank You, Hashem" in an hour. It is in every other sentence. Where did we get it? From יוסף. Even in the worst situation a person can be in, every breath was: "thank You, Hashem. I love You, Hashem. Please help me do this. Help me find my mission in this hole." That is יוסף, and that is true success.

A Lesson for our Children This is what we should teach. You want to be successful? No matter where you find yourself in life, if you have יראת שמים — if שם השם is on your lips — if you know you have a purpose, an awareness that ה' is here with you, that no matter what happened He is with you, that you are here for a mission — that is success. That is why the תורה calls יוסף, in that very chapter of darkness, three times מצליח. That is genuine, authentic Jewish success.

III. A Gift From Heaven — The מנורה in the Tunnel

It is hard to use the words "gift from heaven," but yes — for us, living, breathing yidden, it is a gift that they decided to publicize the footage of those six hostages reciting the ברכות and lighting the מנורה in the tunnel complex in עזה[4].

Every hostage's story is meaningful. But specifically these six lighting the מנורה before חנוכה — it has come out now, and people are watching it. What is the message?

It is very emotional to watch six hostages who, until that moment, had been secular yidden — and ה' please help me say the truth — who were more frum, more ehrlich, more Jewish than all of us put together. They are lighting the מנורה in a tunnel and it moves us.

What They Did

They are lighting the מנורה. Fulfilling the מצוה of פרסומי ניסא. They look very frail and thin. This was two חנוכות ago, about ten months after October 7. We now know they were shot at close range, all six of them, around August 2024.

They are saying the ברכות, smiling, singing. And then there is a comment, I believe by Hersh Goldberg, listen carefully. He says in Hebrew: אתם זוכרים את התמונה? — "Do you remember the picture?" He is referring to a famous photograph: a מנורה by a window with a Nazi flag right across the street. You all know the picture.

IV. The Story Behind the Picture — Kiel, 1932

There was a רב in Germany, in the city of Kiel, in 1932. His name was Rabbi Dr. Akiva Baruch Posner — a doctor of philosophy from a well-known German university, and chief rabbi of the city. The Nazis came to power and established their regional headquarters in Kiel directly across the street from his home.

It was ליל שבת, the last night of חנוכה, Friday evening. As he was about to light the מנורה, his wife — Rebbetzin Rachel Posner — grabbed a camera (cameras were not common back then) and decided to take a photograph of her husband lighting. From her angle, standing behind him, you see the מנורה in the open window, and right there across the street, that big, dark Nazi flag.

She took the picture. On the back, as people did in those days, she wrote the date and the setting. In German she wrote, in essence: Chanukah 1932/1933 — "Death to Judah," so says the flag. "Judah will live forever," so responds the light.[5]

"Death to Judah" — that was Nazism. The eternal flame of the יהודים will be eternal no more. But the light of the מנורה, the flickering flames, show the vibrancy of כלל ישראל — that we are here forever, no matter what.

V. Our Connection Today

We do not need only stories from the שואה anymore. We do not need only the legends of the past. We have something far closer to our hearts. We were all so invested emotionally in the plight of these hostages. They did not come out alive. But by lighting the מנורה — and I do not know which חמאס terrorist filmed that video, but he did us a great favor — when we light the מנורה tomorrow night, when we light the נרות חנוכה every single night this חנוכה, when we make the ברכות, when our neighbors see through our windows, when we gather with our children and our families around the flickering flames — we will feel that connection.

We will feel it from generation to generation. No matter if it is Nazi Germany, no matter if it is חמאס in עזה, no matter who our enemies are — whoever is trying to extinguish our flame — our light, our candle, will never be extinguished.

VI. How? What Is the Secret?

Because we are a nation that is successful. And what is success? Success is: no matter where I am, ה' put me here. ה' is with me. "Thank You, Hashem" — in the dungeon, in the pit, constantly knowing that ה' is with me, realizing it, feeling it, knowing that I am on a mission.

Someone asked me after I shared this in shul: so why didn't they get out? Why were they shot? It is a story of our national mission. When you zoom into the individual, who can understand? Who are we to understand ה''s ways? But one thing we know: our nation's light will never be extinguished.

And if you want to give that over to the next generation, to bequeath it to your children — remember: success in Jewish terms means שם השם on your lips. In a tunnel, in a pit, lighting the מנורה, reciting the ברכות, knowing that ה' is with you. May ה' be with us, and may we share only שמחות together.

  1. בראשית מ"ח:ט"ז — "המלאך הגאל אתי מכל רע יברך את הנערים". — יעקב אבינו's blessing of אפרים and מנשה, asking the מלאך who redeemed him from all evil to bless the children. The phrase entered the standard קריאת שמע על המטה in many סדורים; the elderly survivor's reading is reinterpretive — claiming the המלאך הגאל redeemed him from the שואה but also took his capacity to weep. ↩
  2. בראשית פרק ל"ט — three uses of איש מצליח for יוסף. — The פסוקים: ויהי ה' את יוסף ויהי איש מצליח (ל"ט:ב'); וירא אדניו כי ה' אתו וכל אשר הוא עשה ה' מצליח בידו (ל"ט:ג'); ויהי ה' את יוסף... וכל אשר הוא עשה ה' מצליח (ל"ט:כ"ג). Three uses of מצליח — all in the chapters of slavery and prison, never in the years of light or of power. The יחיד in תנ"ך to receive the title איש מצליח. ↩
  3. רש"י על בראשית ל"ט:ג'; מדרש תנחומא, וישב סימן ח'; cf. בראשית רבה פ"ו ה'. — רש"י: "שם שמים שגור בפיו" — the name of הקב"ה was constantly on יוסף's lips; this is what פוטיפר noticed and wrongly suspected as כישוף. The מדרש develops this as the secret of יוסף's success in slavery — constant דיבוק with הקב"ה through speech. The שיעור connects this to the Jewish מנהג of saying ברוך השם reflexively in conversation, traced back to יוסף הצדיק. ↩
  4. Hostage tunnel footage of נרות חנוכה lighting in עזה (December 2023).
    Note: Footage released publicly in late 2025 shows six hostages — among them Hersh Goldberg-Polin and others — kindling makeshift נרות חנוכה in a Hamas tunnel during חנוכה תשפ"ד (December 2023), reciting the ברכות and singing. All six of the hostages depicted in the footage were murdered by their captors in late August 2024 in a tunnel in Rafah. The video is preserved in IDF and family-released archives. The שיעור treats the public release of the footage as a Heaven-sent פרסומי ניסא for our generation.

  5. Posner family Chanukah photograph, Kiel, 1932 — preserved at Yad Vashem. — Rabbi Dr. Akiva Baruch Posner (1890–1962) served as chief rabbi of Kiel until 1933, when the family fled to ארץ ישראל. The photograph was taken by his wife, Rebbetzin Rachel Posner, on ליל שבת חנוכה 1932 (eighth night). The original German inscription on the back, in Rebbetzin Posner's hand: "Chanukah, 5694 / 'Tod dem Juda,' sagt die Fahne; 'Juda lebt ewig,' antwortet das Licht" — "'Death to Judah,' says the flag; 'Judah will live forever,' answers the light." The original photograph and inscription are preserved in the Posner family collection and at יד ושם; widely reproduced today as one of the most iconic images of pre-שואה European Jewish defiance. ↩
