מכניסי רחמים and the Question of Intermediaries

Special סליחות-Season Shiur of Rabbi Ephrati
Recorded 14 September 2025, after the first night of סליחות תשפ"ו  ·  Reworked 3 May 2026

A sensitive סוגיא: may we daven to מלאכים? May we say מכניסי רחמים? The רמב"ם's absolute prohibition; the ירושלמי on direct access to the מלך without intermediaries; the רמב"ן's דרשה calling מכניסי רחמים כפירה; the חתם סופר's personal practice and the מהר"ל's defense by changing one letter; the מהרי"ט אסאד on not publicly censuring those who say it; the גר"א, Reb Chaim Volozhiner, and Rav Moshe Feinstein's father all refusing; and the crucial distinction between asking a צדיק and asking a מלאך, with the beautiful answer of רבינו יעקב בר יקר (the רבי of רש"י) that מכניסי רחמים never meant מלאכים at all.

I. From שים שלום to the מלאכים

We had nearly completed ברכת שים שלום two Sundays ago. During that שיעור we noted a fascinating point about the ancient נוסח אשכנז: the original חתימה was not "המברך את עמו ישראל בשלום" but rather "עושה השלום". אשכנז still preserves this נוסח — but only during ימים נוראים.

The ספרדים explain: the word "השלום" has a גימטריא of 381, equal to the name of the מלאך סָפְרִיאֵל — the angelic scribe (his name from the root סופר) charged on יום הכפורים with inscribing לחיים or, חס ושלום, otherwise. "עושה השלום" on ימים נוראים is read as a way to invoke the רחמים of סָפְרִיאֵל.

This points us straight at the live, sensitive question: are we permitted to daven to מלאכים? Can we ask a מלאך for רחמים?

II. מכניסי רחמים Itself

Last night[1], Reb Hershi led a beautiful סליחות. At the close he sang מכניסי רחמים. The question presses: to whom are we davening? We are asking the מלאכים to bring our רחמים in, to advocate on our behalf. Is this not תפילה to מלאכים?

III. The רמב"ם — Absolute Prohibition

The רמב"ם[2] states unequivocally that requesting anything from, or davening to, anyone other than הקדוש ברוך הוא is כפירה and עבודה זרה. The principle is foundational to אמונה and to the negation of עבודה זרה: only to Him do we daven.

Yet יהודים have been saying מכניסי רחמים for over a thousand years. How does that fit with the רמב"ם?

IV. The ירושלמי — Direct Access

The ירושלמי[3] draws a sharp contrast. Servants of an earthly מלך בשר ודם cannot reach their ruler directly — they must go through ministers, courtiers, intermediaries. We are different. Every single one of us has direct access to the מלך מלכי המלכים. We do not need שרים or מלאכים to speak to Him. The ירושלמי: a person should never say "I need this מלאך or that מלאך" — go straight to הקדוש ברוך הוא.

This appears to negate מכניסי רחמים outright.

V. The רמב"ן's דרשה

The רמב"ן הקדוש was not only a towering תלמיד חכם but a gifted דרשן. His דרשה for ראש השנה — the famous "תורת ה' תמימה"[4] — runs four to five hours when read aloud.

The רמב"ן takes up our question. The גמרא in שבת י"ב ע"ב[5] forbids asking מלאכים because of the terrible חשש of עבודה זרה — attributing power to separate entities rather than recognizing them as manifestations of הקדוש ברוך הוא. The רמב"ן writes plainly[6]:

Do not say מכניסי רחמים. Remove it from your מחזור. Remove it from your סליחות. מכניסי רחמים is כפירה.

VI. The חתם סופר and the מהר"ל

The Question to the חתם סופר

The חתם סופר[7] was asked by his תלמיד the מהר"ם אש: how can we continue to say מכניסי רחמים? The חתם סופר brings the מהר"ל's answer.

The מהר"ל's One-Letter Solution[8]

The מהר"ל: requesting from מלאכים is forbidden. So change the נוסח. Instead of "הַכְנִיסוּ רחמינו" — a request — say "יַכְנִיסוּ רחמינו": a command. One letter difference. We are not davening to the מלאכים; we are commanding them. They have no independent כח. But because we believe the מלאכים elevate our תפילות upward through the מסכים מבדילים — the spiritual barriers our עברות create — we instruct them to do so. Asking is forbidden; commanding is not.

The חתם סופר's Personal Practice

The חתם סופר writes:

I did not want to change the נוסח. יהודים have said מכניסי רחמים since the very first סידור — the סידור of רב עמרם גאון[9]. But I myself did not wish to say it, as it borders on כפירה per the רמב"ן and the מהר"ל.

His personal practice: at the close of סליחות he would fall into נפילת אפיים and recite a long, drawn-out תחנון. While the קהל sang מכניסי רחמים, he continued his תחנון, finishing exactly when they did. No one noticed; he did not wish to make an issue of it publicly. He held it improper to say, and at the same time refused to impose his view on others.

VII. The מהרי"ט אסאד — Do Not Censure Publicly

The מהרי"ט אסאד[10], a תלמיד of the חתם סופר, was asked about a man who stood up in shul during סליחות and banged on the בימה, declaring that anyone who says מכניסי רחמים is a כופר.

The מהרי"ט אסאד answered sharply: to say such a thing publicly is wrong[11]. Yes, there is a קושיא — the מהר"ל's, the רמב"ן's. But to alter the נוסח publicly and brand those who recite it כופרים is unacceptable.

VIII. The Litvish Position

The גר"א and Reb Chaim of Volozhin

Because of this סוגיא, the גר"א and רב חיים מוולוז'ין did not say "ברכוני לשלום" on Friday night, since it involves speaking to מלאכים.

Rav Moshe Feinstein's Father

Rav Moshe Feinstein[12] records about his father, Rav Dovid Feinstein, the חסידישע רב in Uzda (near Minsk in Belarus — interestingly, Rav Dovid was a מתנגד who served as רב for the חסידים; common in those days):

My father never said ברכוני לשלום in shul. My father never said מכניסי רחמים. My father never recited any of the סליחות that involve requesting from מלאכים. He held strongly to this position.

IX. צדיקים vs. מלאכים — The Crucial Distinction

This raises an obvious question: if we may not request from מלאכים, why is it permitted — even encouraged — to go to a צדיק or a רב and ask for a ברכה?

The גמרא[13] and the ראשונים describe a long-standing מנהג — recorded by רבי יוסף אבן חביב[14] in the late 1300s — that in צרפת, when a family member was ill, they went to a תלמיד חכם to ask רחמים; the תלמיד חכם davened on their behalf. Throughout תנ"ך we see the same: people went to נביאים when they needed a ישועה; they were דורשי ה'. Today, without נביאים, the מנהג is to go to a חכם and ask for a ברכה.

How is this not the same as asking a מלאך?

X. Three Answers

Answer 1 — רבינו יעקב בר יקר: מכניסי רחמים Means צדיקים

רבינו יעקב בר יקר[15] — the רבי of רש"י — wrote a פירוש on תפילה. On מכניסי רחמים he gives a completely different reading:

מכניסי רחמים does not refer to מלאכים at all. It refers to the צדיקים of the generation — the living תלמידי חכמים who are supposed to protect the generation and to serve as advocates on behalf of כלל ישראל. When we say מכניסי רחמים, we are asking these צדיקים to bring our תפילות in with their own תפילות.

This entirely sidesteps the issue. The תפילה is to צדיקים, not to מלאכים; the קושיא dissolves.

Answer 2 — The Halachic Distinction

Some ראשונים draw the distinction this way:

צדיקים in death are still considered alive: "צדיקים במיתתם קרויים חיים"[16]. So one may speak to a צדיק even after his passing, certainly while he is alive.

The reason we cannot ask a מלאך is the חשש of טעות: people might mistakenly think הקדוש ברוך הוא delegated His power to independent entities. That risk leads to עבודה זרה. With a צדיק, no one will make such a mistake. No one thinks the רבי is God; no one believes the צדיק has independent power to grant ישועה. At most, the צדיק elevates the request and brings it before הקדוש ברוך הוא. No חשש of עבודה זרה.

However: if someone does come to think the רבי has independent power, that is a serious problem in its own right.

Answer 3 — מהרי ברונא: A Display of שפלות

מהרי ברונא[17] offers a beautiful פשט:

Perhaps we are not really asking anything of the מלאכים, חס ושלום. Rather, by invoking them, we are demonstrating our שפלות and ענוה. We feel so humbled and embarrassed after a week of סליחות — who are we to approach הקדוש ברוך הוא directly?

So we turn בתחפיסה to the מלאכים — like servants approaching a minister before the king — not because we believe the מלאכים have power, but to express how low and unworthy we feel. מכניסי רחמים on this reading is not a request at all; it is the קהילה's ענוה dressed in liturgical form.

XI. Synthesis

The סוגיא is sensitive. The רמב"ם's absolute principle stands; the רמב"ן's warning stands; the מהר"ל's one-letter shift to "יכניסו" is one path; the חתם סופר's personal abstention coupled with public restraint is another; the גר"א's and Reb Chaim Volozhiner's silence, and Rav Moshe's father's silence, sit at the strict end. רבינו יעקב בר יקר's reading — that מכניסי רחמים never meant מלאכים in the first place — and מהרי ברונא's reading of שפלות are routes by which כלל ישראל has continued to say it without compromise to אמונה.

What does not stand: branding those who recite the נוסח as כופרים in public. The מהרי"ט אסאד's ruling closes that door.

XII. Closing

May we be זוכה to enter the ימים נוראים with תשובה, תפילה, and צדקה — confident in our direct connection to the רבונו של עולם, while honoring the sacred נוסחאות handed down across our generations.

This is invoked in this סוגיא to distinguish requests directed at צדיקים (which are not classified as תפילה לזולת ה') from requests directed at מלאכים (which may risk the חשש of עבודה זרה). ↩

His שו"ת contains the פשט that מכניסי רחמים is not literally a request directed at מלאכים but a public expression of שפלות — using the פיוט as a vehicle to display the קהל's sense of unworthiness to approach הקב"ה directly after a week of סליחות.
