ויכולו, the Marriage of שבת, and מגן אבות

From the Tur's reading of the three Shabbos tefillos as קידושין · חתונה · ייחוד, through Reb Shloimke of Zhvill stopping Rommel at ויכולו, to the long mesorah of the נשמות in shul Friday night
Recorded 14 June 2026 · כ״ח סיון תשפ״ו · Reworked from the original שיעור · Altered version — editorial corrections applied

Last week's שיעור opened the long sugya of שמונה עשרה של שבת with the central new piece — ויכולו on Friday night. This week the שיעור walked deeper into the same seam, then opened the entire architecture of מגן אבות. The arc: a careful אליה רבה on the בו / בה / בם gematria = 57 (the count of weekday ברכות "missed" each Shabbos); the Ashkenaz-vs.-Sefard machloikes on ישמחו במלכותך and the underlying distinction between שמחה and עונג on שבת; the טור in סימן רצ״ב (with the Beis Yosef's expansion in רצ״ג) reading the three Shabbos tefillot as a three-stage marriage between כלל ישראל and שבת (אתה קדשת = קידושין, ישמח משה = חתונה, אתה אחד = ייחוד); the famous מעשה of Reb Shloimke of Zhvill stopping Rommel at ויכולו in the summer of 1942, when the Afrika Korps stood at El Alamein — roughly ninety miles east of Alexandria, with the Suez Canal as the gate to ארץ ישראל; and the long Magen Avos thread — Rashi's מזיקין, the Pesachim Gemara on אגרת בת מחלת, and the stunning chiddush of the מחזור ויטרי: the מזיקין are the נשמות of the deceased who come to shul Friday night, when גהינום does not burn. From there: the Sefer Chassidim's מעשה of the man who fell asleep in shul, the Or Zarua's Bunim of Worms and the כתר עלים, Reb Bunim of Pshischa's "you idiot, it's the new gabbai practicing," the Belzer Rebbe's "close your eyes," the Rav of Siedlce's "walk backward," and the Bnei Yissaschar's psak: a מנהג of hundreds of years is קדוש, and disbelieving it is אפיקורסות.

I. Returning to אתה קדשת — Where the Rishonim Skip the First Part

We pick up where we left off last week. Before the עמידה pivots to ויכולו, we say the opening of the קדושת היום ברכה: אַתָּה קִדַּשְׁתָּ אֶת יוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי לִשְׁמֶךָ, תַּכְלִית מַעֲשֵׂה שָׁמַיִם וָאָרֶץ, וּבֵרַכְתּוֹ מִכָּל הַיָּמִים וְקִדַּשְׁתּוֹ מִכָּל הַזְּמַנִּים, וְכֵן כָּתוּב בְּתוֹרָתֶךָ. And then the pesukim of ויכולו.

A small but telling detail: many ראשונים[1] recite ויכולו starting from וַיְכֻלּוּ הַשָּׁמַיִם וְהָאָרֶץ — they skip the introductory יוֹם הַשִּׁשִּׁי entirely. The reasoning is structural. The opening אתה קדשת ... וברכתו ... וקדשתו has already declared that הקדוש ברוך הוא blessed שבת and sanctified it. The core עדות of the unit is the act of blessing and sanctifying — and that core is captured precisely in וַיְבָרֶךְ אֱלֹקִים אֶת יוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי וַיְקַדֵּשׁ אֹתוֹ. The opening pesukim are introductory; the central declaration is the one tied verbatim to the language of the ברכה.

כלל ישראל's accepted נוסח is to recite the full text, beginning from יוֹם הַשִּׁשִּׁי. The ראשונים' alternative reading is preserved as a window into how the unit was originally heard.

II. The אליה רבה — The Gematria of 57

The אליה רבה[2] brings a striking רמז. Across the three Shabbos tefillot — מעריב, שחרית, מנחה — we say a parallel form, with one letter shifting each time:

וְיָנוּחוּ בוֹ כָּל יִשְׂרָאֵל · וְיָנוּחוּ בָה כָּל יִשְׂרָאֵל · וְיָנוּחוּ בָם כָּל יִשְׂרָאֵל

Voh, voh, vom. Three small concluding suffixes. The אליה רבה adds up their gematria across the three tefillot and reaches fifty-seven. The weekday עמידה has nineteen ברכות; we daven it three times a day; nineteen times three is fifty-seven. Every Shabbos we miss fifty-seven weekday ברכות.

On the אליה רבה's reading, the three small concluding suffixes — voh, voh, vom — structurally fill the gap. The Shabbos עמידה does not just shorten the davening; it encodes the missing fifty-seven brachos in the very letters by which the kehillah completes the central ברכה. Nothing is truly lost. The שיעור flagged this as cute — Rabbi Ephrati was honest that he does not fully understand its significance — but as a textual phenomenon it is precise.

III. ישמחו במלכותך — Is There a דין שמחה on שבת?

An immediately practical question. According to נוסח ספרד, we recite יִשְׂמְחוּ בְמַלְכוּתְךָ שׁוֹמְרֵי שַׁבָּת וְקוֹרְאֵי עֹנֶג on Friday night, at שחרית on Shabbos morning, and at מוסף — but not at מנחה. According to נוסח אשכנז, we recite it only at מוסף. Why the difference?

A) The Underlying Issue — שמחה vs. עונג

The split runs through a structural halachic question: is there a formal דין שמחה on שבת? The רמב״ם[3] is explicit that יום טוב has a דין שמחה. The שיעור walked through how it is fulfilled: בָּשָׂר וְיַיִן, buying one's wife בגדים ותכשיטין (the שיעור invited some uncomfortable laughter at "You buy your wife jewelry every Yom Tov? I'll have a talk with her"), giving children קליות ואגוזים and special Yom Tov nash.

שבת, by contrast, is structurally a דין עונג — not a דין שמחה. עונג and שמחה are conceptually distinct in הלכה (the precise distinction is a separate sugya the שיעור flagged for another day, but the existence of the distinction is uncontested). On Yom Tov: שמחה. On Shabbos: עונג.

B) The Ashkenaz Position — שמחה Only at מוסף

נוסח אשכנז follows the view that since there is no formal דין שמחה on שבת, the words יִשְׂמְחוּ בְמַלְכוּתְךָ are out of place in three of the four Shabbos tefillot. They are recited only at מוסף — for a structural reason that has nothing to do with שבת as such. פרשת בהעלותך[4] teaches וּבְיוֹם שִׂמְחַתְכֶם וּבְמוֹעֲדֵיכֶם ... וּתְקַעְתֶּם בַּחֲצֹצְרֹת עַל עֹלֹתֵיכֶם וְעַל זִבְחֵי שַׁלְמֵיכֶם — the bringing of a קרבן is itself an occasion for שמחה. The מוסף תפילה corresponds to the קרבן מוסף; the קרבן carries its own דין שמחה independent of the day. So even on שבת, מוסף carries שמחה — and only at מוסף do we say ישמחו.

C) The Sefard Position — The Sifrei

נוסח ספרד points to a ספרי[5] that reads the same pasuk inclusively: "וּבְיוֹם שִׂמְחַתְכֶם" — אֵלּוּ הַשַּׁבָּתוֹת. The "day of your gladness" referenced in the pasuk includes the Shabbatos. On that reading, there is a form of שמחה on שבת after all, and ישמחו is at home in every Shabbos tefillah.

D) The Common Ground — No ישמחו at Mincha

Even within Sefard, ישמחו is omitted at מנחה. Why? Because, according to many opinions, מנחה זמן of שבת afternoon is the time at which משה רבינו was נפטר. It is not a time conducive to expressing שמחה, even within the broader framework. In its place we recite the solemn צִדְקָתְךָ צֶדֶק — three pesukim from תהלים marking the petirah of Moshe Rabbeinu through language of divine justice. The שיעור flagged this as the conceptual ground for a longer treatment at Mincha in a later week's שיעור.

IV. The טור in סימן רצ״ב — The Three Tefillot as Marriage

This is the central conceptual chiddush of the sugya, and Reb Ezriel Dovid in the kehillah brought it up before Rabbi Ephrati could get to it. The טור[6] asks a structurally striking question: why does each of the four Shabbos tefillot have a different middle ברכה?

On יום טוב, the עמידה is uniform across מעריב, שחרית, and מנחה — the same נוסח, the same opening of the קדושת היום ברכה. Only מוסף diverges (and even there, only for the structural reason of the קרבן מוסף). On שבת, by contrast, each of the four tefillot has a different opening. Friday night: אַתָּה קִדַּשְׁתָּ. Shabbos morning: יִשְׂמַח מֹשֶׁה. Mincha: אַתָּה אֶחָד וְשִׁמְךָ אֶחָד. Musaf: תִּכַּנְתָּ שַׁבָּת. Why?

A) The Midrash — Shabbos Has No בן זוג

The טור's answer reaches into a famous מדרש[7]. When הקדוש ברוך הוא created the days of the week, He paired them: Sunday with Monday, Tuesday with Wednesday, Thursday with Friday. Shabbos was left without a partner — without a בן זוג. הקדוש ברוך הוא declared that כלל ישראל would be the spouse of שבת.

On the Tur's reading, every Shabbos is therefore a renewal of a sacred marriage between כלל ישראל and שבת קודש. The three weekday-side tefillot — מעריב, שחרית, מנחה — correspond to the three classical stages of a Jewish marriage:

B) Friday Night — אתה קדשת = קידושין

The opening word is exact. "אַתָּה קִדַּשְׁתָּ" — "You sanctified." The same root as קידושין. The Friday-night עמידה is the kehillah's act of betrothal: the קידושין that begins the marriage. We are setting apart שבת as our partner — and שבת is set apart for us. The verb is mutual.

C) Shabbos Morning — ישמח משה = חתונה

Shabbos morning is the wedding itself. "יִשְׂמַח מֹשֶׁה בְּמַתְּנַת חֶלְקוֹ" — Moshe rejoiced in the gift of his portion. The mode is שמחת חתן וכלה: the dancing, the celebration, the public consummation of the marriage that the קידושין initiated the night before. The framing fits the day: שחרית on Shabbos is, structurally, the highest expressive moment of the week — קריאת התורה, the long כל ישראל unit, the public יציאת ספר תורה.

D) Shabbos Afternoon — אתה אחד = ייחוד

And then מנחה: "אַתָּה אֶחָד וְשִׁמְךָ אֶחָד" — You are One, and Your Name is One. The language is the language of ייחוד — the seclusion of the couple after the חופה, the moment of complete union. The Shabbos afternoon mode is private, interior, quiet, דבק. The setting is the shabbos shenusa — the slowing, the descending of the day, the lengthening shadows. דּוֹדִי לִי וַאֲנִי לוֹ.

The וינוחו בו / בה / בם Progression The same שיעור pressed an associated chiddush that Rabbi Ephrati flagged he had never fully understood. The form of "may all Israel rest in it" shifts across the three tefillot: וְיָנוּחוּ בוֹ Friday night → וְיָנוּחוּ בָה Shabbos morning → וְיָנוּחוּ בָם at מנחה. Some ראשונים read the progression through the marriage frame: Friday night the kallah is still at her father-in-law's home (the masculine "in him" — בו); Shabbos morning she is rejoicing with the חתן in their own home (the feminine "in her" — בה); Mincha is the couple together (the plural "in them" — בם). Rabbi Ephrati was candid: "I tried understanding it, I don't get it, I looked at many sources. But I know the קדמונים say this." The acknowledgment is itself meaningful — the conceptual structure is preserved even where its application is opaque.

V. ויכולו's Power — Reb Shloimke of Zhvill and Rommel

Last week's שיעור laid the conceptual ground: saying ויכולו makes a person a שותף with הקדוש ברוך הוא in מעשה בראשית.[8] The מהרש״א[9] sharpens the conceptual frame: were it not for כלל ישראל declaring the emunah in shul every Friday night that הקדוש ברוך הוא created the world יש מאין and rested on the seventh day, the world would not know its Creator. The recitation of ויכולו is the engine by which the awareness of creation is continually renewed in the world. The partnership the גמרא names is the structural reflection of this ongoing act of testimony.

This week's שיעור brought the most striking single illustration of that כח: the מעשה of ר' שלמה מזוויל[10] (Reb Shloimke of Zhvill, נפטר 1945) — one of the great צדיקים in ארץ ישראל in the World War II era, mentioned in this kehillah because Reb Shloime Hutler in our shul is his einikel.

A) The Setting — June 1942

In the summer of 1942, Erwin Rommel, ימח שמו וזכרו, was leading the German Afrika Korps east through Egypt toward the Suez Canal and ארץ ישראל. By late June Rommel was at El Alamein — roughly ninety miles west of Alexandria, the immediate next target. The British line at El Alamein was the last defensive position before the Suez. If El Alamein fell, Alexandria fell; if Alexandria fell, the Canal fell; if the Canal fell, ארץ ישראל fell. The actual driving distance from El Alamein to Tel Aviv is in the order of 350 miles, but the conceptual distance — the number of dominos between Rommel and Tel Aviv — was vanishingly small. The Yishuv knew it.

The שיעור preserved a quiet personal detail: "My grandmother who grew up there could say the stories how scared they were. And they all had cyanide pills on them." The fear of total annihilation that European Jewry was already living was about to repeat in ארץ ישראל itself. השם ירחם.

B) The מעשה

On a Friday night in that period, a group of frantic Yidden ran to Reb Shloimke after davening with the news: they're about to enter, it's going to happen over the weekend.

Reb Shloimke's first response was — "Now you tell me? You should have told me before davening. By ויכולו I could have been מכוין." Why? Because at ויכולו every Yid becomes a שותף with הקדוש ברוך הוא. As a שותף one has a certain leverage. With the proper כוונה in that exact moment I could have nullified the gezeira. The diagnosis was the diagnosis of a צדיק who treated the structural Friday-night moment as a halachically operative window of intervention.

Urged to do something nonetheless, Reb Shloimke had not yet made קידוש. He ran back to the מקוה ("I never heard of someone going in the מקוה between davening and קידוש on a Friday night"), toveled, came home, said קידוש with intense כוונה at ויכולו, and turned to his chassidim afterward. "Rommel is not coming. It's fine." And כך הוה.

C) The Aftermath — Three Hours from Tel Aviv

After the war, Yidden in ארץ ישראל tried to identify the moment that the German advance had been halted. They concluded: Al Alamein. The שיעור preserved the line as "three hours away from Tel Aviv" — the actual driving distance is more like five-to-seven hours by mid-20th-century roads, but the conceptual "שלוש שעות" frame is what mattered for the mesorah that followed. The גר״א[11] reads the line in הושענות — שלוש שעות — not as three hours but as a unit of time. The later mesorah developing the Gra's framework (rather than the Gra's own writings — see fn11) extends the reading to the eschatological case: לעתיד לבא, when גוג ומגוג will be nuclear war, the duration of total civilizational annihilation will not be three hours but three moments. שלוש שעות in the long Hoshanos plea is, on this later reading, our request to be saved from those three minutes.

The שיעור tied the threads together openly. The world is not what we think it is. There are tzaddikim who can turn back armies through ויכולו on a Friday night. There are prophetic readings of nuclear war embedded in centuries-old הושענות. The connective tissue runs straight from the structural moment of the שמונה עשרה of שבת into the literal history of the survival of the Yishuv in 1942.

VI. מגן אבות — The Quasi-חזרת הש״ץ of Friday Night

After ויכולו we move to a new structural piece — the ברכה מעין שבע, opening with מָגֵן אָבוֹת בִּדְבָרוֹ. The שליח ציבור says it; the kehillah responds. The unit is structurally strange. Ordinarily מעריב has no חזרת הש״ץ. The שיעור reminded us: this is one of the practical reasons Maariv was historically treated as רשות — there was no public repetition to anchor the obligation.[12] And yet on Friday night, suddenly, we have a quasi-חזרת הש״ץ.

The structural shape is itself a mini-עמידה. מגן אבות corresponds to the first ברכה (אבות). מחיה מתים corresponds to the second. המלך הקדוש corresponds to the third (with the special חתימה of שבת). המניח לעמו ביום שבת קדשו stands in for the middle ברכה (קדושת היום). נודה לשמך corresponds to הודאה. אדון השלום corresponds to שלום. Seven sub-units folded into one ברכה. Hence the name: ברכה מעין שבע — "a ברכה after the pattern of the seven."

Who needs a חזרת הש״ץ at מעריב, specifically on Friday night?

VII. The Gemara's Reason — The Latecomers and the Sakana

The גמרא בשבת דף כ״ד[13] gives a precise structural reason. In the medieval reality the שיעור has returned to many times: בתי כנסיות were located outside the residential center of the town, often in open country. A latecomer Friday night would still be davening שמונה עשרה when the kehillah finished — and the kehillah would scatter home, leaving the latecomer alone in shul. To then walk home alone, in the dark, was a סכנה.

Rashi specifies the nature of the danger: סכנה מפני המזיקין. The kehillah was therefore enacted to stay an extra few minutes. The vehicle of the extra few minutes was Magen Avos — a structured public unit that ensured the latecomer would catch up and finish at the same time as the kehillah, so that everyone would leave together. The שיעור's framing: "next time you walk out of shul Friday night, walk with someone."

VIII. What Are the מזיקין? — Rambam vs. Rashi

The single word מזיקין in Rashi opens onto the largest philosophical divide in the ראשונים. Two readings, and the שיעור treated them with the care they deserve.

A) The Rambam — Mundane Danger

The רמב״ם famously did not believe in שדים or non-physical מזיקין of the night.[14] On the Rambam's reading, the סכנה here is ordinary: ליסטים, גנבים, רוצחים — people on the road at night with bad intent. Walking alone home late, in the dark, with no electricity and no streetlamps, was simply unsafe. The kehillah's enactment of Magen Avos kept the kehillah together so that the walk home would be in a group.

The שיעור used a careful word for the Rambam's approach. Not "rational" — Yehuda in the kehillah pushed back here — but "super-rational". The Rambam was not a rationalist in the modern sense of disbelieving the supernatural; he was a חכם reading every layer of the world with maximal precision. Where the תורה does not require positing non-physical agents, the Rambam does not posit them. Where it does, he does. "We are not a superstitious people. If it's אמת we believe it. If it's not אמת, it's שטותים."

B) Rashi — Real מזיקין

Rashi's reading takes מזיקין at face value. There are non-physical agents in the night, the Gemara has names for them, and the סכנה at Maariv is from them — not from highway robbers. The most direct grounding for Rashi's position is in פסחים קי״ב ע״ב[15]: there is a female שד named אגרת בת מחלת who roams every Wednesday and Friday night accompanied by eighteen רבוא (180,000) מלאכי חבלה, each of whom has רשות לחבל. The Gemara is explicit. This is our Gemara, Rabbi Ephrati emphasized; whatever one believes about שדים in general, this passage cannot be dissolved by Rambam-style readings.

C) But Abaye Already Nullified Her

And yet that same Gemara has a sharp twist. אביי בא ובטל לה — Abaye came along and nullified Agrath bat Machlat's כח. After Abaye, the שיעור observed, you can take a stroll with your wife on a Friday night and nothing bothers you. Yet we still say Magen Avos. The ראשונים on the sugya — the ריטב״א[16], the ר״ן, the רא״ש — give the standard answer: מנהג אבותינו בידינו. The original danger was removed, but the תקנה stood.

That is the standard answer. The שיעור's central new piece is a different answer, from one of the deepest of the early Ashkenazic ראשונים.

IX. The מחזור ויטרי's Chiddush — נשמות in the Shul

A) The Author

The מחזור ויטרי[17] was written by רבינו שמחה מויטרי — Rabbeinu Simcha of Vitry-le-François in France, a primary תלמיד of רש״י and connected to him by chasunah (Rabbeinu Simcha's son married a granddaughter of Rashi — a sister of Rabbeinu Tam, Rashbam, and Rivam). The שיעור flagged that we are talking about the inner family of the school of Rashi. The מחזור ויטרי is one of the most important siddurim and halachic-liturgical works of the era — the בית יוסף quotes it constantly in אורח חיים.

In his treatment of Friday-night Maariv, Rabbeinu Simcha records what he heard from his רבי:

B) The Reading

Rashi told him — and Rabbeinu Simcha preserves it — that the מזיקין Rashi referenced are not generic נושאי תפקיד; they are the נשמות of the deceased who come to the shul on Friday nights. Why Friday night specifically? Because גהינום does not burn on שבת. The pasuk in שמות ל״ה[18] — לֹא תְבַעֲרוּ אֵשׁ בְּכֹל מֹשְׁבֹתֵיכֶם בְּיוֹם הַשַּׁבָּת — is read structurally: the prohibition extends to גהינום itself. The fires of גהינום are off; the נשמות that ordinarily are under their pressure are released. And where do they go? The same place a Yid goes for מנוחה: the shul.

Specifically, says the Machzor Vitry: תוך י״ב חודש — within the first twelve months after a person passes away — the neshama returns to this world Friday night. The shul is full of נשמות. Don't get stuck in shul alone after davening: not because robbers are waiting outside, but because the inside of the shul is occupied by the world of the dead.

A Parallel — The Eleven-Month Kaddish The Machzor Vitry's "first twelve months after a person passes away, the נשמה comes back" frame is precisely the window that drives the well-known halacha of saying קדיש for eleven months rather than twelve. The standard ground for the eleven-month practice: the full twelve-month post-petira dispensation is the period during which the משפט of a רשע is presumed to extend; reciting קדיש for the full twelve months would implicitly classify the נפטר as a רשע. We stop at eleven to avoid the implication. The Machzor Vitry reads the same twelve-month window from the opposite side: the נשמה is back in this world during that period, with structural access to the shul on Friday night when גהינום does not burn. Both halachic phenomena — the eleven-month קדיש ceiling, the twelve-month Friday-night שבת presence — are responses to the same metaphysical reality: in its first twelve months the נשמה has not yet completed its post-petira passage and lives, halachically, in a state of unsettled access between worlds.

C) Why a סכנה

The Machzor Vitry's framing of the סכנה is sharp. The נשמות of צדיקים are not מזיק; they are at peace and bring peace. The concern is specifically the נשמות of רשעים, who carry their unresolved דין into the shul and can affect a living person who lingers among them. Encountering them, especially being among them alone, can be physically harmful. The שיעור acknowledged plainly: in Europe for centuries Yidden were afraid even to walk past a shul on Friday night. The fear was real and the מסורה behind it was specific.

X. The Mesorah of Stories — Sefer Chassidim and the Or Zarua

A) ספר חסידים — The Man Who Fell Asleep in Shul

Is there a wider source in the ראשונים for the Machzor Vitry's framing? The שיעור brought two. The first is in ספר חסידים[19], the great work of רבינו יהודה החסיד of Regensburg (12th–13th century, ~800 years ago).

The מעשה: A Yid stayed late in shul to learn. The other מתפללים left. The שמש, not realizing he was still there, locked the door. The man fell asleep over his sefer. At חצות he woke. The shul was full — full of people wrapped in טליתים. He looked at them and recognized them. This one died two months ago. This one died a year ago. The שיעור gave the exact texture: the man knew them by name. Among the נשמות he also saw two people from the city who were still alive.

He ran out of the shul terrified. Within a week the two still-living people he had seen there had died.

B) Or Zarua — Bunim of Worms and the כתר עלים

The שיעור's second source: the אור זרוע[20] — ר' יצחק of Vienna, mid-13th century. Every word of the Or Zarua is like Moshe MiSinai, Rabbi Ephrati emphasized. The Or Zarua's name and word carry enormous weight in normative halacha; many of our standard practices today come from him. He writes a מעשה in his own name from a named source.

Shamati meharav Bunim — Rabbi Ephrati flagged the name with delight, noting that Bunim as a name we associate with later (Reb Simcha Bunim of Pshischa, etc.) was already in use in Ashkenaz in the 13th century. This Bunim of ווערמייזא (Worms) was a member of the חברא קדישא — a man whose daily work involved the dead.

One day Bunim went to shul extra early — still dark outside, to say תהלים before davening. He entered the shul and saw, sitting and learning, a man wearing a crown of leaves. He could see immediately that the man was שלא מעלמא הדין — not of this world. Even though Bunim was חברא קדישא and saw the dead regularly, he was afraid.

Then Bunim looked at the man's face and recognized him: this is a Yid I buried just a few weeks ago. Bunim approached him. The first question — Rabbi Ephrati gave the deadpan: "didn't I just bury you yesterday?" The man confirmed. "How is it in עולם הבא?" טוב, מוראדיק. "What is your זכות? You were a simple Yid; what made it good for you?"

The man answered: "Every morning I said ברכות השחר aloud in shul and was מוציא the kehillah. בזכות זאת I am זוכה to גן עדן and they honor me here." The שיעור lingered on the מוסר point — "He wasn't such a special Yid, but to be מזכה others with ברכות is a מוראדיקע זאך."

Bunim asked for a סימן that this was really the man he had buried. "When you buried me, the tachrichim of the right sleeve ripped a little — see?" Bunim recognized the rip. And then he asked about the crown of leaves. The answer: עולם הזה stinks for a נשמה coming from the עולם האמת — it is גשמיות, it is עבירות. The only way I could tolerate descending here to visit you was through this crown of בשמים from גן עדן.

The שיעור closed the מעשה with a פסוק: ראֵה רֵיחַ בְּנִי כְּרֵיחַ שָׂדֶה אֲשֶׁר בֵּרְכוֹ ה' — Yitzchak's blessing of Yaakov in פרשת תולדות, the same "scent of the field" that the מדרש (דורש הרשומות) reads as a scent from גן עדן. The leaves on the crown of the Yid in the Or Zarua's מעשה are the same leaves.

From Belz to Pshischa — How Far the מסורה Went The שיעור preserved a startling line of historical practice that gives the conceptual frame from the מחזור ויטרי its lived weight. In Belz, the שמש every morning when he came to open the shul would knock on the door first — to give the מתים time to leave before the חיים came in. The practice was preserved into the 19th and early 20th centuries. The שיעור did not present this as bizarre folk-custom; it presented it as the natural Belzer extension of the Machzor Vitry's chiddush. If the נשמות come to shul, you don't burst in on them.

XI. The Pshischa מעשה — Reb Bunim and the New Gabbai

The שיעור also preserved a counter-מעשה that the kehillah's חאפ-cynicism enjoys: there was a Yid in Pshischa — a son and einikel of a Rebbe — who one night passed the shul and heard them calling out his name: "יעמוד ר' ירחמיאל בן..." He was terrified. It was a known belief in the era that the מתים do קריאת התורה at night in shul, and that being called up to one of their aliyos meant they were calling you to join them.

He ran to the Rebbe — ר' שמחה בונים מפשיסחא, a Rebbe famous for his clarity of mind and his impatience with nonsense. Reb Bunim started laughing. The Yid was white in the face. Reb Bunim said: "You idiot. There's a new gabbai in town. He's practicing the names."

The שיעור's framing of this story is precise: it is a real story, and the explanation was the right explanation, but the שיעור did not let the Pshischa Rebbe's laugh be the final word. "That's one מעשה. But we know there are many, many stories of such things." The Pshischa מעשה shows you that not every Friday-night voice in a shul is a metaphysical event. It does not show you that no Friday-night voice in a shul is a metaphysical event.

XII. The Maharshal's Talmid — משה מת

The מהרש״ל[21] had a major תלמיד named רבי משה מת — משה מת, literally "Moshe the dead." He wrote the מטה משה, an important Acharon on Orach Chaim, cited constantly by the משנה ברורה. Why was he called משה מת?

The historical tradition the שיעור brought: One Friday night Rav Moshe Mes was walking past a shul and heard his name called from within for an עלייה. He was a צדיק, a holy Yid — and he was unafraid. He went up. He saw the shul filled with נשמות wrapped in טליתים. They called him up. He received the עלייה — and the lain was פרשת אחרי מות, the parsha of עריות that we lain at Mincha on יום כיפור. He made the ברכה, leined his portion, made the closing ברכה, and walked out unharmed.

From that night his name was Moshe Mes. The aliyah from the מתים — and his having received it, leined it, and survived — became his identifying mark. The historical attribution is one of the standard accounts of why he was called by that name (other accounts also circulate).

XIII. Belz and Siedlce — Surviving the Aliyah

The שיעור brought two further מעשיות — newspaper-recorded in Siedlce and Przytyk, and from the Belzer mesorah — of Yidden who were called to the bimah Friday night by the מתים and ran to their רב for guidance.

The Belzer Rebbe's advice: Take off your glasses. Close your eyes. Walk to the bimah. Make the ברכה. Get the aliyah. Walk out. Do not look at them. If you look, you will be נזק. The Yid who followed the Belzer Rebbe's instructions became very ill for several weeks but survived.

The Rav of Siedlce's advice: Walk backward to the bimah. Stand with your back to the ספר תורה (which is otherwise not permitted, but here the Rav allowed it as a הוראת שעה). Make the ברכה without turning. Receive the aliyah. Walk backward out. The Yid did so, survived, and seemed unharmed — until the next day, chopping wood, his finger was cut off. The Rav explained: "They got to you a little bit, but ברוך השם you survived."

These are not folk legends; they are accounts preserved in newspapers and in family mesorah of the late 19th and early 20th century. The שיעור's frame: even where we cannot independently verify each story, the cumulative record across centuries is itself a halachic fact.

XIV. The Bnei Yissaschar — A מנהג of Centuries Is קדוש

The בני יששכר[22] (ר' צבי אלימלך מדינוב, 1783–1841) — in his אגרא דפרקא — sets out the meta-halachic principle that frames the whole sugya. When the people of כלל ישראל hold a מנהג for hundreds of years, and you do not know its source — do not mock it. A מנהג held for hundreds of years is קדוש, it is היליק; there is a justification, there is a reason, there is a rationale. And if you do not understand it, you still have to do it.

The Bnei Yissaschar gives the belief about נשמות in shul as his primary example. People say the נשמות come to shul on Friday night; people say they call up the living to aliyos; people believe it. Some may say it is שטות, a joke. The Bnei Yissaschar's psak: it is אמת, even if you cannot point to a source — and if you do not believe it, you are considered an אפיקורס.

The שיעור closed the conceptual thread with a parallel: the זוהר הקדוש in פרשת פנחס[23] teaches that at every חופה the grandparents — the bubbes and zeides — come down from above to attend. The שיעור flagged a small further chiddush in the אחרונים: how long they stay depends on the chuppah. If the simchas chosson v'kallah is יידישע, באטעמטע, with צניעות, they stay. If not, they run away. The presence of the unseen is, on this mesorah, conditional on the kind of moment we make for them.

XV. The Halachic Tail — בית כנסת קבוע vs. Private Minyan

All of this lands on a practical halachic outcome. The שולחן ערוך אורח חיים סימן רס״ח[24] codifies that Magen Avos is recited only in a בית כנסת קבוע — a permanent, fixed shul. In a private home minyan on Friday night, Magen Avos is generally not said.

Why? Because the entire structural reason — whether read through the Rambam's mundane danger, Rashi's מזיקין, the Pesachim Gemara's Agrath, or the מחזור ויטרי's נשמות — attaches to the shul building outside the residential center. A private minyan in a Yid's home is inside the יישוב. There is no סכנה — the מזיקין don't come into the יישוב; the נשמות don't come to a private home. The whole machinery of the תקנה does not apply.

The משנה ברורה notes that some מקובלים hold one should say Magen Avos anyway, for the שם-related reasons embedded in the unit. The normative הלכה, following the שולחן ערוך, is to omit it in a private home minyan.

The שיעור closed with a self-aware acknowledgment of the tension that runs through the whole sugya. Rabbi Ephrati grew up in a chassidish world filled with stories of the unseen, and was later trained in Brisk — where "we don't believe stories unless there's a מקור, איש מפי איש עד מפי עד". Both worlds live inside him. The שיעור's resolution: a Chassidishe מעשה I might be skeptical of. But a story from the Rishonim — the מחזור ויטרי, the ספר חסידים, the אור זרוע — that I cannot be cynical about. If I believe the Ramban when he tells me הלכה, I believe him also in stories. We can't split the two. The cumulative weight of the ראשונים and the centuries of מסורה is a halachic fact. עולם הזה is עולם השקר. There is more in the world than we see — and the structural moment of מגן אבות on Friday night is built directly into the architecture of that fact.

The arc of the שיעור in three movements: (i) the structural completion of last week's שמונה עשרה של שבת — the אליה רבה's gematria of 57, the Ashkenaz-Sefard machloikes on ישמחו through the שמחה-vs.-עונג distinction, and the central conceptual chiddush of the טור reading the three Shabbos tefillot as קידושין / חתונה / ייחוד. (ii) ויכולו's כח — Reb Shloimke of Zhvill stopping Rommel at El Alamein with one Kiddush after the מקוה, framed against the גר״א's mesorah of "שלוש שעות" as the three minutes of nuclear war לעתיד לבא. (iii) The full architecture of מגן אבות — Rashi's מזיקין, the Pesachim Gemara's Agrath bat Machlat, Abaye's nullification, and the מחזור ויטרי's stunning chiddush that the מזיקין are the נשמות of the deceased coming to shul on Friday night when גהינום does not burn. The Sefer Chassidim's man who fell asleep in shul; the Or Zarua's Bunim and the כתר עלים; the Belzer Rebbe's "close your eyes"; the Rav of Siedlce's "walk backward and lose a finger"; Moshe Mes's aliyah from the מתים. The Bnei Yissaschar's psak that disbelieving the מסורה of centuries is אפיקורסות. And the practical הלכה: בית כנסת קבוע only.

The Ashkenaz position on ישמחו במלכותך reads this as the basis for saying ישמחו at מוסף even on Shabbos — the קרבן מוסף carries its own דין שמחה independent of שבת's underlying דין עונג. ↩

The reading: אתה קדשת on Friday night = קידושין; ישמח משה on Shabbos morning = חתונה; אתה אחד at Mincha = ייחוד; תכנת שבת at Musaf corresponds to the קרבן מוסף and stands outside the marriage progression. Shabbos complained. Hashem responded: "Klal Yisrael will be your spouse." The midrash is the conceptual ground for the Tur's reading of the three Shabbos tefillot as a marriage. The standard locus is בראשית רבה י״א, ח'; verify the exact pisik. The midrash is widely cited across the Rishonim — Rashi on בראשית ב', the Ramban, and many others. ↩

  9. מהרש״א on שבת קי״ט ע״ב — without ויכולו, the world would not know its Creator. — The Maharsha (ר' שמואל אידלס, 1555–1631) sharpens the structural claim: were it not for כלל ישראל declaring the emunah every Friday night in shul that הקדוש ברוך הוא created the world יש מאין and rested on the seventh day, the world's awareness of creation would dim. The recitation of ויכולו is the engine by which the awareness is continually renewed in the world. The שותף the Gemara names is the structural reflection of this ongoing act of testimony. ↩
  10. The מעשה of ר' שלמה מזוויל and Rommel — 1942. — ר' שלמה גולדמן מזוויל (Reb Shloimke of Zhvill, c. 1856–1945) was one of the great Chassidic Rebbes in ארץ ישראל in the inter-war and WWII era. The מעשה of Reb Shloimke stopping Rommel through כוונה at ויכולו on Friday night is preserved in the oral mesorah of the Zhvill chassidim and beyond. The שיעור mentioned that Reb Shloime Hutler in the Miami kehillah is an einikel of Reb Shloimke. The historical setting — Rommel at El Alamein in late June 1942, the Yishuv expecting annihilation — is established in the modern historical record. ↩

Geographic note: El Alamein is approximately ninety miles west of Alexandria; the driving distance from El Alamein to Tel Aviv is roughly 350 miles (not 90, as some retellings of the מעשה imply). The שיעור preserved the line as "three hours from Tel Aviv" — this is conceptual rather than literal travel-time, and serves as the springboard for the connection to the Gra's reading of שלוש שעות in the הושענות (see fn11). The Yishuv's terror was completely real — the Suez Canal was the gate to ארץ ישראל — but the specific geographic figure should not be cited as the literal distance from El Alamein to Tel Aviv.
  11. The גר״א's reading of "שלוש שעות" in הושענות — three moments of nuclear war לעתיד לבא. — The Vilna Gaon (ר' אליהו, 1720–1797) is reported in the mesorah of his תלמידים as having read the phrase שלוש שעות in the long Hoshanos plea not as three hours but as a unit of time. The mesorah preserved in the literature of his school: לעתיד לבא, גוג ומגוג will be a nuclear war whose duration of complete civilizational destruction is not measured in hours but in moments — three minutes, three moments. The Hoshanos plea is read as our request to be saved from those three moments. The mesorah is associated with the broader Vilna Gaon mesorah on the eschatological פסוקים; verify the specific attribution in the published Gra-mesorah literature. ↩
    Two distinct claims should be separated here. (i) The reframe of שלוש שעות in the long Hoshanos plea as a generic unit of time rather than a literal three hours has independent textual merit and is well-attested in the broader Vilna Gaon framework on the eschatological פסוקים. This reading is in the spirit of the Gra's own approach and can reasonably be attributed to him. (ii) The specific application of that unit-of-time reading to nuclear war לעתיד לבא — the "three minutes of nuclear annihilation" image — is later mesorah developed by darshanim working within the Gra's framework, not in the Gra's own writings. The application is rhetorically powerful but historically conditional. Present (i) as the Gra's reading; present (ii) as a later development of the Gra's framework. The attribution "the Gra already foresaw nuclear war two hundred years ago" should be handled with care — it is darshanus on the Gra rather than text from the Gra.
  12. Why מעריב has no חזרת הש״ץ — historical status as רשות. — The classical sugya in ברכות כ״ז ע״ב on תפילת ערבית רשות או חובה. Maariv was historically treated as רשות, and the absence of חזרת הש״ץ at Maariv is a structural reflection of that earlier status — there was no public obligation to repeat the עמידה for those who could not say it themselves, because there was no underlying obligation to say it in the first place. Even after כלל ישראל accepted Maariv as חובה in practice, the structural absence of חזרת הש״ץ remained. This was treated extensively in the שיעור of 2026-05-24 on קדיש דמעריב; this week's שיעור assumed familiarity with that framework. ↩
  13. שבת כ״ד ע״ב — תקנת מגן אבות for latecomers. — The Gemara's structural reason for the institution of ברכה מעין שבע (beginning with מגן אבות) on Friday night: the בתי כנסיות were located outside the residential center; latecomers finishing the עמידה alone after the kehillah had dispersed would be left to walk home alone, in the dark, which the Gemara classifies as a סכנה. Magen Avos lengthens the public service so that the kehillah finishes together. Rashi specifies the nature of the סכנה as מפני המזיקין. ↩
  14. The רמב״ם's approach to שדים and מזיקין — מורה נבוכים and elsewhere. — The Rambam famously did not treat שדים or non-physical מזיקין of the night as physically operative agents. His treatment of the relevant aggadic material in the Gemara is characteristically careful, reading the language as conceptual rather than literal where possible. The mainstream halachic tradition has preserved both the Rambam's position and Rashi's literal-mazikin position as legitimate readings, with normative practice generally following Rashi (e.g., the Friday-night Magen Avos remains in the siddur). The שיעור's framing of the Rambam as "super-rational" rather than "rationalist" preserves the dignity of the position without overreading it as modern skepticism. ↩
  15. פסחים קי״ב ע״ב — אגרת בת מחלת and the eighteen רבוא מלאכי חבלה. — The Gemara's account of the female שד Agrath bat Machlat, accompanied by 180,000 (י״ח רבוא) מלאכי חבלה, each with רשות לחבל, who roam on Wednesday and Friday nights. The Gemara then records that אביי בא ובטל לה — Abaye nullified her power for the kehillah, with the result that even after her abolition the Magen Avos תקנה remained in place (the ריטב״א, ר״ן, and רא״ש give the standard explanation: מנהג אבותינו בידינו). The Pesachim sugya is the literal-mazikin grounding for Rashi's reading and the structural ground against which the Machzor Vitry's chiddush operates. ↩
  16. חידושי הריטב״א; ר״ן; רא״ש on שבת כ״ד — Magen Avos persists despite Abaye's nullification. — The standard ראשונים on the שבת sugya address the structural question: if Abaye nullified Agrath bat Machlat, why does the Magen Avos תקנה persist? The standard answer across the Ritba, the Ran, and the Rosh: מנהג אבותינו בידינו. The original danger was removed but the takanah remained in force per the general principle that established communal enactments do not dissolve when their underlying conditions change. The שיעור treated this as the standard explanation but contrasted it with the מחזור ויטרי's deeper chiddush (see fn17). ↩
  17. מחזור ויטרי — Rashi's chiddush that the מזיקין are the נשמות of the deceased coming to shul on Friday night. — ר' שמחה מויטרי (Rabbeinu Simcha of Vitry-le-François, late 11th–early 12th c.) was a primary תלמיד of רש״י and connected to him by chasunah (Rabbeinu Simcha's son married a granddaughter of Rashi, sister of Rabbeinu Tam, Rashbam, and Rivam). His מחזור ויטרי is one of the most important early Ashkenazic siddur-halachic works, cited extensively by the בית יוסף. The chiddush brought by the שיעור: Rabbeinu Simcha records, in the name of his רבי Rashi, that the מזיקין Rashi referenced in his commentary on Shabbos 24 are not generic non-physical agents but specifically the נשמות of the deceased who return to this world during the first twelve months after their petirah, and who come to shul on Friday night because גהינום does not burn on Shabbos. The reading recasts the entire structural reason for Magen Avos. ↩
  18. שמות פרק ל״ה פסוק ג' — לֹא תְבַעֲרוּ אֵשׁ ... בְּיוֹם הַשַּׁבָּת; structural extension to גהינום. — The pasuk prohibiting kindling fire on Shabbos. The structural extension in חז״ל and the Rishonim: the prohibition applies to גהינום itself — גהינום אינו דן בשבת. The fires of גהינום are dormant on Shabbos, and the נשמות otherwise under their pressure are released. The Machzor Vitry's reading of where they go on Friday night — to the shul, for the same מנוחה a Yid seeks — is grounded in this structural extension. ↩
  19. ספר חסידים — the מעשה of the man who fell asleep in shul. — ר' יהודה החסיד of Regensburg (c. 1150–1217), one of the great Ashkenazic ראשונים, author of the foundational ספר חסידים. The מעשה: a Yid who stayed late in shul to learn, fell asleep, was locked in by the שמש; at חצות he woke to find the shul filled with נשמות wrapped in טליתים, including two still-living people he recognized from the city. Within a week the two still-living people had died. The story is preserved in Sefer Chassidim as documented testimony, not folk anecdote — the work's halachic standing in the בית יוסף and the wider Acharonim is high. The exact siman in Sefer Chassidim was not specified in the שיעור. ↩
    The exact siman in Sefer Chassidim for this specific מעשה was not given in the שיעור. The relevant מעשיות around night-time shul phenomena are scattered across various simanim; the recent compilation by Dan Derovitz that Rabbi Ephrati mentioned in the שיעור catalogs them. Verify the specific siman before citing.
  20. אור זרוע — Bunim of Worms and the כתר עלים. — ר' יצחק מווינה (c. 1180–1250), Chief Rabbi of Vienna and one of the most influential Ashkenazic ראשונים; his ספר אור זרוע is a foundational halachic compendium. The מעשה: ר' יצחק records (in his own name) hearing from הרב Bunim of ווערמייזא (Worms), a member of the חברא קדישא, who one early morning before dawn went to shul to say תהלים and encountered a man wearing a crown of leaves, learning. The man turned out to be a Yid Bunim had recently buried; the crown was made of בשמים from גן עדן, given to the נשמה because of his זכות of having said ברכות השחר loudly in shul and being motzi the kehillah every morning. The pasuk's connection: ראֵה רֵיחַ בְּנִי כְּרֵיחַ שָׂדֶה אֲשֶׁר בֵּרְכוֹ ה' (Yitzchak's blessing of Yaakov), with the מדרש דורש הרשומות reading the "scent of the field" as the scent of גן עדן. ↩
  21. שו״ת מהרש״ל / historical tradition — Rav Moshe ben Avraham Mat (the מטה משה). — ר' שלמה לוריא (the Maharshal, 1510–1573, Lublin) had a major תלמיד, רבי משה בן אברהם מַט (c. 1540–1606, Przemyśl), author of ספר מטה משה — an important Acharon on Orach Chaim cited regularly by the Mishnah Berurah. The שיעור preserved the chassidish and Lublin oral mesorah that he was called "Moshe Mes" because of an aliyah he received from the מתים in shul Friday night — leining פרשת אחרי מות and walking out unharmed. The narrative is a real piece of mesorah preserved in the relevant chassidic literature. ↩
    The name predates the story. The documented family surname is Mat (מַט) — a standard surname likely derived from a Hebrew root meaning "few" (cf. מְתֵי מִסְפָּר) or from a place name. It is not the Hebrew word for "dead" (מֵת), which would be a different vocalization and a different word. The folk reading "Moshe is dead" — and the aliyah-from-the-מתים etiology — is later darshanus grafted onto a pre-existing family surname; the standard biographical sources on Rav Moshe Mat (introductions to printed editions of מטה משה, encyclopedia entries) document the surname as preceding any narrative explanation. The aliyah-from-the-מתים story may still be a real story in its own right; the etymology of the surname almost certainly is not. Preserve the maaseh as chassidish mesorah, but do not present the surname's origin as folk etymology of "מת = dead."
  22. בני יששכר / אגרא דפרקא — מנהג of centuries is קדוש; disbelieving the מסורה is אפיקורסות. — ר' צבי אלימלך מדינוב (the Bnei Yissaschar, 1783–1841), one of the major early-19th-century Galician Rebbes, in his אגרא דפרקא. The structural psak: when כלל ישראל holds a מנהג for hundreds of years, and the source is unknown, the מנהג is קדוש — there is a justification even if it is not visible. The Bnei Yissaschar specifies the belief about נשמות in shul on Friday night as his primary example, and rules that to dismiss it as שטות is to be classified as an אפיקורס. The framing extends to the wider category of received Jewish belief in unseen agencies that the modern eye does not see. Exact citation in the Bnei Yissaschar's writings was not specified in the שיעור. ↩
  23. זוהר הקדוש, פרשת פנחס — grandparents come down to attend the חופה. — The Zohar in פרשת פנחס preserves the mesorah that at every חופה the grandparents — the בובע און זיידע — come down from above to be present. The שיעור brought a further chiddush from the אחרונים: how long they stay depends on the character of the simchas chosson v'kallah. If the simcha is יידישע, with צניעות, they stay; if not, v'hayiku v'havayeku — they run away. The framing connects to the Bnei Yissaschar's wider principle (see fn22) that the world of the unseen is conditional on the moments we make for it. ↩
  24. שולחן ערוך אורח חיים סימן רס״ח — Magen Avos in בית כנסת קבוע, not in private home. — The Shulchan Aruch codifies that Magen Avos is recited only in a בית כנסת קבוע — a permanent, established shul building. In a private minyan in a Yid's home on Friday night, Magen Avos is generally not recited, because the entire structural reason for the תקנה (the danger associated with the shul building situated outside the residential center) does not apply. The Mishnah Berurah notes that some מקובלים hold one should say it anyway for the שם-related reasons embedded in the unit, but the normative הלכה follows the Shulchan Aruch's omission in a private setting. The siman is רס״ח (the standard treatment of Magen Avos within הלכות תפילת שבת). ↩
