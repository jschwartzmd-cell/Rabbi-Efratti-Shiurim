Az Yashir, Yishtabach, and the Throne of Shlomo HaMelech
Shiur of Rabbi Ephrati — Sunday Morning Series on Tefillah
Recorded 8 September 2024 · Reworked 30 April 2026 · Mareh Mekomos upgraded 5 May 2026

L'iluy nishmas the Skulener Rebbe, zt"l — niftar the previous evening at age 68. The shiur opens with a tribute and continues into the structure of Az Yashir, the puzzle of Yishtabach's authorship, and the legend of Shlomo HaMelech's throne.

I. The Skulener Rebbe

The Skulener Rebbe was niftar last night — a tzaddik revered across Litvish, Hassidic, misnagdish, and Sephardic communities. The Rebbe's father had been niftar a few years earlier; he himself had been ill for a long time. Borough Park has lost three towering tzaddikim in close succession — Rav Moshe Wolfson, the Kossover Rebbe, and now the Skulener Rebbe.

What stays in memory: at a gathering for Mir Yerushalayim bachurim a few months ago, the Rebbe arrived attached to oxygen, barely able to speak. The visiting bachurim were Hebrew-speaking. When the Rebbe heard a drashah beginning in Yiddish — Skulen being affiliated with Satmar — he interrupted in a whisper: "Speak lashon ha'kodesh so the bachurim understand." He then danced with them, gave them chizzuk, sent them off strengthened.

The first time anyone met him in his bungalow, he would emerge from the beis ha'kisei, turn to the wall, and recite Asher Yatzar for five or six full minutes. Asher Yatzar was real to him in a way it is for few Yidden.

(Also niftar last week: Rav Avraham (Vrema) Loizban, a major marbitz Torah.)

May our learning today be l'iluy nishmasam.

II. Where Az Yashir Sits — Halachic Problem and Geonic Practice

The Geonim Did Not Say It Daily

We continue from last week. Az Yashir creates, on its face, a halachic hefsek: Pesukei d'Zimra is bracketed by Baruch She'amar at the start and Yishtabach at the end; Az Yashir is from Chumash, not Tehillim, and inserting it between the two brachos looks like an interruption.

The siddurim of the Geonim reflect this concern. Rav Amram Gaon[2] writes that the yeshivos of Sura and Pumbedisa did not recite Az Yashir daily. He adds — significantly — that one should not protest those communities that did say it; the practice is permissible, even if not standard. The Rambam[1] rules that one wishing to recite Az Yashir should do so after Yishtabach, avoiding the hefsek entirely. No community today follows this; the universal practice is to say it before Yishtabach.

Ha'azinu as a Daily Alternative

The Rambam also records that some communities of his day recited Ha'azinu daily in place of Az Yashir. Ha'azinu is short enough; both contain the structure of shirah. Some communities even substitute Ha'azinu for Az Yashir on Tisha B'Av. The minhag illustrates the underlying flexibility — what mattered was that some shirah close Pesukei d'Zimra, not specifically which.

III. The Italian Origin — R' Moshe ben Kalonymos

The most carefully traced source for the daily Az Yashir appears in the Rokeach (R' Elazar of Worms),[3] who cites a k'sav yad attributing the institution to Rabbi Moshe ben Kalonymos — an Italian chacham from the Geonic period.

R' Moshe ben Kalonymos was the author of two piyutim every Jew sings to this day:[4]

  • Kol Mekadesh Shevi'i — sung at every Shabbos meal
  • Menuchah v'Simchah — Friday night zemiros

In Italy, Az Yashir had been recited daily since the era of the Churban. The Italian Jewish community traces back to those exiled from Yerushalayim after Bayis Sheni; the Minhag Roma (still preserved at the Roman synagogue today, with its own distinctive variations) carries mesoros from that early period. R' Moshe ben Kalonymos traveled from Italy to Germany — where, following the Babylonian Geonic practice, Az Yashir was not recited daily. He instituted the Italian practice in Ashkenaz, and from there it spread.

Mahzor Vitry (R' Simchah ben Shmuel of Vitry, a bar plugta of Rashi)[5] records a complementary testimony: the Roman kehillah sent him a teshuvah affirming that their minhag to recite Az Yashir before Yishtabach traced back to the post-Churban exile.

IV. The Aruch HaShulchan's Order-of-Sources Question

The Aruch HaShulchan[6] raises a sharp kashya on the placement of Az Yashir:

We have a klal, derived from Rosh Hashanah[7] on the structure of Malchuyos, Zichronos, and Shofros: when reciting multiple pesukim together, the order is Torah → Nevi'im → Ketuvim. Yet in Pesukei d'Zimra, we recite the Halleluyos first (which are Ketuvim) and only then Az Yashir (which is Torah). This inverts the canonical order.

The Aruch HaShulchan writes: "I had no resolution until I found that the Zohar HaKadosh asked the same question."

The Zohar's Resolution

The Zohar HaKadosh[8] reframes the question. It is true that under standard halachic ordering, Az Yashir should precede the Halleluyos. But al pi sod, Az Yashir is bound to Yotzer Or — the next major brachah after Yishtabach. Az Yashir serves as the spiritual gesher from Pesukei d'Zimra into Birchos Kriyas Shema. Therefore, Az Yashir belongs at the end of Pesukei d'Zimra, immediately before that transition.

The Kabbalistic reasoning trumps the chronological-source ordering. After the Arizal, this became universal practice — even where the standard halachic logic would have placed Az Yashir earlier or after Yishtabach.

V. The Three Fifteens — Az Yashir, Yishtabach, Generations

A remarkable structural symmetry connects Az Yashir to Yishtabach:

  • The Mechilta[9] identifies fifteen shevachim (praises) within Shiras HaYam itself.
  • Yishtabach contains fifteen praises: shir, shevach, hallel, zimrah, oz, memshalah, netzach, gedulah, gevurah, tehillah, tiferes, kedushah, malchus, brachos, hoda'os.
  • From Avraham Avinu to Shlomo HaMelech — the yichus traced at the end of Megillas Rus[10] through David and continuing one generation to Shlomo — is exactly fifteen generations: Avraham, Yitzchak, Yaakov, Yehudah, Peretz, Chetzron, Ram, Aminadav, Nachshon, Salmon, Boaz, Oved, Yishai, David, Shlomo.

This three-way symmetry is no accident. Shiras HaYam's fifteen lead into Yishtabach's fifteen, which mirror the fifteen generations from Avraham to Shlomo — and as we will see, Yishtabach itself was composed by Shlomo, with its closing chasimah by Avraham. The tefillah is structured.

The Magen Avraham on the Fifteen

The Magen Avraham notes a minhag (not codified by the Mishnah Berurah as obligatory) to recite the fifteen praises of Yishtabach b'neshimah achas — in a single breath — if one is able. The Zohar, as we will see below, treats interruption between them as a serious spiritual matter.

VI. The Eighteen Names and the Added Pesukim

The Rishonim count the Sheim Havayah in the broader Shiras HaYam narrative — including the introductory and concluding verses — as appearing eighteen times. Eighteen times four letters = seventy-two: the Sheim Ayin-Beis, one of the most central sheimos in Kabbalah.

The problem is that when we recite Az Yashir daily in our siddur, some occurrences of the Name appear in narrative pesukim we don't include. To "complete" the count to eighteen, several extra pesukim were appended at the end of Az Yashir before Yishtabach.

The Vilna Gaon's Skepticism

The Vilna Gaon in Ma'aseh Rav[11] rejected these added pesukim. He held they were a mistaken interpolation from Malchuyos of Yamim Noraim that had migrated into the daily nusach by error, and he refrained from saying them. Some printed siddurim bracket these pesukim in parentheses (e.g., "V'sarav chasum lemor") to flag the disagreement; the mekubalim, who hold the eighteen-name completion is meaningful, retain them; most communities recite them.

VII. Yishtabach — Whose Bracha Is It?

The Pri Chadash's Question

We saw earlier that the Pri Chadash[12] challenged Baruch She'amar: how can we recite a brachah not found in Chazal? The same question presses on Yishtabach: it does not appear as a fixed brachah text in Bavli or Yerushalmi.

The Yerushalmi That Resolved It

Last Shabbos, the Rav cited a Yerushalmi at shalosh seudos that bears directly on this. Parshas Shoftim describes who returns from the battlefield — including one who has committed a small aveirah. The Yerushalmi defines "small aveirah" with a striking example: even one who spoke between Yishtabach and Yotzer Or is to be turned back from war, lest his aveirah cause Klal Yisrael to lose.

This Yerushalmi does not appear in our extant Yerushalmi texts, but multiple Rishonim cite it. Their citation is decisive: if Chazal already had a fixed issur against speaking between Yishtabach and Yotzer Or, then Yishtabach must have existed as a fixed brachah in their day. Our girsa is missing the passage; the mesorah is intact.

The Magen Avraham's Leniency

The Magen Avraham[13] rules that, given the post-Talmudic textual status of Baruch She'amar and Yishtabach, certain leniencies apply that do not apply to standard brachos — for example, answering amen mid-brachah. But the core stringency — not interrupting between Yishtabach and Yotzer Or — remains in full force.

Multiple Attributions of Authorship

The Rishonim divide on who actually composed Yishtabach:

  • R' Shlomo of Worms (the Rokeach's predecessor) and others: the Rabbanan Saberoyim (between the close of the Talmud and the Geonim).
  • The Rambam, the Rif: "tikkenu chachamim" — instituted by Chazal proper.
  • The Meiri: from the Geonim.

And then a fourth, more legendary attribution.

VIII. Yishtabach and the Throne of Shlomo

The Rokeach's Tradition

An Ashkenazic Rishon (cited by the Rokeach) records a remarkable mesorah: Shlomo HaMelech composed the opening of Yishtabach. To grasp why, we have to know about Shlomo's throne.

The Throne in Tanach

The pesukim in Melachim[14] describe Shlomo's throne briefly:

וַיַּעַשׂ הַמֶּלֶךְ כִּסֵּא שֵׁן גָּדוֹל וַיְצַפֵּהוּ זָהָב מוּפָז... שֵׁשׁ מַעֲלוֹת לַכִּסֵּא... וּשְׁנֵים אֲרָיוֹת עֹמְדִים אֵצֶל הַיָּדוֹת, וּשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר אֲרָיִים עֹמְדִים שָׁם עַל שֵׁשׁ הַמַּעֲלוֹת.
"The king made a great throne of ivory and overlaid it with finest gold... six steps to the throne... two lions standing beside the armrests, and twelve lions standing on the six steps."

The pasuk concludes:

וְלֹא נַעֲשָׂה כֵן לְכָל מַמְלָכוֹת.
"And nothing like it was made for any kingdom."

The Targum Sheni's Expansion

Chazal expand the brief pasuk dramatically. The Targum Sheni on Megillas Esther[15] — a midrashic work in targumic Aramaic — describes the throne over many pages as a marvel of mechanical sophistication:

  • As Shlomo ascended each step, golden lions positioned themselves to escort him upward.
  • As he sat on the throne, golden eagles rose into the air and circled him.
  • Crafted animals on the steps would emit sounds in coordinated sequence.
  • When witnesses stood to testify, the lions and animals would roar — terrifying false witnesses into confessing the truth.

The level of detail invites the speculation that Shlomo had access to advanced engineering. Some have wondered whether he understood electricity. The midrashic language is consistent with mechanical systems — gears, springs, hidden levers — rather than electrical ones, which is also more historically plausible.

Halachic Note — The Throne Was Not Beis Din

The roaring lions inducing confession is striking but halachically constrained: the throne could not function as beis din. Halachic testimony requires shenei eidim ne'emanim — two trustworthy witnesses — not psychological devices. The throne's spectacle pushed false witnesses to confess by other means; once they confessed, the actual halachic process resumed in normal form.

The Throne's Odyssey

Tanach and Midrash trace the throne's history through several non-Jewish hands:

  • Shishak, king of Mitzrayim (Melachim Aleph 14:25–26)[16] plundered the treasures of Yerushalayim; some sources say the throne went to Egypt and was later recovered.
  • Pharaoh Necho (Melachim Beis 23:29ff)[17] appears in Tanach. The Midrash explains his epithet "Necho" (נכה, "smitten" or "lame"): he attempted to sit on Shlomo's throne, the lions attacked him, and he was injured — hence Pharaoh ha'Necheh, Pharaoh the Lame.
  • Achashverosh: the Targum Sheni records that he sought the throne; his obsession with sitting on it figures in the midrashic background of Esther.

Centuries later, Charlemagne commissioned a replica throne in his palace, attempting to evoke the same image of regal sanctity. When Napoleon visited Charlemagne's palace after his conquests, the painting depicting that visit shows him standing on the steps of the throne, gazing up — but he refused to sit on it. Even Napoleon did not have the audacity.

Yishtabach as Shlomo's Hodaah

The midrashic account: when Shlomo's throne was completed and he ascended it for the first time, all of Klal Yisrael gathered to celebrate. They wished to praise the king himself — for the technical wonder, for the splendor, for the malchus that the throne represented.

Shlomo refused. "Not to me," he said, "is the praise. To HaKadosh Baruch Hu alone." And he opened his mouth and recited Yishtabach:

יִשְׁתַּבַּח שִׁמְךָ לָעַד מַלְכֵּנוּ, הָאֵל הַמֶּלֶךְ הַגָּדוֹל וְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בַּשָּׁמַיִם וּבָאָרֶץ.

The fifteen praises that follow, in this account, were Shlomo's redirection of the tzibbur's admiration upward. Every morning we recite the tefillah Shlomo composed at the moment he refused to absorb honor that belonged to HaKadosh Baruch Hu.

IX. The Avraham Acrostic in the Closing Chasimah

Rav Chaim Vital[18] records — uncharacteristically not in the name of the Arizal, but on a separate authority — that while Shlomo composed the body of Yishtabach, the closing chasimah was composed by Avraham Avinu. Read the closing fifteen praises with the first letters in mind:

אֵל הַהוֹדָאוֹת, אֲדוֹן הַנִּפְלָאוֹת, בּוֹרֵא כָּל הַנְּשָׁמוֹת, רִבּוֹן כָּל הַמַּעֲשִׂים, הַבּוֹחֵר בְּשִׁירֵי זִמְרָה, מֶלֶךְ יָחִיד אֵל חֵי הָעוֹלָמִים.

The first letters: א (Adon), ב (Borei), ר (Ribon), ה (HaBocher), מ (Melech) — אברהם. Avraham's name is hidden as an acrostic in the chasimah. The composition combines the first av (Avraham) with the king of the fifteenth generation (Shlomo); fifteen generations frame fifteen praises.

X. The Tosfos Question — Why No Baruch at the Start?

Tosfos[19] raises an obvious structural question: every extended brachah begins with Baruch and ends with Baruch. Yishtabach ends with the standard Baruch Atah Hashem — but at the opening, there is no Baruch. Why?

Tosfos' answer: Yishtabach is a brachah ha'semuchah l'chaverta — a brachah immediately following another brachah. The Baruch at the opening of Baruch She'amar serves as the opening Baruch for the entire bracketed unit. From Baruch She'amar through Yishtabach, we are inside one extended brachah structure; everything between is the body of that brachah.

The practical halachic implication is severe: speaking between Baruch She'amar and Yishtabach is interrupting inside a single brachah, not merely between brachos. That is why the Mishnah Berurah rules that one who spoke must repeat Baruch She'amar, all of Pesukei d'Zimra, and Yishtabach from the beginning.

XI. The Zohar on Interrupting Between the Praises

The Zohar HaKadosh[20] describes the fifteen praises of Yishtabach as exceedingly holy — to be recited b'retzef, in a single flow. Interrupting between them is treated with vivid Kabbalistic rebuke:

From beneath the wings of the keruvim, says the Zohar, a flame emerges and announces by name the person who interrupted: "Such-and-such has cut himself off between the praises of the holy King." The Zohar warns of being nifsak — spiritually severed from the source. The Mishnah Berurah echoes (more soberly) that one is particularly cautious not to mafsik within these specific fifteen praises.

The Zohar also draws a connection to Shir HaShirim asher li'Shlomo, supporting the attribution of Yishtabach to Shlomo HaMelech.

XII. Inserting Shir HaMaalos on Yamim Noraim

If hefsek between Yishtabach and Yotzer Or is so severe, how do we insert Shir HaMaalos on Yom Kippur and Hoshana Rabbah, as Nusach Sefard communities do?

  • Many Nusach Sefard and mekubalim recite Shir HaMaalos in the designated places; the Vilna Gaon and many Litvish kehillos refrain.
  • The halachic justification, brought in the poskim: the issur against hefsek applies to devarim beteilim — idle speech. Divrei tefillah with established minhag and reason — Shir HaMaalos, Kaddish, Barchu — are not classified as hefsek.

The Rebbe's reflection: how exactly the mekubalim received the authorization to insert Shir HaMaalos here, given the gravity of the hefsek issue, is one of the recurring tensions between halachah and Kabbalah in our siddur. We have arrived at a working synthesis, but the question deserves the recognition it merits.

XIII. Standing and Sitting

The minhagim for posture during Pesukei d'Zimra:

  • Most stand from Baruch She'amar (or from Vayivareich David or Baruch Hashem L'olam) until Barchu.
  • Some mekubalim hold one should specifically not stand during Az Yashir itself.
  • Yotzer Or is tefillah b'meyushav — recited sitting; standing is generally discouraged unless one lacks a seat.
  • Shemoneh Esrei, of course, is the only tefillah requiring full standing.

XIV. Synthesis

The end of Pesukei d'Zimra sits at the convergence of four currents:

  1. Halachic caution against hefsek between framing brachos (Geonim, Rambam).
  2. Historical robustness of an Italian/Roman minhag tracing back to the Churban, brought to Ashkenaz by R' Moshe ben Kalonymos.
  3. Kabbalistic reframing (Zohar): Az Yashir belongs at the end, bridging into Yotzer Or.
  4. Compositional elegance: fifteen praises in Az Yashir, fifteen in Yishtabach, fifteen generations from Avraham to Shlomo, and the Avraham acrostic embedded in the chasimah.

The tefillah we say each morning is the result of two thousand years of layered mesorah. The halachah protects its integrity, the Kabbalah reveals its inner architecture, and the midrashic tradition preserves the moments when great tzaddikim — Shlomo at his throne, Avraham implicit in the closing — first formulated the words. The tefillah is our nation's history compressed into a daily act.

Practical consequence: speaking between them is hefsek within a single brachah, not merely between brachos — hence the Mishnah Berurah's ruling to repeat from the beginning. ↩
  20. Zohar, Terumah (general reference). — The fifteen praises of Yishtabach as exceedingly holy, to be recited b'retzef (in a single flow). Vivid Kabbalistic rebuke for interrupting: a flame from beneath the wings of the keruvim announces by name the person who interrupted. The Mishnah Berurah echoes this (more soberly) as a special caution against hefsek within these specific fifteen praises. The Zohar also draws a connection to Shir HaShirim asher li'Shlomo, supporting the attribution of Yishtabach to Shlomo HaMelech. ↩
