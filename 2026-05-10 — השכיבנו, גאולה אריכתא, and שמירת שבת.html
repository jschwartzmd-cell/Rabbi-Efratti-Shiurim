Rabbi Ephrati · Sunday Morning שיעור on תפילה

השכיבנו, גאולה אריכתא, ושמירת שבת

From the סוכת שלום to the שמירה of שבת — the Bridge from גאולה into תפילה
Recorded 10 May 2026 · Reworked from the original שיעור

Last week's שיעור closed with the riddle of השכיבנו — a ברכה of "lay us down to sleep" first uttered, by רבינו יונה's reading, on the night of יציאת מצרים. This week we open it. Why is it placed where it is? Why does it begin without ברוך? What is סוכת שלום doing in a bedtime ברכה? And why does the entire נוסח change on Friday night — replaced by language that no longer asks for שמירה because שבת itself is the שמירה? The answers carry us from a גמרא in ברכות through רבינו יונה, the אבודרהם, רות, the זוהר, the בח, and the ט״ז.

I. שבע ביום הללתיך — The Seven Daily Brachot of שמע

חז״ל count seven daily ברכות around קריאת שמע: two before שמע and one after in the morning, two before and two after at night[1]. The number is not arbitrary — it is anchored in a pasuk in תהלים פרק קי״ט פסוק קס״ד[2]: שֶׁבַע בַּיּוֹם הִלַּלְתִּיךָ עַל מִשְׁפְּטֵי צִדְקֶךָ — "seven times a day I praise You for the laws of Your righteousness." Our seven daily ברכות-of-שמע are the structural fulfillment of that praise.

The asymmetry between morning and night is therefore deliberate. The morning needs only one ברכה after שמע (אמת ויציב); the night needs two — אמת ואמונה and השכיבנו. Why does the night need an additional ברכה? Because night is a time of חשיכה and מזיקין — a time of physical and spiritual vulnerability that the morning does not share. Even with electricity, even in our well-lit bedrooms, the structural quality of nighttime — the disruption of consciousness, the surrender of will to sleep — is what calls for the additional ברכה of protection.

II. The אבודרהם — The Liminal Minutes Before Sleep

Within השכיבנו sits a curious phrase: ותקננו בעצה טובה מלפניך — "and prepare us with good counsel from before You." Why specifically here?

The אבודרהם[3] describes a lived experience that any of us can verify. As a person lies in bed and struggles to fall asleep, those minutes are usually filled not with peace but with the tasks of tomorrow: the unanswered email, the unresolved conversation, the dilemma that has been waiting all day for a quiet moment to surface. That liminal time, when the noise of the day has stopped but sleep has not yet begun, is the prime time to daven: הקב״ה, תן לי ישוב הדעת, תן לי עצה טובה. The אבודרהם reframes ותקננו בעצה טובה as a request precisely for those minutes — for clarity, orientation, and ה'ש presence in the deliberations that the dark hours always seem to contain.

The phrase עֲצַת ה' לְעוֹלָם תַּעֲמוֹד — "the counsel of Hashem stands forever" (תהלים ל״ג) — frames the request. We are not asking for clever counsel; we are asking for counsel that endures, that holds up, that becomes the עצה of tomorrow's actions.

III. Why השכיבנו Begins Without ברוך

A small structural feature: השכיבנו opens directly with השכיבנו ה' אלקינו לשלום — no ברוך אתה ה' at the start. The pattern is well-known: this is a ברכה הסמוכה לחברתה[4] — a ברכה juxtaposed to the previous one, drawing on the ברוך of the preceding ברכה rather than opening with its own. The same pattern appears in the closing portions of שמונה עשרה and other places where multiple ברכות are linked into a continuous unit.

This is not a stylistic choice — it is a structural claim. The very absence of ברוך tells us: השכיבנו does not stand alone. It is bound to אמת ואמונה that precedes it. It is the same liturgical breath, continuing.

IV. סומך גאולה לתפילה — and the Problem of השכיבנו

The גמרא בברכות דף ד' ע״ב[5] establishes a principle: a worshiper must connect גאולה directly to תפילה — that is, the closing of גאל ישראל in the brachot of שמע must be followed immediately by שמונה עשרה, with no interruption. This principle holds at שחרית and at מעריב alike.

This creates a serious problem at night. After גאל ישראל closes אמת ואמונה, the next ברכה is השכיבנו — and only after that comes שמונה עשרה. Isn't השכיבנו an interruption? How can we recite an entire ברכה between גאולה and תפילה and still claim to be מסומך גאולה לתפילה?

The Answer — גאולה אריכתא

The answer is one of the most beautiful structural readings in the siddur: השכיבנו is not an interruption of גאולה — it is part of גאולה itself. The ראשונים call this גאולה אריכתא — a lengthened redemption. We are not breaking from גאולה to say something else and then returning. We are extending the גאולה, deepening it, articulating it more fully before pivoting into תפילה.

רבינו יונה על ברכות ד' ע״א[6] grounds this in a hashkafic principle: the daily mention of זכרון יציאת מצרים is not a historical exercise. It is the preface to עבודת ה'. Historically, that עבודה was קרבנות; today, it is תפילה. By experiencing יציאת מצרים anew each day — both in the morning ברכה of אמת ויציב ending in גאל ישראל, and in the night ברכה of אמת ואמונה ending in גאל ישראל — we anchor אמונה. From that anchored אמונה, we can move into תפילה without seam.

V. Why Night Carries the Longer Arc of גאולה

Why is the night the place where this lengthening happens? Why is the morning content with one ברכה after שמע, while the night needs two?

The answer lies in the historical shape of יציאת מצרים. The redemption did not happen in an instant. It began at night — at חצות, with מכת בכורות — and continued through the night until morning, when בני ישראל actually walked out of מצרים. The night of יציאת מצרים is therefore the longer, more structurally extended portion of the redemption. Our liturgy reflects that shape: the night carries the longer arc of גאולה in the form of השכיבנו, because the historical night that the liturgy commemorates was itself the longer arc.

And there is a protective dimension as well. On the night of מכת בכורות, even with דם on the משקוף ומזוזות, there was סכנה outside the homes. The pasuk in שמות פרק י״ב פסוק כ״ג[7] commands explicitly: לֹא תֵצְאוּ אִישׁ מִפֶּתַח בֵּיתוֹ עַד בֹּקֶר — "no man shall go out from the door of his house until morning." כלל ישראל needed שמירה during that night even in the midst of redemption. Our nightly השכיבנו is the echo of that needed שמירה: גאולה is here, but the night is still the night, and we still ask to be guarded through it.

VI. ופרוש עלינו סוכת שלומך — The Sukkah of Peace

Within השכיבנו sits another striking phrase: ופרוש עלינו סוכת שלומך — "spread over us Your sukkah of peace." The image is rich. Why a סוכה specifically, and why a סוכה of שלום?

The Connection to רות

The אבודרהם[8] ties the language to a moment in מגילת רות פרק ג' פסוק ט'[9]. רות approaches בועז on the threshing floor and asks: וּפָרַשְׂתָּ כְנָפֶךָ עַל אֲמָתְךָ כִּי גֹאֵל אָתָּה — "spread your wing over your maidservant, for you are a redeemer." The verb ופרש echoes the verb in השכיבנו. The image — a kanaf (wing, corner of a garment) extended in protective embrace — is the source of our liturgical פרוש. רות's request is also a request for גאולה: she is asking בועז to be her גואל, the redeemer who will marry her and bring her into the family of ישראל.

We ask the same of הקב״ה. Spread Your wing over us — be our גואל, draw us under the canopy of redemption itself. The protective embrace and the redemptive embrace are one and the same.

Why סוכה?

Two layers explain the choice of סוכה:

The אבני נזר[10] brings a practical vort. In a house, with all its private spaces, tensions can flare — family arguments, marital frictions, the wear of close quarters. In a סוכה, by contrast — open to the neighbors, exposed to the community, structurally communal — peace prevails. The very nature of a סוכה cultivates שלום because it removes the privacy that allows conflict to fester. סוכת שלום is therefore not a metaphor; it is the kind of dwelling that structurally produces peace.

The deeper layer: the סוכה signifies the ענני הכבוד — the divine clouds that surrounded כלל ישראל in the מדבר. The סוכה is the archetype of being encompassed by ה'. סוכת שלום is therefore both social peace (the kind a real סוכה structurally produces) and metaphysical peace (the kind that comes from being encompassed by ענני הכבוד) — and the two are the same, because the presence of ה' that calms the soul is also the presence that calms the household.

VII. שבת as שמירה — The Friday Night נוסח

On Friday night, the נוסח of השכיבנו changes. Where the weekday version asks for protection from אויב, דבר, חרב, רעב, ויגון — the full list of nighttime threats — the Friday night version omits these and instead speaks of שבת itself spreading peace over us.

Why? Because שבת itself is the שמירה.

The טור, אורח חיים סימן רס״ז[11] brings a מדרש (often associated with the זוהר[12]) that on שבת, מזיקין are stilled. The Jew on שבת stands in something analogous to the בית המקדש — the entire day is a sanctuary in time conferring intrinsic protection. There is therefore no need to ask for שמירה in the Friday-night השכיבנו; the שמירה is built into the day itself. Our request becomes a celebration of that שמירה rather than a petition for it.

More Than We Have Kept Shabbos The hashkafic point of this נוסח change is captured in the famous formulation, often attributed to Achad Ha'am: "More than the Jews have kept שבת, שבת has kept the Jews." The Friday-night השכיבנו is the liturgical articulation of that thought. The אבן עזרא's פיוט says it directly: כִּי אֶשְׁמְרָה שַׁבָּת אֵל יִשְׁמְרֵנִי — "for if I guard Shabbos, God will guard me." The mutuality is exact: שמירת שבת produces, on its own terms, divine שמירה. The Friday night השכיבנו does not ask for what is already happening; it acknowledges it.

VIII. The בח and the ט״ז — A Dispute Over Shmira-Language

The בח, אורח חיים סימן רס״ז[13] takes the Friday-night nusach principle to its logical conclusion: he criticizes the inclusion of any weekday-style petitions for שמירה in the Friday-night השכיבנו. If שבת itself is the שמירה, then asking for שמירה on שבת implicitly suggests that שבת's protection is incomplete — a small but real diminution of כבוד שבת.

The ט״ז[14] nuances the בח. He distinguishes between two kinds of language:

שבח (praise) language: "You guard us, You watch over us, You spread Your sukkah of peace" — language acknowledging that the שמירה is happening. This is acceptable on Friday night because it celebrates rather than petitions.

בקשה (petition) language: "Guard us, watch over us, save us from the night's dangers" — language that asks for what is not yet present. This implicitly suggests the protection is needed and absent, and is therefore problematic on Friday night.

The practical takeaway: communities should follow מנהג המקום, but with awareness of which language they are using. The same words can shift register from celebration to petition depending on framing, and the Friday-night חזן should know which mode he is in.

IX. Insertions After גאל ישראל — Are They Interruptions?

If השכיבנו is גאולה אריכתא, what about other items that appear between גאל ישראל and שמונה עשרה? Are they also extensions of גאולה, or are they real interruptions?

קדיש appears formally as an interruption — yet the קדיש text itself includes ויצמח פורקנה ויקרב משיחה — "may His salvation flourish and may He bring near His Mashiach." That very phrase is גאולה-language. The ultimate completion of שם ה' as articulated in קדיש is identical with the גאולה השלמה. Therefore קדיש is not an interruption of גאולה — it is itself part of the same arc, in a different register. The pattern is consistent: anything that lives between גאל ישראל and שמונה עשרה in our מעריב is some form of גאולה, lengthened and elaborated.

X. The אבודרהם's אבינו Variant

The אבודרהם[15] records a נוסח variant: השכיבנו אבינו לשלום — "lay us down, our father, in peace." Most contemporary nuschaot omit אבינו; it is preserved in only a few traditions. But the concept is powerful enough to warrant attention.

When a small child cannot fall asleep — restless, worried, unable to settle — what does a parent do? Holds him. Sings to him. Soothes. The parent's presence is the שמירה. השכיבנו, in the אבודרהם's variant, frames our nightly plea in exactly that key. We are the child. הקב״ה is אבינו. The petition is not formal; it is intimate. Lay us down, Father, in peace; guard us through the night; calm our restlessness; restore our composure.

XI. Sleep, המפיל, and Re-Entry into מלכות שמים

השכיבנו's request — השכיבנו ה' אלקינו לשלום — is on the אבודרהם's reading the request of a child to a father. But it is also a halachic structure. Sleep is not automatic; sleep is a מתנה. That is why חז״ל instituted the separate ברכה of המפיל for the moment of going to bed itself.

The גמרא בברכות דף נ״ז ע״ב[16] teaches: שינה אחד משישים במיתה — sleep is one-sixtieth of death. Sleep is a liminal state in which the conscious mind temporarily relinquishes its hold on the body. Upon waking, we must re-enter the conscious avodah of קבלת עול מלכות שמים — beginning with the second appearance of שמע in the morning service. The closing line of השכיבנו — לחיים טובים ולשלום — is therefore not just a sleep blessing; it is a request that the night-passage from this world to its sixtieth (death-like sleep) be a passage into life and into peace, ready for tomorrow's service.

XII. שבת, פשט, and הלכה — רשב״ם, אבן עזרא, and the מסורה Under Pressure

The שיעור turned at length to a deeper question that the Friday-night nusach raises: how do we read פסוקים when the strict פשט seems to diverge from established הלכה? The classic test case is וַיְהִי עֶרֶב וַיְהִי בֹּקֶר יוֹם אֶחָד in בראשית פרק א פסוק ה. Read literally — "and it was evening and it was morning, one day" — the verse can be heard to imply that the day proper ends at morning, with evening as its lead-in. That reading would put the start of the calendar day at morning, not at sunset. And if the day starts at morning, שבת would begin Saturday morning rather than Friday at sundown — which is, of course, the exact opposite of how כלל ישראל has kept שבת for three thousand years. The crisis is not philological; it is liturgical. To follow the literary פשט here is to break שבת.

The רשב״ם — A Tosafist Devoted to Pure פשט

רשב״ם, ר' שמואל בן מאיר[17] (c. 1085–c. 1158) was a grandson of רש״י through his mother Yochevet, and the older brother of רבינו תם. Together with his brother and a circle of cousins and students, he was central to the early Tosafist enterprise in northern France — the school that produced the running halachic-talmudic commentary that fills the outer margins of every printed gemara. רשב״ם's distinct project, however, was not Talmudic. It was biblical. He set out to write a commentary on the תורה that read pesukim with rigorous literary פשוטו של מקרא — not as a vehicle for halachic deduction, not as a launching point for דרש, but as plain literary reading.

This was not safe ground. There is a well-known story preserved in רשב״ם's own commentary on בראשית ל״ז ב: רשב״ם relates that he visited his grandfather רש״י near the end of רש״י's life and pressed him on this very point. רש״י's commentary, רשב״ם told him, was magnificent — but it had given up too much פשט for דרש. רש״י, according to רשב״ם's testimony, accepted the critique and said that if he had time, he would write a new commentary following the פשט that develops fresh each day. רשב״ם took up that mantle. His Torah commentary is the inheritance of that conversation. Where רש״י blends פשט and דרש freely, רשב״ם separates them: when he gives a דרש reading, he says so; when he gives פשט, he commits to it even when the result is uncomfortable.

רשב״ם on ויהי ערב ויהי בקר — The Uncomfortable Reading

His commentary on בראשית פרק א פסוק ה is the locus classicus of "uncomfortable פשט." רשב״ם reads וַיְהִי עֶרֶב וַיְהִי בֹּקֶר יוֹם אֶחָד as a single literary unit: after the events of the first day, evening came, then morning came — completing one day. The reading is structurally clean. It also implies that the day proper begins at morning. רשב״ם himself was a שומר שבת according to the standard halachic timing — Friday-evening to motzei-Shabbos. He never proposed changing the practice. But he refused to retroactively adjust the literary reading of בראשית just because the halachic practice runs the other way. The peshat reads what it reads.

This is a courageous methodological position — and a precarious one. The danger is that a reader without strong rabbinic formation, encountering רשב״ם's commentary on its own, might conclude that the תורה itself supports a morning-to-morning Shabbos. And in the 12th century, that danger was not theoretical.

The Karaite Backdrop — From Anan to Cyprus

By the 12th century, the Karaite movement had been a feature of Jewish life for nearly four hundred years. ענן בן דוד in 8th-century Babylon had founded the proto-Karaite school by rejecting the תורה שבעל פה and reading the תורה שבכתב in radical literalism. The movement had grown across the centuries — flourishing in Egypt, in the Land of Israel, in Constantinople, in Crimea, and in scattered island and coastal communities of the eastern Mediterranean. Two of the Karaites' most distinctive practices flowed from their literalist reading of the תורה: they read לא תבערו אש בכל מושבותיכם (Shemos 35:3) as forbidding any fire to be burning in a Jewish home on שבת — even one lit before שבת — so they sat in cold, dark houses on Friday night. And they read ויהי ערב ויהי בקר as setting the day from morning to morning — so their שבת ran from Saturday morning until Sunday morning.

The geographic concentrations of these practices were uneven. Pure Karaite communities were few. But mixed communities — communities where Karaite and Rabbanite Jews lived side by side, intermarried, and influenced each other's halachic practice — were not rare. בנימין מטודלה[19], the great Spanish-Jewish traveler whose travelogue covers most of the inhabited Jewish world of the 12th century, documents this in unflinching detail. In Cyprus in particular he reports communities where שבת observance had drifted: some Jews kept it from Friday evening; others kept it from Saturday morning; the line between Karaite and Rabbanite had blurred into incoherence. The Cyprus communities are emblematic — not unique. The pressure on the מסורה in those years was real.

אבן עזרא — A Wandering Defender of the מסורה

ר' אברהם אבן עזרא[18] (c. 1089–c. 1167) was born in Tudela, in northern Spain, just before that region passed from Muslim to Christian rule. The first half of his life he spent in Spain — writing poetry, composing astronomical and grammatical works, and traveling among the great Andalusian Jewish centers. Around 1140, fleeing the Almohad persecutions sweeping across al-Andalus and North Africa, he began the long wandering that would define the rest of his life. His itinerary is staggering: from Spain to North Africa to Italy (Rome, Lucca, Mantua, Verona); from Italy across the Alps into southern France (Narbonne, Béziers); from southern France to northern France (Rouen, Dreux); and finally, late in life, across the channel to England — where he stayed in לונדריש (London) for an extended period and composed several of his last major works. He died around 1167, possibly back in Spain or in southern France; the exact place is uncertain.

Throughout these travels אבן עזרא wrote — Torah commentary, grammatical treatises, mathematical and astronomical works, philosophical writings, hundreds of שירים. His maxim אֵין מִקְרָא יוֹצֵא מִידֵי פְשׁוּטוֹ ("a verse never departs from its פשט") became one of the most cited principles in medieval פרשנות. But he also engaged seriously with Karaite biblical scholarship — citing Karaite commentators by name (a rarity among Rabbanite מפרשים), respecting their textual rigor while sharply opposing their halachic conclusions. His was a particular kind of defense of the מסורה: not by ignoring the Karaites but by out-arguing them on their own grammatical and philological turf.

The Night of the Vision — איגרת השבת, London 1158

איגרת השבת[20] is the polemical work that crystallized this defense. אבן עזרא wrote it in לונדריש in the winter of 1158 (or possibly 1159 — the dating is slightly uncertain). The opening of the work is a remarkable narrative — אבן עזרא's own account of the night that produced it.

The previous day, אבן עזרא tells us, someone had brought him a manuscript: a respected scholar's commentary on the תורה reading ויהי ערב ויהי בקר in the morning-to-morning sense. אבן עזרא set the manuscript aside, troubled. That night — ליל שבת — he had a vision. A figure appeared to him bearing a letter (an איגרת), and the letter spoke directly. You are sleeping while the שבת of your people is being attacked. A scholar's פירוש is teaching that the day begins at morning. If this reading spreads — if it reaches communities that already wobble between rabbinic and Karaite practice — שבת itself is in danger. Wake up and defend the day. אבן עזרא woke (in the narrative frame), and immediately sat down to write the work whose title איגרת השבת takes its name from that very letter in the dream.

The work itself runs in three parts. The first defends the halachic timing — שבת as Friday-evening to Saturday-evening — against the morning-to-morning reading. The second is a sustained refutation of the alternative פירוש and its grammatical claims. The third addresses related cosmological and calendrical questions that the dispute opens up. The polemic is sharp, learned, and unmistakably anxious — the anxiety of a 70-year-old wanderer who has watched, across his decades of travel, how thin the membrane is between scholarly disagreement and communal halachic drift.

Was the Manuscript רשב״ם's?

Was the manuscript that triggered איגרת השבת רשב״ם's commentary? Some scholars have argued yes — the timing fits (רשב״ם died around 1158, the year of the איגרת), the reading matches, and רשב״ם's commentary on Bereishis 1:5 is the most famous Rabbanite text giving the morning-to-morning peshat. Others argue that אבן עזרא's target was a Karaite commentary or a non-Rashbam Christian-influenced reading. The matter remains scholarly disputed; אבן עזרא himself does not name the author. What is clear, regardless of the identification, is the shape of the danger he saw: a respected reading, presented as pure פשט, traveling outward into communities where the line between halachah and literary commentary had already weakened. אבן עזרא's polemic targets the structural problem, not just one author.

The Lesson

The lesson the שיעור drew is more careful than a simple "halachah beats פשט." Both רשב״ם and אבן עזרא were defenders of פשוטו של מקרא — they were on the same side of the larger methodological question. The dispute between them is over what to do when פשט and halachah pull in different directions. רשב״ם's answer: preserve the פשט as commentary, trust readers to know that halachah governs practice. אבן עזרא's answer: in an era of pressure on the מסורה, that trust is misplaced. The reader of a manuscript in 12th-century Cyprus or northern France or southern Italy is not always equipped to keep פשט and הלכה separate. When the cost of confusion is the loss of שבת itself, the polemic must come into the open. The איגרת is that polemic. בענייני מעשה, ההלכה שולטת — in matters of practice, הלכה governs.

XIII. Learning הלכות שבת — Avoiding the Inadvertent

The משנה ברורה[21], in his introduction to חלק ג' (סימנים רמ״ב–שמ״ד), stresses an unromantic point. שמירת שבת is impossible without consistent learning of הלכות שבת. Without that learning, inadvertent חילול שבת is nearly inevitable: the small mistakes — turning the wrong knob on the warmer, carrying outside the eruv, separating in a way that is מבדיל — proliferate when knowledge is thin.

Many קהילות institute brief weekly reviews to address this: a two- or three-minute halachah reminder before לכה דודי on Friday night, or a short shiur after davening on Shabbos morning. The reviews are not for advanced lomdus; they are for the basics that we forget without weekly contact.

XIV. Children, Lullabies, and לשון הקודש

A note from the קב הישר[22] that the שיעור framed as both halachah and hashkafah. The קב הישר laments parents who teach their children foreign languages and use non-Jewish lullabies in the cradle. The point is not xenophobic; it is formative. From the earliest moments, a child is being oriented to a particular world. The sounds, the rhythms, the words that surround the cradle form the substrate on which everything else will be built.

The practical alternative the שיעור suggested: instead of generic lullabies, sing המלאך הגואל אותי — Yaakov's blessing to Yosef's children, used as a bedtime niggun in many homes — or other holy lullabies that are also language-of-קודש. The night, the cradle, the moment of going to sleep is the same liminal moment that השכיבנו addresses for the adult. For the child, it is the foundational moment. The words sung over a child's bed travel.

The arc of the שיעור: השכיבנו is not what it first appears. It is not a bedtime petition tacked onto the evening service; it is a deliberate extension of the גאולה of יציאת מצרים, lengthened to fill the structural shape of the historical night that the redemption itself occupied. It is also a request for orientation — for those liminal minutes before sleep when tomorrow's questions surface and only ה'ש counsel can answer them. And on Friday night, it ceases to be a petition entirely and becomes celebration: שבת itself is the שמירה, and the words shift from asking to acknowledging. To say השכיבנו with awareness of all of this is to say it three times in three different keys, depending on which night it is and what we need from it.

מראה מקומות

  1. משנה ברכות פרק א משנה ד; ברכות י״א ע״א. — Establishes the seven-fold structure: בשחר מברך שתים לפניה ואחת לאחריה ובערב שתים לפניה ושתים לאחריה. The asymmetry — three brachot in the morning, four at night, totaling seven — is the structural reason that the night has a uniquely-positioned ברכה (השכיבנו) that the morning lacks. ↩
  2. תהלים פרק קי״ט פסוק קס״ד. — שבע ביום הללתיך על משפטי צדקך. The pasuk is cited by חז״ל as the textual hook for the seven daily ברכות around קריאת שמע: morning's three plus night's four equals seven. The Tehillimic praise is structurally enacted in the daily liturgy. ↩
  3. אבודרהם, ברכת השכיבנו, על "ותקננו בעצה טובה." — ר' דוד אבודרהם (14th century, Sevilla) on the phrase ותקננו בעצה טובה מלפניך. He explains that the moments before sleep are the contemplative moments of a person's day: the worshiper inevitably thinks about tomorrow, and therefore asks הקב״ה for good counsel precisely in those minutes. This is the standard medieval reading and remains the principal hashkafic frame for the phrase. ↩
  4. Principle of ברכה הסמוכה לחברתה; ברכות מ״ו ע״א. — A ברכה placed immediately after another ברכה does not require its own opening ברוך אתה ה'. The principle is established in ברכות מ״ו ע״א regarding ברכת המזון and is applied throughout the siddur — to השכיבנו after אמת ואמונה, to the closing brachot of שמונה עשרה, and elsewhere. The structural claim is that the absence of ברוך indicates continuity, not independence. ↩
  5. ברכות דף ד' ע״ב — דין סומך גאולה לתפילה. — The גמרא: כל הסומך גאולה לתפילה אינו ניזוק כל היום כולו — one who connects גאולה directly to תפילה is not harmed all day. The principle is codified for both שחרית and מעריב. The apparent problem at מעריב — that השכיבנו seems to interrupt the connection — is the central question of this שיעור and is answered by the doctrine of גאולה אריכתא. ↩
  6. רבינו יונה על ברכות דף ד' ע״א מדפי הרי״ף. — רבינו יונה establishes the hashkafic frame for daily זכרון יציאת מצרים: it is not commemoration alone but the preface to עבודת ה'. By experiencing יציאת מצרים anew each morning and night, we anchor the אמונה that drives תפילה (and historically, קרבנות). השכיבנו, on this reading, is the lengthened arc of יציאת מצרים at night, deepening the move from גאולה into תפילה rather than interrupting it. ↩
  7. שמות פרק י״ב פסוק כ״ג ופסוקים מ״א–מ״ב. — The pasuk: לא תצאו איש מפתח ביתו עד בקר — "no man shall go out from the door of his house until morning." This commands כלל ישראל to remain inside during the night of מכת בכורות despite the redemption already underway. The combination of גאולה + sustained nighttime סכנה is the structural ground for our nightly השכיבנו: גאולה is here, but the night still requires שמירה. ↩
  8. אבודרהם, ברכת השכיבנו, על "ופרוש עלינו סוכת שלומך." — The אבודרהם connects the imagery of ופרוש עלינו in השכיבנו to the verb ופרשת in רות. Both are protective-redemptive embraces; both use the same root. The kabbalistic-liturgical reading therefore frames the request as one for שמירה under the גואל's wing. ↩
  9. מגילת רות פרק ג' פסוק ט'. — ויאמר מי את ותאמר אנכי רות אמתך ופרשת כנפך על אמתך כי גואל אתה. רות approaches בועז on the threshing floor and asks him to spread his wing — both literally (his garment's corner, in token of betrothal) and figuratively (his role as גואל, the kinsman-redeemer who will marry her and bring her into the family of ישראל). The request is the structural source for the language of ופרוש עלינו סוכת שלומך. ↩
  10. אבני נזר on the structural שלום of a סוכה. — The אבני נזר (ר' אברהם בורנשטיין of Sochaczew, 1838–1910) develops the practical observation that a סוכה — open to neighbors, exposed to the community — structurally produces שלום in a way that the closed home does not. The vort grounds the choice of סוכה specifically (rather than בית) in השכיבנו: שלום is built into the very form of the dwelling. ↩
    The exact citation within the אבני נזר's writings (likely either שו״ת אבני נזר or his עניני סוכה) was not specified in the שיעור; the vort is widely transmitted in his name. Verify the precise responsum or location before citing as halachah.
  11. טור, אורח חיים סימן רס״ז. — The Tur (ר' יעקב בן הרא״ש, early 14th century) records the change in the נוסח of השכיבנו on Friday night, citing earlier sources that שבת itself constitutes שמירה for כלל ישראל. The Tur's discussion is the principal halachic locus for the substantive difference between weekday and Shabbos מעריב at this seam. ↩
  12. זוהר הקדוש on the שמירה of שבת. — The זוהר in multiple places (especially זוהר חלק ב', פרשת תרומה, and זוהר חלק ג', פרשת אמור) develops the theme that on שבת, מזיקין are stilled and the Jew enjoys שמירה analogous to standing in the בית המקדש. The image is the kabbalistic ground for the halachic shift in nusach: where the protection is intrinsic, the petition for protection becomes celebration of it. ↩
  13. בח, אורח חיים סימן רס״ז. — ר' יואל סירקיש, the בח (1561–1640), critiques the inclusion of weekday-style petitions for שמירה in the Friday-night השכיבנו. His reasoning: if שבת itself is the שמירה, asking for שמירה on שבת implicitly suggests the protection is incomplete, which is a small diminution of כבוד שבת. The בח's position is the strict reading of the structural principle. ↩
  14. ט״ז על אורח חיים סימן רס״ז. — The ט״ז (ר' דוד הלוי, c. 1586–1667) nuances the בח's position by distinguishing between שבח (praise) language and בקשה (petition) language. Praise language acknowledging שבת's intrinsic שמירה is acceptable; petition language that asks for שמירה implies absence of what is already present, and is the language the בח objects to. The distinction governs proper Friday-night practice and the kavanah of the שליח ציבור. ↩
  15. אבודרהם, נוסח השכיבנו — variant השכיבנו אבינו לשלום. — The אבודרהם records a textual variant in the opening of השכיבנו: אבינו as a vocative addressed to הקב״ה. The mainstream Ashkenazic and Sefardic nuschaot omit it, but a few traditions preserve it. The variant accentuates the parent-child intimacy of the petition — the worshiper is the restless child, הקב״ה the father called to soothe. ↩
  16. ברכות דף נ״ז ע״ב — שינה אחד משישים במיתה; ברכת המפיל at ברכות ס' ע״ב. — The גמרא records that sleep is one-sixtieth of death — establishing the liminal-mortal nature of going to sleep. The separate ברכה of המפיל, instituted for the moment of going to bed, addresses sleep itself as a divine gift requiring recognition. The closing of השכיבנו — לחיים טובים ולשלום — frames the night-passage as a transit toward life, rather than toward its opposite. ↩
  17. רשב״ם על בראשית פרק א' פסוק ה'; הקדמותיו לפרשנות פשט. — ר' שמואל בן מאיר (רשב״ם, c. 1085–c. 1158), grandson of רש״י and brother of רבינו תם, was the leading champion of פשוטו של מקרא in the school of צרפת. His commentary on בראשית א:ה reads ויהי ערב ויהי בקר in a way that — at the level of pure literary פשט — could be heard as suggesting that the day proper begins at morning, with evening as its lead-in. רשב״ם himself was a שומר שבת according to the halachic timing; his commentary preserves the literary reading without suggesting a halachic departure. ↩
  18. אבן עזרא על שמות פרק י' פסוקים כ״א–כ״ג; איגרת השבת. — ר' אברהם אבן עזרא (c. 1089–c. 1167) was the great פשט מפרש whose maxim אין מקרא יוצא מידי פשוטו is foundational for medieval פרשנות. He was nonetheless deeply concerned about commentary that — read out of context by the public — could appear to support non-halachic timings of שבת. His writings repeatedly safeguard the מסורה against drift. ↩
  19. ספר המסעות מאת בנימין מטודלה (12th century). — Benjamin of Tudela's travelogue documents Jewish communities across the medieval Mediterranean. He reports — among many other observations — communities in Cyprus where שבת observance had drifted under Karaite influence toward a morning-to-morning timing rather than the rabbinic Friday-night-to-motzei-shabbos framework. The report is one of the historical anchors for אבן עזרא's polemics about preserving the halachic שבת. ↩
  20. איגרת השבת מאת אבן עזרא, c. 1158/1159. — A short polemical work in which אבן עזרא, in לונדריש (London), recounts a vision urging him to defend the halachic שבת — Friday night to motzei-Shabbos — against alternative readings that would set the day morning-to-morning. Whether the literary frame is historical reportage or rhetorical device, the text's purpose is unambiguous: in matters of practice, הלכה governs, and פשט is preserved as commentary, not as halachic norm. ↩
  21. משנה ברורה, הקדמה לחלק ג' (סימנים רמ״ב–שמ״ד). — The חפץ חיים, in his introduction to the שבת volume of the משנה ברורה, stresses the practical necessity of consistent learning of הלכות שבת. Without that learning, inadvertent חילול שבת is nearly inevitable: the small recurring decisions of שבת — heating, carrying, separating, opening — require a living knowledge of dinim that fades quickly without weekly review. The introduction is the standard reference for the principle. ↩
  22. קב הישר מאת ר' צבי הירש קאידאנובר (פפד״מ ת״ע, 1710), פרק נ״ב. — The קב הישר, a popular mussar work that reached enormous circulation in 18th-century Ashkenaz, devotes a chapter to the formative power of the cradle. He laments parents who teach their children foreign languages and use non-Jewish lullabies, arguing that the sounds of a child's earliest months and years form the substrate on which all later עבודה is built. The שיעור's practical alternative — המלאך הגואל אותי as a holy lullaby — is widely adopted in Chassidic and yeshiva-affiliated homes today. ↩
