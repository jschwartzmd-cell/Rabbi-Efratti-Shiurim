הַקְּטֹרֶת — הִיסְטוֹרְיָה, סוֹד וְהַשְׁפָּעָה

The Ketores: History, Mystery, and Impact
Shiur of Rabbi Ephrati — Sunday Morning Series on Tefillah
Recorded 23 June 2024 · Reworked 30 April 2026 · Mareh Mekomos upgraded 5 May 2026

L'ilui nishmas Rav Moshe Wolfson, zt"l (זצ״ל), niftar the morning of this shiur.

I. Opening — A Living Model of Tefillah

On the way to shiur this morning, word arrived that a great Yid (איד), Rav Moshe Wolfson zt"l, had been niftar (נפטר), and that the levayah (לוויה) would be taking place shortly. With Hashem's help, I will go straight from the shiur to the levayah.

I had a shaychus (שייכות) with him from my years in Miami. Every winter, for two or three months, he would stay by Avi Sholtzen, and that is how I came to know him. He was a true tzaddik (צדיק) — and especially apt for a series on tefillah, because I have never seen anyone daven like him.

Our minyan began at 9:00 AM. He arrived around 8:10 or 8:15 AM, opened to Korbanos (קרבנות), and proceeded with painstaking deliberation through every word. By the time the rest of the tzibbur reached Shochen Ad (שכן עד) at roughly 9:45 AM, he had also just arrived at that point in his own unhurried, deeply focused prayer. What took us forty-five minutes took him close to an hour and a half. Every morning, it would take me a full half-hour just to "catch up" with him.

I used to daven Pesukei d'Zimra (פסוקי דזמרא) aloud at the amud (עמוד) — as is the custom in many Chasidish (חסידיש) communities — and he would often sit right next to me. I could hear him; I could see him. His entire body would shudder when he reached "Nishmas kol chai" (נשמת כל חי). He would cry — tears of joy, of emotion, of gratitude, of appreciation. I observed this from when he was about 88 years old until he was 93 or 94, when I left Miami; he was 99 at his petirah. It brought me chizuk (חיזוק) in my own davening just to witness a Yid who prayed as one is truly meant to pray. May our learning today be l'ilui nishmaso (לעילוי נשמתו).

II. The Place of Korbanos and Ketores in Tefillah

Why the Yeshiva World Skips — and Why That Is Justified

A few weeks ago, we began discussing the kavanos (כוונות) of tefillah, and today we arrive at the section of Ketores (קטורת). In the yeshiva (ישיבה) world, it is common to begin davening straight from Baruch She'amar (ברוך שאמר), skipping Korbanos and Ketores entirely.

While I hope most people who have the time do say them, there is a real justification for this practice. The Mishnah Berurah (משנה ברורה)[1] paskens that if a person is osek b'Torah (עוסק בתורה), he is not obligated to recite Korbanos. This is the rationale behind the practice in yeshivos.

Of course, one might argue that if there is time for a "kiddush club," there is time to say Korbanos before davening. This touches the famous concept of רבי שמעון בר יוחאי, whose Torah study was itself his form of prayer (Torasan umnasan — תורתן אומנותן); he only had to recite Kriyas Shema (קריאת שמע). For the rest of us, reciting Korbanos is "extra credit." Even old siddurim reflect this: the Babish siddur, which I just returned, skips many parts of Korbanos and does not have the standard set we are accustomed to.

The Theological Foundation

The central idea is that tefillah was instituted to correspond to the daily sacrifices: תְּפִלּוֹת כְּנֶגֶד תְּמִידִין תִּקְּנוּם.[2] Therefore, when we begin our prayers by reciting the parshiyos (פרשיות) of the korbanos — discussing the halachos of how they were brought, learning the relevant passages from the Chumash, Mishnah, and Gemara — it is considered as if we had actually brought the korban. This recitation enhances the tefillah that follows. We discussed this at length a few weeks ago; today the focus narrows to the Ketores.

III. The Influence of Kabbalah on the Siddur

When we study the Seder HaTefillah (סדר התפילה), it is crucial to recognize the profound impact that Kabbalah (קבלה), the Zohar (זוהר), and the Arizal (האר״י ז״ל) have had on the structure of our davening. We take the layout of our siddurim for granted, but the prayer book looked very different before the teachings of Kabbalah were widely revealed. Many additions — particularly before Shemoneh Esrei (שמונה עשרה) and after — stem from the Sefer HaZohar (ספר הזוהר) of רבי שמעון בר יוחאי.

The principal example is the recitation of parshas ha'ketores (פרשת הקטורת). Every morning we say the passage from the Torah and continue with the Baraisa of "Pitum HaKetores" (פיטום הקטורת) from the Gemara,[3] which lists the sammanei ha'ketores (סממני הקטורת) and various halachos. Where did this practice originate? The source is the Zohar HaKadosh (הזוהר הקדוש).

IV. The Mystery and Authenticity of the Sefer HaZohar

1,100 Years of Silence

Tradition teaches that the Zohar was authored by רבי שמעון בר יוחאי, a Tanna from the second century — a talmid of Rabbi Akiva, not long after the Churban. Yet for over 1,100 years, Klal Yisrael had no knowledge of this sefer.[4] It is not mentioned in the Mishnah — even though its compiler, רבי יהודה הנשיא, was a talmid of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai — nor in the Gemara, nor in the writings of the Geonim (גאונים).

The Discovery of Rabbi Moshe de León (c. 1255)

Then, in the 13th century — around the year 1255 — a great mekubal (מקובל) from Spain named Rabbi Moshe de León announced that he had discovered an ancient manuscript containing the deepest secrets of the Torah (sodos haTorah — סודות התורה) as written by Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai. He explained that the text had been transmitted secretly among a select few yechidei segulah (יחידי סגולה), but the time had finally come for it to be disseminated to Klal Yisrael.

The claim was met with immediate skepticism. Many mekubalim objected: "We have never heard of this — who says it is authentic?"

The Quest of Rabbi Yitzchak of Akko

During this period, a Rishon named רבי יצחק דְּמִן עַכּוֹ (Rabbi Yitzchak of Akko), a talmid of the Ramban, dedicated his life to investigating the matter.[5] He embarked on the long, arduous journey from Eretz Yisrael to Spain — a trip that, in those days, took years.

He finally reached the city where Rabbi Moshe de León lived and begged for an audience. Rabbi Moshe de León gave him his solemn promise, swearing an oath: "בְּשֵׁם ה׳ — In the name of God, I have the ancient copy at home. Come back tomorrow and I will show it to you."

When Rabbi Yitzchak returned the next day, he was met with the sounds of wailing. Rabbi Moshe de León had passed away just an hour before. After shiva (שבעה), Rabbi Yitzchak approached the widow and explained his quest. Her response was shattering: "What are you talking about? My husband wrote it. He made it all up himself." She added that he had attributed it to Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai for purely commercial reasons: "If I were to write it in my own name, there would be no koinim (קונים) — but by attributing it to Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, it's a bestseller." Indeed, it became a bestseller.

Two versions even before he left. It is striking that already before Rabbi Yitzchak departed Eretz Yisrael, two competing traditions had taken shape: one held that the Ramban himself possessed an ancient copy of the Zohar, which he transmitted to Rabbi Moshe de León for copying; the other held that the entire work was a fabrication. So the controversy was not born only at the deathbed in Spain — it accompanied the Zohar from the moment of its appearance.

His Later Reversal — A Mystery

Yet later in life, in a different chibur (חיבור), Rabbi Yitzchak of Akko writes that he is now convinced the Zohar is authentic. What changed his mind he never tells us. It remains one of the great mysteries of Jewish intellectual history.

V. Sabbatean Misuse and the Yaavetz's Challenge

In the 17th century, the false messiah Shabsai Tzvi (ימח שמו וזכרו) tragically misappropriated the mystical language of the Zohar. Anyone with even a basic familiarity with Kabbalah knows that everything is built on zachor u'nekeivah (זכר ונקבה) — masculine and feminine — with concepts like zivugim (זיווגים), partzufim (פרצופים), and Adam Kadmon (אדם קדמון). With a corrupt mind, this language can be twisted, and Shabsai Tzvi did precisely that — translating mystical concepts into actual antinomian practice.

Jacob Frank and Eva Frank. Worse still was Yaakov Frank, a talmid of the Sabbatean tradition who took the corruption further. His daughter Eva ("Chava") Frank built a massive cult that spread through Poland and Czechoslovakia. In the 1700s, mysticism was the most popular discipline in Jewish life — more popular even than halacha — and that hunger left people vulnerable. Many Yidden fell into this avoidah zarah; Hashem yerachem.

In the wake of all this, the Yaavetz (יעב״ץ) — Rabbi Yaakov Emden — wrote Mitpachas Sefarim (מטפחת ספרים),[6] in which he methodically argued that not every piece of the Zohar can be authentically attributed to רבי שמעון בר יוחאי. By his day, the Zohar had already been embraced by Klal Yisrael — largely because of how heavily the Arizal built upon it — so this caused great rash. But there was a real need for someone to point out that not every line of the work should be treated as Halacha l'Moshe miSinai.

R' Elazar Fleckeles and Linguistic Proofs

Following the Yaavetz, Rabbi Elazar Fleckeles, a prominent talmid of the Noda biYehudah (נודע ביהודה) and Av Beis Din of Prague, made a stunning declaration in his sefer Teshuvah Me'Ahavah (תשובה מאהבה):[7] "I swear that not all of the Zohar is from Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai."

His proofs were largely linguistic. The Aramaic of the Zohar does not match the Aramaic spoken in Eretz Yisrael in the second century — compare it with the Aramaic of the Gemara of those centuries, and the differences are evident. There are even what appear to be Spanish loan-words. He concludes that some of the work was indeed composed by Rabbi Moshe de León in 13th-century Spain.

Fleckeles even expresses gratitude that Emperor Franz Joseph of the Austro-Hungarian Empire had prohibited the printing of the Zohar in his country, since the Frankists were so distorting it that the local non-Jewish authorities themselves came to view it as dangerous. (Of course, the Zohar later returned to print and is, today, one of the most studied sefarim in the Jewish world.)

VI. Reconciling Authenticity with Tradition

Where We Stand

I do not have a definitive answer that will satisfy every objection. My position is rooted in mesorah. I am part of a lineage — our fathers and grandfathers learned from sefarim and from rabbonim who all accepted that the Zohar is authentic. That is the side I am on.

The figure most responsible for cementing the Zohar's place in Klal Yisrael is the Arizal, who authenticated it from a source we cannot access — the olamos ha'elyonim (עולמות העליונים). Ultimately, Klal Yisrael trusts the Arizal. Many halachos in Shulchan Aruch are practiced today exactly as he taught them, even where they appear to differ from the printed text.

R' Yonason Eibeschitz's ruling. It is important to know that Rabbi Yonason Eibeschitz — himself a great master of Kabbalah — writes that one who does not believe in the authenticity of the Zohar is not a koifer (כופר).[9] The same is implied by other poskim. This is no small matter; it shows the gravity with which the question was treated even by those who themselves accepted the Zohar.

Academia vs. Mesorah

If you turn to secular academics — most famously Professor Gershom Scholem of Hebrew University — you will find entire volumes dedicated to "proving" that the Zohar is the work of 13th-century Spanish mystics. When I was a bachur in Brisk, I had Thursday nights free, and I used to slip into the Hebrew University library where his bequeathed collection is kept — a special Cheder Gershom Scholem requiring permission to enter. I read his papers and saw his manuscripts. As frum Yidden, this scholarship does not sway our core beliefs, because we have a mesorah. Who are we to question it?

The Vilna Gaon's Position

The position of the Vilna Gaon (הגאון מווילנא) is particularly interesting. He revered every word of the Arizal, and we have no indication that he was critical of the Zohar's general attribution. However, his talmid Rav Chaim of Volozhin writes in Nefesh HaChaim (נפש החיים) that there were certain pieces in the Zohar that the Gaon did not accept — without specifying which.[8]

The picture is further complicated because the talmidei haGra themselves had a machloikes on this issue. Rav Mendel of Shklov and Rav Chaim Volozhin transmitted somewhat different versions of the Gaon's view. The same goes for the Gaon's commentaries on Sefer Yetzirah (ספר יצירה) and Tiferes Tznius (תפארת צניעות) — the precise scope of his reservations is not preserved with full clarity.

So we are left with the Zohar as a whole, and we accept it in its entirety. It has changed our lives — and the way we daven — beyond recognition.

VII. The Enduring Power of the Ketores

More Beloved Than Tefillah Itself

With this background in place, we can finally turn to the Zohar HaKadosh's teachings on the ketores. The Zohar reveals something astonishing: the parsha of the ketores is more chaviv (חביב) — beloved and cherished — by HaKadosh Baruch Hu (הקדוש ברוך הוא) than even tefillah itself. This is a revolutionary idea. Because of this elevated status, the Zohar instructs that we recite the parsha of the ketores both before and after our daily prayers — it acts as a frame, an enhancement.

There is also an internal reason for the doubling: in the Beis HaMikdash (בית המקדש), the ketores was offered after the korban tamid (קרבן תמיד), so the order of our tefillah mirrors the avodah of the day.

The Zohar adds a condition: if you recite it daily with kavanah, you will be protected. The Chofetz Chaim (חפץ חיים) writes in the Mishnah Berurah that today there is no excuse not to have kavanah. Take five or ten minutes to learn the passage and understand what it means. A practical recommendation: Rabbi Aryeh Kaplan's anthology — sometimes referred to as the "purple chumash" — has an excellent treatment of parshas ha'ketores and the eleven samimanim, going through every ingredient with clarity.[11]

Rabbi Shimon's Famous Words

אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, אִלּוּ הֲווֹ יָדְעִין בְּנֵי נָשָׁא כַּמָה עִילָאָה עֲבוֹדָתָא דִקְטֹרֶת קֳדָם קוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא, הֲווֹ נָטְלִין כָּל מִלָּה וּמִלָּה מִנֵּיהּ וְסַלְקִין לָהּ עַל רֵישַׁיְיהוּ כְּכִתְרָא דִדְהֲבָא.
Rabbi Shimon said: If human beings would know how elevated the service of the ketores is before the Holy One, Blessed be He, they would take every single word of it and place it upon their heads like a crown of gold.

Berachos and Segulos

The Zohar HaKadosh in Parshas Vayakhel (פרשת ויקהל)[10] elaborates that daily recitation has the power to annul negative decrees, to shield from hirhurim ra'im (הרהורים רעים) — bad thoughts — and from harsh dinim (דינים). And if a person needs a yeshuah (ישועה), this is what to say.

It is also a well-known segulah (סגולה) for ashirus (עשירות) — material wealth. This is why many shuls commission a parchment with parshas ha'ketores written in ksav Ashuris (כתב אשורית) — the script of a Sefer Torah — to be read after davening. (Note the wordplay: reading from Ashuris [aleph] is a segulah for Ashirus [ayin]. Many people try it and report that it works.)

This brings to mind related segulos — like reading Parshas HaMan (פרשת המן) on the Tuesday of Parshas Beshalach. The Chasam Sofer (חתם סופר) famously commented: "It is a great segulah… but the greatest segulah is to go to work!"[12] One is not a koifer for not believing in every segulah, but we do believe in them because they are transmitted through our mesorah.

VIII. The Ketores Enters the Siddur — The Shelah HaKadosh

Reciting the ketores before and after davening was not always standard. The Babish siddur, Rabbi Yaakov Emden's siddur, and others from before the Arizal's influence took hold do not contain the ketores at all. The change was instituted by the Arizal, based on the Zohar.

Rabbeinu Yeshaya HaLevi Horowitz

A pivotal figure in this liturgical shift was the Shelah HaKadosh (השל״ה הקדוש) — Rabbeinu Yeshaya HaLevi Horowitz.[13] He was from Prague, in present-day Czechoslovakia. His father was a talmid of the Rema (הרמ״א), placing him squarely within the great Ashkenazic mesorah.

In 1622, he traveled to Eretz Yisrael. The journey took roughly a year, by way of Syria. He arrived first in Yerushalayim, but a serious incident there forced him to relocate; he settled in Tveria (טבריה) and is buried there beside the Rambam.

Why "Shaar HaShamayim"?

The week he stepped off the boat — at Haifa or Akko — was Parshas Vayetzei. That week's leining contains the verse אֵין זֶה כִּי אִם בֵּית אֱלֹקִים וְזֶה שַׁעַר הַשָּׁמָיִם ("This is none other than the house of God, and this is the gate of Heaven"). He took it as a siman from Heaven and named the siddur he was preparing Shaar HaShamayim (שער השמים).

In his last will and testament — written in his final years in the late 1620s — he instructed his children to publish the siddur, emphasizing one crucial addition he had nearly forgotten: the ketores. "Don't forget to add the ketores." Since the publication of Shaar HaShamayim, virtually all subsequent siddurim have included the parsha of the ketores before and after davening.

An Aside — The Title "Hakadosh"

It is worth pausing on this. There are only a small number of personalities to whom Klal Yisrael has appended the title Hakadosh. By tradition: Rabbeinu Hakadosh (Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi), the Ari Hakadosh, the Shelah Hakadosh, the Or HaChaim Hakadosh, and (the title of the work itself) the Zohar Hakadosh. Some include the Alshech Hakadosh; some apply it to the Baal Shem Tov. The point my grandfather, an old-timer, used to make is that not every great Yid earns this title — and the modern habit of attaching "heilig" to anyone is a departure from how it once was used.

IX. The Three Pesukim — Israel's Battle Cry

Immediately following the ketores, we recite three pesukim, each repeated three times. This too is from the Arizal, based on the Zohar, and it is connected to the deep secrets of the ketores, which signifies a kesher (קשר) — a bond — to HaKadosh Baruch Hu. The very word ketores shares this root: it expresses a binding, a connection, a tying-together of the human and the Divine.

The Yerushalmi Source

The first two pesukim are rooted in the Yerushalmi in Berachos,[14] which says that a person should have these verses constantly upon his lips. The Yerushalmi itself does not include the third pasuk; the Arizal added it and instructed that each be repeated three times.

The Hidden Radak — A Battle Cry

The last of the three pesukim carries another remarkable significance. A little-known Radak on Tehillim[15] reveals that this pasuk was the battle cry of Klal Yisrael. Every nation in the ancient world had its battle cry; the cry the Jewish people raised on the battlefield was:

ה' הוֹשִׁיעָה, הַמֶּלֶךְ יַעֲנֵנוּ בְיוֹם קָרְאֵנוּ.
"Hashem, save! May the King answer us on the day we call."

Some have noted parallels — Mi LaShem Eilai from the Chashmonaim, and earlier still from Moshe Rabbeinu — but the Radak is explicit that this verse was the cry that accompanied Klal Yisrael into war. It is the kind of detail to share with a soldier in Eretz Yisrael today: the verse you say every morning is the same one your ancestors shouted as they ran toward the enemy.

X. Closing

The structure of our tefillos, so rich and layered, is a testament to the confluence of Halacha, Midrash, and Kabbalah. From the Zohar's revelation about the ketores, to the Arizal's integration of it into our daily prayers, to the Shelah HaKadosh's decision to embed it permanently in the siddur — we see how the profound depth of our Torah continuously shapes and elevates our service to Hashem.

And the ketores stands at the very heart of it: more chaviv than tefillah itself, a kesher between the Jew and his Creator, a segulah for protection and prosperity, and a daily reminder that even in the smallest detail of our davening, we are continuing the avodah of the Beis HaMikdash.

Recommended in the shiur as a practical aid for kavanah when reciting parshas ha'ketores. ↩
  12. Chasam Sofer — drashos and teshuvos on Parshas HaMan and the practice of segulos (general reference). — The famous quip — "It is a great segulah… but the greatest segulah is to go to work" — is preserved in the Chasam Sofer literature in connection with Parshas HaMan said on the Tuesday of Parshas Beshalach. The line is widely cited in poskim and drashos rather than from a single fixed siman. ↩
  13. R' Yeshaya HaLevi Horowitz (the Shelah HaKadosh), Shnei Luchos HaBris; Siddur Shaar HaShamayim. — The Shelah's tzava'ah to his children to publish his siddur and to "not forget to add the ketores" is the historical pivot point for the ketores entering the standard nusach. The Shelah arrived in Eretz Yisrael in 1622, settled in Tveria, and is buried near the Rambam. The siddur takes its name from the verse אֵין זֶה כִּי אִם בֵּית אֱלֹקִים וְזֶה שַׁעַר הַשָּׁמָיִם read on Parshas Vayetzei the week he disembarked. ↩
  14. Yerushalmi Berachos, perek 5 (the sugya of ה׳ צבאות עמנו / ה׳ צבאות אשרי אדם). — The Yerushalmi teaches that a person should have these two verses constantly upon his lips. The third verse — ה׳ הוֹשִׁיעָה, הַמֶּלֶךְ יַעֲנֵנוּ — is not in the Yerushalmi; the Arizal added it and instituted that each of the three be said three times. (Standard siddurim reflect this Arizal arrangement.) ↩
  15. Radak on Tehillim 20:10. — ה' הוֹשִׁיעָה, הַמֶּלֶךְ יַעֲנֵנוּ בְיוֹם קָרְאֵנוּ — identified by the Radak as the battle cry of Klal Yisrael: the verse the Jewish nation called out on the battlefield, paralleling the war-cries of the surrounding nations. A striking detail to bear in mind every morning, when these very words are recited after parshas ha'ketores. ↩
