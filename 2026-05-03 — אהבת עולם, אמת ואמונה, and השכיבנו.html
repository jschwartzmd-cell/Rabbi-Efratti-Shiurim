Rabbi Ephrati · Sunday Morning שיעור on תפילה

The Blessings of קריאת שמע at Night

אהבת עולם · אמת ואמונה · השכיבנו — Halachah, Hashkafah, and the Siddur Wars
Recorded 3 May 2026 · Reworked from the original שיעור

Last week's שיעור opened the ברכות of קריאת שמע at night with המעריב ערבים. This week we move to the second ברכה before שמע — אהבת עולם — and to the two ברכות after: אמת ואמונה and השכיבנו. Each holds a debate that runs deep: a 9th-century Geonic split over which words to use, an 18th-century grammar war that scarred the printed siddur, and a midrashic origin for השכיבנו that reaches back to יציאת מצרים.

I. Two ברכות Before, Two After

The framework, established in משנה ברכות פרק א משנה ד[1], places two ברכות before קריאת שמע and one after in the morning, and two before and two after at night. The first ברכה of מעריב — המעריב ערבים — was the focus of the previous שיעור. The second is אהבת עולם: a thanksgiving for the gift of תורה and a request that we be granted the understanding to learn, guard, and fulfill it.

The closing phrase of the ברכה — כי הם חיינו ואורך ימינו ובהם נהגה יומם ולילה — is among the most beautiful summary statements of the Jewish relationship to תורה in the entire siddur: not a tool, not a discipline, but our very life and the length of our days.

II. אהבה רבה or אהבת עולם?

The מחלוקת in ברכות י״א ע״ב

The גמרא בברכות דף י״א ע״ב[2] records a sharp dispute between שמואל and the רבנן over the opening words of the second ברכה of שמע. שמואל holds the proper opening is אהבה רבה — "a great love." The רבנן hold it is אהבת עולם — "an eternal love."

The substantive difference is more than poetic. אהבה רבה describes a love of immense intensity — but conceptually finite. אהבת עולם reads from a different scriptural source — ירמיהו ל״א ב[3], where הקב״ה speaks through the prophet: ואהבת עולם אהבתיך — "with an eternal love I have loved you." For the רבנן, the choice is decisive: an eternal love is the only fitting language for the ברכה.

The תוספות' פשרה

How do we hold למעשה? The תוספות, ברכות י״א ע״ב, ד״ה ולא פליגי[4] propose a compromise that has shaped Ashkenaz nusach ever since: say אהבה רבה in the morning and אהבת עולם at night.

The structure is reminiscent of רב פפא in many places in ש״ס, who would resolve sharply opposed positions with the formula הלכך נימרינהו לתרווייהו — "let us therefore say both." For this peace-making approach to halachah, the גמרא records, רב פפא was blessed with ten sons, all תלמידי חכמים[5].

Why אהבת עולם Specifically at Night?

The choice of night for the eternal-love version is not arbitrary. The pasuk in ירמיהו opens: מרחוק ה' נראה לי — "from afar, Hashem appeared to me." Night is the time of הסתר פנים — divine concealment. It is when the relationship feels distant, when faith is tested, when the language of great love is harder to mean. The whole tefillah of מעריב is, structurally, a tefillah of אמונה — of holding firm in the dark. To open with ואהבת עולם אהבתיך is therefore to declare: even when the love feels distant, even when nothing is visible, the love is eternal. It was here yesterday, it is here tonight, it will be here tomorrow.

The Geonic Split — מר רב שר שלום, רב שרירא, ורב האי

The תוספות' compromise traces back to מר רב שר שלום גאון[6], ראש ישיבה in Sura in the 9th century. About 150 years later, רב שרירא גאון and his son רב האי גאון[7] rejected the compromise: they held that אהבת עולם is the superior expression and should be said morning and night. The Sefardic and many Eastern nuschaot follow רב שרירא and רב האי. The standard Ashkenaz nusach follows the תוספות' פשרה — אהבה רבה morning, אהבת עולם night.

The אריז״ל[8] adds a layer: even in communities that say אהבת עולם on weekday mornings, on Shabbos morning one returns to אהבה רבה. This is a kabbalistic distinction rooted in סוד and is beyond the scope of the basic halachic discussion.

An Anecdote on This מחלוקת The מאור שמש and אור שמח, ר' מאיר שמחה הכהן of Dvinsk, once wrote a letter to a dear friend — entirely תורה, but at the close he signed off with the cryptic phrase: החותם כשמואל וכרבנן — "He who signs, like Shmuel and like the Rabbanan." The friend puzzled for years, until a תלמיד חכם explained: this מחלוקת — אהבה רבה versus אהבת עולם in ברכות — is the only dispute in all of ש״ס between "Shmuel" and "the Rabbanan" by name. The גאון was telling his friend, in a single signature, "I love you with both — a great love (Shmuel) and an eternal love (the Rabbanan)."

III. אמת ויציב by Day, אמת ואמונה by Night

The גמרא בברכות דף י״ב ע״א[9] establishes a parallel obligation for the ברכות that come after שמע. In the morning we say אמת ויציב; at night we say אמת ואמונה. One who fails to recite each at its proper time has not fulfilled his obligation.

Why two different texts? רש״י על ברכות י״ב ע״א[10] reads the difference as temporal. The morning ברכה — culminating in גאל ישראל — is a הימן of praise for past redemption: the splitting of the sea, יציאת מצרים, the great salvific events of our history. The night ברכה — אמת ואמונה — pivots toward the future: night is the time of גלות and דין, and the ברכה functions as a plea: just as You redeemed us once, redeem us again from our current and future troubles.

The בח's Reading — Past as the Engine of the Future

The בח על אורח חיים סימן ס״ז[20] notes an apparent tension: if אמת ואמונה is meant primarily as a request for future redemption, why does the ברכה still close with גאל ישראל, language that seems to refer to past redemption? The בח resolves: the עיקר of the ברכה really is יציאת מצרים — the past act of redemption — but חז״ל built into it a בקשה on the future, woven into the body of the ברכה without disturbing its closing form. The closing remains גאל ישראל, on past redemption, because that is the historical guarantee on which the future request rests. The past redemption is not the topic alongside the future request; it is the engine of the future request. Just as You did then, do now.

A Grammatical Subtlety — וְאָמְרוּ or וְאָמְרוּ?

Within אמת ואמונה, in the recollection of the splitting of the sea, the text reads:

...בָּנֶיךָ רָאוּ גְבוּרָתְךָ, בָּקַעְתָּ יָם לִפְנֵי משֶׁה. זֶה אֵלִי עָנוּ וְאָמְרוּ: ה' יִמְלךְ לְעֹלָם וָעֶד.

The pronunciation of the word ואמרו divides communities. The standard Ashkenaz pronunciation places a קמץ under the מ: v'amru — "and they said." This reads the verb as introducing the quote: "This is my God," they answered, "and they said: Hashem will reign forever and ever."

The Chassidic pronunciation, in many siddurim, places a שווא under the מ: v'omru. This reads the verb as referring back to זה אלי: what they "answered and said" was the phrase זה אלי, and the line ה' ימלוך לעולם ועד stands as a separate declaration in the continuous present.

This is not a small matter. It is a question of past tense versus ongoing — whether the verse describes what was said then, or what we say now.

IV. ר' זלמן הענא and the יעב״ץ — The Siddur Wars

Behind that one vowel lies one of the great scholarly conflicts of the 18th century. ר' זלמן הענא[11], a paramount expert in Hebrew grammar, embarked on a revolutionary project: to publish a new siddur — שערי תפלה, c. 1720 — that "corrected" what he perceived as grammatical irregularities in the inherited nusach. Many of his proposed changes were vowel-shifts of exactly this kind: ואמרו with שווא rather than קמץ; verb-forms shifted to align with what he held to be the strict grammatical reading. He paired the siddur with a companion sefer — צוהר התבה[21] — built on a play on הקב״ה's command to נח to make a צהר for the תיבה: the Hebrew word תיבה means both "ark" and "word," and the title therefore declares the project as "shining a light on the word."

The reaction was fierce. ר' יעקב עמדין, the יעב״ץ (1697–1776)[12], son of the חכם צבי, mounted a sustained defense of the inherited nusach. He composed לוח ארש (1769) as an extended critique of הענא's siddur, and the work eventually grew into his own siddur, עמודי שמים. The principle was sharp: a continuous mesorah of how a text has been said in tens of thousands of mouths over centuries is itself a halachic-grammatical fact, not to be overruled by a 18th-century grammarian's deduction from rules.

The Disputed הסכמה of the חכם צבי

One of the יעב״ץ's sharpest accusations against הענא was that the הסכמה of the חכם צבי printed at the front of שערי תפלה was a forgery — that the יעב״ץ's own father would never have endorsed הענא's revisions. The accusation was, on its surface, decisive: a son's testimony about his own father.

Centuries later, the matter was complicated. The Bibliotheca Rosenthaliana in Amsterdam — the great Rosenthal collection of Hebraica, founded by the bibliophile Leeser Rosenthal in the 19th century — preserves an enormous archive of early-modern Jewish manuscripts and printed books. The chief librarian of the Rosenthaliana published, in recent decades, an analysis of an autograph manuscript among the holdings that, by his reading, supported the authenticity of the חכם צבי's הסכמה[22]. The matter is not fully resolved in the academic literature, but the easy assumption that the יעב״ץ definitively refuted his opponent's claim must be qualified.

An Aside on the Survival of דקדוק Sefarim The שיעור recounted a personal anecdote: years ago, learning with a young man in Boro Park, the שיעור-giver noticed that the boy's father kept a closet of antique דקדוק sefarim. When asked, the father — not himself a דקדוק scholar — explained that he simply collected them; nobody else does. He gave the שיעור-giver photocopies of three. The point: דקדוק, once a central rabbinic discipline studied alongside גמרא, has fallen so far into disuse that one can fill a personal library with works that nobody else still reads. The sefarim of הענא and the יעב״ץ are themselves part of that nearly-vanished library.
Three Other Battlegrounds The same siddur war ran across multiple words in our daily davening:
  • שמונה עשרה: יהיו לרצון אמרי פי — should it be יִהְיוּ (future) or נִהְיוּ (present)? The בעל התניא and משנה ברורה[13] went with נִהְיוּ; the יעב״ץ defended יִהְיוּ.
  • קדיש: יִתְגַּדַּל (קמץ, Aramaic form) or יִתְגַּדֵּל (Hebrew form, צירי)? The הגר״א[14] ruled like the יעב״ץ for the Aramaic יִתְגַּדַּל; this is now the dominant practice in Ashkenaz.
  • אז ישיר: the pause after ואנוהו in זה אלי ואנוהו אלהי אבי וארממנהו. ר' חיים קניבסקי זצ״ל[25] stressed the importance of pausing here so that the reader does not run ואנוהו into אלהי אבי, which would produce the meaningless string "ve'anveihu Elokei avi". The שיעור recalled an incident at רבי חיים's davening: a baal tefillah paused properly after ואנוהו, and רבי חיים went out of his way after davening to give him a special שכויח, noting that almost nobody pauses there correctly.

V. השכיבנו — A Prayer From the Night of יציאת מצרים

We now turn to the second ברכה after שמע at night: השכיבנו ה' אלקינו לשלום — "Lay us down, Hashem our God, in peace." This ברכה is unique to מעריב; it has no morning counterpart.

ותקננו בעצה טובה מלפניך — A Prayer for Counsel

Within the ברכה sits a peculiar phrase: ותקננו בעצה טובה מלפניך — "and prepare us with good counsel from before You." Why specifically here, at the moment of going to sleep?

The אבודרהם[15] answers: the moments before sleep are unique. They are the only quiet moments in a person's day — when the noise stops, the day's activity is reviewed, and the next day is contemplated. Major decisions are made in those serene moments. The worshiper therefore asks הקב״ה to be present in his thinking precisely then — to grant עצה טובה in the moments when עצה matters most.

רבינו אברהם בן הרמב״ם בספר המספיק לעובדי ה'[16] sharpens the practice: as a person lies in bed before sleep, he should engage his mind in מחשבות קדושות. He should review the תורה learned that day; or use the כח הדמיון — the imagination — to envision the coming of משיח, the rebuilding of the בית המקדש, the gathering of the exiles. The mind that goes to sleep on those images is shaped by them.

The Origin in יציאת מצרים

The רבינו יונה[17] reveals a fascinating origin. The words השכיבנו ה' אלקינו לשלום were first uttered by כלל ישראל on the night of יציאת מצרים. As the מכת בכורות raged outside, the Jews were commanded to remain inside their homes. While chaos erupted across מצרים, they collectively recited a prayer for peace and safety.

But השכיבנו literally says "lay us down to sleep." How could they have been thinking of sleep at such a moment? רבינו יונה reads השכיבנו לשלום not as "let us sleep" but as "let our evening pass in peace" — let the night go quietly enough that we can leave in proper order, without the dogs of מצרים barking after us, without panic. Our nightly השכיבנו is therefore not a private bedtime prayer; it is a commemoration of that first communal prayer for a peaceful night, on the night of redemption.

The גמרא[18] calls השכיבנו part of the גאולה אריכתא — the "long redemption" — connecting our nightly prayer for protection with the original yetzias mitzrayim, and forward through every גלות we are still walking through.

The אור החיים — Light at Night

There is a striking detail in the יציאת מצרים narrative that the אור החיים הקדוש[23] develops in his commentary on שמות י״ב מ״ב. The pasuk says: ליל שמורים הוא לה' להוציאם מארץ מצרים — "It is a night of watching for Hashem, to bring them out of the land of Egypt." Why is the night of יציאת מצרים called a ליל שמורים? The אור החיים reads it together with the Tehillimic ולילה כיום יאיר — "and night will shine like day." On the night of יציאת מצרים, he writes, the night was supernaturally illuminated. The chaos in מצרים was visible. The Jews were not in darkness. The light was part of the redemption itself — for the Jews, light; for the Egyptians, the dark of judgment.

This deepens רבינו יונה's reading. השכיבנו לשלום is asked not in literal darkness but in a night that, for the Jews, was already lit by salvation. The "peace" requested is not relief from fear of the dark but composure in the midst of the redemptive event itself — the practical composure to leave in order. Our nightly השכיבנו carries forward both layers: protection from the night's dangers, and preparation for the redemption that always-already begins in the night.

רבינו אברהם בן הרמב״ם — A Forgotten Voice

A note on the source itself: רבינו אברהם בן הרמב״ם, whose המספיק לעובדי ה' is the deepest of the medieval works on the inner avodah of tefillah, has been until recently a forgotten voice in the English-language world — overshadowed by the towering presence of his father. The work was originally composed in Judeo-Arabic. Feldheim's recent English edition, translated by ר' Yaakov Wincelberg[24], brings it to a wider audience. The translator spent years studying classical Judeo-Arabic in order to produce the work properly. For anyone serious about the inner avodah of tefillah at night, that volume is now the door.

VI. One Mitzvah Twice, or Two Mitzvos?

A final aside the שיעור flagged for future development: there is a major dispute among the ראשונים over the structure of the מצוה of קריאת שמע itself. Is the obligation to say שמע morning and night a single mitzvah performed twice a day — or are they two distinct mitzvos, each independent? The רמב״ם בספר המצוות, מצוות עשה י[19] and the בה״ג hold differently on this; the answer affects whether one who fails to say שמע in the morning has missed half of one obligation or has missed an obligation entirely. The שיעור flagged this for a later session.

The pattern that runs through the night-ברכות is, in retrospect, the same pattern that runs through קבלת שבת: each layer is the deposit of a particular era responding to a particular need. The Talmudic core (one ברכה morning, two at night). The Geonic clarification (אהבה רבה or אהבת עולם, day or night). The Rishonic compromise (תוספות, אבודרהם, רבינו יונה). The 18th-century grammar wars (הענא vs. יעב״ץ, kept alive in our printed siddurim). Each layer is visible if you know how to look. The mussar of the שיעור: when the words come out of our mouths, we should at least know we are speaking text that has been argued over for a thousand years, and that we are voting, in our own quiet way, with whichever pronunciation we choose.

מראה מקומות

  1. משנה ברכות פרק א משנה ד. — Establishes the framework of ברכות around קריאת שמע: בשחר מברך שתים לפניה ואחת לאחריה ובערב שתים לפניה ושתים לאחריה. The frameworks of שחרית and מעריב are deliberately asymmetric — one ברכה after שמע in the morning, two after at night — and this asymmetry is the structural reason for both אמת ואמונה and השכיבנו existing as nighttime-only ברכות. ↩
  2. ברכות דף י״א ע״ב — מחלוקת שמואל ורבנן. — The גמרא records: מאי מברך - אמר רב יהודה אמר שמואל אהבה רבה. וכן אורי ליה רבי אלעזר לרבי פדת בריה אהבה רבה. Conversely the רבנן (or, in some readings, the inherited Eretz-Yisrael nusach) maintain אהבת עולם. The dispute is preserved as a live one in the גמרא; the practice was settled differently in different communities. ↩
  3. ירמיהו פרק ל״א פסוק ב. — מרחוק ה' נראה לי ואהבת עולם אהבתיך על כן משכתיך חסד. The full verse is the scriptural anchor for אהבת עולם in the ברכה. The opening phrase מרחוק ה' נראה לי is itself a key text for the hashkafic claim that night is the time of הסתר פנים — and therefore the right time for the language of eternal-rather-than-momentary love. ↩
  4. תוספות, ברכות י״א ע״ב, ד״ה ולא פליגי. — תוספות propose the פשרה: שמואל's אהבה רבה is recited in the morning; the רבנן's אהבת עולם is recited at night. The compromise reflects the ראשונים' general method of preserving multiple inherited customs by assigning each its proper time. This becomes the standard Ashkenaz nusach. ↩
  5. רב פפא's method of הלכך נימרינהו לתרווייהו; בבא קמא ס״ה ע״ב. — Rav Papa is famous in many sugyot for resolving sharply opposing positions by ruling that both should be said. The phrase הלכך נימרינהו לתרווייהו appears in בבא בתרא ס״ה ע״א, פסחים קי״ד ע״ב, and many other places. The traditional connection between his peace-making in halachah and his merit of ten sons who were all תלמידי חכמים is widely attested: Rav Papa is said to have harmonized ten distinct controversies in ש״ס by ruling נימרינהו לתרווייהו, and was therefore granted ten תלמידי חכמים sons measure-for-measure. The Rama (יו״ד רמ״ו, ס״ק כ״ו, and the closely related sources) brings a complementary explanation tied to his wealth and to the siyumim he hosted for his sons; the peace-making version is the more common derash. Both versions circulate in standard derush literature on the closing of a מסכת. ↩
    The peace-making version (versus the Rama's wealth/siyum version) does not have a single canonical printed citation; it is preserved primarily in the standard הדרן derashos and in commentaries on בבא קמא ס״ה. Yossi: if you have Bar-Ilan, עיון יעקב on ברכות נ״ט and the איי הים on סיום מסכת are good places to look for the explicit connection.
  6. מר רב שר שלום גאון, ראש ישיבה in Sura, mid-9th century. — Author of one of the earliest preserved Geonic responsa-collections. He is cited as the source for the תוספות' compromise of אהבה רבה morning / אהבת עולם night, indicating that the practice was already established in Babylonian Geonic Sura before the תוספות codified the reasoning. ↩
  7. רב שרירא גאון (c. 906–1006) ורב האי גאון (939–1038). — Father and son, the last great גאונים of Pumbedita; their responsa form the bedrock of much of post-Geonic halachah. They held that אהבת עולם is the superior version and should be recited morning and night, against the תוספות' פשרה. Their position is preserved in the Sefardic and many Eastern nuschaot. ↩
  8. האריז״ל — שבת morning אהבה רבה even for those who say אהבת עולם during the week. — The אריז״ל's distinction is preserved in the kavanot works of the תלמידים: even communities that follow רב שרירא ורב האי and recite אהבת עולם on weekday mornings revert to אהבה רבה on Shabbos morning. The reasoning is on the level of סוד and is briefly summarized in פרי עץ חיים, שער קריאת שמע. ↩
  9. ברכות דף י״ב ע״א. — The גמרא: לא אמר אמת ויציב שחרית ואמת ואמונה ערבית לא יצא ידי חובתו — one who fails to recite אמת ויציב in the morning and אמת ואמונה at night has not fulfilled his obligation. This establishes that the two are independently required, each at its proper time. ↩
  10. רש״י על ברכות י״ב ע״א, ד״ה וחותם באמת ואמונה. — רש״י explains the substantive difference: אמת ויציב is the praise of past redemption (גאל ישראל being the closing); אמת ואמונה orients to the future, with the plea that the past redemption be re-enacted and brought to its completion. This grounds the night ברכה in the language of גלות-and-redemption. ↩
  11. ר' זלמן הענא (1687–1746) — שערי תפלה, ספר הדקדוק. — A leading Hebrew grammarian of his era, ר' זלמן הענא published שערי תפלה, a siddur with extensive grammatical "corrections" to the inherited nusach. His changes triggered a generation of polemical literature; the disputes over individual words (ואמרו/ואמרו, יהיו/נהיו, יתגדל/יתגדל) trace primarily to his proposals. ↩
  12. היעב״ץ, ר' יעקב עמדין (1697–1776) — לוח ארש (אלטונא תקכ״ט) וסידור עמודי שמים. — Son of the חכם צבי, the יעב״ץ was one of the most prolific defenders of the inherited siddur tradition. לוח ארש is his sustained critique of הענא's שערי תפלה; from it grew his own סידור עמודי שמים, with extensive commentary defending the traditional vocalizations. The polemic between them shaped the printed siddur for the next two centuries. ↩
  13. בעל התניא בסידור; משנה ברורה סימן קכ״ב סעיף ב'. — The Alter Rebbe's siddur and the משנה ברורה rule that the closing of שמונה עשרה — יהיו לרצון אמרי פי — should be vocalized with the present-tense form נִהְיוּ in some contexts, and the future יִהְיוּ in others. The exact rulings differ between the two sources; the dispute reflects the broader argument over how strictly to apply grammatical rules to inherited liturgical vocalization. ↩
  14. הגר״א — יִתְגַּדַּל as Aramaic form. — The Vilna Gaon ruled, like the יעב״ץ and against הענא, that the opening word of קדיש should be vocalized יִתְגַּדַּל with קמץ — preserving the Aramaic form rather than imposing a Hebrew vocalization. This is preserved in מעשה רב and is the dominant practice in Ashkenaz today. ↩
  15. אבודרהם, ברכות קריאת שמע של ערבית. — ר' דוד אבודרהם (14th century, Sevilla) on the phrase ותקננו בעצה טובה מלפניך. He explains that the moments before sleep are uniquely contemplative, when major life decisions are made, and the worshiper therefore asks הקב״ה to be present in his deliberations at precisely that hour. The אבודרהם's commentary is the standard medieval reference for the meaning of unusual phrases in the liturgy. ↩
  16. רבינו אברהם בן הרמב״ם, המספיק לעובדי ה'. — The son of the רמב״ם, in his major work on עבודת ה', describes the proper avodah of the moments before sleep: a person should engage his mind in מחשבות קדושות, reviewing his learning of the day or using the כח הדמיון to envision the coming of משיח and the rebuilding of the בית המקדש. The work was originally composed in Judeo-Arabic; the modern Hebrew edition is by Rabbi Nissim Dana. ↩
  17. רבינו יונה על ברכות דף ד' ע״א מדפי הרי״ף. — רבינו יונה reads השכיבנו לשלום not as "lay us down to sleep" but as a prayer for the night to pass peacefully. He grounds this reading in the historical claim that the words were first uttered by כלל ישראל on the night of יציאת מצרים, when the Jews were commanded to remain in their homes during מכת בכורות; the prayer was for a peaceful evening that would allow them to depart from מצרים in proper order. ↩
  18. ברכות דף ד' ע״ב — גאולה אריכתא. — The גמרא addresses the apparent problem that השכיבנו after שמע at night seems to interrupt the connection between גאולה and תפילה. The answer: השכיבנו itself is part of גאולה אריכתא — the long redemption — and therefore does not interrupt; it continues the theme of redemption rather than leaving it. This is the structural reason that השכיבנו is positioned where it is, and the source for understanding it as a continuation of the יציאת מצרים theme of אמת ואמונה. ↩
  19. רמב״ם, ספר המצוות, מצוות עשה י; בה״ג. — The רמב״ם counts קריאת שמע as a single מצוה, performed both morning and night. The בה״ג (בעל הלכות גדולות) is read by some as counting them as two separate מצוות. The dispute affects whether one who fails to recite שמע in the morning has missed "half" of a single obligation or has missed an entire obligation. The shiur flagged this as a topic for a future session and did not develop it here. ↩
  20. בח, אורח חיים סימן ס״ז. — ר' יואל סירקיש, the בח (1561–1640), addresses the apparent contradiction between the body of אמת ואמונה (which speaks of future redemption) and its closing גאל ישראל (which speaks of past redemption). His resolution: the עיקר of the ברכה is יציאת מצרים — the past act — but חז״ל wove a בקשה for the future into the body without altering the closing form. The closing remains גאל ישראל because the past redemption is the historical guarantee on which every future request rests. ↩
  21. צוהר התבה, מאת ר' זלמן הענא (פפד״מ ת״פ). — הענא's companion sefer to his siddur שערי תפלה. The title plays on הקב״ה's command to נח — צהר תעשה לתבה — exploiting the double meaning of תיבה as both "ark" and "word." The work systematically defends הענא's grammatical "corrections" to the inherited nusach, with extensive treatment of vowel patterns, verb tenses, and the rules of biblical Hebrew applied to liturgical text. It is among the texts the יעב״ץ targeted directly in לוח ארש. ↩
  22. The Hanau / חכם צבי הסכמה controversy; Johann Christoph Wolf, Bibliotheca Hebraea vol. I (Hamburg 1715); Bibliotheca Rosenthaliana, Amsterdam. — The dispute over whether the הסכמה of the חכם צבי printed at the front of הענא's בית תפלה / שערי תפלה (Jessnitz 1725) was authentic or forged is well documented. The יעב״ץ — son of the חכם צבי — alleged forgery and built the case in לוח ארש (אלטונא תקכ״ט, 1769). The earliest external account of the dispute is in Johann Christoph Wolf, Bibliotheca Hebraea, vol. I (Hamburg 1715), where Wolf describes Hanau and the contested approbation. The Bibliotheca Rosenthaliana at the University of Amsterdam — founded by Leeser Rosenthal (1794–1868) — holds significant primary materials related to the dispute, and curator Adri K. Offenberg published extensively on the Rosenthaliana's Judaica collection (including the catalogue Hebrew and Judaic Manuscripts in Amsterdam Public Collections, 1973, and Bibliotheca Rosenthaliana: Treasures of Jewish Booklore, 1994). The שיעור's specific claim that a Rosenthaliana librarian published an analysis supporting the authenticity of the הסכמה (against the יעב״ץ's forgery accusation) is plausible given that scholars of the Rosenthaliana have addressed the Hanau-Emden dispute, but I was unable to locate the specific paper through open-source academic search. ↩
    Verifiable: Wolf's Bibliotheca Hebraea (1715) treats the Hanau dispute; Offenberg was a curator who published on the Rosenthaliana collection; Studia Rosenthaliana is the journal where such an article would most likely appear. Not yet verified: a specific Rosenthaliana article supporting the הסכמה's authenticity. The scholarly literature on the controversy includes Jacob J. Schacter's Harvard dissertation, Rabbi Jacob Emden: Life and Major Works (1988), which would be a stronger starting point for tracking the specific paper.
  23. אור החיים על שמות פרק י״ב פסוק מ״ב (ליל שמורים); תהלים פרק קל״ט פסוק י״ב. — The אור החיים הקדוש (ר' חיים בן עטר, 1696–1743) on the pasuk ליל שמורים הוא לה' להוציאם מארץ מצרים develops the theme of the night of יציאת מצרים as supernaturally illuminated for the Jews. He reads it together with the Tehillimic ולילה כיום יאיר, presenting the night as a moment when the usual phenomenology of darkness was suspended for those leaving Egypt. This grounds the practical question of how כלל ישראל could have said השכיבנו at all in such circumstances: the night was, for them, already lit. ↩
  24. המספיק לעובדי ה', מאת רבינו אברהם בן הרמב״ם; English edition translated by ר' Yaakov Wincelberg, Feldheim Publishers. — The original Judeo-Arabic work is the major medieval treatise on the inner avodah of tefillah and serving הקב״ה. The recent Feldheim English edition, translated by ר' Yaakov Wincelberg, makes the work accessible for the first time to a wide English-speaking audience. The translator spent years studying classical Judeo-Arabic in order to produce the volume; the resulting edition has been broadly received as the standard English-language reference for this work. ↩
  25. ר' חיים קניבסקי זצ״ל on the pause after ואנוהו; cf. טעמי המקרא on שמות ט״ו ב. — A teaching from רבי חיים, transmitted orally by תלמידים: in the pasuk זה אלי ואנוהו אלהי אבי וארממנהו, one must pause after ואנוהו. The grammatical basis is independent of any specific Kanievsky teaching: the מסורה places an אתנחתא (the major mid-verse pause) under ואנוהו in שמות ט״ו ב, dividing the pasuk into two parallel halves — "this is my God, and I will glorify Him" // "the God of my father, and I will exalt Him." Reading without the pause runs the two halves into one continuous string and obscures the parallelism. The שיעור recalled an instance at רבי חיים's davening where a baal tefillah paused correctly at the אתנחתא and was singled out by רבי חיים afterward for the unusual care. רבי חיים's broader emphasis on careful reading of טעמי המקרא in tefillah is well attested in collections such as שיח השדה, דרך שיחה, and אורחות יושר. ↩
    The grammatical-מסורה point — that there is an אתנחתא under ואנוהו demanding a pause — is verifiable in any pointed תנ״ך and is solid. The specific anecdote about רבי חיים singling out a baal tefillah for it is preserved orally; I was unable to locate it in a searchable published source. שיח השדה חלק ב (HebrewBooks #47701) and דרך שיחה on פרשת בשלח are the most likely places to find a written version, but I cannot read those volumes directly through open search to confirm.
