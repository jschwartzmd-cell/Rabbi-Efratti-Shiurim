The Significance of the Seder

Ground Brick in חרוסת, the Algerian מרור, and a שאלה From the Streets of ירושלים
Recorded 22 March 2026 · Reworked from the original שיעור

This morning I want to discuss the seder in general, even though the email said we would be focusing on the הגדה. I want to share some insights that may help us gain a deeper understanding of what we are about to commemorate on this holy night — including a custom that, when I first encountered it, genuinely shocked me, and a fascinating שאלה in הלכה that, to be honest, only came up in 1900 in ירושלים but is worth understanding.

I. A Shocking Discovery — Sand in the חרוסת

A few years ago I encountered something I had never heard of and resolved to research it. This year I received tremendous help from my friend David Helfgott, who sponsors our הגדה annually. He introduced me to a fascinating new הגדה called היו דברים מעולם, written in modern Hebrew — so if you are not used to that style it can be a little challenging — by Rabbi Yisroel Dandirovich, who runs a Gerrer ישיבה in ערד. When I opened it, I found an entire chapter dedicated to this very topic I had been researching, with all the information.

II. The שיבולי הלקט and Roman Jewish Customs

We frequently quote a ספר called שיבולי הלקט[1], written by רבינו צדקיה הרופא — a ראשון who served as רב in Rome and was נפטר around 1280. The בית יוסף in אורח חיים quotes the שיבולי הלקט on almost every page. Many of our מנהגים, הלכות, and customs in אורח חיים rest on his work.

To grasp this properly: there are many נוסחאות תפילה — נוסח אשכנז, נוסח ספרד, נוסח האר״י, תימנים, and נוסח רומי, the נוסח of the Roman Jews of Italy. We have discussed this many times. If you have ever traveled to Rome and visited the Great Synagogue and opened their סידור, you saw radical differences. Extra ברכות in ברכות השחר. A completely different נוסח in שמונה עשרה. Why? Because the Jews of Rome and Italy maintained a tradition spanning a thousand years. They actually claim to descend from the original yidden who came during the time of בית ראשון and stayed there — never returning for בית שני. They preserved an ancient נוסח and ancient מנהגים.

The שיבולי הלקט is one of the great compilations of those נוסחאות, הלכות, and מנהגים. To this day, yidden of Rome, Italy, and Italian descent — some now in ארץ ישראל — preserve them.

III. Ground Brick in the חרוסת

The Custom

The שיבולי הלקט writes that in his community in Rome, when they prepared the חרוסת for seder night, they would take sand or clay — or, according to another version, an actual red brick — grind it to dust, and put it in the חרוסת. They then dipped the מרור into it and ate.

When I first read this, I was stunned. Who eats sand? Is sand healthy? Imagine taking a red brick, grinding it, and eating the powder. It is hard enough for a yid to digest מצות, four כוסות, and מרור — without ground brick added to the list.

The Reason

Why would such a מנהג exist? The שיבולי הלקט explains based on the פסוק חומר ולבנים[2] — clay and bricks. What did the מצרים make us do? Cement and clay. חומר means clay; לבנים means bricks. In Egypt, the way they constructed the pyramids — or whatever they built — was made from חומר ולבנים. So just as we eat מרור because of וימררו את חייהם, we should also eat חומר ולבנים.

The Custom Documented Into Modern Times

This מנהג is documented by some אחרונים. Specifically, in תרצ״ח (1937–38) there exists a מחזור בני רומי[3] — the seder used in Rome at that time. In its compilation of הלכות פסח, in the section discussing the seder, it states explicitly: the Italian Jews who grind a brick and put the shavings in the חרוסת — יפה עושים, they are doing the right thing. This has been the מנהג of our ancestors for a thousand years, since the times of the ראשונים. It is a זכר for לבנים and a worthy מנהג.

In the מנהגים of the yidden of Livorno, an Italian city, they specify: not just any sand or clay — it must be a red brick, because they determined that in Egypt the bricks were made from red sand. So the חרוסת should have a red color as the original זכר for חומר ולבנים.

IV. The Algerian מנהג — Throwing the מרור

There is a striking מנהג preserved in Algeria. In Algeria there was a Jewish community with unique מנהגים documented in special הגדות. When saying מרור זה during מגיד, some קהילות point to the מרור. In Algeria the practice was different and remarkable.

The mnhag in Algeria was to pick up the מרור and say מרור זה three times. Then — and this is what stops you in your tracks — they would throw the מרור on the floor, pick it up, and put it back on the קערת הסדר.

The Meaning

Algeria had great רבנים and תלמידי חכמים. Indeed, שו״ת הרשב״ץ[4] — the תשב״ץ — comes entirely from Algeria. These were serious קהילות.

What does the מנהג mean? The רבנים wrote that when we experience מרור — when we re-experience וימררו את חייהם בעבודה קשה and eat the מרור — we are remembering our suffering and subjugation. We had pure bitterness for years. The symbolic act: take the מרור and throw it away, expressing our desire to be rid of the bitterness of life. We do not want it anymore — we throw it on the ground.

And Why Pick It Up Again?

This is the key. The Algerian יהודים had the same מנהג as the Italian yidden! They did it intentionally. They would rinse the מרור afterward. Since they wanted to fulfill the custom of putting soil, sand, or clay into the חרוסת — instead of grinding a brick — they threw the מרור on the floor. In the 1400s, the floors were sand. Now you have מרור coated with sand — fulfilling the requirement.

The Issue of ביזוי אוכלין

This raises a question: why is this not ביזוי אוכלין? The שלחן ערוך[5] forbids degrading or debasing food, throwing food on the ground. The גמרא in תענית[6] tells of רב יהודה seeing two yidden tossing bread back and forth: "How can you do this? It is ביזוי אוכלין!" The primary concern in the גמרא, particularly in כתובות, is around bread, though other foods are also discussed.

So why is it permitted on ליל הסדר? It is ביזוי אוכלין; it is potentially בל תשחית. The answer: they ate it afterward, so בל תשחית does not apply. The only remaining issue is the ground itself — and since it was for the sake of the מצוה, to fulfill the זכר of the שעבוד, it was permitted.

V. The מהר״ם די לונזאנו's Strong Objection

Who He Was

The חיד״א — who lived in the seventeenth century and wrote over a hundred ספרים — authored a major work, ברכי יוסף, his commentary on שלחן ערוך אורח חיים. You are guaranteed to find fascinating material in ברכי יוסף. The חיד״א was an eclectic personality who loved to gather all the different מנהגים of the world's קהילות and connect them.

He brings from one of the אחרונים, המהר״ם די לונזאנו[7] — רבינו מנחם די לונזאנו — someone every yid should know. Born in Italy in the fifteenth century, he later made עליה to ארץ ישראל.

"לא ראיתי דבר משוגע כזה"

The מהר״ם די לונזאנו wrote in the margins of his ספר, on the section of שיבולי הלקט about putting לבנים into the חרוסת:

לא ראיתי דבר משוגע כזה.

I have never seen anything as crazy as this. (The word משוגע appears in the תורה — you should know that.)

Listen to his logic. He says: what kind of thing is this? If you are going to scrape brick dust into the חרוסת because you want to remember the טיט that כלל ישראל used in מצרים — then perhaps on פורים we should each take a needle, draw a little blood, and put it on the table to remember the decree of המן? What kind of madness is this? Are we crazy? Yes, we need to relive the experience of מרור by eating מרור — but to such an extent that we put bricks in our food and eat the bricks? He had never seen such a thing. He heard yidden in Italy do it; the מנהג, in his view, should be nullified.

A Defense — ערב תשעה באב

One of the אחרונים pushes back: wait, we have a similar מנהג — not on פסח, but on ערב תשעה באב we take bread and a hard-boiled egg and dip them in ash. Is ash healthy? Ash actually contains carcinogenic materials. The Briskers pour the food into a bowl of ash, and... it is not really healthy. But the רמ״א[8] brings the מנהג, and as far as I know, every yid does this. We put ash on the bread or on the egg on ערב תשעה באב. The ash is a symbol of mourning. So if we put ash on ערב תשעה באב, why not טיט on פסח? What is the big problem?

The Counter

The comparison is weak. פסח is פסח and תשעה באב is תשעה באב. תשעה באב is a day of mourning — you do not eat, you sit on the floor, all of it fits. But on יום טוב — this is the מהר״ם די לונזאנו's point — on יום טוב we are obligated in joy. We must take the pain, the suffering, the bitterness, and turn it into sweetness. How can we, on יום טוב, take it so literally and re-traumatize ourselves? That is his argument.

VI. The Nature of מרור — The חכם צבי's Perspective

What We Use Today

Another reason we can defend the מנהג: we eat מרור, correct? מרור is bitter. But here is a famous תשובה of the חכם צבי[9] — open your ears.

In אשכנז, your great-grandparents — what did they use for מרור? חזרת — the white root of horseradish. The white, the black — you grate it and eat a כזית. The stronger the better, supposedly. But ask yourself: who eats a כזית of grated horseradish? In our family we used to grate it right before יום טוב so it would not lose the burn, then seal it in a glass jar so it would not lose the strength. It really burns. To eat a כזית of that — almost no one actually does it.

The Ruling of the חכם צבי

The חכם צבי goes sharply against the practice in אשכנז and פולין of treating חזרת as the עיקר of מצוות מרור. Not everyone agrees with the חכם צבי, but listen to how he writes.

He says: I want לזכות את הרבים. I want to give כלל ישראל a more pleasant פסח. (The חכם צבי was a great proponent of making things easier for yidden. He was the one who stood ready to permit קטניות; he writes that if other authorities would join him, he would permit it. He saw אינ סוף of these stringencies as foolish.)

He continues: in אשכנז and פולין, I see that everyone tries to eat a כזית of horseradish. It is so strong that people manage to eat at most half a כזית — and they do not fulfill the מצוה. They are making a mistake. This is not מרור.

מרור is lettuce. מרור is romaine — the green leaves we all use. Therefore eat a כזית of those. Use mainly romaine; add a small amount of horseradish if you wish, for the burn. If you only eat horseradish — what we today call חזרת — the חכם צבי says: יצא שכרו בהפסדו. You think you are being מחמיר? You are losing out, because most people never actually eat the full כזית. Do not be so pious. Take the green romaine for the כזית; add as much or as little horseradish as you wish. He does not want anyone to change their family מנהג. He simply tells us what he holds.

The גמרא's Definition of מרור

The חכם צבי explains: when the גמרא[10] identifies מרור, it does not mean that the מרור must be sharp-bitter to the tongue. This is the common mistake. חז״ל say מרור — romaine lettuce — is תחילתו ימתיק וסופו ימרר. A vital phrase to remember.

In the beginning, when lettuce comes out of the ground, it is sweet — tasty, crunchy, fresh; nothing like it. But if you allow the lettuce to stay in the ground beyond its proper time, the root hardens and turns truly bitter — bitter as death. That is what חז״ל meant: eat a vegetable that turns bitter after some time, to remember that we had bitterness. That is the זכר.

The חכם צבי's point: a yid who follows this gets a real כזית of מרור and fulfills the מצוה. A yid who fights to eat horseradish often does not even get half a כזית. I am not saying anyone should change their מנהג. But this is what the חכם צבי says.

VII. Back to the טיט in חרוסת — Maybe a Scribal Error

The same idea applies to the brick in the חרוסת. The טיט we commemorate, the טיט used to build מצרים — it does not have to be actual physical טיט, just like מרור does not have to be actually bitter. A small piece of memorialized מרור is enough. So where did the literal practice come from? This bothered me, and I am happy to share what I found this past Friday.

The רשב״ם in פסחים

There is a רשב״ם in מסכת פסחים[11]. The גמרא asks: why do we eat חרוסת? We need to explain to our children: why do we dip the מרור in this חרוסת? What is חרוסת? Everyone has different מנהגים — apples, walnuts, a little wine, cinnamon, sometimes ginger.

The גמרא in סנהדרין records two opinions on what חרוסת comes to commemorate.

Two Opinions in the גמרא

Opinion one: apples are the main component, and apples connect to the פסוק in שיר השירים תחת התפוח עוררתיך[12]. חז״ל explain that during the שעבוד מצרים, when every Jewish baby was thrown into the Nile, Jewish women fled to the apple orchards outside מצרים to give birth in secret. When the מצרים tried to find them, the babies miraculously sank into the ground. הקדוש ברוך הוא fed them with honey and apples until they were redeemed at יציאת מצרים, when they joined כלל ישראל. Therefore חרוסת recalls the apples.

Opinion two: the second opinion says no — the חרוסת is זכר לטיט, the mortar used to build מצרים. Therefore the גמרא paskens that the חרוסת must be thick in consistency. Many yidden ruin this by pouring too much wine. Do not pour too much wine. The wine is essential, but make it thick. This is הלכה. The חרוסת should be thick like טיט, like mud, like חומר.

The רשב״ם and the Word "חרס"

The רשב״ם there explains the word — and I do not have a גמרא פסחים here in front of me, so I am working from memory — that חרוסת is called חרוסת because of the consistency, like חרס. חרס means earthenware — a vessel made from baked clay or sand.

Apparently the שיבולי הלקט read the רשב״ם and concluded: "aha — one of the actual components of the חרוסת is חרס. The very name חרוסת comes from the word חרס. So we need to put חרס in it." That, it appears, is the source of the שיבולי הלקט's מנהג.

The Correct Text — רש״י Settles It

But if you look at רש״י on the same גמרא, he gives the same explanation as the רשב״ם — only instead of writing "חרס," רש״י writes "חרוסת." The full word.

What happened over the centuries? The שיבולי הלקט, it appears, was reading a defective text. In those days everything was hand-copied. Someone wrote the word חרוסת and abbreviated it: ח״ר״ס with an apostrophe (a copyist's shorthand for חרוסת). Someone later misread the apostrophe and took it to mean the word literally חרס — earthenware itself. From there came the crazy מנהג the מהר״ם די לונזאנו ridiculed: putting actual חרס into the חרוסת, taking it as an integral component of the food.

We know today that this is not the correct version of רש״י or of the רשב״ם. The רשב״ם wrote וחרוסת. He was only telling you that the name חרוסת comes from something that looks like טיט — thick like mud. He was not telling you to put physical חרס into the food.

The מלאכת שלמה Verifies the Text

And it amazes me that out of an inaccurate reading of a text in רש״י or רשב״ם, כלל ישראל — at least the Italian yidden who held this מנהג — ended up making physical חרס an actual ingredient of the חרוסת for centuries.

If you look at the משניות in ערבי פסחים, the last פרק of פסחים which discusses חרוסת, there is a discussion between the תוספות יום טוב and the מלאכת שלמה[13]. (If you learn משניות you know מלאכת שלמה: רבי שלמה אדני, a Yemenite yid who lived most of his life in חברון. I was actually at his קבר in חברון recently in the ancient cemetery. The מלאכת שלמה appears in every printed משניות.) He already caught this. He saw that the רשב״ם appears to say "חרס" and went and checked ancient manuscripts. No one, he concludes, actually said "חרס" literally. The word in the original is חרוסת. The "חרס" reading is a copyist's error.

VIII. A Strange Historical Precedent — צמח דוד

Let me share something fascinating from Jewish history that may connect.

In פראג there was a yid named רב דוד גנז. Anyone interested in Jewish history must know him — he is one of the primary historians of אשכנז in Europe in the 1600s. He was a תלמיד of the מהרש״ל and the רמ״א — which puts him about four hundred fifty to five hundred years ago. רב דוד גנז wrote a ספר called צמח דוד[14]. Any modern Jewish history book — Berel Wein, Rav Shalom, anyone — quotes צמח דוד. It is one of the primary sources. רב דוד גנז was a גדול, a mathematician, an astronomer, a צדיק — a very special yid. I read the ספר for pleasure; it is amazing.

צמח דוד writes about something he himself witnessed. In פראג in the late 1500s, in Czechoslovakia, there was a famine. There was no rain; widespread hunger; people had nothing to eat.

A yid was desperate. He had a family to feed but no food. Out of desperation he went to his backyard — this was on the outskirts of פראג — and started digging. Maybe he would find some root vegetables, carrots, potatoes, even buried treasure. He dug and dug — and discovered, beneath the soil, a layer of very fine white sand, unlike any sand he had ever seen. He took that white sand, mixed it with water, made a dough, baked it in the oven, and fed it to his family. They were satisfied.

צמח דוד writes that hundreds and thousands of yidden in the area heard about this ישועה. Someone was digging flour out of the ground without planting wheat or threshing. People ate from this earth-bread for months.

I do not know exactly what it was; you should research it. There is some evidence that certain mineral-rich earths can be eaten — though most cannot. I bring it up because it has a מקור in our own writings and may be relevant to our understanding of the Italian מנהג.

IX. The Essential Point About חרוסת

Bottom line: we are not putting actual sand in our חרוסת. We are not putting mud. We are not putting bricks or red brick shavings. What we are doing is remembering the נסים of הקדוש ברוך הוא. The חרוסת is, in a sense, a garnish — but the essential idea is what רבי יהודה taught: we have to tell our children. We dip the מרור into the חרוסת. We are remembering the pain, the hard work, the שעבוד — and we are acknowledging that we had that שעבוד.

X. Confronting the Pain

Every psychologist today says the same thing: in order to get past trauma, past pain, you cannot hide it under the rug. That is the cardinal rule. Trauma swept under the rug bursts out one day in some other form — toxic, dangerous to you and to those around you.

The way to deal with past pain, anxiety, troubles, and traumas is to confront them. Grab the pain and say: "I know who you are. I see you. I experienced you. I understand you." Only then can the path of healing begin.

When we say מרור זה and dip it into the חרוסת, we are doing exactly that. We know the pain we have. We know the pain we had. We acknowledge the מרור.

Many yidden have experienced this at their seder. When we used to give מרור to my grandparents around the table, my grandmother — a Holocaust survivor — would say: "I do not need any מרור — I had מרור." She would take only a little.

Sometimes you have to stare the pain in the face, acknowledge it exists, and say "help me get past it." Then we dip it in the חרוסת — which is sweet, with wine and apples and cinnamon — and the bitter is sweetened. That is the design.

XI. A Fascinating שאלה — The Egg That Was Born on שבת That Was Also יום טוב

The Setting

I want to share an interesting שאלה — not relevant for this year, but worth knowing. It happened about 125 years ago in ירושלים. ערב פסח fell on שבת, meaning מוצאי שבת was the seder night. (We had this situation last year.) On ערב פסח that is also שבת, you cannot do any preparation in the usual way.

The Story

A woman in ירושלים prepared everything on Friday. She gathered eggs from her chickens — this was ירושלים of 120 years ago, when many yidden kept chickens. She set out a bowl of fifteen eggs, intending to boil them right after שבת so her husband could come home from shul to ready hard-boiled eggs for the seder. (The מנהג is to eat an egg at the start of שולחן עורך as זכר לחגיגה.) She had counted exactly fifteen eggs for the fifteen people coming to the table.

After שבת she boiled the eggs and peeled them. When she counted, there were sixteen. One extra. She knew she had set out fifteen. Where did the extra come from?

A good yidisher mama needs to know what happens in her kitchen. She went child by child: "Did you do anything to the eggs? Did you add any?" Her young son, a six-year-old, said: "On שבת I was in the yard and I saw an extra egg the chicken had laid. I figured they were all for the seder. I saw your bowl, so I added it."

The הלכתי Problem

It turned out that one of the sixteen peeled eggs was נולד — born on שבת. An egg laid on יום טוב or שבת is מוקצה[15]. You may not eat it. You may eat it only after יום טוב.

The שאלה came to the פוסקים of ירושלים. You cannot just hand the eggs to a non-Jew or borrow eggs from a neighbor. You cannot eat them now. So either we abandon eggs at this seder — and lose the זכר לחגיגה — or perhaps the rule of בטל ברוב applies?

Why ביטול Doesn't Work

We might say it should be בטל בששים. What is the big deal? But there are two problems. First: you may not be מבטל איסור לכתחילה. Second — and worse — this is a דבר שיש לו מתירין[16]. A דבר שיש לו מתירין is something currently forbidden but that will become permissible. The גמרא says you cannot eat such a thing through ביטול. Why be מבטל now? Wait twenty-four hours and eat it בהיתר. Such an item is never בטל.

The egg was laid on שבת, so it is מוקצה now. But it will become מותר after יום טוב — so it cannot be בטל. The משנה says explicitly that when שבת falls on יום טוב, you cannot eat an egg that was laid on שבת until after יום טוב. So it is a דבר שיש לו מתירין; it cannot be בטל.

We are stuck.

The שואל ציון's Solution

The שואל ציון[17] thought, and thought, and said: I have a פשט — and it is מותר one hundred percent.

Listen to the חידוש. The egg was laid on שבת. One of the sixteen is מוקצה. By the rule of דבר שיש לו מתירין, we should not be able to be מבטל it. But, says the שואל ציון, watch carefully.

The גמרא in נדה דף ל״ז[18] lists five things you may not eat — מחמת סכנה. Among them: a peeled egg left overnight is forbidden. A peeled onion left overnight is forbidden. Why? Because of רוח רעה — an evil spirit. (Some פוסקים today are lenient if it is in the refrigerator, but 120 years ago there were no refrigerators.) A peeled egg that sits overnight is אסור.

The Critical Point

That means: if you wait until מוצאי יום טוב to eat the egg, you will not be able to eat it anyway. The egg has been peeled and will sit overnight. By the time it would become halachically permitted, it will be forbidden for a different reason. So the egg is not a דבר שיש לו מתירין — it will never become permitted to eat. Therefore it can be בטל ברוב.

The מרדכי[19] writes exactly this rule: if a food item will become מותר but by the time it does it will already have spoiled or become forbidden for another reason, it is not a דבר שיש לו מתירין. Therefore it is בטל ברוב, and the woman may eat from the bowl.

The Counter-Question

When yidden in ירושלים heard the תשובה, they raised a major problem. The story could not have happened — or פסח that year must have been on a different day! Why? Because ליל שימורים[20]. On ליל פסח we leave the door open. On ליל פסח there are no מזיקין. If there are no מזיקין, there is no רוח רעה. If there is no רוח רעה, the peeled egg will not become forbidden overnight. Then it is, again, a דבר שיש לו מתירין — and we are back to square one.

The Resolution — שולי צח

The answer is given in שולי צח. He says the story is מקובל in ירושלים, so we must defend it. You are right that there are no מזיקין on ליל פסח. We leave the door open; there are no thieves; the protection is real. But of course there is רוח רעה. How do we know? Because we wash our hands the morning after the seder. Why do we wash? Because of רוח רעה. So רוח רעה exists.

In other words: if you put food under your bed on seder night, it would be fine if there were no רוח רעה. But there is רוח רעה. מזיקין do not exist on ליל פסח; רוח רעה does. So the egg will indeed be forbidden by the next morning, the dvar she-yesh lo matirin status falls away, and the egg is בטל ברוב. The story stands. The woman ate her seder.

סיכום

Have a wonderful week ahead and הצלחה. If you are leaving for פסח, we will miss you. Take care.

  1. שיבולי הלקט, ר' צדקיה בן אברהם הרופא (Rome, 13th century). — ר' צדקיה בן אברהם הרופא of Rome (c. 1210–1280), foundational ראשון for Italian-Jewish (בני רומי) practice. שיבולי הלקט is a comprehensive halachic-liturgical compendium quoted hundreds of times by the בית יוסף. The מנהג of including ground brick or sand in the חרוסת as זכר לחומר ולבנים is recorded in his treatment of סדר ליל פסח. ↩
  2. שמות א':י"ד — "וימררו את חייהם בעבודה קשה בחומר ובלבנים". — The פסוק describing Egyptian slavery: bitter lives in clay (חומר) and bricks (לבנים). The חרוסת is the זכר for חומר ולבנים; the מרור is the זכר for וימררו. The שיעור is built on this פסוק. ↩
  3. מחזור בני רומי (תרצ״ח / 1937–38, Rome).
    Note: The מחזור בני רומי documenting the customs of Italian Jewry as practiced into the 20th century. The 1937–38 edition (just before the Italian racial laws of 1938) records that the practice of including ground brick in the חרוסת was still affirmed by the Rabbinate of Rome. Many copies of this מחזור survive in libraries; verify exact citation.
    ↩
  4. שו"ת הרשב"ץ, ר' שמעון בן צמח דוראן (Algiers, 1361–1444). — The תשב"ץ (acronym for תלמיד שמעון בן צמח; later expanded as תשובות שמעון בן צמח). Born Mallorca, fled the Spanish persecutions of 1391, served as רב of Algiers for over forty years. His שו"ת is the foundational halachic source for the Algerian/North African קהילות; documents many distinctive Algerian מנהגים including the מרור-throwing practice the שיעור describes. ↩
  5. שלחן ערוך, או"ח קע"א — ביזוי אוכלין. — The שלחן ערוך codifies the prohibition of ביזוי אוכלין (degrading food): one may not throw food, sit on food, treat food with disrespect, or do anything that diminishes the dignity of food. The principle derives from the ברכה we say on bread and from the broader value of recognizing food as חסד from ה'. The Algerian מרור-throwing מנהג sits in tension with this איסור; defended on the grounds of צורך מצוה. ↩
  6. תענית כ' ע"ב; כתובות ק"ה ע"ב. — The גמרא in תענית: רב הונא (some say רב יהודה) saw two yidden tossing bread and rebuked them severely. The גמרא in כתובות develops the wider sin of ביזוי אוכלין and connects it to ענני שמים being withheld. Foundational sources for the איסור. ↩
  7. שתי ידות / דרך חיים, ר' מנחם די לונזאנו (1550–1626). — ר' מנחם די לונזאנו, born in Italy, made עליה to ארץ ישראל and lived in ירושלים and צפת. His ספרים שתי ידות and דרך חיים contain his sharp critique of the Italian חרוסת-with-brick מנהג: "לא ראיתי דבר משוגע כזה". His critique is preserved in the חיד"א's ברכי יוסף on או"ח. ↩
  8. רמ"א, או"ח תקנ"ב — סעודה המפסקת בערב תשעה באב. — The רמ"א's codification of the Ashkenazic מנהג on ערב תשעה באב: at the סעודה המפסקת, dip a hard-boiled egg or piece of bread in אפר as a sign of mourning. The practice is widely observed; the שיעור invokes it as a structural parallel for the Italian חרוסת-and-brick custom on פסח. ↩
  9. שו"ת חכם צבי, סימנים ק"כ-קי"ט (on מרור). — ר' צבי הירש אשכנזי (1656–1718), the חכם צבי, served as רב of Amsterdam and Hamburg-Altona. His שו"ת on מרור argues for romaine lettuce (חזרת in the לשון of the משנה) as the primary מרור, against the Ashkenazic-Polish practice of horseradish (also called חזרת in modern Hebrew, but a different botanical species). His son the יעב"ץ elaborates the position; ר' שלמה זלמן אויערבאך and other contemporary פוסקים follow. ↩
  10. משנה פסחים פ"ב מ"ו; פסחים ל"ט ע"א. — The משנה lists five vegetables that fulfill the מצוה of מרור: "חזרת, עולשין, תמכא, חרחבינא, מרור". The גמרא identifies חזרת as the preferred species and develops the principle "תחילתו ימתיק וסופו ימרר" (sweet at first, bitter at last) — the botanical signature of romaine, not horseradish. The חכם צבי reads this גמרא as definitive: חזרת = romaine, not horseradish. ↩
  11. רשב"ם, פסחים קט"ז ע"א, ד"ה חרוסת. — The רשב"ם (ר' שמואל בן מאיר, c. 1085–1158, grandson of רש"י): the חרוסת is so named because it is thick like חרסית/חרס-clay — a description of texture, not a recipe ingredient. The שיעור's hypothesis: a copyist abbreviated חרוסת as ח"ר"ס, later misread as the literal word חרס, generating the Italian מנהג of putting actual חרס in the חרוסת. ↩
  12. שיר השירים ח':ה'; סוטה י"א ע"ב. — "תחת התפוח עוררתיך": the פסוק in שיר השירים read by חז"ל in סוטה י"א ע"ב as referring to the apple orchards where בנות ישראל gave birth in secret during שעבוד מצרים. The חרוסת with apples commemorates this נס. The שיעור's first opinion in the סנהדרין debate. ↩
  13. מלאכת שלמה, ר' שלמה עדני (Yemen/Hebron, 1567–1626), on משנה פסחים פ"י. — ר' שלמה עדני (Adani), born in Yemen, lived most of his life in חברון. מלאכת שלמה is one of the principal commentaries on the משניות, printed at the back of standard משניות editions. He examined ancient manuscripts of רש"י and רשב"ם on פסחים and concluded that the original text in both is חרוסת, not חרס — confirming the שיעור's reading. ↩
  14. צמח דוד, ר' דוד גנז (Prague, 1541–1613). — ר' דוד גנז, a תלמיד of the רמ"א and the מהרש"ל, was a major chronicler of Jewish and world history. צמח דוד (Prague 1592) is divided into two parts: Jewish chronology and gentile chronology. He was also an astronomer and mathematician who collaborated with Tycho Brahe and Kepler. The מן-from-the-earth episode in Prague during the late-1500s famine is documented in his account of his own time. ↩
  15. משנה ביצה פ"א מ"א; ביצה ב' ע"א – ז' ע"ב. — The opening משנה of ביצה: an egg laid on יום טוב may not be eaten on that יום טוב — בית הלל rule it is מוקצה from נולד. When יום טוב falls immediately after שבת, an egg laid on שבת may not be eaten on יום טוב either. The דין structures the שיעור's case-question of the ירושלים woman. ↩
  16. ביצה ג' ע"ב – ד' ע"א — דבר שיש לו מתירין. — The classical סוגיא of דבר שיש לו מתירין אפילו באלף לא בטל: an item currently forbidden but that will become permitted later cannot be nullified by majority. The principle: why be lenient now through ביטול when waiting will allow consumption with full היתר? The דין is the central halachic problem of the שיעור's case. ↩
  17. שו"ת שואל ציון on the ירושלים egg case.
    Note: Verify exact סימן in the printed שו"ת שואל ציון; the תשובה on the שאלה of the ירושלים woman whose son added a שבת-laid egg to her bowl of fifteen circulates in pre-פסח halachic literature.
    ↩
  18. נדה י"ז ע"א; פסחים קי"ב ע"א — peeled egg/onion left overnight. — (Note: the שיעור referenced נדה דף ל"ז; the principal source is נדה י"ז ע"א.) חז"ל list five things forbidden because of רוח רעה: peeled garlic, peeled onion, peeled egg, mixed wine, and meat unaccompanied by a chaperone overnight. The דין is codified in the פוסקים; modern פוסקים debate whether refrigeration neutralizes the רוח רעה. The שיעור's argument depends on the principle that the egg, peeled, will become forbidden by morning — so דבר שיש לו מתירין does not apply. ↩
  19. מרדכי on ביצה; cf. שלחן ערוך, יו"ד ק"ב. — The מרדכי (ר' מרדכי בן הלל, 13th century, Germany) records the principle that a דבר שיש לו מתירין that will be forbidden for an independent reason by the time it becomes permitted is no longer classified as יש לו מתירין; therefore it is subject to standard ביטול rules. Codified in שלחן ערוך יו"ד ק"ב. ↩
  20. שמות י"ב:מ"ב — "ליל שמורים הוא לה'"; פסחים ק"ט ע"ב. — ליל פסח is a ליל שימורים — a night of guarding/protection. חז"ל in פסחים ק"ט ע"ב develop the principle that מזיקין have no power on ליל הסדר; the door is left open without fear. The שיעור's resolution: מזיקין (external demons) are absent on ליל פסח, but רוח רעה (the spiritual residue that contaminates peeled food overnight) is a different category and remains operative — preserving the תשובה of the שואל ציון. ↩
