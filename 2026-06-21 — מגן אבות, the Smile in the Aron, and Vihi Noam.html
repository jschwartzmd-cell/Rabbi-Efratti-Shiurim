מגן אבות, the Smile in the Aron, and וִיהִי נֹעַם

Doubling down on the נשמות in shul on Friday night — through the Chida's chain to the Rosh's family מעשה, and onward to the gates of גהינום at מוצאי שבת
Recorded 21 June 2026 · ה׳ תמוז תשפ״ו · Reworked from the original שיעור
Dedication Today's שיעור and breakfast are sponsored by Reb Chaim Yishayel לעילוי נשמת his father-in-law Shlachter, אברהם מנחם בן אליהו מאיר. May his נשמה have an עלייה in גן עדן, ועל ידן הישועה — אמן.

Last week's שיעור opened the long sugya of מגן אבות — ברכה מעין שבע — the quasi-חזרת הש״ץ of Friday night that the גמרא says is technically unnecessary but instituted משום סכנה. Rabbi Ephrati closed last week with the מחזור ויטרי's chiddush in the name of Rashi — that the מזיקין are the נשמות of the deceased who come to shul Friday night when גהינום does not burn. This week — in response to "at least twenty different people" from the Spotify listeners and the kehillah pushing back on the chiddush — Rabbi Ephrati "doubled down," bringing a second source from the ראשונים: the חיד״א's שם הגדולים, the chain through ר' חיים ויטאל, and an איגרת from רבינו יהודה בן הרא״ש preserving a family מעשה: the bris of רבינו יחיאל with שמעון הכהן, the candle that went out on Yom Kippur, the smile in the aron at the levaya in Worms, and the Friday night after Sukkos when רבינו יחיאל's נשמה appeared מלובש בגוף in the shul. From there the שיעור opened the halachic frame — the ירושלמי's reading of מגן אבות as a quasi-קידוש-coverage device, the שולחן ערוך's prohibition on speaking during this exact seam (Mishna Berura's rare maaseh from ספר חסידים — the deceased חסיד with the green face), the Rivash's question about Yom Tov on Friday, and מזמור לדוד as שמירה — then crossed to מוצאי שבת and the gates of גהינום reopening at וִיהִי נֹעַם, with Reb Shmelke of Nikolsburg, the Pshelelever Rebbe, and the Chofetz Chaim's chiddush that your personal Shabbos in shamayim starts when you were מקבל שבת.

I. Where We Left Off — The Two Rationales for the מזיקין

We pick up directly from last week. The גמרא in שבת כ״ד ע״ב[1] teaches that מגן אבות — the ברכה מעין שבע recited after ויכולו — is מעיקר הדין unnecessary. It was instituted משום סכנה. רש״י[2] in his commentary specifies: סכנה מפני המזיקין.

But which מזיקין, and exactly where is the סכנה? The שיעור drew last week's machloikes into sharp practical relief.

A) The פשט — Rashi's Latecomer Walking Home Alone

On the פשט reading, the danger is the walk home. A latecomer Friday night arrives after the kehillah has begun davening; he is still davening שמונה עשרה when everyone else finishes; the kehillah disperses; he is left alone in the shul to finish, and then has to walk home in the dark. The שיעור's framing was striking: "the entire tzibbur has to add something to the davening so that this latecomer shouldn't walk home alone."

Rabbi Ephrati lingered on the מוסר. We always make fun of latecomers. The latecomer over here — we cater to him; the entire tzibbur caters to him. One of the ראשונים — the ריב״ש, in his teshuvos — writes לעולם: every shul has at least one guy who's going to come late. For that one Yid we add a whole תפילה. The reach for inclusion is the engine.

B) The מחזור ויטרי — Neshamos in the Shul

Last week's central chiddush, from the מחזור ויטרי[3] in the name of his רבי Rashi: the מזיקין are the נשמות of the deceased who descend to the shul on Friday night, because גהינום does not burn on שבת. The danger is not the walk home; it is being left alone in the shul among the נשמות. The פסחים Gemara on אגרת בת מחלת — Abaye nullified her — does not address this case at all; the מחזור ויטרי's נשמות in shul are an ongoing reality that was never nullified.

The structural difference is sharp. On Rashi's פשט, you protect the latecomer by walking him home; the shul is fine. On the מחזור ויטרי, the shul is the dangerous place; getting people out together is the solution. Both rationales converge on the same practice — extend the davening with מגן אבות so no one is left alone — but the conceptual map is different.

C) The Strapkover Rebbe's Reassurance

Rabbi Ephrati flagged a complementary piece from the Strapkover mesorah. In the book Reb Tuvia had lent him, a מעשה is recorded of one of the Strapkover einikelach — a young woman afraid to walk by a shul on Friday night. The Strapkover Rebbe reassured her: "Don't worry. Only the נשמות of צדיקים come down; they will not be מזיק you." The reassurance does not deny the מחזור ויטרי's chiddush; it qualifies it. The נשמות in the shul are real; the danger is from those whose דין has not yet settled.

II. Doubling Down — A Second Source from the ראשונים

This week opened with a candid acknowledgment. "I got at least twenty comments from twenty different people this week — you really believe what you said last week? Really, the נשמות coming down to shul every Friday night?" Rabbi Ephrati's response was direct: "I'm doubling down. I don't care if you don't believe it. I'm doubling down because I found another source from the ראשונים."

The source is a chain that runs through three different generations of the post-Rishonim Sefardic mesorah, anchored in the חיד״א's שם הגדולים[4]. To understand the weight of the chain, the שיעור opened with the Chida himself.

III. The חיד״א — Biography, מעגל טוב, and the Quarantine

החיד״א — ר' חיים יוסף דוד אזולאי (1724–1806) — was one of the great צדיקים and prolific authors of the 18th century. The שיעור drew his biography out at length because the credibility of the chain depends on understanding who is testifying.

A) שד״ר

The Chida lived in ארץ ישראל and served as a שד״ר — שלוחא דרבנן — the שליח sent by the impoverished kehillot of ארץ ישראל (Tzfas, Tveria, Yerushalayim) to collect tzedakah from European communities for the support of the Yishuv. The שיעור's framing: "You think shnorrers from ארץ ישראל started coming to collect by us American Jews this generation? They've been coming to Europe for generations. The Yidden in ארץ ישראל never had פרנסה — there was one שליח each kehillah sent, and they went for months collecting money. That's how we've been supporting Yidden in ארץ ישראל since time immemorial — and we should continue doing so."

B) מעגל טוב — The Chida's Travel Journal

The Chida wrote over eighty ספרים. He also wrote a unique travel journal — מעגל טוב[5] (literally "good circle," chronicling his circling of the globe on his שליחות trips). In מעגל טוב he records meeting nearly every גדול הדור of his era. The שיעור lingered on one meeting in particular: the Chida sat with the פני יהושע and described him as דומה למלאך ה' צבאות. (Last week's שיעור had described the Chida visiting the פני יהושע in connection with the rebbetzin who argued with her husband at the Shabbos table; the same set of visits is recorded in מעגל טוב.)

C) The 30-Day Quarantine — The Origin of שם הגדולים

And it was on one of his travels — returning from ארץ ישראל to Italy, the boat docking at an Italian port (likely Naples) — that the Chida wrote his most famous reference work. The שיעור set the scene precisely. The boat docked. There was a pandemic in Europe — a plague. (Rabbi Ephrati flagged the contemporary echo: "Right now, news from this morning, Ebola virus is knocking in Africa, השם ירחם. A guy just landed in ארץ ישראל from Congo and he has Ebola. רבותי, it's a scary thing, not a joke.") The authorities did not let the Chida off the boat. Quarantine — thirty days alone. Remember, the שיעור observed wryly, COVID to the tenth power. The Chida had no ספרים with him; someone brought him kosher food; otherwise, גארנישט.

What did he do? He wrote a sefer. From memory. And not just any sefer — he wrote שם הגדולים, the most comprehensive biographical-bibliographical anthology of the great Jewish scholars from the תנאים and אמוראים through the ראשונים and אחרונים of his own day. Each entry: a brief account of who the gadol was, when he lived, where he served as Rav, which ספרים he wrote. The work has served every rabbi for two centuries who has wanted to find out about a specific personality in the Jewish historical record. And it was written, from memory, in thirty days of quarantine.

Why This Biographical Detail Matters The שיעור opened the Chida's biography not as digression but as scaffolding. The chain of testimony that follows — "the Chida saw a כתב יד of Rav Chaim Vital, who saw an איגרת from Rabbeinu Yehuda ben Harosh" — depends entirely on the credibility of its first link. The Chida wrote שם הגדולים from memory in quarantine. He met every gadol of his era and recorded those meetings in מעגל טוב. His memory and his reliability as a transmitter of biographical-historical mesorah are the foundation on which everything that follows rests. "It's not היגי בייגי. The Chida says he saw it — we trust the Chida."

IV. The Chain — Chida → Rav Chaim Vital → Rabbeinu Yehuda ben Harosh

In שם הגדולים, the Chida records what he saw with his own eyes. He saw a כתב יד — a handwritten manuscript — of ר' חיים ויטאל[6]. Rav Chaim Vital, of course, was the primary תלמיד of the האר״י ז״ל, the principal redactor of Lurianic kabbalah, living roughly 100–200 years before the Chida (1542–1620). In that manuscript, Rav Chaim Vital writes that he himself saw a כתב יד — an איגרת, a letter — from רבינו יהודה בן הרא״ש.

We are now standing inside the royal family of the Rosh. The שיעור took a moment to anchor the family tree.

V. The רא״ש — The Bridge Between אשכנז and ספרד

רבינו אשר בן יחיאל (the Rosh, c. 1250–1327) was one of the great ראשונים. He grew up in אשכנז, the תלמיד of מהר״ם מרוטנבורג; when the Maharam was imprisoned by the German imperial authorities and died in prison, the Rosh moved his entire family from אשכנז to ספרד — specifically to טולדו, where with the הסכמה of the רשב״א he became chief rabbi.

This is what makes the Rosh unique among the ראשונים. Every other ראשון was either purely אשכנזי or purely ספרדי in his mesorah. The Rosh took the best of both worlds — grew up in אשכנז, transplanted to ספרד, transmitted both traditions. The Maharshal famously observes that the Rosh has access to more layers of mesorah than any other ראשון for exactly this reason. "That's why we're so into the Rosh."

The Rosh had several sons. The oldest was רבינו יעקב, the בעל הטורים, whose ארבעה טורים would become the structural scaffolding for the שולחן ערוך. The youngest was רבינו יהודה, who took over his father's position in Toledo after the Rosh was נפטר. (A small biographical detail the שיעור preserved: Rabbeinu Yehuda ended up marrying his oldest brother's daughter — his own niece, who was his same age. He was a son-in-law of his brother.) Rabbeinu Yehuda is the author of an איגרת that preserves a family מעשה from his grandfather's generation.

VI. The Igeres — Moving from אשכנז at Age 13

Rabbeinu Yehuda writes in his איגרת: "I moved from אשכנז to ספרד when I was a young boy. My father (the Rosh) was about 45 or 50; I was 13. It was a traumatic experience — אשכנז is one type of Yiddishkeit, ספרד is a different type of Yiddishkeit." He goes on: "I don't remember much as a child from אשכנז because I was too young; I rely on what my father told me."

And what his father — the Rosh — told him about his own father, רבינו יחיאל, is the מעשה that anchors the entire chain. The Rosh was the son of Rabbeinu Yechiel. Rabbeinu Yechiel was an early Ashkenazi ראשון (born approximately 1170–1180). (The שיעור flagged carefully — this is not רבינו יחיאל מפריז of the בעלי התוספות; a different Rabbeinu Yechiel from a different part of אשכנז.)

VII. The Bris — Rabbeinu Yechiel and Shimon HaKohen

When Rabbeinu Yechiel was a fifteen-year-old בחור in אשכנז, he had a best friend — שמעון הכהן — also a fifteen-year-old בחור, also a serious תלמיד חכם and tzaddik. The two young men made a ברית, a covenant: to share the זכויות of all their תורה, תפילה, and מצוות. Whatever Rabbeinu Yechiel learned, davened, and did, Shimon HaKohen would receive a share of the זכות. Whatever Shimon HaKohen learned, davened, and did, Rabbeinu Yechiel would receive a share.

This is a real מושג in Yiddishkeit — like יששכר וזבולון but a shtickel different. The שיעור flagged comparable examples across the historical record: the Chida and his close friends; the Ramchal and his חבורה. The bris is a metaphysically operative arrangement — "a חשבון in שמים" — recognized by the system. The boys learned together בחברותא for years.

VIII. The Yom Kippur Candle Minhag

And it was on one Yom Kippur, years later in their lives, that the מעשה begins. The שיעור took a moment to explain a Yom Kippur minhag now mostly discontinued.

A) The Original Practice

In the old days in אשכנז and across Eastern Europe, every man brought his own personal wax candle to shul for Yom Kippur — separate from the יארצייט candle, separate from the wife's home candles, separate from the נר ששבת for הבדלה. The candle was lit on ערב Yom Kippur and stayed lit through the entire fast. The conceptual frame: נר ה' נשמת אדם (משלי כ׳:כ״ז) — the candle is the soul. If your candle went out during Yom Kippur, it was taken as an extremely bad סימן — that the person would die that year.

B) The Halachic Response

The practice has been largely discontinued in our generations. The קיצור שולחן ערוך records that the proliferation of large wax candles in the shuls became an actual סכנה — fire hazard, heat, density. The רמ״א[7] codified a related concern: even where the practice continued, one should give the candle to the gabbai upon arrival rather than place it himself — so that he would not know which candle was his and watch it obsessively throughout the day. The kavod-of-tefillah problem was that "they weren't being מכוון; they were looking at the candle" the entire davening.

IX. Rabbeinu Yechiel's Candle Goes Out

On one Yom Kippur night, later in his life, Rabbeinu Yechiel was learning in shul together with his chavrusa Shimon HaKohen. Rabbeinu Yechiel's candle went out. Rabbeinu Yechiel shivered: "אוי, וואס וועט זיין, וואס וועט זיין". He took it as a very bad סימן.

And indeed: that חול המועד סוכות, Rabbeinu Yechiel was נפטר. The סימן was correct. He was a big rav in his community in אשכנז and there was a large levaya.

X. The Smile in the Aron — The Levaya in וורמייזא

The minhag at the time — Rabbeinu Yehuda ben Harosh records this in his איגרת — was that when the aron was brought from the levaya to the בית הקברות (in this case in וורמייזא / Worms), they would open the aron one last time before burial, on a large rock at the cemetery, to verify that none of the meis's limbs had shifted during the carrying. The רישונים had this practice; we do not preserve it (the chevra kadisha today does the inspection privately, not in the family's presence).

(Rabbi Ephrati flagged a quiet personal aside here — when the שיעור had visited Worms together a year earlier, there had been a large rock at the בית הקברות that he wondered might be the same rock. "I have to ask Yehuda Geber." A small detail of the kehillah's own pilgrimage to the same site the מעשה records.)

At Rabbeinu Yechiel's levaya, when the aron was opened on the rock, his chavrusa שמעון הכהן stepped forward. He addressed his departed friend: "Reb Shloime — you remember our agreement? You left the world early. Our agreement is still on."

Everyone was watching. And — recorded by Rabbeinu Yehuda ben Harosh, transmitted to the Chida through Rav Chaim Vital — Rabbeinu Yechiel smiled in the aron. The deal was confirmed from the other side.

A Source-Critical Frame The שיעור was alert to the strangeness of the maaseh and addressed it head-on. "The olam doesn't believe what I'm saying. I can give you the source. I can email the source if you want, so you see I'm not making stuff up." The point: this is not a חסידישע מעשה. It is a maaseh from the Rishonim, recorded in the Igeres of Rabbeinu Yehuda ben Harosh, copied by Rav Chaim Vital, and brought by the Chida in שם הגדולים. The structural credibility of each link in the chain is what makes the conclusion that follows weighty rather than dismissible.

XI. The Friday Night After Sukkos — רבינו יחיאל Returns

The following Friday night — after Sukkos — Shimon HaKohen went to shul after the seudah. He did not know what was about to happen. He walked into the shul and רבינו יחיאל was there. Rabbeinu Yechiel had been buried a week earlier. His נשמה was now in the shul, מלובש בגוף — clothed in a physical body sufficient to be seen and recognized.

Shimon HaKohen asked: "What's going on? Why are you here?" Rabbeinu Yechiel's answer — and the שיעור underlined this as the operative halachic-conceptual data point: "I have רשות from שמים. They gave me רשות to come down on Friday night. There is no בית דין of שמים in session on Shabbos — everything is closed. The נשמות come down on Friday night to have מנוחה, and they hang out in the shuls."

And then specifically: "I know what you said by my levaya. You opened up the aron and you said our deal is still on. The deal is still on. You'll get my schar from בית דין של מעלה; I'll get your schar from this world below." The two carried on an entire conversation. The covenant they made at fifteen years old held across the boundary.

"מעשה שהיה from יהודה בן הרא״ש." If the מחזור ויטרי alone is not sufficient — Rabbi Ephrati pressed — this is the second source. Two independent ראשונים, from two different families and two different countries, testifying to the same metaphysical reality: נשמות come down to shul Friday night.

XII. רבינו הקדוש — A Third Source

And there is yet another. Reb Chaim Yishay had texted Rabbi Ephrati earlier in the week reminding him: there is a גמרא בכתובות ק״ג[8] that records that רבינו הקדוש — רבי יהודה הנשיא, the author of the משניות — returned every Friday night to his home after his petira to make קידוש for his wife and children. The תוספות[9] on the sugya discusses the framework.

The שיעור framed this as the strongest possible anchor: "If you don't believe that, walk out — I don't want you to come to my שיעורים anymore." A Gemara is a Gemara. The תוספות discusses the mechanism. Rabbeinu HaKadosh's weekly visitation is not חסידישע מעשה; it is the page of the Gemara.

XIII. שלושה עולמות — The Yerushalmi Shovavim and the שטענדערס

Rabbi Ephrati then brought a further maaseh from a much later but still serious historical source. A friend of his learning in Boro Park had asked what the שיעור had covered the previous week; on hearing the topic, the friend had pointed Rabbi Ephrati to a book he had at home — שלושה עולמות[10] by ר' חיים המבורגר (Chaim Hamburger), a member of the well-known Hamburger family of ירושלים (Old Yishuv lineage). Born approximately 150 years ago in ירושלים from the first or second עלייה of תלמידי הגר״א, Chaim Hamburger wrote at age 80 (approximately 1910–1915) a memoir of life in ירושלים in his youth.

He records a memory from his childhood. As Yerushalmi kids — shovavim, troublemakers (the שיעור flagged with affection: "You ever look at the Yerushalmi kids from מאה שערים? They have a twinkle in their eye. They don't have Netflix; they're busy making trouble") — he and his friends had a regular game with the older men who came to shul on Friday night after the seudah to learn. The men would inevitably fall asleep over the Gemara ("the best time to fall asleep is Friday night after the seudah, you open up a Gemara"). The kids would use tabak pishkelach (snuffboxes) — put them under the sleeping men's noses so the man would sneeze himself awake.

One man was a particularly heavy sleeper. He woke up from the sneeze but fell right back asleep. The kids decided to teach him a lesson for once and for all. They took all the שטענדערס (lecterns) of the shul. They arranged them in the front of the shul by the ארון קודש. Over each shtender they draped a טלית. By the עמוד they set up a doubled shtender with a larger טלית. From behind, it looked like a kehillah of men standing wrapped in talleisim, davening.

The kids went outside, closed the door, gave a loud קלאפ on the door to wake the sleeper, and waited. He woke up. He looked toward the front of the shul. He saw the whole front filled with the נשמות of the deceased wrapped in talleisim. Because every Yid in ירושלים of that era knew that נשמות came down to shul on Friday night. He freaked out and ran outside.

The kids — laughing their heads off — played along. "What happened? What happened?" The man told them, shuddering, what he had seen. They told him with mock seriousness that the only way to remove the spiritual contamination of having seen נשמות was to go to the מקוה in the חורבה shul (Rav Yehuda HeChassid's shul in the Old City, which had a mikveh in the basement) and to be טובל seven times. They walked him there at midnight, sneering behind his back. He toveled. They told him: "You can never come back to shul on Friday night again — because if this happens again, even going to the mikveh won't help." They never saw the man in shul Friday night again.

Why Rabbi Ephrati Brought This מעשה The point of the שלושה עולמות מעשה is not to prove the existence of the נשמות. The point is to establish — as historical record — that the belief that נשמות come to shul on Friday night was the lived consciousness of every Yid in ירושלים in the late 19th century. The mischievous kids' prank worked precisely because the underlying belief was universal. The mesorah from the מחזור ויטרי and Rabbeinu Yehuda ben Harosh was not preserved as obscure ראשונים-text; it was the operating system of the Old Yishuv's spiritual life. "Even in ירושלים they believed that נשמות of the deceased come to the shul Friday night."
A Practical Halacha for the שליח ציבור Rabbi Ephrati emphasized a structural-practical point that often gets botched in shuls. The proper sequence: the שליח ציבור begins ברוך אתה ה' אלוקינו ואלוקי אבותינו... and continues through to קוֹנֶה שָׁמַיִם וָאָרֶץ — no one answers אמן at this point, because this is not the חתימה of the ברכה; מגן אבות is the המשך. The tzibbur then collectively recites "מגן אבות בדברו..." slowly with a niggun. The שליח ציבור then repeats מגן אבות aloud, then continues with אלוקינו ואלוקי אבותינו רצה נא במנוחתנו, and concludes ברוך אתה ה' מקדש השבת. The common mistake: the שליח ציבור says the congregational text בלחש together with the tzibbur and fails to repeat it aloud. The structural design is to extend the davening — roughly four to five minutes from start to finish — so the latecomer can catch up. "Next time you get a kibbud to be שליח ציבור Friday night, don't say מגן אבות together with the tzibbur or בלחש. The halacha is, very important — the tzibbur says together מגן אבות slow with a niggun and then the שליח ציבור repeats מגן אבות. The whole point is to stretch out the davening a little longer for the latecomers."

XIV. The ירושלמי's Reason — מגן אבות as Quasi-קידוש Coverage

Having dispatched the doubting twenty commenters, Rabbi Ephrati pivoted from the סוד rationale to the פשט rationale. The ירושלמי ברכות פ״ד ה״א[11] provides a completely different reading.

Standard structural baseline: מעריב has no חזרת הש״ץ during the week. Why? Because מעריב is רשות — historically optional, and even after כלל ישראל accepted it as חובה, the absence of חזרת הש״ץ remains as a structural residue of the older status. So why on Friday night does suddenly מעריב acquire a quasi-חזרת הש״ץ in the form of מגן אבות?

The ירושלמי's answer is not about סכנה at all. It is about קידוש. The structural function of חזרת הש״ץ is to be מוציא the people in the kehillah who cannot daven themselves — those who do not know how to read or how to daven properly. In Rabbi Ephrati's framing: "Today everybody knows how to daven, but in those days not everybody knew how to daven. So the chazaras hashatz — you listen, you say אמן, you're יוצא."

At מעריב on Friday night, ויכולו inside the עמידה constitutes קידוש היום — the formal halachic קידוש obligation. Even those who cannot say their own קידוש at home need to be מוציא through the public structure. So חז״ל instituted מגן אבות as a condensed echo of the שבע ברכות of the Shabbos עמידה, with the שליח ציבור reciting it and concluding מקדש השבת — sufficient to be מוציא someone who cannot make קידוש himself.

Rabbi Ephrati emphasized: "If you don't want to believe in anything else, this reason suffices to say מגן אבות." The two rationales — סכנה of being alone with the נשמות, and the kiddush-coverage function — converge on the same practice without contradicting each other.

XV. לפניו נעבוד ביראה ופחד — A Kehillah Chiddush

John in the kehillah asked a sharp structural question. The מגן אבות text contains the line לפניו נעבוד ביראה ופחד — "before Him we serve in fear and trembling." But Shabbos is the day of שמחה (or עונג, per last week's discussion) — not יראה ופחד. Why does the מגן אבות text speak in language that does not align with the mode of Friday night?

Rabbi Ephrati conceded he could not place a precise source for the answer but accepted the questioner's own suggested chiddush: the language of יראה ופחד is the language appropriate to giving עדות. We are bearing testimony at this moment to the creation of the world; testimony is a structural act that requires gravity. "You're saying testimony about הקדוש ברוך הוא — that's the connection. I want to give you credit. Take the credit. You're making a very good connection."

XVI. The Prohibition of Speaking — שולחן ערוך and the ספר חסידים מעשה

The same eidus framing surfaces in another structural halacha. The שולחן ערוך אורח חיים סימן רס״ח סעיף ב'[12]: אין לדבר בשעה שהש״ץ אומר ויכולו ולא בשעה שאומר השליח ציבור ברכה מעין שבע. The prohibition on speaking extends from the end of one's silent שמונה עשרה, through ויכולו, through מגן אבות, until after the first קדיש.

This is a stricter standard than the surrounding halachos. During the week, between שחרית/מנחה lachash and חזרת הש״ץ, the שיעור observed, the halacha is more lenient — one can technically leave shul and speak. Friday night between לחש and ויכולו through קדיש, שטיל בגאנצן — completely silent.

The משנה ברורה[13] on this halacha does something exceptionally rare: he tells a story. The שיעור took a moment to flag how rare this is. "In my life I have found only three stories in the משנה ברורה. The Beis Yosef in the entire שולחן ערוך only tells one story." (Rabbi Ephrati noted that he had once put this as a trivia question to the kehillah and several people had called him on motzaei Shabbos searching for the answer — it is in קמ״א in הלכות קריאת התורה. Even the משנה ברורה quotes that story only because it is in the שולחן ערוך already.) The fact that the משנה ברורה brings a story here is itself a marker of how serious the issue is.

The story he brings is from ספר חסידים[14]. A pious man dreamed that his recently-deceased close friend — a known חסיד and tzaddik — appeared to him with a pale-green face (ופניו מורקות). The dreamer challenged him: "You're a tzaddik in גן עדן — you should look luminous, with the light of the divine on your face. Why is your face green?" The deceased friend answered: "I was a tzaddik. I kept everything, קלה כבחמורה. One thing only did I do wrong: מפני שהייתי מדבר בויכולו בשעה שהיה הציבור אומר מגן אבות — I spoke during ויכולו while the kehillah was saying מגן אבות."

The ספר חסידים draws no other conclusion. "It doesn't say this about other talking," Rabbi Ephrati observed. "I know you're not allowed to talk during leining, you're not allowed to talk during davening. But this time is unique." The connective tissue back to John's chiddush is intact: the moment is the moment of giving עדות; the dignity required for עדות is the conceptual ground for the absolute silence; failure to honor that dignity carries weight even for an otherwise complete tzaddik. Rabbi Ephrati landed it plainly: "It should not be our worst aveirah — be careful."

XVII. Yom Tov on ערב שבת — The Rivash's Question

The שיעור pivoted to one structural edge case. The ריב״ש[15] — Rabbeinu Yitzchak bar Sheshet — raised in his teshuvos a sharp question. If the entire reason for מגן אבות is to accommodate the latecomer Friday night, what about when Yom Tov falls on Friday? On יום טוב, everyone is already yom-tov-dig by the afternoon. They've eaten the seudah; they're dressed; they're ready. There is no last-minute mikveh rush, no last-minute work to finish. Who would come late? Should the תקנה of מגן אבות apply?

The Rivash's answer was, as Rabbi Ephrati put it, "a moiredike zach" that he himself could not fully understand. The Rivash writes that even in this case, people still come late — שהיו אוכלין סעודת יום טוב, "because they were eating the יום טוב seudah." Rabbi Ephrati struggled with this: "You ever heard of someone eating סעודת יום טוב at seven PM on Yom Tov?" The kehillah pushed back with suggestions — was it נעילת החג? But "tell me one shul in the world that makes נעילת החג on a Shabbos that falls after Yom Tov." The שיעור landed on a tentative answer: Simchas Torah. When Simchas Torah falls on Friday, the kehillah leaves shul at 3 or 4 PM; by the time they finish the seudah, it is genuinely late. That is one historically plausible reading of what the Rivash had in mind.

הלכה למעשה: yes, say מגן אבות even on a Friday night that falls after Yom Tov. That is the codified halacha.

XVIII. The יחיד Davening Alone

And the structural complement: a יחיד davening alone does not say מגן אבות, because the entire תקנה was for the latecomer in the tzibbur. The רמ״א[16] notes that if the יחיד wishes, he may recite the body of מגן אבות without the opening and closing ברכות — to avoid a ברכה לבטלה. The text without the formal ברכה structure is permitted; the full ברכה structure for a יחיד is not.

XIX. מזמור לדוד — Nusach Sefard's Shemira

Rabbi Ephrati had nearly closed Friday night when he caught himself: "I missed something. Why do we say מזמור לדוד Friday night?"

The structural fact: Nusach Sefard recites מזמור לדוד (תהלים כ״ג)[17] on Friday night; Nusach Ashkenaz does not. The published סידורים's explanation is על פי קבלה, על פי סוד — not a פשט-level reason.

Sammy in the kehillah suggested a chiddush of his own: the mizmor functions as שמירה for the very situation the original מגן אבות תקנה addresses — the Yid walking home alone on Friday night, with the pasuk גַם כִּי אֵלֵךְ בְּגֵיא צַלְמָוֶת לֹא אִירָא רָע כִּי אַתָּה עִמָּדִי. Rabbi Ephrati's response was direct: "גוואלד, Sammy, maybe. I haven't seen that reason — and guess what, I don't have a reason to tell you. But I'm going to write it down in your name. I like that a lot." The kehillah's organic chiddush became part of the שיעור's record.

XX. Transition to מוצאי שבת — וִיהִי נֹעַם and the Gates of גהינום

The שיעור crossed at this point from Friday night to מוצאי שבת. The arc that follows runs through one connecting principle: גהינום does not burn on Shabbos. What happens at the seams — when Shabbos arrives, when Shabbos departs — is the structural ground for several pieces of liturgy and several pieces of mesorah.

A) The Pasuk and the זוהר

The structural ground is שמות פרק ל״ה פסוק ג'[18] — לֹא תְבַעֲרוּ אֵשׁ בְּכֹל מֹשְׁבֹתֵיכֶם בְּיוֹם הַשַּׁבָּת. The זהר הקדוש reads בְּכָל מֹשְׁבֹתֵיכֶם structurally: in all your dwellings — including the spiritual ones. The fires of גהינום are subject to the same שבת-prohibition on burning. On Shabbos, גהינום does not burn.

B) The סנהדרין Gemara on Turnusrufus

The גמרא בסנהדרין ס״ה ע״ב[19] dramatizes this in a Roman-context dispute. Turnusrufus HaRasha — the Roman governor — challenged Rabbi Akiva: how do you Yidden even know which day is Shabbos? How can you prove anything special about it? Rabbi Akiva responded with several proofs. One was the שמבטיון river (mentioned in previous שיעורים) — the river that stops flowing on Shabbos. Another was structurally sharper. Rabbi Akiva told Turnusrufus: "Go to your father's kever. Your father was a גוי בן גוי. Watch his kever the entire week — smoke rises out. Watch it on Shabbos — no smoke. Even non-Jewish neshamos in גהינום are off on Shabbos. The cessation is universal."

Turnusrufus tested it. The smoke was as Rabbi Akiva said. Even goyim are off גהינום on Shabbos — "there is no גהינום for them on Shabbos. They could have done whatever they did in this world, but the Gemara is explicit."

C) The Zohar's Machlokes — מחלל שבת בפרהסיא

The זוהר הקדוש[20] presses the sharp counter-question. If even goyim get the reprieve on Shabbos, what about a Yid who was מחלל שבת בפרהסיא during his lifetime? Does he get the reprieve?

The Zohar records a מחלוקת between רבי יהודה and רבי יוסי (or, in some readings, two other Tannaim). One position: a mechalel Shabbos b'farhesya does not get the reprieve — לא ינוחו ולא ישקטו. For him, גהינום burns on Shabbos too. He was מחלל שבת down here; שבת is מחלל him up there. "It comes out that a Yid who was מחלל שבת בפרהסיא is worse than a goy."

XXI. The Tur's Medrash — When the Last Shul Finishes וִיהִי נֹעַם

And the structural complement: what happens at מוצאי שבת? The טור[21] brings a striking Medrash. Throughout שבת, הקדוש ברוך הוא has to hold back all the מזיקין, all the מחבלים, all the מקטרגים in גהינום so that they do not act. They are locked up.

At the moment of מוצאי שבת — specifically the moment after the last shul in the world finishes saying וִיהִי נֹעַם וְיוֹשֵׁב בְּסֵתֶר — הקדוש ברוך הוא opens the gates. The Medrash's image: like locking up a child who wants to get out, then opening the door — כתינוק הבורח מבית הספר. All גהינום breaks loose at once. The נשמות have to go back. The Zohar identifies the released forces with the Sama-mem and his army — kalvim, dogs. "We say 'who let the dogs out?' We let the dogs out when we daven מעריב מוצאי שבת."

And — sharply — every Yid's personal גהינום restart happens at the moment he himself finishes וִיהִי נֹעַם. The clock starts on him at the moment of his own davening.

XXII. The Pshelelever Rebbe — Late הבדלה to Save נשמות

Rabbi Ephrati brought a contemporary application. There is a Rebbe in Borough Park — the Pshelelever Rebbe (not the Amshenover Rebbe of Eretz Yisrael; the Pshelelever specifically). The שיעור flagged him: "a הייליגער יד, but eccentric." He makes הבדלה every Motzaei Shabbos at the absolute last possible moment — the latest זמן that one can still make הבדלה before עלות השחר.

Why? Because the Tur's Medrash describes the gates opening when the last shul in the world finishes וִיהִי נֹעַם. The Pshelelever Rebbe holds that delaying his own havdalah delays the structural restart of גהינום for the נשמות whose clock has not yet started. He extends the reprieve for as long as he halachically can. (The Ribnitzer Rebbe was famous for similar practices.) The שיעור framed this as the practical application of the יסוד ושורש העבודה[22]'s principle that the עיתוי of each individual Yid's מוצאי שבת affects the structural picture.

XXIII. The Rebbe Reb Shmelke of Nikolsburg — עליונים למטה ותחתונים למעלה

The שיעור brought a maaseh from one of the great early Chassidic Rebbes — the רבי שמואל שמעלקא הלוי הורוויץ of Nikolsburg[23]. The Miami kehillah had visited his kever in Nikolsburg approximately a year earlier, so the maaseh carries weight from personal connection.

One שלוש סעודות, Reb Shmelke was saying Torah at the table. He was in an unusually jolly mood — laughing to himself, smiling at something. The chassidim asked: "Rebbe, what happened?" The Rebbe answered in cryptic language: עליונים למטה ותחתונים למעלה. (The שיעור flagged: this phrase is from the Gemara about the Amoraim who went up to שמים — Rav Yosef, I think — and came back to describe the inversion of who is where.) People who were 'up' in this world are 'down' there. People who were 'down' here are 'up' there. עולם הפוך ראיתי.

Asked to explain, Reb Shmelke continued. There had been a Yid in Nikolsburg — just נפטר that week. Every שלוש סעודות, while Reb Shmelke was saying Torah, this man would come over and say: "Rebbe, נו, der zman, נו, let's go. I see three stars. Let's go!" He was always the one trying to hurry Shabbos out. Reb Shmelke would respond, week after week, "נו, נו, נו, soon, soon."

The man had died that week. At the first moment of his מוצאי שבת in שמים — at the moment his personal גהינום restarted — Reb Shmelke had seen him from below. The newly-dead man was now in גהינום, pointing down to where Reb Shmelke was still saying Torah at שלוש סעודות in Nikolsburg: "Look — Reb Shmelke is still in middle of שלוש סעודות! Wait! Wait!"

"עליונים למטה ותחתונים למעלה." The Yid who in this world had been rushing toward the three stars was now in the next world pointing down to the three stars and pleading for the rebbe to delay. The same gesture, the same words — pointing at the three stars and saying "let's go" — but the meaning had inverted. The שיעור landed the maaseh with delight: "a cute story for the Reb Shmelke."

XXIV. The Chofetz Chaim — Your Personal Shabbos Clock

The שיעור closed with a chiddush from the חפץ חיים[24] in his ליקוטי הלכות. The Chofetz Chaim writes — "a chiddush atzum, no one says it besides the Chofetz Chaim that I know of" — that your personal Shabbos clock in שמים begins at the moment you were מקבל שבת, not at the formal halachic זמן.

The application is sharp. If you start your Shabbos early — early מקבל שבת, early candle-lighting, early מעריב — your personal off-clock from גהינום begins earlier. If you push your הכנה for Shabbos into Friday morning (not Thursday night — the halacha does not recognize Thursday night הכנה; see שולחן ערוך אורח חיים סימן ר״מ ב'), the אורות של שבת already attach to your Friday. Your personal reprieve from גהינום extends backward.

The framing reaches back to the maaseh of Reb Shmelke from the opposite end. The man in Nikolsburg who hurried Shabbos out shortened his own reprieve. The Chofetz Chaim's chiddush is the structural converse: the Yid who extends Shabbos forward — into Friday morning, through early מקבל שבת, into the latest possible הבדלה — extends his own reprieve correspondingly. שבת, on the Chofetz Chaim's reading, is not only a fixed objective time. It is also a personal-spiritual time that responds to how the Yid receives it.

The arc of the שיעור in three movements. (i) Doubling down on the נשמות in shul Friday night — the מחזור ויטרי's chiddush from last week is reinforced by a second source through the חיד״א's שם הגדולים: the chain through Rav Chaim Vital back to the איגרת of רבינו יהודה בן הרא״ש, preserving the family maaseh of the bris between Rabbeinu Yechiel and Shimon HaKohen, the Yom Kippur candle that went out, the smile in the aron at the levaya in Worms, and the Friday night after Sukkos when Rabbeinu Yechiel's נשמה appeared מלובש בגוף in the shul. With the Gemara's report of רבינו הקדוש's weekly Friday-night kiddush and Chaim Hamburger's שלושה עולמות מעשה from Old Yishuv ירושלים completing the picture, the mesorah is not היגי בייגי. (ii) The halachic frame — the ירושלמי's reading of מגן אבות as a quasi-קידוש-coverage device, the Shulchan Aruch's prohibition on speaking from לחש through קדיש (John's eidus-chiddush, the Mishna Berura's rare maaseh from ספר חסידים — the green-faced חסיד who had been a complete tzaddik except for speaking during מגן אבות), the Rivash's question on Yom Tov-on-Friday and the יחיד's אפשרות to recite without ברכות, and Sammy's chiddush on מזמור לדוד as שמירה. (iii) Crossing to מוצאי שבת — גהינום does not burn on Shabbos (Sanhedrin's Turnusrufus, the Zohar's machloikes on מחלל שבת בפרהסיא), the gates reopen when the last shul finishes וִיהִי נֹעַם, the Pshelelever Rebbe and the Ribnitzer extending their havdala to save נשמות, Reb Shmelke of Nikolsburg's עליונים למטה ותחתונים למעלה, and the חפץ חיים's chiddush that your personal Shabbos clock begins when you were מקבל שבת.

מראה מקומות

  1. בבלי שבת כ״ד ע״ב — מגן אבות instituted משום סכנה. — The foundational Gemara: ברכה מעין שבע is מעיקר הדין unnecessary on Friday night מעריב; חז״ל instituted it for reasons related to סכנה. The structural anomaly that the institution addresses — that מעריב has no חזרת הש״ץ during the week, that the Friday-night quasi-חזרת הש״ץ is the only such structure in the מעריב landscape — is the launching point for the entire sugya. The שיעור treats the Gemara's משום סכנה as the necessary frame within which the multiple ראשונים readings (Rashi's לתחומ; the מחזור ויטרי's נשמות; the ירושלמי's quasi-קידוש) play out. ↩
  2. רש״י על שבת כ״ד ע״ב ד״ה משום סכנה. — Rashi's gloss on the Gemara: "מפני המזיקין". The interpretation of מזיקין opens onto the entire Rashi/מחזור ויטרי split treated last week and this week. On the פשט reading, מזיקין are operative agents (physical or spiritual) that endanger the latecomer walking home alone in the dark. On the מחזור ויטרי reading, מזיקין are specifically the נשמות of the deceased coming to shul. Rashi himself does not specify which reading; the מחזור ויטרי attributes the נשמות reading to Rashi orally. ↩
  3. מחזור ויטרי, סימן פ״ו — מזיקין as נשמות descending to shul on Friday night. — ר' שמחה מויטרי, primary תלמיד of Rashi, records in the מחזור ויטרי the reading that the Friday-night מזיקין are the נשמות of the deceased who descend to the shul because גהינום does not burn on Shabbos. The persistence of the מגן אבות תקנה after Abaye's nullification of אגרת בת מחלת is resolved on this reading by the ongoing-reality of the נשמות (which were never nullified). The structural reading was treated at length in last week's שיעור (2026-06-14). ↩
  4. חיד״א, שם הגדולים — the chain Chida → Rav Chaim Vital → איגרת of רבינו יהודה בן הרא״ש. — ר' חיים יוסף דוד אזולאי (the Chida, 1724–1806) records in שם הגדולים that he saw a כתב יד of ר' חיים ויטאל reporting that R' Chaim Vital had seen an איגרת of רבינו יהודה בן הרא״ש preserving the family מעשה of the bris between רבינו יחיאל and שמעון הכהן, the candle on Yom Kippur, the smile in the aron at the levaya in Worms, and the Friday-night appearance after Sukkos. The chain is one of the strongest single anchors in the post-Rishonim literature for the מחזור ויטרי-tradition that נשמות come to shul on Friday night. The שיעור treats the chain as historically credible because each link (Chida → Vital → Yehuda ben Harosh) is a documented serious scholar transmitting from a documented serious source. ↩
    The exact entry in שם הגדולים for this chain was not specified in the שיעור — the relevant entries are in the חלק הספרים under שם הגדולים השלם, possibly under the entry for ר' חיים ויטאל or for רבינו יחיאל. Verify the exact siman before citing.
  5. חיד״א, מעגל טוב — travel journal of the Chida's שליחות trips. — The Chida's travel diary recording his journeys across Europe and the Mediterranean as a שד״ר for ארץ ישראל kehillot. The journal contains his meetings with major גדולי הדור — among them the פני יהושע, whom the Chida described as דומה למלאך ה' צבאות. The 30-day quarantine in an Italian port (likely Naples or possibly Livorno) during which the Chida wrote שם הגדולים from memory is recorded in מעגל טוב. The biographical detail is essential to the credibility of the שם הגדולים chain: it establishes the Chida's memory and reliability as the foundation on which everything that follows rests. ↩
  6. ר' חיים ויטאל (1542–1620) — primary תלמיד of the האר״י ז״ל, principal redactor of Lurianic kabbalah. — ר' חיים ויטאל was the primary תלמיד of the האר״י (ר' יצחק לוריא, 1534–1572) and the principal redactor of Lurianic kabbalah after the האר״י's premature petira. His writings — עץ חיים, פרי עץ חיים, שער הכוונות, שערי קדושה, etc. — are the foundation of the Lurianic kabbalistic mesorah. The Chida's report of having seen a personal כתב יד from R' Chaim Vital reporting on the איגרת of Rabbeinu Yehuda ben Harosh inserts the chain through R' Chaim Vital's biographical-historical mesorah rather than his kabbalistic mesorah. The chain runs from a 14th-century Sefardic Rishon (Rabbeinu Yehuda) through a 16th-century Tzfas kabbalist (R' Chaim Vital) to an 18th-century Sefardic shadar (the Chida). ↩
  7. רמ״א אורח חיים תר״י סעיף ד' — the Yom Kippur candle minhag and the gabbai's role. — The Rema codifies the practical concern in the wax-candle Yom Kippur minhag: even where the practice continued, the person bringing the candle should give it to the gabbai upon arrival and not know which candle was his — both to avoid the kavanah-distraction of watching it during davening and to mitigate the psychological weight of the candle going out. The practice has been largely discontinued in our generations due to the fire safety concerns raised by the קיצור שולחן ערוך. The שיעור's reference is in connection with the maaseh of רבינו יחיאל's candle going out. ↩
  8. בבלי כתובות ק״ג ע״א — רבינו הקדוש returning home every Friday night to make קידוש. — The Gemara records that רבינו יהודה הנשיא (Rabbeinu HaKadosh, the author of the משניות) returned to his home every Friday night after his petira to make קידוש for his wife and children. The Gemara is the strongest possible anchor for the broader claim that נשמות of the deceased return to this world on Shabbos. Whereas the מחזור ויטרי's chiddush addresses a generic class of נשמות coming to shul, the Gemara's account of Rabbeinu HaKadosh addresses a specific named tzaddik returning to a specific home. The conceptual frame is the same; the documentation is impeccable. ↩
  9. תוספות על כתובות ק״ג — discussion of the mechanism of Rabbeinu HaKadosh's weekly return. — The Tosafos on the כתובות sugya discusses the structural-halachic framework of the return: how a נשמה without a body can perform a halachic action like קידוש, what the relationship is between this return and the broader category of post-petira appearances, and how the practice relates to the general rule that the dead do not interact with the living. The שיעור flagged the Tosafos as the appropriate resource for anyone who wants to press the structural mechanism harder. ↩
  10. ר' חיים המבורגר, שלושה עולמות — memoir of Old Yishuv ירושלים circa 1910–1915. — ר' חיים המבורגר, born in ירושלים approximately 1840 to a family of the first or second עלייה of תלמידי הגר״א, wrote at approximately age 80 a three-volume memoir of his youth in ירושלים — שלושה עולמות ("Three Worlds"). The memoir is a primary historical source for the lived consciousness of the Old Yishuv. The שיעור's maaseh of the Yerushalmi shovavim and the heavy sleeper in shul comes from this memoir; it serves the שיעור's structural point that the belief in נשמות coming to shul on Friday night was the universal consciousness of pre-state ירושלים, not exotic mysticism. The book is in print in Hebrew and is available in some major Judaica libraries. ↩
  11. ירושלמי ברכות פרק ד' הלכה א' — מעריב as רשות; the structural absence of חזרת הש״ץ. — The Yerushalmi's framework on מעריב's structural status as רשות — historically non-obligatory, later accepted by כלל ישראל as חובה — and the structural consequence that there is no חזרת הש״ץ at מעריב during the week. The Friday-night exception (מגן אבות as quasi-חזרת הש״ץ) on the Yerushalmi's reading is grounded in the Kiddush-coverage function: those who cannot say their own קידוש need to be מוציא through the public structure. The reading complements rather than contradicts the מחזור ויטרי's נשמות-reading. ↩
  12. שולחן ערוך אורח חיים סימן רס״ח סעיף ב' — prohibition on speaking during ויכולו and מגן אבות. — The Shulchan Aruch: אין לדבר בשעה שאומרים ויכולו ולא בשעה שאומר השליח ציבור ברכה מעין שבע. The prohibition extends from the end of the silent שמונה עשרה, through ויכולו, through מגן אבות, until after the first קדיש. The standard is stricter than the surrounding שולחן ערוך halachos on talking during davening; the שיעור treats this as a structural marker of the eidus quality of the moment. ↩
  13. משנה ברורה סימן רס״ח סעיף קטן ו' — the rare מעשה from ספר חסידים. — The Chofetz Chaim's Mishna Berura is one of the rare places in the entire commentary where the Chofetz Chaim brings an entire מעשה. The שיעור flagged this as one of only three stories in the entire Mishna Berura. The שיעור also noted that the Beis Yosef in the entire Shulchan Aruch brings only one story (in סימן קמ״א in הלכות קריאת התורה). The fact that the Mishna Berura brings the מעשה here is itself a structural marker of how seriously the issue should be taken. ↩
  14. ספר חסידים סימן תשע״ו — the deceased חסיד with the green face. — ר' יהודה החסיד of Regensburg (12th–13th century, ~800 years ago), in ספר חסידים: a pious man dreams that his recently-deceased close friend (also a known חסיד and tzaddik) appears to him with a pale-green face (ופניו מורקות). When asked why his face is not radiant in the manner of a tzaddik in Gan Eden, the friend answers: "שהייתי מדבר בויכולו בשעה שהיה הציבור אומר מגן אבות". The ספר חסידים draws no further consequence: this was a complete tzaddik in every respect but for the single failing of speaking during the מגן אבות seam. The structural payload: the eidus dignity of the moment is halachically and metaphysically operative. ↩
  15. שו״ת הריב״ש סימן של״ו — Yom Tov falling on Friday and the latecomer question. — ר' יצחק בר ששת ברפת (the Rivash, 1326–1408), in his teshuvos: when Yom Tov falls on Friday, who is the latecomer that the מגן אבות תקנה is addressing? The Rivash's answer — that people still come late because of the סעודת יום טוב — was treated by Rabbi Ephrati as conceptually difficult; the שיעור landed on Simchas Torah as the most plausible historical case. The teshuva's halachic conclusion is unambiguous: yes, מגן אבות is recited even on a Friday night that falls after a Yom Tov. ↩
  16. רמ״א אורח חיים סימן רס״ח סעיף י' — the יחיד and מגן אבות without ברכות. — The Rema's gloss: a יחיד davening alone does not say מגן אבות with the full ברכה structure, because the entire תקנה was institutionally directed at the tzibbur and the latecomer. If the יחיד wishes to recite the body of the unit for personal kavanah-reasons, he may do so without the opening and closing ברכות to avoid a ברכה לבטלה. The Mishna Berura discusses; the practical halacha is the Rema's framing. ↩
  17. תהלים פרק כ״ג — מזמור לדוד as שמירה in Nusach Sefard's Friday-night liturgy. — מִזְמוֹר לְדָוִד ה' רֹעִי לֹא אֶחְסָר. The mizmor of "הַשֵּׁם is my shepherd" is recited in Nusach Sefard on Friday night between מגן אבות and the closing of מעריב; Nusach Ashkenaz does not include it. The published Nusach Sefard סידורים attribute the practice to סוד without elaborating the פשט reason. The kehillah's chiddush (Sammy) — that the pasuk גַם כִּי אֵלֵךְ בְּגֵיא צַלְמָוֶת לֹא אִירָא רָע כִּי אַתָּה עִמָּדִי functions as שמירה for exactly the Friday-night walk-home scenario that the original מגן אבות תקנה addresses — was accepted by Rabbi Ephrati as a plausible פשט-level chiddush worth recording. ↩
  18. שמות פרק ל״ה פסוק ג' — לֹא תְבַעֲרוּ אֵשׁ ... בְּיוֹם הַשַּׁבָּת; structural extension to גהינום. — The pasuk prohibiting kindling fire on Shabbos. The structural extension by חז״ל and the זוהר הקדוש: the prohibition applies to גהינום itself — גהינום אינו דן בשבת. The fires of גהינום are dormant on Shabbos; the נשמות otherwise under their pressure are released. The same pasuk anchors last week's discussion of the מחזור ויטרי's chiddush and this week's discussion of the gates of גהינום reopening at מוצאי שבת. ↩
  19. בבלי סנהדרין ס״ה ע״ב — Turnusrufus and Rabbi Akiva on the smoke at the kever. — The Gemara records the Roman governor Turnusrufus challenging Rabbi Akiva on what makes Shabbos verifiable. Among Rabbi Akiva's proofs: "Go to your father's kever; watch the smoke." Throughout the week smoke rises from the kever; on Shabbos there is no smoke. Even goyim are off גהינום on Shabbos. The Gemara is the structural Torah-textual anchor for the wider claim about the universal cessation of גהינום on Shabbos that the Zohar and the Tur build on. ↩
  20. זוהר הקדוש — the מחלוקת רבי יהודה ורבי יוסי on the מחלל שבת בפרהסיא. — The Zohar Hakadosh debates whether the universal-Shabbos-reprieve extends to a Yid who was מחלל שבת בפרהסיא during his lifetime. One opinion: he does not receive the reprieve — לא ינוחו ולא ישקטו; for him, גהינום burns on Shabbos. The other opinion preserves the reprieve. The Tannaim cited in the Zohar passage vary in different girsaos; the conceptual machloikes is consistent. The שיעור flagged the practical conclusion as sobering: a Yid who was מחלל שבת בפרהסיא is, in one view, structurally worse than a goy. Exact citation in the Zohar was not specified in the שיעור. ↩
    The Zohar's discussion of the שבת-reprieve and the מחלל שבת בפרהסיא is distributed across multiple sections of the Zohar (most prominently in the שמות section commenting on the שבת pesukim of פרשת ויקהל and פקודי). The שיעור did not give a precise citation; verify against the standard Zohar text and the מקדש מלך / נצוצי זוהר commentaries before citing the exact source.
  21. טור אורח חיים סימן רצ״ה — the Medrash on וִיהִי נֹעַם and the reopening of the gates. — The Tur, in his treatment of מוצאי שבת liturgy, brings a Medrash describing the structural moment at which the gates of גהינום reopen: at the moment the last shul in the world finishes saying וִיהִי נֹעַם וְיוֹשֵׁב בְּסֵתֶר. The structural payload: the personal עיתוי of an individual Yid's מוצאי שבת corresponds to the personal restart of his own clock vis-à-vis גהינום. The Zohar Hakadosh's image — the released forces are כלבים (dogs), agents of the סמא״ל — is the conceptual ground for the chassidic practices of late-havdala that the שיעור treated next. The siman is in the standard Tur treatment of מוצאי שבת מעריב. ↩
  22. יסוד ושורש העבודה — personal עיתוי of מוצאי שבת and individual גהינום reprieve. — ר' אלכסנדר זיסקינד מהוראדנא (the יסוד ושורש העבודה, 18th century) — the foundational kabbalistic-musar treatment of תפילה and עבודה day-by-day, sefer-by-sefer of the Torah, that has shaped the chassidic and post-chassidic devotional landscape for two centuries. The structural application brought in the שיעור: each individual Yid's personal Shabbos-restart of גהינום corresponds to his own מוצאי שבת timing rather than to a universal cosmic clock. The practical conclusion: extending one's own Motzaei Shabbos extends one's personal reprieve. The chassidic late-havdala practices of the Pshelelever Rebbe and the Ribnitzer Rebbe are direct applications. ↩
  23. הרבי ר' שמעלקא הלוי הורוויץ מניקלשבורג (1726–1778) — the עליונים למטה ותחתונים למעלה maaseh. — ר' שמואל שמעלקא הלוי הורוויץ, Rebbe of Nikolsburg (Mikulov in Moravia), one of the great early Chassidic Rebbes and rav of an important kehillah in central Europe. The maaseh of seeing the recently-deceased congregant in גהינום at the first moment of motzaei Shabbos, pointing down to where Reb Shmelke himself was still in middle of שלוש סעודות, is preserved in the Nikolsburg chassidic mesorah and in the broader hagiographic literature on Reb Shmelke. The Miami kehillah had visited his kever in Nikolsburg approximately a year earlier — the personal connection is part of the שיעור's framing. Exact published source for the maaseh was not specified in the שיעור. ↩
  24. חפץ חיים, ליקוטי הלכות — personal Shabbos clock and the early-מקבל-שבת chiddush. — The Chofetz Chaim's ליקוטי הלכות: the chiddush — Rabbi Ephrati flagged that no other source he knows of brings this — that an individual Yid's personal Shabbos clock in שמים, including the timing of the structural reprieve from גהינום, begins at the moment he was מקבל שבת rather than at the formal halachic זמן. Practical application: early מקבל שבת extends the personal reprieve backward; even Friday-morning הכנה לשבת (not Thursday night — שולחן ערוך אורח חיים סימן ר״מ ב') brings the אורות של שבת into Friday. Exact siman in the Chofetz Chaim's ליקוטי הלכות was not specified in the שיעור. ↩
