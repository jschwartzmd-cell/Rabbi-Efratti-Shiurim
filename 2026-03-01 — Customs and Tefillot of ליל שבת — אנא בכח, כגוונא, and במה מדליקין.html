Rabbi Ephrati · Sunday Morning שיעור on תפילה

Customs and Tefillot of ליל שבת

אנא בכח, כגוונא, ובמה מדליקין — Their Origins, Disputes, and Spiritual Logic
Recorded 1 March 2026 · Reworked from the original שיעור

After tracing the structure of קבלת שבת last week, the שיעור turned this Sunday to the texts that bracket it on either side — אנא בכח within the procession itself, and כגוונא and במה מדליקין on its threshold. Each of the three carries its own history; one was inserted by the אריז״ל's circle, one by the בעל שם טוב's, and one became, over centuries, a quiet battlefield between חסידים and מתנגדים. The geography of where each prayer sits in our siddur is, in effect, the geography of those disputes.

I. אנא בכח — The Forty-Two Letter Name

Between מזמור לדוד and לכה דודי we say אנא בכח. The text is brief — seven verses, each opening with a phrase of supplication and closing with a refrain. The structure is not poetic ornament; it is built around the שם בן מ״ב — the forty-two-letter Name[1]. Each of the seven verses opens with one of the seven words of the Name, and the initial letters of all forty-two of those words spell out the שם itself.

Authorship is traditionally attributed to רבי נחוניא בן הקנה[2], the תנא from whose בית מדרש the early היכלות literature emerges. Whether the attribution is historically airtight or, as the academic literature of יהדות holds, the prayer surfaces somewhat later in early kabbalistic circles, the centrality of the name itself goes back to חז״ל. The גמרא בקידושין דף ע״א ע״א[3] describes the שם בן מ״ב as the Name through which הקב״ה created the world — בו נברא העולם. That is precisely the link to קבלת שבת: שבת is itself the testimony to creation, and the Name through which creation occurred therefore belongs in the procession that crowns the seventh day.

Why This Placement? The placement specifically between מזמור לדוד and לכה דודי is the אריז״ל's institution. The seven קולות of מזמור לדוד correspond to the seven ברכות of שבת; the seven verses of אנא בכח correspond to the seven words of the שם בן מ״ב. The same number-pattern repeats, deliberately, on the seam where we move from praising the King to greeting the כלה.

II. Standing and Bowing at לכה דודי

Standing

The mainstream minhag — recorded in שולחן ערוך, אורח חיים סימן רס״ב[4] with the רמ״א's addition — is to stand only for the final stanza, בואי כלה, and to turn toward the entrance. There is, however, a stricter custom — found among certain חסידים and in some printings of the אר״י's nusach — to stand for the entirety of לכה דודי, on the principle that the worshiper is enacting the procession of the תנאים who literally walked out to greet the שבת.

Bowing — Left then Right, or Right then Left?

Two minhagim exist. Some bow first to the left, then to the right, then to the center; others reverse the order. The reasoning given for the "left first" custom is structural: a person facing forward has the שכינה behind him; the שכינה's right is the worshiper's left. The שיעור's pragmatic ruling: both customs are valid; what matters is the הרגש — the inner sense — that one is greeting the שבת. The act is choreography, not arithmetic.

III. ליל שבת That Is Also יום טוב

When ערב שבת falls on יום טוב, many minhagim omit לכה דודי entirely or in part, and adjust the structure of קבלת שבת. The שולחן ערוך, אורח חיים סימן תס״ח and the משנה ברורה on רס״ב[5] codify variants on this practice. Two reasons compete:

The kavod-of-יום-טוב reason: A separate elaborate procession for שבת in the middle of יום טוב would suggest that יום טוב is somehow inadequate, requiring an additional welcoming ceremony. Out of כבוד יום טוב, the special שבת greeting is muted.

The kedushah-already-present reason: A worshiper on יום טוב is already in a state of קדושה. The transition into שבת is not from חול to קודש but from קודש to greater קודש. The dramatic procession that suits the weekday-to-Shabbos transition does not fit a kedushah-to-kedushah one.

The שיעור pressed on the second reason: the only thing שבת objectively adds over יום טוב, in halachic terms, is the prohibition of אוכל נפש-מלאכות (cooking and similar) and the presence of the נשמה יתירה. Those are not nothing — they are part of why שבת is described as a higher kedushah than יום טוב — but they are subtler than the dramatic chol-to-קודש shift that the full לכה דודי enacts.

IV. במה מדליקין — Three Reasons, One Mishnah

After קבלת שבת and מעריב, our minhag is to recite the second perek of מסכת שבת — the famous במה מדליקין[6]. The perek itself is straightforward halachah: which oils and wicks are kosher for נר שבת, and which are not. The mishnah at the end touches on עירובי תבשילין and other ערב שבת preparations. So why this perek of all perakim, and why here, on Friday night?

Reason 1 — The Latecomers

This reason, brought by the טור, אורח חיים סימן ר״ע[7] in the name of the Geonim, is the most touching. Back in the era of the Geonim and the Rishonim, shuls were typically located outside the city walls — in the fields, away from population centers. A late-arriver on Friday night, finishing his עמידה after the rest of the קהל had already left, would face a long walk home in the dark, alone, without a torch (which he could not carry on שבת), exposed to מזיקין — the ambient dangers of nighttime travel.

חז״ל and the Geonim, anxious to protect that one late-arriver, instituted a five-to-seven minute reading at the end of davening that the rest of the קהל would say in unison — keeping everyone in the shul long enough for the late-comer to finish his תפילה and walk home together with the others. במה מדליקין, by length and content, was the right size for the job.

A Pattern in Halachah The שיעור pointed out that this is one of many examples — not the only one — of חז״ל restructuring the davening to protect latecomers. The doubled ברכה structure of עליות (one before, one after, rather than one for the first and one for the last עולה only), the two-bracket structure of certain ברכות, and several other features of our nusach are recorded by the Geonim as accommodations for those who arrive late or are still davening. The texture of our siddur is, in this sense, profoundly merciful.

Reason 2 — A Reminder of שבת Preparations

The second reason, given by the אבודרהם[8] and brought in the טור, is mnemonic. The mishnah of במה מדליקין reviews — in compact halachic language — the practical things one must have done by sunset on Friday: the right oils, the right wicks, an עירוב חצירות if needed, an עירוב תבשילין if it is also יום טוב, and the קביעות of one's סעודה.

The אבודרהם argued that, given this reminder function, it should be said before מעריב — so that a person who realizes he has forgotten something can still go home and fix it before שבת actually enters. This was, by his testimony, the original minhag. The shift to saying it after מעריב — which is now nearly universal Ashkenaz practice — happened later, on the reasoning that by the time we daven מעריב today, שבת is already in; nothing can be "fixed" anymore. The recitation becomes more a lesson for next week than a corrective for this week.

Reason 3 — The Polemic Against the Karaites

The third reason, brought by the בית יוסף, אורח חיים סימן ר״ע[9] in the name of earlier sources, is theological-polemical. In the eighth century, in Babylon, a man named ענן בן דוד[10] emerged from the household of the ראש גלות. After being passed over for the position of ראש גלות in favor of his uncle, he turned in protest against the rabbinic authorities of his day and aligned himself with what would become the Karaite movement — a sect that rejected the תורה שבעל פה and read the תורה שבכתב in a strict literalist manner.

One of the most visible Karaite practices was their reading of לא תבערו אש בכל מושבותיכם (Shemos 35:3) as forbidding any fire to be burning in a Jewish home on שבת — even one that had been lit before שבת entered. The Karaites therefore sat in cold, dark houses on Friday night.

In response, the rabbinic tradition emphasized — through the public, congregational recitation of the second perek of מסכת שבת, the very perek that codifies which fires may be lit before שבת and continue to burn — the legitimacy of the Oral Law's reading. To recite במה מדליקין in shul on Friday night was to make a public, communal declaration: we are חכמי המסורה; we read the text through the lens of חז״ל; our שבת is illuminated, our food is warm, and our homes are alive precisely because of the תורה שבעל פה.

V. כגוונא — The Baal Shem Tov's Insertion

Between קבלת שבת and מעריב, חסידים say כגוונא דאינון מתייחדין — a passage in Aramaic from the זוהר, פרשת תרומה[11]. The passage describes the elevation of the קב״ה's sefirot at the entry of שבת, the unification of the various spiritual worlds, and the dissolution of judgments. The text concludes: ואיהו רזא דאחד — and this is the secret of the One.

The text is from the זוהר, but the זוהר itself does not say to recite it on Friday night. The practice of inserting it into the formal davening is a takanah of the בעל שם טוב הקדוש and his תלמידים[12], as part of the broader Chassidic project of integrating zoharic texts into communal liturgy. Most Chassidic siddurim print כגוונא in bold or with a marginal mark indicating its added status.

The Piaseczner Rebbe

The שיעור highlighted the writings of the פיאסעצנער רבי — ר' קלונימוס קלמיש שפירא (1889–1943)[13], a descendant of the נועם אלימלך. The Piaseczner was murdered in the Holocaust; his manuscripts buried in milk cans in the Warsaw Ghetto were recovered after the war and published as אש קודש. He was famous as a mashpiya in pre-war חסידות and is increasingly read today across denominational lines.

The Piaseczner's particular guidance for כגוונא: it should be said after קבלת שבת but before מעריב, with deliberate joy — not rushed through, not whispered. The text is itself a kavanah, and the act of saying it with hispayl is part of welcoming the שבת. His writings on this point are scattered in בני מחשבה טובה and in his other published works.

The "ברוך הוא" Question

A small textual concern: at the end of the כגוונא passage, the words בריך הוא appear. There is a halachic principle that one does not answer "ברוך הוא" to a ברכה that one is not actually hearing — i.e., one does not answer "ברוך הוא" while davening privately (ביחיד). The question: does saying בריך הוא as part of the כגוונא text run afoul of this rule?

The answer brought in the שיעור: when one recites the כגוונא passage, one is reading the זוהר's text — the words בריך הוא are part of the recitation, not an answer to a separate ברכה being made. The principle against "ברוך הוא ביחיד" applies to a worshiper who hears (or thinks he hears) a ברכה being recited and adds "ברוך הוא" — not to a worshiper who is reading a text in which those words appear.

VI. חסידים and מתנגדים — A Liturgical Front

The placement of במה מדליקין in modern Ashkenaz davening turns out to be a small monument to the great dispute of the late 18th century between חסידים and מתנגדים. To trace the dispute through this single liturgical decision:

The Sefardi-original placement: after מעריב, at the very end of davening. This is the original minhag preserved in the Sefardi nuschaot and is the oldest placement attested in the Geonim and the early Rishonim.

The Chassidic placement: חסידים inserted כגוונא after קבלת שבת and before מעריב — a position that the Vilna Gaon and his school regarded as illegitimate, an importation of kabbalistic text into the halachic structure of davening, and worse, an importation that elevated the זוהר above the halachic siddur. To the מתנגדים, this was a reversal of the proper hierarchy: halachah determines the structure of davening, not kabbalah.

The Misnagdic placement: the Vilna Gaon's circle — most notably ר' אברהם דאנציג, the חיי אדם[14] — moved במה מדליקין from its old after-מעריב position to a position between קבלת שבת and מעריב, before ברכו. The polemical logic is sharp: where the חסידים place a kabbalistic text (כגוונא) at the seam between קבלת שבת and מעריב, the מתנגדים place a halachic text (במה מדליקין, the Mishnah's own halachah of שבת) at that same seam. The placement says: halachah comes first; kabbalah is subordinate.

Most Ashkenaz siddurim today print במה מדליקין exactly there — between קבלת שבת and ברכו — and this is the most widespread current Ashkenaz practice. The placement is the inheritance of the מתנגדים' liturgical protest. The Sefardim, untouched by the Chassidic-Misnagdic dispute, retained the original after-מעריב placement.

A Living Map The next time you open a siddur on Friday night, the geography of where each prayer sits — אנא בכח between מזמור לדוד and לכה דודי, כגוונא or במה מדליקין between קבלת שבת and ברכו, במה מדליקין at the very end (or not at all) — is not arbitrary. It is the legible record of three movements: the אריז״ל's circle, the בעל שם טוב's circle, and the Vilna Gaon's. Each layer was added in response to its predecessor.

The week's takeaway: the seemingly minor texts of ליל שבת — אנא בכח, כגוונא, במה מדליקין — are not filler. Each is a deliberate insertion by a specific master at a specific moment, designed to shape the worshiper's transition from חול into שבת in a particular way. To say them with awareness of their history is to participate, however briefly, in the conversations that produced our siddur.

מראה מקומות

  1. שם בן מ״ב — the forty-two letter Name. — The שם בן מ״ב, derived from the opening verses of בראשית (per one tradition) or built from the seven words of אנא בכח (per the kabbalistic tradition), is referenced in היכלות רבתי and developed in early kabbalistic literature including ספר הבהיר and ספר יצירה commentaries. The structural correspondence — seven verses of אנא בכח, each opening with one word of the seven-word שם — is the key reason the prayer is built the way it is. ↩
  2. ר' נחוניא בן הקנה, traditional author. — ר' נחוניא בן הקנה is the תנא from whose בית מדרש much of the early kabbalistic-mystical tradition emerges (cf. ברכות כ״ח ע״ב; היכלות רבתי; the attribution of ספר הבהיר in some kabbalistic traditions). The traditional attribution of אנא בכח to him reflects this association rather than a halachic citation. Academic scholarship dates the prayer's first attested liturgical use to early kabbalistic circles in 12th–13th century Provence/Spain. ↩
    The traditional attribution to ר' נחוניא בן הקנה is widely accepted in Jewish liturgical tradition but is not directly attested in early Talmudic or Geonic sources. The earliest manuscript witnesses to אנא בכח as a unit appear in early kabbalistic siddurim of the 13th century.
  3. קידושין דף ע״א ע״א — the שם בן מ״ב. — The גמרא records: שם בן ארבעים ושתים אותיות אינו נמסר אלא למי שצנוע ועניו ועומד בחצי ימיו — the forty-two letter Name is transmitted only to one who is modest, humble, and stands at the middle of his days. The same passage discusses the use of the Name and the spiritual conditions for its transmission. This is the principal Talmudic source for the existence and significance of the שם. ↩
  4. שולחן ערוך, אורח חיים סימן רס״ב והגהת הרמ״א. — The שולחן ערוך codifies the practice of standing for the final stanza of לכה דודי and turning to greet the שבת. The רמ״א adds the Ashkenazic detail of turning to face the entrance of the shul and bowing at the words בואי כלה. The custom of taking three steps is attested in some Ashkenazic communities; others stand in place. The minhag of standing for the entire לכה דודי (rather than only the final stanza) is found in some חסידישע nuschaot and in some Sefardic communities. ↩
  5. שולחן ערוך, אורח חיים סימן תס״ח; משנה ברורה על סימן רס״ב. — The interaction between שבת and יום טוב on the same eve is treated in multiple places in the שולחן ערוך and the משנה ברורה. The general principle: when יום טוב is also the eve of שבת, the standard קבלת שבת service is modified, with omissions or abbreviations of certain elements of לכה דודי and the מזמורים, depending on the specific minhag of the community. ↩
  6. משנה שבת פרק ב (במה מדליקין). — The second perek of מסכת שבת deals with the laws of שבת candles: which oils may be used (specifically those that draw to the wick properly), which wicks are permitted, and the laws of the שמן and the פתילה generally. The closing mishnah of the perek, שלושה דברים צריך אדם לומר, lists the practical preparations for שבת. The perek is approximately 5–7 minutes when recited at conversational pace. ↩
  7. טור, אורח חיים סימן ר״ע בשם הגאונים. — The Tur (ר' יעקב בן הרא״ש, early 14th century) brings the Geonic tradition for reciting במה מדליקין on Friday night. The reason of "the latecomers" — that shuls were located outside the city and that the perek was recited to delay the קהל so that latecomers could finish davening and walk home together — is one of the principal reasons recorded in the Geonic literature. ↩
  8. אבודרהם, סדר ליל שבת. — ר' דוד אבודרהם (14th century, Sevilla) wrote one of the most thorough commentaries on the siddur. He brings the reasoning for במה מדליקין as a reminder of שבת preparations, and argues that on this reasoning it should be said before מעריב, in time for a worshiper to fix any forgotten preparation. His position on the ordering reflects the older minhag. ↩
  9. בית יוסף, אורח חיים סימן ר״ע. — ר' יוסף קארו's commentary on the טור, the בית יוסף, brings multiple reasons for the recitation of במה מדליקין on Friday night, including the Geonic reason of the latecomers, the אבודרהם's mnemonic reason, and a third reason connected to refuting Karaite practice. The polemical reading is well attested in the medieval literature and is alluded to in earlier Geonic responsa concerning Karaite controversy. ↩
  10. ענן בן דוד (8th century, Babylon). — ענן בן דוד is the figure traditionally identified as the founder of the Karaite sect, although the modern academic literature distinguishes ענן and his immediate followers (sometimes called Ananites) from the broader Karaite movement that consolidated in the 9th century. The general narrative — that ענן, after being passed over for the position of ראש גלות, broke from the rabbinic establishment and rejected the תורה שבעל פה — is recorded in Geonic sources including the responsa of ר' שרירא גאון and ר' האי גאון. ↩
  11. זוהר, פרשת תרומה דף קל״ה ע״א–ב — כגוונא דאינון מתייחדין. — The passage in the זוהר describes the entry of שבת as the moment when the various unifications of the upper sefirot occur, when judgments depart, and when the worshiper is supposed to disconnect entirely from the language and concerns of the weekday. The phrase ואיהו רזא דאחד serves as the conclusion. The passage is one of the most quoted in the זוהר and is the principal kabbalistic text for the entry of שבת. ↩
  12. בעל שם טוב הקדוש — takanah for כגוונא in the Friday-night davening. — The integration of the כגוונא passage into the Friday night davening between קבלת שבת and מעריב is attributed to the בעל שם טוב (ר' ישראל בן אליעזר, 1698–1760) and his תלמידים. The practice is recorded in the early Chassidic siddurim — the סידור האריז״ל as edited by the תלמידים of the בעל שם טוב, the siddur of the בעל התניא, and others — and from there spread throughout the Chassidic world. ↩
    The exact takanah of the בעל שם טוב is preserved through Chassidic oral and printed tradition rather than through a single signed document. The first printed Chassidic siddurim (late 18th century) consistently include כגוונא in this position.
  13. הפיאסעצנער רבי — ר' קלונימוס קלמיש שפירא (1889–1943); אש קודש; בני מחשבה טובה. — ר' קלונימוס קלמיש שפירא, a descendant of נועם אלימלך of Lizhensk, was the rebbe of Piaseczno before WWII and one of the most influential mashpiyim of the pre-war Chassidic world. He was murdered in the Holocaust in 1943; his manuscripts, buried in milk cans in the Warsaw Ghetto, were recovered after the war. אש קודש contains his Shabbos drashas in the ghetto. בני מחשבה טובה contains his pre-war guidance on tefillah and avodas Hashem; the שיעור referenced his guidance on כגוונא from this corpus. ↩
    The exact citation within the Piaseczner's writings for his specific guidance on saying כגוונא after קבלת שבת and before מעריב with deliberate joy should be verified in בני מחשבה טובה and in the relevant published shiurim. The שיעור attributed the guidance broadly to his corpus.
  14. חיי אדם מאת ר' אברהם דאנציג (1748–1820); סדר התפילה בנוסחו. — ר' אברהם דאנציג, a תלמיד of the Vilna Gaon and author of the popular halachic compendium חיי אדם, codified the Vilna Gaon's siddur. His placement of במה מדליקין between קבלת שבת and ברכו — rather than at the end of מעריב, the older Sefardi position — became the standard Ashkenazi-Misnagdic practice. The polemical edge of the placement (a halachic text where the חסידים put a kabbalistic one) was widely understood at the time. ↩
