Rabbi Ephrati · Sunday Morning שיעור on תפילה

The רוקח, ברכו, and ורחם

Returning to תפילת ערבית — and a Story Told by רב חיים ויטאל
Recorded 19 April 2026 · Reworked from the original שיעור

Today, 19 April 2026, marks the יארצייט of ר' יום טוב קמנצקי זצ״ל, who was instrumental in establishing the Five Towns קהילה. We encourage everyone to attend the לימוד this evening in his memory. After about a six-week break, we resume our exploration of weekday תפילת ערבית, picking up from ברכו.

I. ורחם — Why We Say It Before ערבית

The מדרש in the Tur

The טור in סימן רל״ב[1] brings a fascinating מדרש that warrants careful examination. The מדרש states that בית דין של מעלה receives מלכות — the authority of judgment — between מנחה and ערבית each night. This requires clarification, because as it stands it is perplexing.

The זרע קודש[2] and other אחרונים explain that there is likely a textual error here. The correct understanding: in גהינום the punishment intensifies at night — the fire burns at double intensity after nightfall. Therefore, before night begins, we say ורחם — asking הקדוש ברוך הוא to diminish the punishment and show רחמים.

Multiple Reasons

The ראשונים give several explanations for ורחם:

First reason — the missing קרבן. The אבודרהם[3] explains: we had תמיד של שחר in the morning and תמיד של בין הערבים at מנחה. There is no קרבן at night corresponding to ערבית. In place of the missing קרבן — which would normally provide כפרה — we say ורחם, asking for atonement.

Second reason — daily judgment. Today, without קרבנות at all, it is sobering that every yid faces judgment each day. We need כפרה at day's end. As the day concludes and we prepare to daven ערבית before הקדוש ברוך הוא, we begin with ורחם — an extra plea for רחמים and כפרה.

The Holy שם After ורחם

After ורחם, many סידורים include a holy שם[4], though not all ancient נוסחאות contain it. Those who learn ספרי קבלה and ספרי חסידות know that שם יוד כא ואו כא is a very sacred name of הקדוש ברוך הוא.

It represents the numerical value of three names combined:

  • אדנ-י = 65
  • הוי״ה spelled out (יוד קי ואו קי) = 26
  • אקי-ה = 21
  • Total = 112 (יאב״ו)

This holy transition helps us move from day to night — from the time when דינים intensify. As the זרע קודש explained, the רשעים in גהינום experience fire at double temperature at night. Therefore we transition to nighttime, the period of דין, with ורחם and this שם הקדוש, before proceeding to ברכו.

II. Why We Don't Say ורחם on שבת

By the reasons just given, this is straightforward. If the reason is גהינום, then on שבת there is no intensified punishment to ask Him to diminish — there is no גהינום operating on שבת. (The reason about the missing קרבן and daily judgment would seemingly apply on שבת too, but the dominant reason is the first.) In נוסח ספרד they actually do recite it on Friday night; in נוסח אשכנז we do not.

The פסוק

The ראשונים explain that the primary reason connects directly to גהינום. The פסוק says לא תבערו אש בכל מושבותיכם ביום השבת[5] — "do not kindle fire in all your dwellings on שבת." בכל מושבותיכם includes even the realm above. There is no גהינום operating on שבת.

גהינום begins burning when, כביכול, they "turn the knob" from off to on — precisely at the moment yidden below say ורחם. Rather than denying the רשעים even one extra minute of מנוחה, we refrain from saying ורחם entirely on Friday night, because we are תוספת שבת — we add to שבת and daven ערבית early. (However, this is not the complete picture — we will return to it when we reach the close of ערבית.)

The נועם מגדים's Question

The נועם מגדים[6], one of the תלמידים of the מזריטשער מגיד, asks a fair philosophical question: if by saying ורחם you could save the רשעים for one more day of relief from גהינום, why wouldn't you want to do so? It is a deep question. The ראשונים maintain this is the reason for the מנהג; you can sit with the question.

III. ברכו — Why at Night Too?

We already say ברכו in the morning at שחרית. Why again at ערבית?

The מטה משה's Midrash

The ספר מטה משה[7] (authored by ר' משה מאט — his family name was מ-א-ט, hence the clever title מטה משה) brings a unique מדרש. The מטה משה is the only source for it, which raises questions about its provenance — but the מדרש is beautiful.

There is a מלאך in שמים called החייל. On this מלאך's forehead is engraved, כביכול, the word אמת. Every morning when the sun rises, the אמת on his forehead lights up. This is the signal to all the מלאכים: "now it is morning; time to daven." Corresponding to it, כלל ישראל below says ברכו at שחרית. At night, when the sun sets, the word אמונה lights up on his forehead instead — אמת in the morning, אמונה at night. Again the מלאך proclaims, ברכו את ה' המבורך, summoning the מלאכים. We below say it too. Just as the מלאכים have שירה in the morning and שירה at night, so do we.

While the only source is this מטה משה, ברכו itself appears in סידורים from time immemorial — long before the מטה משה (who lived in the late 1500s and early 1600s).

IV. The הלכתי Status of ברכו

ברכו is a דבר שבקדושה[8] — it requires a מנין of ten.

An Important Warning From the משנה ברורה

The משנה ברורה[9] warns about a problem prevalent in his day, and prevalent in many shuls today: once you answer ברכו, you may not be מפסיק — you may not interrupt with speech.

The משנה ברורה writes: רבים נכשלים בזה — many yidden stumble in this. After they answer ברכו, they go right back to talking, before they have completed שמע with its ברכות. This is an איסור גמור.

Once you begin ברכות קריאת שמע, you carry the דין of no interruption. The first משנה of the second פרק of ברכות[10]: היה קורא בתורה והגיע זמן קריאת שמע, פוסק — when reading תורה and the זמן for קריאת שמע arrives, you stop. The obligation is so serious that one even interrupts תורה study. Once you begin, no more talking.

The משנה ברורה stresses that yidden simply do not realize this and must be extra careful.

V. The ב״ח's Connection — ברכו as ברכת התורה

The ב״ח[11] explains that just as we say ברכו before יוצר אור before ברכות קריאת שמע at שחרית — because יוצר has the דין of ברכת התורה, and a yid who forgot ברכת התורה may be יוצא through יוצר — so too at night, before ערבית, before the ברכות of קריאת שמע, we say ברכו. Beautiful.

VI. The חיד״א's סגולה

The חיד״א[12] brings from the great רב האי גאון, one of the גאונים and the בעל ההלכות, a קבלה מרבותיו. It is a הבטחה: a yid who has the merit of answering ברכו at night — not on שבת, but on weeknights — will have a successful week. Who does not want a successful week? Therefore, even if the ציבור rushes through, you should personally take a moment to say ברכו slowly and with כוונה. Some קהילות sing ברכו every night, perhaps connected to this very promise.

VII. The רוקח

His Name and His Place

Now I want to share something from the ספר רוקח. The רוקח is one of our great ראשונים: ר' אלעזר מווירמייזא[13]. Two years ago we visited his קבר in Worms.

Why "רוקח"? The numerical value of רוקח (ר-י-ק-ח) is 308. His own name, אלעזר, is also 308. So he called his ספר "רוקח."

His Significance

The רוקח wrote many ספרים, many פיוטים, and many parts of our נוסח התפילה. He was a giant who stood at the head of the movement called חסידי אשכנז. This is crucial Jewish history that many yidden do not know — and it is vital, because most of us descend from that part of the world. The רוקח is enormously influential on our נוסח התפילה. He wrote a סידור; the שלחן ערוך constantly cites his rulings on what we do in davening.

VIII. The רוקח and the Origins of מנהג קטניות

The custom regarding קטניות on פסח has very ancient roots. (The term itself derives from a Latin word — "cinnamomum" — and always refers to legumes in halacha.)

One of the primary early sources is the רוקח, who delivered a דרשה on it in his קהילה in Worms around 1170. The רוקח was extraordinarily influential in establishing the מנהגים of אשכנז. Many practices we observe today — practices we may not realize originate with him — are based on the רוקח. For example, the three steps we take before שמע ישראל come from him. (While he uses a גמרא as basis, he does not simply quote it; he develops his own interpretation and application. That is his method: take a source from חז״ל and form a מנהג around it.)

IX. The רוקח's Background — and a Fitting Connection

The רוקח was born in מגנצא (Mainz) and later settled in וורמייזא (Worms). He was a close relative — a nephew or first cousin — of רבי יהודה החסיד[14], the author of ספר חסידים. He learned under רבי יהודה החסיד and his son-in-law. Beyond being a בעל הלכה and a דרשן, the רוקח was a significant מקובל.

This was particularly noteworthy because he lived during the period just before the זוהר הקדוש was revealed — roughly 1150–1250. In that era קבלה was not yet widespread, and many גדולי ישראל were actually opposed to the public study of קבלה.

X. The Story That רב חיים ויטאל Records

Why We Believe It

What I am about to relate comes from רב חיים ויטאל[15], and if רב חיים ויטאל records something we believe it. The יארצייט of רב חיים ויטאל was just a few days ago — ל' ניסן, 17 April. So much of what we do in our מנהגים traveled through the conduit of the האריז״ל, primarily through רב חיים ויטאל. Anyone who learns קבלה — and indeed anyone who follows the מנהגים we practice today — owes an enormous debt to רב חיים ויטאל. We may think we have no connection to קבלת האריז״ל, but we follow the האריז״ל constantly, all of it transmitted through רב חיים ויטאל. When רב חיים ויטאל records something in ספר הגלגולים, we believe it — at least as his testimony.

The רמב״ן Before His Transformation

רב חיים ויטאל relates the following about our ראשונים. The רמב״ן lived in ג'ירונא, Spain (in קטלוניה). To this day, in ג'ירונא, you can still see the house of the רמב״ן intact — it is a known landmark. The רמב״ן was also a physician, a significant doctor of his day. That is why throughout his פירוש על התורה, in passages dealing with biology, medicine, and science, the רמב״ן has so much to say. He cites other physicians and offers medical analyses, much like the רמב״ם.

But the רמב״ן had one notable characteristic in his early life: he did not learn קבלה. Indeed, before the זוהר הקדוש began to surface and people began discussing it, the רמב״ן was actually opposed to קבלה. This was the position of most of כלל ישראל at that time. The רמב״ן was first and foremost a man of הלכה — a halachist who also knew sciences and philosophy. קבלה was simply not in his orbit.

Then the רמב״ן underwent a complete metamorphosis. He went from being publicly and vehemently opposed to קבלה to becoming the greatest מקובל among the ראשונים — arguably even surpassing the רמב״ם in this regard. (While the רמב״ם never saw the זוהר, according to what we know, and while some מקובלים claim that everything the רמב״ם wrote aligns with קבלה, this is difficult to demonstrate; the רמב״ם was fundamentally a man of logic and philosophy.) The רמב״ן's later writings are filled with קבלה.

What happened to cause this dramatic change?

XI. ספר מעשה נסים and רב חיים ויטאל's Account

The story has a מסורה — a tradition, not merely a legend. There is a ספר called מעשה נסים[16], written by a yid in the 1500s named רבי יוסף יוזפא בעל שמש (Rabbi Yosef Yuzpa Shamash). If you learn הלכות from him, you recognize his name — he is quoted very often. רבי יוסף בעל שמש learned in Worms and was a תלמיד of the מהר״ל of Prague — a great חכם of his day. (The שמש in Worms was not merely the synagogue attendant; he was a תלמיד חכם who wrote גיטין and served as a שוחט. Many of our מנהגים follow רבי יוסף בעל שמש. His full name was רבי יוסף הלוי מנתפח — an unusual surname spelled with final letters.)

רבי יוסף בעל שמש compiled a ספר of מנהגי וורמייזא. He wrote ספר מעשה נסים in the 1600s, though he himself did not publish it; his son did, after his פטירה. The ספר contains twenty-five stories he received as קבלה from his רבנים, who lived in Worms for generations — stories of the great heroes and רבנים of Worms.

Why It Is Largely Unknown

There is a reason ספר מעשה נסים is not well-known today: it was published in Yiddish, but the Yiddish of the 1600s was an old, heavy German Yiddish — an ancient, difficult Yiddish that we cannot read easily today. (If you look at ליקוטי שושנים you see this old Yiddish.) Consequently the ספר became unpopular and inaccessible. Today you will not easily find a copy. I have an old one at home — antique Judaica from my grandfather and father.

XII. The Story — The רוקח Travels to ג'ירונא on a Cloud

A Heavenly Dream on ערב פסח

On ערב פסח, after completing בדיקת חמץ, רבי אלעזר בן יהודה מוורמייזא — the רוקח — went to sleep and had a remarkable חלום. In the dream he heard a קול from שמים asking: who is willing to go teach the רמב״ן in ספרד קבלה?

The רמב״ן had numerous questions in הלכה, in תורה, in נגלה — questions whose answers required the mystical dimension of תורה. Clearly in שמים they felt it essential for the רמב״ן to know קבלה, as we see reflected throughout his ספרים and חיבורים. The רוקח immediately volunteered: I will go. No problem. I will do it.

But there was a problem — it was ערב פסח. How would he get to ג'ירונא in time?

The Crisis in ג'ירונא

And there was a second sorrow in ג'ירונא at that time. The governor — a שונא ישראל throughout his career — found every excuse to harm the קהילה. Life under his oppression was extraordinarily difficult for the רמב״ן and the קהילה. The רוקח understood that someone needed to address this threat too. But he required one condition: permission from שמים to use קבלה מעשית[17] — the practical application of קבלה through שמות הקדושים to alter the natural order.

The Journey on a Cloud

The רוקח received the permission. He woke on ערב פסח — having already baked מצות (the רוקח was extremely מקפיד to bake מצות himself) — and said a שם. He performed what he calls in his ספרים a קפיצת הדרך[18], invoking the principle that כלל ישראל left מצרים סרוחים בסיני — meaning miraculously, the animals carrying everything without anyone needing to provide for himself. After saying the שם, a cloud descended from heaven. He stepped onto the cloud and traveled to ג'ירונא, landing ערב יום טוב.

The Seder With the רמב״ן

The רוקח arrived at the shul of the רמב״ן. After davening, the רמב״ן — at least forty years younger — noticed this guest who appeared to be a חסיד. The רוקח had just arrived from אשכנז; the רמב״ן had never heard of him. The רמב״ן invited him home for the seder.

At the seder, surrounded by תלמידי חכמים, the רוקח sat quietly in the corner — being מכבד שמים but not participating. The רמב״ן repeatedly invited him into the discussions, but he stayed silent. At the end of the seder the רמב״ן asked: who are you? What is this about?

The רוקח answered: I am here for a reason, but I cannot tell you right now. However, I would like to speak in shul tomorrow. The רמב״ן was hesitant but agreed to consider it.

Warning and Danger

Before going to sleep, the רמב״ן warned his guest: tomorrow morning when you wake, do not walk to shul on your own. We live in ג'ירונא; this is a ghetto. Any yid who walks outside the designated quarter will be killed by the governor's order. That year, פסח coincided with Easter. If a yid was caught outside the ghetto during their celebrations, he would be burned at the stake — a practice that existed in ספרד long before the Inquisition under Ferdinand and Isabella ימ״ש.

The רוקח's Plan

The next morning the רמב״ן woke to find his guest gone. The רוקח had left early, intentionally walking outside the ghetto — waiting to be caught. A policeman arrested him and brought him before the governor, who excitedly ordered: set up the stake right where the yidden must pass after leaving shul. Let this be their פסח — they will watch a yid burn alive.

The Miracle at the Stake

When the רמב״ן heard, on his way to shul, he was devastated. His guest was about to be burned — and worse, this would bring further libels on the entire קהילה on יום טוב. After davening, the קהילה passed by hundreds of Christians eagerly awaiting the execution. The רמב״ן's eyes locked with the רוקח's. The רוקח said calmly: do not worry, רבי. I will be at your home for קידוש. Wait for me. Do not make קידוש without me.

At the very last moment before being thrown into the fire, the רוקח said a שם. Using שמות הקדש, he switched himself with the governor. Within seconds, the governor was the one about to be burned, while the רוקח sat on the throne. The governor screamed, "I am the governor! What are you doing?" — but the executioners assumed it was simply a yid trying to save himself at the last instant. They threw him into the fire, and within minutes he was reduced to ash. The רוקח then arrived at the רמב״ן's home for קידוש.

Teaching קבלה to the רמב״ן

The רמב״ן was stunned. He understood immediately: this is no ordinary yid. The רוקח explained his mission: I was sent to answer your questions, and to explain these matters through תורת הקבלה.

That afternoon the entire קהילה of ג'ירונא gathered. The רוקח began his שיעור by listing an extraordinarily difficult set of questions — and every single one had been troubling the רמב״ן for years without resolution. After an hour of questions, the רוקח declared: to answer these through נגלה alone is impossible. Only if a yid knows קבלה — תורה שבעל פה and תורת הנסתר — can these questions be answered. He then delivered an extensive דרשה on קבלה. For the first time, the רמב״ן truly understood what קבלה was. From that moment on the רמב״ן became the greatest supporter and champion of קבלה in ספרד.

The רוקח remained in ג'ירונא for thirty days, teaching the רמב״ן קבלה, before returning to וורמייזא.

Epilogue — The Governor's Disappearance

The governor of ג'ירונא mysteriously disappeared that day. The authorities searched extensively and never discovered what happened to him. We know the truth: he died in the fire. The decree against the yidden was immediately nullified, and the קהילה of ג'ירונא was freed.

XIII. The רוקח's Personal Tragedy

The רוקח documented much of his own life in his writings[19]. He records that on י״ב כסלו, before חנוכה, while learning the פסוק וישב יעקב בטח — ironically "Yaakov dwelt securely" — two non-yidden burst into his home. They murdered his wife Dulcea and his daughters. He describes how his thirteen-year-old daughter Belat and his six-year-old daughter Hannah were killed before his eyes. He and his son survived only because they managed to close and lock a door. The רוקח writes that he screamed to הקדוש ברוך הוא, and הקדוש ברוך הוא answered — something happened to stop the murderers. One of them was caught and killed a week later.

This was not during the Crusades; it remains a historical mystery what prompted the attack. The רוקח lost everything that day and fell into a deep depression. It was during this period of mourning that he began writing his חידושים על התורה, which we have today.

XIV. שימושא דשדין דאויר and the Length of והוא רחום

The ability to travel via cloud is not fantasy — it is called שימושא דשדין דאויר in קבלה, a specific practice involving the mystical "use of the air." It is an entire chapter within קבלה literature.

The רוקח is the original source for many קבלה practices. He established the מנהג of davening מעריב with prolonged devotion — in particular saying והוא רחום at length. He taught it to his רבנים and תלמידים, and the tradition is that the מנהג spread throughout כלל ישראל because of him. Since the רוקח was able to fly to ג'ירונא to teach the רמב״ן קבלה, surely he understood what occurs in שמים — that the gates of גן עדן open only after מעריב. So the next time you daven מעריב, take your time with והוא רחום.

סיכום

The opening of our weekday מעריב — ורחם, ברכו, the יוד-כא-ואו-כא name, the slow מתון of והוא רחום — is shaped by hands we may not realize. The רוקח of Worms wrote some of it. The אריז״ל transmitted some of it. רב האי גאון promised a successful week to a yid who answers ברכו at night. רב חיים ויטאל recorded that on a single ערב פסח, a קול from שמים asked who would teach the רמב״ן קבלה — and the רוקח said I will go, and changed the course of how Spain learned תורה. The story is not in the שלחן ערוך. It is part of our family history. Take a few extra seconds with והוא רחום this week.

Founder of the ראפשיץ dynasty (which produced צאנז, Sanz-Klausenberg, Bobov, and others). His resolution of the טור's מדרש — that גהינום's fire intensifies at night and ורחם is a plea for mercy as the night begins — is widely cited in חסידי commentary on סדור. ↩
  3. אבודרהם on סדר תפילת ערבית. — ר' דוד אבודרהם (Seville, mid-14th century), the foundational commentary on the סדור. He explains ורחם as compensation for the absence of a nighttime קרבן: where the תמיד would have provided כפרה, we now use תפילה with this opening plea for רחמים. ↩
  4. שער הכוונות, ר' חיים ויטאל — שם יאב"ו (יוד-כא-ואו-כא) before ברכו. — The אריז"ל's כוונות for the transition from ורחם to ברכו include the יחוד of three names — אדנ-י (65), הוי"ה in its expanded spelling (26), and אקי-ה (21) — totaling 112. Recorded by ר' חיים ויטאל in שער הכוונות on ערבית. The שם appears in נוסח ספרד and נוסח האר"י סדורים; not in standard נוסח אשכנז. ↩
  5. שמות ל"ה:ג' — "לא תבערו אש בכל מושבותיכם ביום השבת". — The פסוק in פרשת ויקהל: the prohibition of kindling fire on שבת. The ראשונים read "בכל מושבותיכם" expansively: not only in dwellings on earth but in any dwelling — including the realm of גהינום above. חז"ל (סנהדרין ס"ה ע"ב) develop the principle that גהינום ceases on שבת, which underlies the omission of ורחם on Friday night. ↩
  6. נועם מגדים, ר' אליעזר הורוויץ (תלמיד המגיד ממעזריטש, late 18th century). — נועם מגדים on the תורה and on תפילה, by a close תלמיד of the מגיד ממעזריטש. He raises the philosophical question on the ראשונים's explanation: if reciting ורחם would extend רחמים to רשעים in גהינום, why omit it on שבת — wouldn't we want to extend their relief? The שיעור leaves the question open as a meaningful kabbalistic-philosophical tension within the מנהג. ↩
  7. מטה משה, ר' משה מאט (Przemyśl, 1551–1606). — ר' משה מאט, תלמיד of ר' שלמה לוריא (the מהרש"ל) and the רמ"א. מטה משה (Krakow 1591) is a major halachic-liturgical compendium covering סדר תפילה and the year cycle. The unique מדרש on the מלאך החייל, with אמת illuminating his forehead at sunrise and אמונה at sunset, is associated with him; the שיעור flags it as an isolated source. ↩
  8. מגילה כ"ג ע"ב; ברכות כ"א ע"ב; שלחן ערוך, או"ח נ"ה. — ברכו as a דבר שבקדושה requiring a מנין of ten. The סוגיא in מגילה establishes the principle that public sanctification of השם requires a quorum; או"ח נ"ה codifies the rules for ברכו, קדיש, and קדושה. ↩
  9. משנה ברורה, או"ח נ"ז ס"ק ב'; או"ח נ"ד ס"ק י"ג. — The משנה ברורה's warning about the prevalent failure of בני הציבור to refrain from speech after answering ברכו: "רבים נכשלים בזה". The principle follows the משנה in ברכות פ"ב מ"א: once ברכות קר"ש begin, even the supreme תורה study yields to the requirement of completing without הפסק. ↩
  10. משנה ברכות פ"ב מ"א. — "היה קורא בתורה והגיע זמן הקריאה — אם כיוון לבו יצא, ואם לאו לא יצא; בין הפרקים שואל מפני הכבוד ומשיב, ובאמצע שואל מפני היראה ומשיב, דברי רבי מאיר". The משנה sets the framework for permissible interruption during קר"ש and its ברכות: very limited even between paragraphs, more so within them. Foundational source for the משנה ברורה's warning on speech after ברכו. ↩
  11. ב"ח, או"ח רל"ב; cf. או"ח מ"ז. — The ב"ח (ר' יואל סירקיש, 1561–1640) connects ברכו at ערבית to the parallel function of ברכו at שחרית. Just as the ברכת יוצר אור can serve as ברכת התורה for one who forgot it, so the parallel ברכת המעריב ערבים serves the same function at night — and both are introduced by ברכו. ↩
  12. חיד"א citing רב האי גאון; cf. מורה באצבע; קשר גודל. — The חיד"א's pre-davening guides include the קבלה from רב האי גאון that one who answers ברכו at מעריב with כוונה is granted הצלחה for the week. רב האי גאון (Pumbedita, 939–1038), the last of the great גאוני בבל, is here cited as the conduit for an even older oral tradition. The סגולה is widely cited in חסידי and ספרדי liturgical literature. ↩
  13. ספר הרוקח, ר' אלעזר בן יהודה מוורמייזא (c. 1176–1238). — Foundational ראשון of the חסידי אשכנז movement, ר' אלעזר served as רב in וורמייזא (Worms). Author of ספר הרוקח, ספר השם, פירוש על התורה, פירוש על התפילה, and many פיוטים still recited today. The numerical value רוקח = אלעזר = 308 explains the title. He was a major source for Ashkenazic מנהגי תפילה; the שלחן ערוך cites him repeatedly. ↩
  14. ספר חסידים, ר' יהודה החסיד (Speyer/Regensburg, c. 1150–1217). — ר' יהודה החסיד, central figure of חסידי אשכנז, son of ר' שמואל החסיד and רבי of the רוקח. ספר חסידים is the foundational ethical-pietistic compendium of medieval Ashkenazic חסידות (a movement distinct from later 18th-century Eastern European חסידות). His צוואה contains many widely observed practices in אשכנז. ↩
  15. שער הגלגולים, ר' חיים ויטאל (Tzfat/Damascus, 1542–1620). — ר' חיים ויטאל, principal תלמיד of the אריז"ל and the conduit of nearly all of the אריז"ל's teaching. שער הגלגולים is part of the שמונה שערים, his magnum opus reconstructing the אריז"ל's קבלה. The story of the רוקח's journey to ג'ירונא to teach the רמב"ן קבלה is preserved in his writings; verify exact location in printed editions of שער הגלגולים or the parallel collections. ↩
  16. מעשה נסים, ר' יוזפא שמש מוורמייזא (1604–1678).
    Note: מעשה נסים by ר' יוסף יוזפא הלוי (the Worms שמש) records 25 stories of the רבנים of Worms, including the רוקח's journey to ג'ירונא. Originally published in Old Yiddish (Amsterdam 1696, posthumously by his son), which limited its accessibility. Modern Hebrew editions exist; verify exact citation. ר' יוזפא's other works — מנהגי וורמייזא — are foundational to Ashkenazic-Worms מנהגים. The שיעור attributes the story's preservation in our מסורה to מעשה נסים.
    ↩
  17. קבלה מעשית in the writings of the חסידי אשכנז. — The חסידי אשכנז developed an extensive tradition of קבלה מעשית (practical קבלה) — the use of divine שמות to alter the natural order in moments of need. The רוקח's ספר השם is a major source. Mainstream פוסקים warn that practical קבלה is dangerous and forbidden without explicit heavenly permission; the רוקח's use of it in the ג'ירונא story is depicted as conditional on such permission. ↩
  18. קפיצת הדרך — סנהדרין צ"ה ע"א; בראשית רבה on פרשת חיי שרה. — קפיצת הדרך ("the leaping of the way"), the miraculous compression of distance attested in חז"ל for אליעזר עבד אברהם traveling to ארם נהריים, for יעקב to בית אל, and for several other figures. The חסידי אשכנז incorporated it as a קבלה מעשית practice. The רוקח's journey on a cloud to ג'ירונא is described as an instance of this principle. Verify exact references in סנהדרין צ"ה ע"א and parallel מדרשים. ↩
  19. קינות הרוקח; פירוש הרוקח על התפילה; preface to ספר הרוקח. — The רוקח's autobiographical writings record the violent attack of י"ב כסלו תתקנ״ז (December 1196) in which his wife Dulcia and his daughters Belette (Belat) and Hannah were murdered in their home in Worms. He composed קינות for them and wrote the introduction to ספר הרוקח in this period of mourning. Modern critical editions of his works (especially Klar's Sefer HaRokeach) preserve these autobiographical passages and their dating; the attack remains a significant data point in 12th-century Ashkenazic-Jewish history independent of the Crusade chronicles. ↩
