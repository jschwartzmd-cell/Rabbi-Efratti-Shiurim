Mizmor L’Todah, Holding the Tzitzis, Yehi Ch'vod, and Ashrei
Shiur of Rabbi Ephrati — Sunday Morning Series on Tefillah
Recorded 18 August 2024 · Reworked 30 April 2026 · Mareh Mekomos upgraded 5 May 2026

Sponsored by Boruch Mandel l'iluy nishmas his mother Esther bas Ra, on her yahrtzeit. Today completes Mizmor L'Todah, addresses the widespread minhag of holding the tzitzis at Baruch She'amar (and the surprising opposition to it), and concludes with Yehi Ch'vod and the structure of Ashrei.

I. Mizmor L'Todah — The Korban That Will Never Be Nullified

The Mechaber's Striking Citation

The Shulchan Aruch[1] rules that Mizmor L'Todah should be recited "b'zimrah, b'ne'imah, b'niggun" — with song, sweet melody, and tune. The Mechaber uncharacteristically cites a Midrash[2] as the underlying reason: l'asid lavo, all tefillos and all korbanos will be nullified — except for korban todah.

This statement is jarring on its face. We pray daily for the return of the avodah and the korbanos; what does it mean that they will be "nullified"? The Rishonim resolve it: the midrash refers specifically to chatas and asham — sin-offerings. In the era of Mashiach, Klal Yisrael will not sin, and so the offerings that atone for sin will become unnecessary. The Rambam, the Radak, and others differ on the precise scope (the Radak, for instance, distinguishes between chatas and asham; there are nuances regarding shogeg, yodea vs. lo yodea) — but the central point is firm: the korban todah is eternal, because hakaras hatov is eternal.

Hence Mizmor L'Todah stands out from the rest of Pesukei d'Zimra and is the one mizmor the Mechaber rules should be sung.

Rashi's Siddur — Moshe Rabbeinu as Author

In his siddur, Rashi[3] records a tradition that Moshe Rabbeinu was the original author of Mizmor L'Todah. We know from Daf Yomi recently that not every kapitel in Tehillim was composed by David HaMelech — David HaMelech edited and incorporated earlier compositions. According to Rashi's siddur, Mizmor L'Todah was composed by Moshe Rabbeinu for the korban todah as offered in the Mishkan.

Why It Sits Where It Does

In Nusach Ashkenaz, Hodu concludes with "Ashirah la'Hashem ki gamal alai" — a personal expression of gratitude — which segues directly into Mizmor L'Todah, the formal song of thanksgiving. The structural logic is tight.

Standing or Sitting

The siddurim show a machloikis on standing for Mizmor L'Todah. Many record "minhag ha'olam" to stand, with parenthetical notes that it depends on local minhag. Practically, those who follow Nusach Sefard and stand for Baruch She'amar typically remain standing. Nusach Ari has its own framework — some sources direct one to sit, despite the surrounding minhag.

II. The Minhag of Holding the Front Tzitzis at Baruch She'amar

The Question

Every morning, throughout Klal Yisrael, when one reaches Baruch She'amar, one takes the two front tzitzis of the tallis gadol in hand, holds them through the brachah, kisses them at "malchus u'memshalah", and lays them down. At kriyas Shema, one gathers all four. Why? Most Yidden do this from first grade onward without ever being told a reason.

The Magen Avraham — Kabbalistic Origin

The Magen Avraham[4] records the minhag and cites "ha'kesavim" — a code-phrase that, when used unattributed by Acharonim, refers to the Kisvei HaArizal as transmitted by Rav Chaim Vital.[5] The source is, in other words, Kabbalah. The precise terminology — oros, partzufim, the relationship between the front and back of the tallis — is technical and well beyond the nigleh. Most Yidden do this minhag following the Magen Avraham's ruling without engaging the underlying sod.

Rimzei Elul — A 19th-Century Rabbi's Effort

An obscure 19th-century sefer called Rimzei Elul[6] records the author's surprise: he asked the gedolim, tzaddikim, and rabbonim of his generation why we hold the tzitzis at Baruch She'amar. None of them could give him a clear reason. He decided to construct his own remazim — symbolic explanations — even though they are admittedly his own creation rather than received mesorah.

The Aderes's Sharp Dissent

One of the towering gedolim of late-19th-century Lithuania — Rav Eliyahu Dovid Rabinovitz-Teumim, the Aderes[7] — went further. He writes openly: "I have no idea what kissing and holding the front tzitzis has to do with Baruch She'amar, and therefore I do not do it."

Aderes — Biographical Detail

The Aderes served as Rav of Mir and Rav of Ponivezh, among other major positions. He had a twin brother, Rav Tzvi Yehudah Teumim (sometimes Tu'umim — the surname itself means "twins").

His daughter married the future Chief Rabbi of Eretz Yisrael, Rav Avraham Yitzchak HaKohen Kook. (At that point, Rav Kook was universally recognized as one of the gedolei ha'dor: a talmid of Volozhin, having learned by Rav Chaim Brisker, the Netziv, and others; the later polemics around his role as Chief Rabbi reframed perceptions in some circles, but in his own day his standing was clear.) Three years later, the daughter passed away. The Aderes, unwilling to lose his son-in-law from the family, asked his twin brother to take Rav Kook as his son-in-law — meaning Rav Kook subsequently married the Aderes's niece. Years later, the Aderes's own son became the son-in-law of Rav Kook. The threads are tangled but they speak to the closeness of the family.

Late in life, the Aderes was summoned to Yerushalayim by Rav Shmuel Salant, who was advanced in years and had become blind, to assist in leading the growing kehillah. The Aderes bought an apartment directly opposite Har HaBayis. He left an autobiography — a rarity among Acharonim — in which he describes how every morning he would say Modeh Ani, get out of bed, look out at Har HaBayis, and weep over the galus of the Shechinah. He felt the daily anguish was shortening his life. He was indeed niftar only four years after arriving — predeceasing Rav Shmuel Salant.

Despite his short tenure in Yerushalayim, the Aderes wrote over 120 sefarim, of which only two were published in his lifetime; manuscripts continue to be edited and released to this day, including by Rav Yankov Vilel of Eretz Yisrael.

The Vilna Gaon's Concern of Hefsek

The Aderes further reports that the Vilna Gaon did not hold the tzitzis either — neither at Baruch She'amar nor at kriyas Shema.[8] The Gaon's halachic concern is structural: Baruch She'amar is itself a brachah; the entire stretch from Baruch She'amar through Yishtabach is bracketed in a single brachah-frame. Pausing to handle and kiss the tzitzis mid-brachah potentially constitutes hefsek — an interruption between the brachah and the mitzvah of saying Pesukei d'Zimra.

This same concern of hefsek shapes the Gaon's position on kissing tefillin, kissing the mezuzah, and similar practices that the Geonim already questioned twelve centuries ago: "Why do we kiss the tefillin at Shema? Nobody runs to kiss the mezuzah when we mention it!" Communities that follow the Gaon refrain from these handlings.

How the Minhag Spread — The Kitzur Shulchan Aruch

The popularity of holding the tzitzis at Baruch She'amar in mainstream Ashkenazic kehillos owes a great deal to Rav Shlomo Ganzfried (early-mid 1800s), Rav of Ungvar (Hungary), and author of the Kitzur Shulchan Aruch.[9] Before the Mishnah Berurah, the Kitzur was the most popular halachic sefer in Klal Yisrael. Its short, lucid, accessible style made it the standard text for daily-halacha learning in shuls and the central sefer taught to children in cheder. Rav Ganzfried codifies holding the front tzitzis — and through the wide reach of his sefer, the minhag became universal.

(Rav Ganzfried also wrote Kesef HaSofer[10] on hilchos stam — sealed by the Chasam Sofer's declaration: "Any sofer who has not learned Kesef HaSofer, I will not accept his mezuzos or tefillin.")

Or Makif — A Kabbalistic Frame

One kabbalistic frame: there are two kinds of spiritual light around a person — or pnimi (interior light) and or makif (encompassing light). The tallis gadol uniquely embodies the or makif; only a few mitzvos envelop the entire body — sukkah, mikveh, the tallis when worn properly, and possibly yishuv Eretz Yisrael. The mekubalim describe the or makif as unfolding in stages: at Baruch She'amar, the encompassing light "faces forward" — hence we hold the front two tzitzis. By kriyas Shema, the light has fully enveloped — hence we gather all four.

A Pshat-Based Origin

The Rav of the Rimzei Elul proposed a pshat-based explanation that resonates more naturally for those of us not steeped in Kabbalah:

In the era of the Tannaim, daily davening began at Baruch She'amar — there were no korbanos additions, no Hodu, no Mizmor Shir Chanukas HaBayis, no Hashem Melech. The tefillah opened with Baruch She'amar directly. The Mishnah at the start of Berachos identifies the zman for kriyas Shema as "mi'sheyakir bein techeles l'lavan" — when there is enough light to distinguish the blue techeles thread of tzitzis from the white. There were no clocks, no calendars, no gabbai announcing z'man. So as Yidden began Baruch She'amar in the predawn light, they would literally take out their front tzitzis and check whether the techeles (or, in our era, the contrast between threads) was visible — to know whether kriyas Shema's zman had arrived.

Today we have clocks and printed zmanim, but the minhag of taking the front two tzitzis at Baruch She'amar survives as a living memory of that ancient practical check. This pshat explains the holding; the kissing is more naturally rooted in Kabbalah and chavivus ha'mitzvah.

Ten Knots — Ten Beruchin

Another remez from the mekubalim: when one holds the two front tzitzis together, the visible knots total ten (five and five). This corresponds to the ten Baruchin in Baruch She'amar, which themselves correspond to the Asarah Ma'amaros by which HaKadosh Baruch Hu created the world.

III. Yehi Ch'vod — Twenty-One Names of Hashem

After Baruch She'amar, we recite Yehi Ch'vod Hashem L'olam — a collage of pesukim drawn from Tehillim, Mishlei, Divrei HaYamim, and possibly Iyov. There is no single kapitel with this opening; it is a deliberate composition.

Ancient Pedigree

Yehi Ch'vod appears in Maseches Sofrim[11] (the braisa appended to Maseches Avodah Zarah); in the siddurim of Rav Amram Gaon and Rav Saadia Gaon; and in the Rambam. It is among the older fixtures of our davening.

Twenty-One Names of Hashem

The Rishonim note that Yehi Ch'vod contains 21 mentions of the Name of Hashem, paralleling the 21 pesukim of Ashrei that follows. Yehi Ch'vod is, in effect, an introduction to Ashrei.

The Levush's Counting Problem

Counting carefully, however, yields only nineteen mentions, not 21. The Levush[12] raised this very kashya: where are the missing two? He resolves it: the form "Kah" (י־ה) — the first two letters of the Sheim Havayah — is itself counted by the Rema[13] as a Name min ha'Torah. That accounts for the twentieth. The twenty-first comes from the pasuk "Yismechu ha'shamayim v'sageil ha'aretz": the final letters of shamayim and the first letters of tageil form, when read across the word break, the Sheim Havayah embedded between contiguous words.

IV. Ashrei — Acrostic and Memory

The Gemara's Promise

The Gemara[14] states a striking promise: "Whoever recites Tehillah l'David three times daily — he is assured a portion in olam ha'ba." Such language is rare. Why is Ashrei so unique?

The Gemara's answer combines two features:

  1. Its aleph-beis acrostic structure (one pasuk per letter, with nun omitted for reasons we will discuss).
  2. The pasuk "Posei'ach es yadecha u'masbi'a l'chol chai ratzon" — Hashem provides every living creature with what it needs.

Other kapitlach have one or the other. Ashrei is the only one with both; that is what earns the dual acclaim.

The Sefer HaEshkol's Chiddush

The Sefer HaEshkol — by Rav Avraham ben Yitzchak Av Beis Din of Provence (the second of three Rishonim called Ravad; not the Ravad of Posquières who debated the Rambam)[15] — offers a remarkable chiddush. He explains that David HaMelech composed certain kapitlach in acrostic form for one practical reason: to make them memorizable. In the era before printed sefarim, every Jew needed to know Ashrei by heart; the alphabetic structure was a mnemonic scaffold.

The Eshkol then extends the principle to Megillas Eichah: Yirmiyahu HaNavi composed Eichah in aleph-beis structure (the four chapters use the aleph-beis; chapter three triples it) for the very same reason — so that every Jew, throughout the long galus, would be able to memorize Eichah. Just as the Jewish people needed to know Ashrei by heart for daily tefillah, they needed to know Eichah by heart so that the reasons for galus — and the call to teshuvah — would never be forgotten.

The Vilna Gaon and Memorization as Avodah

Rav Zalman of Volozhin, talmid of the Vilna Gaon,[16] said of his rebbe: "My rebbe knew the entire Torah the way we know Ashrei." He could recite Torah forward and backward; the entire corpus sat in his mind with the same easy fluency that Ashrei sits in ours. (Try saying Ashrei backward — without breaking — and you understand the order of magnitude.)

Coming Late — Halachic Priorities

If one arrives late to davening: l'chatchila, of course, arrive on time. B'di'eved, the Acharonim follow the Geonim's ruling that the core of Pesukei d'Zimra is Baruch She'amar — Ashrei — Yishtabach. Rashi, by contrast, held the core was the Halleluyos at the end. We paskin like the Geonim: if pressed for time to align with the tzibbur, say at minimum Baruch She'amar → Ashrei → Yishtabach; the rest of Pesukei d'Zimra can be completed afterward without the brachos.

V. Sefer HaEshkol — A Question of Authenticity

The Eshkol's insight on memorization is precious — but the sefer itself sits at the center of one of the most consequential forgery controversies in our literature.

The Halberstadt Edition

In the mid-1800s, a respected rav in Halberstadt (Germany) named Rav Tzvi Binyamin Auerbach announced that he had located a long-lost manuscript of the original Sefer HaEshkol. He published it — adding to the original kernel roughly three quarters more material that had not appeared in any prior edition.

When pressed for the manuscript's provenance, Rav Auerbach explained that he had copied it from the personal manuscript of a Jew in his town. Asked to produce the original, Rav Auerbach replied that the man had since been niftar — and his will had instructed that all his writings be buried with him. By a tragic mistake, the family had buried this manuscript along with the rest. There was no surviving original to verify against.

Professor Albeck's Investigation

Professor Shmuel Albeck (father of Chanoch Albeck)[17] took up the question and demonstrated, on textual grounds, that large portions of the Halberstadt edition could not be authentic. His critique parallels in its substance the linguistic critiques offered by the Yaavetz against parts of the Zohar centuries earlier.

The result is uncomfortable: substantial portions of the Eshkol as we have it — including passages quoted by the Mishnah Berurah — are likely later forgeries. There is an authentic core Eshkol, cited by the Beis Yosef and the Tur; but the expanded Halberstadt material requires careful skepticism.

The Phenomenon of Forgery

The Eshkol is not unique. The most famous case in modern times is the spurious Yerushalmi on Kodshim[18] — produced by a forger of remarkable Talmudic capacity who fabricated an entire masechta in Yerushalmi style on Kodshim (a seder for which no Yerushalmi exists), traveled through the major European batei midrash showing his "manuscript," and convinced most of the olam. Among the few gedolim who saw through the forgery were the Maharsham and the Rogatchover Gaon, whose unique sensitivity to textual style and internal logic exposed it.

The lesson: even a forger must possess deep talmid chacham capacity to convince other talmidei chachamim. And we, in turn, must approach "newly discovered" manuscripts with discernment, fidelity to mesorah, and humility before the structures our gedolim have already vetted.

VI. Synthesis

The tefillah we say between Baruch She'amar and Yishtabach is layered: a brachah that fell from heaven, a song of korban todah that will never be nullified, a minhag of tzitzis-handling that may originate in pre-dawn tzitzis-checking and now lives as Kabbalistic framing, an introductory tefillah with twenty-one Names of Hashem matching the twenty-one pesukim of Ashrei that follows, and an acrostic kapitel shaped for memorization.

And alongside, the mesorah includes its own internal critical apparatus: dissents like the Aderes and the Vilna Gaon, forgery exposures like Albeck's, the careful preservation of authentic core texts. Mesorah is not credulity; it is vetted faithfulness.
