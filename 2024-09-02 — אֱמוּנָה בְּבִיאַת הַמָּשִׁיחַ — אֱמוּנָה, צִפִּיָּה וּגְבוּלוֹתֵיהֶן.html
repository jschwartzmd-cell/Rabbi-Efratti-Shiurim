אֱמוּנָה בְּבִיאַת הַמָּשִׁיחַ — אֱמוּנָה, צִפִּיָּה וּגְבוּלוֹתֵיהֶן

Emunah B’Bias HaMashiach: Belief, Anticipation, and the Limits of Both
Labor Day Legal-Holiday Shiur — Rabbi Ephrati
Recorded 2 September 2024 · Reworked 30 April 2026 · Mareh Mekomos upgraded 5 May 2026

L'iluy nishmas the mother of our dear friend Rav Feige. The shiur was given the morning after the news from Eretz Yisrael of six chatufim being murdered in captivity — at roughly day 330 of the war. Hashem yirachem.

I. Context — Why This Topic, Today

This shiur was not in the original plan. But after yesterday's news — six Yidden, after roughly 330 days of davening for them, found murdered — and as parents this morning are sending their children off to yeshiva ketana with hope and pride for a new Elul, we cannot let the moment pass without addressing the emunah that holds Klal Yisrael together: emunah b'Bias HaMashiach.

The contrast is brutal: in America we beam with pride at the start of a new year of Torah; in Eretz Yisrael, families spent yesterday burying loved ones whose return they had been begging for. Both realities ask the same question of us: do we still believe Mashiach is coming, and does that belief survive intact when it is most tested?

A Note on Sensitivity

Halachah tolerates machloikis; ikrei emunah tolerate it less easily. Asking sharp questions about the foundations of faith is legitimate and necessary — provided we walk away with the underlying confidence intact. The principle: even when we don't have a complete answer, we hold to the mesorah our avos received from Moshe Rabbeinu. With that frame, we can examine the questions honestly.

II. Where Does Ani Ma'amin Come From?

At the end of Shacharis in many siddurim, we recite "Ani ma'amin be'emunah sheleimah" — the thirteen ikrim. There is no list of thirteen in Torah Shebiksav, no list of thirteen in Torah She'baal Peh. So where does it come from?

The Rambam's Hakdamah to Perek Cheilek

The source is the Rambam's Hakdamah to the tenth perek of Sanhedrin (Perek Cheilek) in his Peirush HaMishnayos[1] — the perek that addresses Mashiach, olam ha'ba, and techiyas ha'meisim. There the Rambam formulates the thirteen ikrim, with the twelfth being belief in the coming of Mashiach.

The Rambam writes that he did not arrive at thirteen casually — he was not boyde milibo. He researched the pesukim and ma'amrei Chazal at length until he could distill "all of our emunah into thirteen tenets of faith."

The Popular Text — and 1492

The "Ani Ma'amin" text printed in our siddurim is based on the Rambam but its precise author is unknown. Its first known appearance in a printed siddur is from Portugal in 1492 — the very year of the Spanish Expulsion. Some historians have wondered whether the timing is coincidence; whether or not, the formulation became universally familiar.

III. Two Distinct Obligations — Belief and Anticipation

The Rambam's twelfth ikar contains two separate components, not one:

  1. Belief that Mashiach will come — that there will be a personal Mashiach, descended from David HaMelech through Shevet Yehudah, through Shlomo specifically, who will rebuild the Beis HaMikdash, gather the exiles, and restore the avodah.
  2. Active anticipation — chikui — of his coming. Not just abstract belief; ongoing readiness, alignment, hope, and yearning.

The Rambam writes plainly: a person who is safek on whether Mashiach will come — even just unsure — is a koifer in the Torah, in the Nevi'im, and in HaKadosh Baruch Hu. Denial of any of the specific lineage details — zera Beis David, through Shlomo — is also kefirah. In Hilchos Melachim[2] he codifies the criteria: restoration of malchus Beis David, rebuilding the Beis HaMikdash, kibbutz galuyos, return of all the mishpatim. In Hilchos Teshuvah[3] he counts ha'kofrim b'Vias HaGo'el among those without cheilek l'olam ha'ba.

IV. Sources in Tanach

The Rambam's position is grounded in explicit pesukim:

Yeshayahu 11 — The Stem of Yishai

וְיָצָא חֹטֶר מִגֵּזַע יִשַׁי, וְנֵצֶר מִשָּׁרָשָׁיו יִפְרֶה. וְנָחָה עָלָיו רוּחַ ה'...
"A shoot shall come forth from the stump of Yishai... the spirit of Hashem shall rest upon him..."[4]

An explicit prophecy of a descendant of Yishai (i.e., David, then Shlomo) who will execute justice and bring universal peace. The haftarah read on the seventh day of Pesach in Eretz Yisrael is precisely this prophecy — and the Chief Rabbinate of 1948 enacted that it be read on Yom Ha'atzmaut.

Malachi 3:1 — The Master Enters His Palace

הִנְנִי שֹׁלֵחַ מַלְאָכִי וּפִנָּה דֶרֶךְ לְפָנָי, וּפִתְאֹם יָבוֹא אֶל הֵיכָלוֹ הָאָדוֹן.
"Behold, I send My messenger, and he shall clear the way before Me; and suddenly the master shall come to his palace."[5]

The mefarshim identify "ha'adon" here not as HaKadosh Baruch Hu Himself but as a designated person — angelic or human — who will redeem.

Chavakuk 2:3 — The Source of Anticipation

כִּי עוֹד חָזוֹן לַמּוֹעֵד, וְיָפֵחַ לַקֵּץ וְלֹא יְכַזֵּב; אִם יִתְמַהְמָהּ חַכֵּה לוֹ, כִּי בֹא יָבוֹא לֹא יְאַחֵר.
"For there is still a vision for the appointed time... if it tarries, await it, for it will surely come."[6]

This is the pasuk that explicitly commands chikui — to await even when the coming seems delayed. This is the source for the second component of the Rambam's ikar.

Bilam — A Star from Yaakov

And in Parshas Balak:[7] "Darach kochav mi'Yaakov v'kam shevet mi'Yisrael" — a star will emerge from Yaakov, a scepter rise from Israel. Chazal read this as referring to Mashiach (and famously, Rabbi Akiva applied it briefly to Bar Kochba before withdrawing the application).

V. Disagreement Among Rishonim — Are There Only Thirteen?

The Rambam's list was influential but not universally accepted:

  • Rav Saadia Gaon, in Emunos v'Dei'os,[8] presents ten principles.
  • Rabbi Yosef Albo, in Sefer HaIkarim,[9] reduces them to three core principles, with the others derived from them.
  • Other Rishonim have other counts — six, eight, ten.

Most disagreements are categorical rather than substantive: one principle in one system encompasses two or three in another. Klal Yisrael in practice accepted the Rambam's thirteen as the working list, especially regarding emunah b'Mashiach.

The Maharshal and the Mukubalim

Not all gedolim recited Ani Ma'amin or Yigdal daily. The Maharshal[10] objected on principle: who gave the Rambam the right to choose thirteen as the ikrim? Every detail of the Torah is essential; we have no authority to designate a hierarchy of importance. The Arizal,[11] similarly, did not say Yigdal (which is a poetic version of the thirteen ikrim attributed to Immanuel of Rome[12]). Many siddurim note: "Ha'Arizal lo amar Yigdal". Many Hassidic communities, following the Arizal, do not say it.

This dissent is liturgical, not doctrinal — none of these gedolim denied the underlying beliefs; they questioned whether to ritualize them in fixed daily formulae.

VI. The Marc Shapiro Controversy

Dr. Marc B. Shapiro, an academically trained scholar (Harvard-trained, currently a professor), published in 2003 a book titled The Limits of Orthodox Theology: Maimonides' Thirteen Principles Reappraised.[13] The book exhaustively catalogs Rishonim and Acharonim who disagreed with one or another of the Rambam's thirteen.

The book caused an uproar in the yeshivish world. Shapiro's argument — that the thirteen are not in fact universally accepted in Klal Yisrael's entire literary mesorah, and therefore one is not necessarily a koifer for failing to accept any one of them — was read by his critics as a license to relax the framework altogether.

The Rebbe's stance: Shapiro has the right to write what he writes; the academic mapping is real. But for those of us who locate ourselves in the ultra-Orthodox mesorah, our rabbeim, yeshivos, and manhigim have cleaved to the Rambam's framework — particularly on the twelfth ikar. That is the working halachic reality of our community, even if the broader Rishon literature is more diverse than the popular telling allows.

VII. The Difficult Gemara — Rabbi Hillel

The Gemara in Sanhedrin[14] records something startling. One of the Amoraim, Rabbi Hillel (one of fourteen Hillels in the Talmud; likely a grandson of Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi), said:

אֵין מָשִׁיחַ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁכְּבָר אֲכָלוּהוּ בִּימֵי חִזְקִיָּהוּ.
"There is no Mashiach for Israel, for they have already 'consumed' him in the days of Chizkiyahu."

An Amora denying Mashiach. The Rishonim approach this Gemara with trepidation.

Rashi's Reading

Rashi[15] clarifies that Rabbi Hillel did not deny geulah; he denied a personal Mashiach. He held that HaKadosh Baruch Hu would bring redemption directly, without an intermediary melech ha'mashiach riding a donkey. The Gemara immediately responds: "Amar Rav Yosef: shari leih marei l'Hillel" — May his Master forgive Hillel; he made an interpretive mistake.

Why This Matters

If a recognized Amora held this view and was simply mistaken, then the Rambam's classification of kefirah for those who deny Mashiach can't mean Rabbi Hillel was a koifer. The resolution: once Chazal rejected Rabbi Hillel's view and paskened that there will be a personal Mashiach, denying that today is kefirah regardless of original interpretive uncertainty. Rabbi Hillel pre-dates the psak; we live after it.

VIII. The Spectrum of Anticipation — Brisk vs. Chofetz Chaim

The Rambam's requirement of chikui — active awaiting — admits two main readings, with great poskim on each side.

The Brisker Position — Rav Moshe Feinstein and the Brisker Rav

Rav Moshe Feinstein, in a teshuvah in Igros Moshe,[16] takes a striking position. Not only must one believe maybe Mashiach will come today — that is too weak. The Rambam's true requirement, says Rav Moshe, is that one must believe certainly Mashiach will come today; if not today, then certainly tomorrow; if not tomorrow, then certainly the day after. Anything less than positive daily certainty falls short of chikui.

The Brisker Rav[17] takes a similar maximalist line. Posed with the obvious kashya — how can one believe Mashiach will certainly come today, when the Gemara states he does not come on Erev Shabbos, and the Shaagas Aryeh shows the machloikis over whether he comes in Nisan or Tishrei (i.e., only two months a year)? — the Brisker Rav famously replied: "When Mashiach comes, I'll answer that question too."

His tachlis: the siddur says "b'chol yom achakeh lo she'yavo" — every day I await him. That is the emunah. The various Gemara's describing situations where Mashiach won't come are puzzles to be resolved later; they do not erode the daily chikui.

The Brisker Rav on Daily Cheishbon

The Brisker Rav extended the principle further. The brachah in Shemoneh Esrei reads "...ki l'yeshuasecha kivinu kol ha'yom". Not only every day — but the entire day. Constant, moment-to-moment expectancy. Mi zoche — who reaches that madreigah? Few. But that is the standard the Brisker Rav identified.

The Chofetz Chaim's Position

The Chofetz Chaim[18] rejected this maximalism — and the disagreement is significant. The Chofetz Chaim read the Rambam's requirement of chikui not as positive daily certainty but as the absence of yeush: a Jew must never despair, must never conclude "Mashiach is not coming". As long as one's tefillah includes hopeful, sincere yearning for geulah, the ikar is fulfilled. Lacking moment-to-moment certainty is not kefirah; only despairing of the eventual geulah is.

The Pashkevil Against Reb Aharon Leib Shteinman

Roughly seven or eight years before this shiur, Rav Aharon Leib Shteinman publicly transmitted the Chofetz Chaim's position. The extreme chareidi faction in Eretz Yisrael responded with pashkevilim labeling him a koifer in the emunah of Bias HaMashiach.

The Rebbe's reaction: this is precisely the kind of carelessness that misuses the term koifer. To call a gadol ha'dor a koifer for transmitting the Chofetz Chaim's view — when the Chofetz Chaim is the most universally accepted halachic authority of the past century — discredits the accusers, not the accused. There are different opinions on the precise meaning of chikui; an opinion within the spectrum of Acharonim cannot be branded kefirah.

Reb Chaim Brisker's Distinction

That said, the Brisker camp's distinction is real. Reb Chaim Brisker is reported to have said: a Jew who fails to keep halachah due to ignorance is an am ha'aretz, not a koifer. Hashem will forgive him; he can do al cheit on Yom Kippur and learn the halachah for next year. But a Jew who is missing one of the thirteen ikrim — who genuinely doesn't believe in Mashiach, even if "by mistake" — is, in factual reality, a koifer. Emunah is not graded by intent; it is graded by content. The Brisker Rav holds the same. Whether to apply this with full force in practice is exactly the question that divides Brisk from the Chofetz Chaim.

IX. The Halachic Ripples — Acting on Anticipation

If the belief in imminent Mashiach were taken to its full halachic conclusion, life would change in concrete ways. The Gemara in fact debates several such cases — and the rulings reveal that even the Rambam himself does not push the emunah to maximal halachic application.

Kohanim and Wine

The Gemara[19] rules: a kohen who knows his mishmar (the Beis HaMikdash rotated through 24 priestly families, each serving a designated week per year) may not drink wine during his family's week — because if the Beis HaMikdash is suddenly rebuilt that week, he will need to perform the avodah, and the avodah may not be performed under the influence.

If the kohen does not know his mishmar (as virtually all of us do not today), the Gemara holds he should not drink wine at all, all year — for he might be the very kohen needed if Mashiach comes.

Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi rejected this stringency. He could not accept that every kohen must abstain from wine all year on the basis of shema yibaneh ha'Beis HaMikdash. The Rambam himself follows Rabbi: every kohen may drink. The reasoning: if the Beis HaMikdash is suddenly rebuilt, we will find kohanim who happened not to drink that day to perform the avodah; the entire community of kohanim need not abstain.

This is striking. The very Rambam who requires daily chikui as emunah rules halachically that one need not abstain from wine on the assumption of imminent avodah. The emunah is daily; the operational halachah is not. This is a critical signal that the Brisker maximalism, taken to its full halachic conclusion, runs into the Rambam himself.

Haircuts Before Yom Tov

The Shulchan Aruch[20] rules that one takes a haircut erev Yom Tov. Beyond the obvious reason (k'vod ha'yom), some poskim add: shema yibaneh ha'Beis HaMikdash. If Mashiach arrives during Yom Tov, we will run to Eretz Yisrael to bring korbanos, and the halachah forbids entering the Azarah in a menuval (disheveled, untrimmed) state. So we trim before Yom Tov, ready for the call.

Pregnant Women and the Beis HaChaim

There is a widespread minhag that pregnant women do not enter a beis ha'chaim (cemetery). Various reasons are given (concerns of tumah, of slipping, of spiritual sensitivity), but one striking explanation connects to Mashiach:

If we will need a parah adumah (red heifer) once the Beis HaMikdash is rebuilt, we will need kohanim who have never been exposed to tumas meis to draw the mei chatas. Maseches Parah[21] records that in the era of the Beis HaMikdash, women would relocate to a special section of Yerushalayim to give birth. Walkways were built with deliberate gaps between the bridge surface and the ground, so that tumah min ha'meis from any underground burials could not rise through to contaminate the children. From the moment of birth, these children were kept in absolute taharah; at three or four years old they would be the ones who drew the water.

Some poskim apply this reasoning today: a pregnant woman who enters a beis ha'chaim may transfer tumah to her unborn child, and we might need precisely such kohanic children when Mashiach comes. Hence the minhag. (Other poskim find this reasoning attenuated; the minhag stands across communities regardless of the precise rationale.)

The Nazir Whose Vow Begins When Mashiach Comes

A person may take a neder: "On the day Mashiach comes, I will become a nazir." Once vowed, he may never again drink wine — because at any moment Mashiach may come, his nezirus begin, and any wine in his system render him a nazir tamei retroactively. The Gemara rules he may drink wine on Shabbos and Yom Tov — because Mashiach does not come on Shabbos or Yom Tov.

The Shaagas Aryeh[22] raises the obvious challenge: if Mashiach also doesn't come on Shabbos/Yom Tov, and there's a machloikis whether he comes only in Nisan or Tishrei (i.e., possibly only two months a year), what becomes of the emunah that he could come every day? The Brisker Rav's answer — "when Mashiach comes, we'll answer that" — is the only one available within the maximalist framework.

X. The Daily Practice — How to Live This Emunah

What does daily chikui look like in practice, for someone not at the madreigah of the Brisker Rav?

  • Daily consciousness — not abstract belief but real expectation, woven into the day. Ani metzapeh li'shu'ah as a lived disposition.
  • Kavanah in the relevant brachos — "Es tzemach David avdecha m'heirah satzmiach"; "V'lirushalayim ircha b'rachamim tashuv"; "Tek'a b'shofar gadol l'cheiruseinu". These brachos are saturated with chikui; saying them with their meaning is the daily fulfillment.
  • Avoiding yeush — the Chofetz Chaim's baseline. Refusing to despair is itself the ikar; positive moment-to-moment certainty is a middas chassidus.
  • Avoiding date-setting — the Rambam writes "lo yeida adam heiach yih'yu ad she'yih'yu"; speculative timelines are not part of the emunah. The emunah is in that Mashiach will come, not in when.
  • Acting in alignment with geulah values — Torah, mishpat, chesed; preparing the world we are praying to enter.

XI. Closing

Klal Yisrael has carried this emunah for nineteen hundred years through every conceivable tzarah. The first six chatufim buried yesterday in Eretz Yisrael are not the first korbanos, and our refusal to despair on their behalf is the same refusal carried by every generation since the Churban. "Im yismah'meh chakeh lo, ki vo yavo, lo y'acher."

Mashiach will come. The questions of how, when, and in what form are for next time. But the emunah itself — that he is coming, and that we await him — is the foundation that lets us send our children to yeshiva this morning even while six families in Eretz Yisrael are sitting shiva. May HaKadosh Baruch Hu bring geulah sheleimah b'meheirah b'yameinu, amen.

Shulchan Aruch, Orach Chaim 529 (haircut erev Yom Tov); cf. 260 (Erev Shabbos). — Beyond k'vod ha'yom, some poskim add the rationale of shema yibaneh ha'Beis HaMikdash: if Mashiach arrives during Yom Tov, one must run to Eretz Yisrael for the avodah and may not enter the Azarah in a menuval state. A halachic ripple from emunas chikui that does register in operational practice. ↩
  21. Maseches Parah 3; cf. Rambam, Hilchos Parah Adumah. — The protocol of preserving kohanic children in absolute taharah for the drawing of the mei chatas: a special section of Yerushalayim for births, walkways with deliberate gaps to prevent tumas meis rising through, isolation through age 3–4. The basis for the contemporary minhag that pregnant women avoid the beis ha'chaim — preserving the possibility of parah adumah-eligible children for the era of Mashiach. ↩
  22. Shaagas Aryeh (R' Aryeh Leib Gunzberg), teshuvos (general reference). — The kashya on the daily chikui: the Gemara in fact rules that Mashiach does not come on Shabbos / Yom Tov, and the Acharonim debate whether he comes only in Nisan or only in Tishrei. How then can he "come every day"? The Brisker Rav's reply — "when Mashiach comes, we'll answer that too" — is the only response available within the maximalist framework. ↩
