Barchu: Origins, Power, and Practice
Shiur of Rabbi Ephrati — Sunday Morning Series on Tefillah
Recorded 22 September 2024 · Reworked 30 April 2026 · Mareh Mekomos upgraded 5 May 2026

L'iluy nishmas Rachel bas Shloima. After completing Pesukei d'Zimra, we now turn to Barchu and the entrance into Birchos Kriyas Shema. Where does Barchu come from? Why is it positioned here? And what is the strange phenomenon of "Baruch Hu u'Baruch Shemo"?

I. Where Does Barchu Belong?

Every morning, immediately before Yotzer Or, the shliach tzibbur calls out "Barchu es Hashem ha'mevorach", and the tzibbur answers "Baruch Hashem ha'mevorach l'olam va'ed". This is universal practice — but the Gemara's discussion of Barchu in Maseches Berachos[1] is almost entirely framed around Birchas HaTorah: the call-and-response when one receives an aliyah at Kriyas HaTorah. Where does the use of Barchu as a preface to Yotzer Or come from?

Maseches Sofrim — The Earliest Source

The earliest source is Maseches Sofrim[2] — one of the masechtos ketanos appended to the back of Maseches Avodah Zarah in standard Vilna Shas. It was compiled by the Amoraim from earlier Tannaitic material, so the substance is Tannaitic. Maseches Sofrim records explicitly that the shliach tzibbur says Barchu before beginning Yotzer Or u'Vorei Choshech. From there it entered standard practice across Klal Yisrael.

So Barchu serves a dual function: as the call-and-response at Kriyas HaTorah, and as the communal call before Birchos Kriyas Shema. Both contexts share the same logic: a public invitation to a brachah, requiring a minyan, requiring audible declaration.

II. The Zohar — Five Levels of the Neshamah

The Zohar HaKadosh[3] teaches a striking Kabbalistic dimension to Barchu. When a Jew wakes in the morning, he says Modeh Ani and acknowledges that HaKadosh Baruch Hu has restored his neshamah — but the restoration is only partial. The full integration of the neshamah into the body unfolds in stages, paralleling the structured progression of tefillah.

The neshamah itself comprises five levels:

  1. Nefesh (נפש)
  2. Ruach (רוח)
  3. Neshamah (נשמה)
  4. Chayah (חיה)
  5. Yechidah (יחידה)

Each level corresponds, in Kabbalistic terms, to a different stage of davening. The five words "Baruch Hashem ha'mevorach l'olam va'ed" mark the moment, says the Zohar, when the neshamah is fully at peace within the body — the spiritual completion of waking. Reciting Barchu with awareness is, on this view, an act of internal alignment as much as communal call.

III. The Tanna D'Bei Eliyahu — The Power of "Baruch Hashem Ha'mevorach"

The Tanna D'Bei Eliyahu[4] — a compilation of teachings transmitted to Rav Anan by Eliyahu HaNavi in his daily chavrusa — preserves a startling story attributed to Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai:

Walking on the road, R' Yochanan ben Zakkai encountered a man gathering wood. He greeted him with "Shalom aleichem"; the man did not respond. After a moment, the man explained: "I cannot return your greeting — I am not among the living. I am from the dead."

Surprised, R' Yochanan asked why a dead person would need wood. The man explained:

"My friend and I committed aveiros in our lifetimes. The beis din of Heaven decreed sereifah upon us. The arrangement is brutal: each day I gather wood for my own burning while my friend burns. The next day, my friend gathers wood for his own burning while I burn. We alternate, eternally."

R' Yochanan asked: "Is there any way out?"

The man answered: "There is one path, and I need your help. When I died, my wife was pregnant. I know — by knowledge granted to those in the next world — that the child will be a boy. When he turns five, take him to cheder and teach him Chumash. And most critically, when the tzibbur says Barchu, train him to answer 'Baruch Hashem ha'mevorach l'olam va'ed' aloud and clearly. At that moment — when his answer rises with the communal answer — the malachim in Heaven who hold the punishment will release me."

The midrashic point: Barchu is not just a procedural call. It carries communal kedushah with the same koach we associate with Kaddish. Kaddish is famous for its capacity to elevate a parent's neshamah; Barchu, less famously, has parallel power. The Gemara in Chagigah and Rosh Hashanah[5] discusses the twelve months of judgment for serious sinners; even within that period, communal kedushah — including Barchu — can effect reprieve.

IV. The Halachic Source — "Ki Sheim Hashem Ekra"

The biblical foundation for the call-and-response structure is Devarim:[6]

כִּי שֵׁם ה' אֶקְרָא, הָבוּ גֹדֶל לֵא־לֹקֵינוּ.
"For I will call out the name of Hashem; ascribe greatness to our God."

Chazal read this as the model for communal tefillah: Moshe Rabbeinu calls; the tzibbur responds. Barchu requires a minyan for the same reason Kaddish and Kedushah do — it is a communal davar shebikedushah.

V. The Sefer Chasidim's Sharp Rebuke

Because Barchu is a communal davar shebikedushah, the tzibbur must hear the call — not merely sense it.

The Sefer Chasidim[7] rebukes the universal phenomenon of bar mitzvah boys (and shy adults) who, receiving an aliyah for the first time, mumble "Barchu es Hashem ha'mevorach" too softly to be heard. He calls this "gozel kedushah" — robbing kedushah from the tzibbur. If the tzibbur cannot hear the call, they cannot answer; the entire structure of communal davar shebikedushah collapses. Parents must train their children to declare it audibly.

The Mishnah Berurah (and Biur Halacha) codifies the Sefer Chasidim's ruling. He goes further: it is a mitzvah to actively guide and correct people who say Barchu too quietly — even if they will be embarrassed. The kedushah of the tzibbur takes priority.

Hearing the Initiator — Not Just the Crowd

The poskim rule that to be yotzei the answer to Barchu, one must actually hear the shliach tzibbur or the oleh — not just hear the tzibbur's response from those closer to the bimah. If the call itself was inaudible to you, hearing other people's responses does not allow you to "jump on the train" and join. Shomea k'oneh requires shemiah from the originator.

VI. The Tannaitic Machloikis — R' Akiva and R' Yishmael

The Gemara[8] records a fundamental dispute on the nusach:

  • Rabbi Akiva: The leader calls only "Barchu es Hashem" (without ha'mevorach). The tzibbur responds "Baruch Hashem ha'mevorach l'olam va'ed".
  • Rabbi Yishmael: The leader says the full "Barchu es Hashem ha'mevorach"; the tzibbur responds the same way.

(R' Akiva and R' Yishmael are the canonical pair of Tannaim who disagree across the entire Mishnah; they were close friends but disputed almost every halachic point.)

The Story of Rav Acha bar Papa in Abu Jobar

The dispute came to a head in a memorable Gemara. Rav Acha bar Papa — one of the ten famous sons of Rav Papa, often quoted in Bavli and Yerushalmi — once arrived at the shul of Abu Jobar, near Damascus. He received an aliyah and called out "Barchu es Hashem" — without ha'mevorach, following Rabbi Akiva.

The beis midrash erupted. Where is "ha'mevorach"?! The Gemara says Rava was present and intervened — addressing Rav Acha bar Papa as "pas yochmo" (פס יוחמא). Rashi and Tosfos[9] dispute whether this is praise or rebuke; many Rishonim read it positively: "black bottom of a pot" — meaning a talmid chacham so blackened by Torah that he resembles a pot left long on the fire. Rava's instruction: do not insist on R' Akiva's exact nusach when the public is upset — add ha'mevorach to avoid machloikis. From this incident on, the universal practice followed R' Yishmael: "Barchu es Hashem ha'mevorach".

Why the Bach Insists the Chazzan Repeat

The Bach[10] raises a subtle kashya: when the tzibbur answers "Baruch Hashem ha'mevorach", the word ha'mevorach is grammatically passive — "who is/has been blessed" — and could be read in past tense. To ensure the shliach tzibbur himself participates in a present-tense blessing, he should repeat "Baruch Hashem ha'mevorach l'olam va'ed" together with the tzibbur. This is now universal practice.

VII. The Beis Knesses of Abu Jobar — A Living Legacy

The shul where Rav Acha bar Papa davened is itself one of the most remarkable buildings in our history.[11] Abu Jobar stood just outside Damascus — what is now the metropolitan area of the Syrian capital, originally a separate Jewish village. It was actively used in the era of the Amoraim; the Gemara references it many times ("Beis Kenishta d'Abu Jobar"), and significant halachic decisions were rendered there. Rav Papa himself davened there, and Syrian tradition holds that his kever is nearby — competing with traditions in Eretz Yisrael.

The Eliyahu HaNavi Tradition

Syrian Jewish tradition reaches further back. A stone in the shul bore an inscription claiming the site dated to the time of Eliyahu HaNavi — roughly 2,700 years ago, predating the Amoraim by centuries. The pesukim in Sefer Melachim describing Eliyahu HaNavi hiding from persecution and being fed bread and water by ravens are placed, in this tradition, at this very site. A grotto / cave (מערה) on the property was identified as Eliyahu's hiding place; the same site, per tradition, is where Eliyahu anointed his talmid Elisha ben Shafat as navi.

The Tanna R' Elazar ben Aroch — well-known from the Mishnah — is said to have built or restored the shul. By the era of Rav Papa, the structure had stood for many centuries. It was bombed in recent decades during Syrian instability; one researcher published a book based on interviews and photographs collected from Damascus Jews before they fled.

The Abu Chatzeira Connection

The shul remained spiritually significant for centuries. The Chida, in Shem HaGedolim[12] — his encyclopedic biographical work on gedolim from the Tannaim through his own day — records the origin of the famous Abu Chatzeira family of tzaddikim.

The patriarch was not, as commonly believed, the Abir Yaakov (R' Yaakov Abu Chatzeira). The actual founder was R' Shmuel Elbaz, who lived approximately 400 years ago in Damascus. He spent his days and nights in the chatzer — courtyard — of the Abu Jobar shul, in deep meditation and learning. The Arabic "Abu" means "father"; "Chatzir" derives from the courtyard; thus Abu-Chatzeira — "the father of the courtyard". The family later relocated to Morocco, where Moroccan Jewry continues to revere them today (Rabbi Dan Abuchatzeira and others descend from this line).

VIII. Baruch Hu u'Baruch Shemo — A Maharam of Rothenburg Innovation

When the shliach tzibbur mentions Hashem's name in any brachah, the tzibbur interjects "Baruch Hu u'Baruch Shemo" — "Blessed is He, and blessed is His Name." Where does this phrase come from?

The Maharam's Setting

The Tashbatz (R' Shimshon ben Tzaddok)[13] — the talmid who came daily to the Maharam's prison cell at Ensisheim Fortress and recorded his chiddushim — preserves the Maharam's explanation. The setting is critical to the answer.

In medieval Ashkenaz, very few Jews could daven independently. Siddurim were rare and expensive; many Yidden were amei ha'aretz who knew only fragments. Communal structures emerged to handle this:

  • Chazaras ha'shatz for Shemoneh Esrei — the shliach tzibbur repeats aloud to be motzi those who couldn't say it themselves.
  • For Birchos Kriyas Shema (Yotzer Or, etc.), there is no formal chazaras ha'shatz, but the shliach tzibbur would similarly recite the brachos to be motzi the tzibbur through shomea k'oneh.

The Mechanism of Baruch Hu u'Baruch Shemo

For shomea k'oneh to work, the tzibbur had to be paying attention. The Maharam explained: when the shliach tzibbur began to be motzi them, he would in effect alert them — "listen, I am about to bless Hashem, be ready to be yotzei". The tzibbur's response — "Baruch Hu u'Baruch Shemo" — was their acknowledgment: "yes, we are with you, ready to be yotzei through your brachah."

Today most Yidden have siddurim and recite Birchos Kriyas Shema on their own. The functional reason for Baruch Hu u'Baruch Shemo has largely fallen away. But the phrase remained in minhag — now serving as a generic communal affirmation of kavod shamayim whenever Hashem's name is invoked.

Should One Answer Amen to "Baruch Hu u'Baruch Shemo"?

In some Hassidic communities — Hungarian, Satmar, others — the tzibbur answers "amen" after the chazzan's repetition of Baruch Hu u'Baruch Shemo. The Sha'arei Ephraim[14] by R' Ephraim Zalman Margolius writes that there is no halachic reason to answer "amen" there; one has already responded Baruch Hu u'Baruch Shemo itself, and adding amen serves no function.

The Mishnah Berurah notes that one who does answer amen has authorities to rely upon, but most kehillos follow the Sha'arei Ephraim's view and do not.

R' Ephraim Zalman Margolius — Biographical Note

R' Ephraim Zalman Margolius (early 1800s) was both an Acharon of major stature and one of the wealthiest Jews of his generation — the rare combination of Torah u'gedulah b'makom echad. All his sefarim bear his name in their titles: Sha'arei Ephraim (on Kriyas HaTorah), Beis Ephraim, Yad Ephraim, and others. Sha'arei Ephraim remains the standard halachic reference for the laws of Kriyas HaTorah in many kehillos today.

IX. Inviting Latecomers — Barchu After Maariv

A widespread minhag in many shuls: after Maariv, the chazzan calls out Barchu a final time, so that latecomers who missed the original Barchu at the start of Maariv can be yotzei.

The Ritva's Strong Objection

The Ritva on Maseches Megillah[15] objects sharply, citing his own rebbe. Barchu is, by its nature, an invitation to a brachah that follows. To call out "Barchu es Hashem ha'mevorach" when no brachah follows is, the Ritva says, halachically incoherent. The tzibbur's response would not actually be functioning as the answer to a brachah-invitation.

The Rema's Defense

The Rema defends the minhag. His reasoning: Baruch Hashem ha'mevorach has independent kedushah — it is not solely a functional preface. The kedushah of the call itself stands on its own, and so it can be recited even without a following brachah. Many communities therefore continue the practice — particularly on Friday night, where Reb Avish suggests the original reason was that the tzibbur often arrived late from the mikveh.

Practically, the minhag is preserved in some communities universally, in others only on Shabbos night, and in others not at all. Follow your mesorah.

X. The Maharam of Rothenburg, Continued

It is worth pausing on the connection threading these last several shiurim. We have now seen the Maharam of Rothenburg appear three times in our discussions of Pesukei d'Zimra and Birchos Kriyas Shema:

  • The closing Baruch Hashem L'olam cluster — instituted to seal Pesukei d'Zimra when forced interruptions during German persecutions made the brachah-frame structurally vulnerable.
  • The opening of Baruch Hu u'Baruch Shemo — explained by the Maharam to his talmid the Tashbatz as the tzibbur's acknowledgment that the shliach tzibbur is being motzi them.
  • His role as the central Rishon through whom Ashkenazic halachah in our siddur was transmitted.

The Tashbatz visited him daily at the prison window in Ensisheim. Klal Yisrael's tefillah as we know it was, in significant measure, formulated in that prison cell, by a talmid chacham who had no sefarim and refused his own ransom rather than open the door to perpetual extortion of Jewish leaders. Every morning when we say Barchu and respond Baruch Hu u'Baruch Shemo, we are saying words shaped by his ruling. The Maharam's kever in Vermayza — beside Alexander Wimpen, who paid for the body to be released after fourteen years — is the resting place of the man who, more than any other single figure, made our siddur what it is.[16]

XI. Closing

The few words "Barchu es Hashem ha'mevorach" compress everything we have studied this year into a single moment of communal tefillah: a Tannaitic machloikis resolved by minhag, a Kabbalistic elevation of the neshamah, a midrashic redemption of a soul in Gehinnom, a Geonic-era shul in Damascus, the medieval German redifus of the Maharam, and a halachic structure of shomea k'oneh that binds the shliach tzibbur to every member of the tzibbur.

None of it should be mumbled. Barchu is the call; the response, said clearly and audibly with the full tzibbur, is the kedushah we have been trained for nineteen hundred years to deliver.

Next week, with Hashem's help: Yotzer Or u'Vorei Choshech.
