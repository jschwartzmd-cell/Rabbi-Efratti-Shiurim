Rabbi Ephrati · Sunday Morning שיעור on תפילה

וִיהִי נֹעַם, the Malach Duma, and the Three Goyrem Ra L'atzmo

The mechanics of מוצאי שבת — the gates of גהינום, the Zohar's three self-inflicted curses, and Rav Avraham Adadi's chiddush on the Yom-Tov-mid-week exception
Recorded 28 June 2026 · י״ב תמוז תשפ״ו · Reworked from the original שיעור
Dedication Today's שיעור and breakfast are sponsored by Motti Akh and his rebbetzin לעילוי נשמת his father-in-law מרדכי אברהם בן בנימין ע״ה. May the נשמה have an עלייה in גן עדן — הנשמה לעלייה.

Last week's שיעור crossed from Friday-night מגן אבות into מוצאי שבת and the gates of גהינום reopening at וִיהִי נֹעַם. This week's שיעור lives inside that seam and walks the full structural geography. The arc: the Zohar, Medrash Tanchuma, Medrash Rabbah, Mechilta, and תנא דבי אליהו all converge on the same claim — that גהינום reactivates only when כלל ישראל finishes קדושה דסידרא on מוצאי שבת; the Tur's chiddush of the מלאך דומה waiting for the last shul in the world (with Rabbi Ephrati's tentative speculation that the English word "doomed" derives from this מלאך); the יסוד ושורש העבודה's alternative reading that each individual's clock follows his own lifelong מנהג; the striking coincidence — today is the יארצייט of the Tur, ר' יעקב בן הרא״ש, with the kehillah learning Tur on his yahrtzeit; the Zohar Hakadosh in the הקדמה on the three categories of גורם רעה לעצמו — improper bread disposal, cursing oneself, and doing מלאכה on מוצאי שבת before Vehi Noam (because the resha'im in גהינום, pushed back into their suffering, curse the Yid who triggered the restart); the Chortkover Rebbe and the famous מעשה of זלמן השותק of Vienna; the Arizal's reading of לא יחל דברו ככל היוצא מפיו יעשה; the structural functions of וִיהִי נֹעַם (drawing kedusha into the week), יושב בסתר (Shir Shel Pega'im, shemira from מזיקים), and ואתה קדוש (the world-sustaining force per סוטה מ״ט ע״ב); the long-standing machloikes on whether to say Vehi Noam in a week containing Yom Tov, with the chiddush nifla of ר' אברהם הדאדי of Tripoli (גהינום doesn't reopen at all that week) against the בן איש חי's Kabbalistic insistence that one must say it to draw Shabbos kedusha forward; and closing with the Baal Shem Tov / Bach / Sassover Rebbe maaseh of the tzaddik who passes through גהינום first to lift up his followers before entering גן עדן himself.

I. Where We Left Off — Multiple חז״ל Sources on Gehinom's Closure

Last week we crossed into מוצאי שבת and the gates of גהינום. This week opens with a broader source-canvass. The claim — that גהינום does not burn on שבת and only reactivates when כלל ישראל recites קדושה דסידרא on מוצאי שבת — is not a single isolated source. It appears in the זוהר הקדוש, in מדרש תנחומא, in מדרש רבה, in the מכילתא, and in תנא דבי אליהו[1]. A long, convergent list of חז״ל sources points to the same structural reality.

The sequence of Motzei Shabbos prayers — after מעריב, we recite וִיהִי נֹעַם, then יושב בסתר עליון (תהילים צ״א), then קדושה דסידרא opening from ואתה קדוש יושב תהילות ישראל[2] — is the structural infrastructure that delays the reactivation. Each piece has its own function within the larger architecture.

A) Where Vehi Noam Lives in תנ״ך

וִיהִי נֹעַם ה' אֱלֹקֵינוּ עָלֵינוּ וּמַעֲשֵׂה יָדֵינוּ כּוֹנְנָה עָלֵינוּ is the last pasuk of תהילים פרק צ'. The very next pasuk — opening תהילים פרק צ״א — is יושב בסתר עליון בצל שדי יתלונן. The two are sequentially adjacent in תנ״ך; the liturgy preserves that adjacency. We then move into קדושה דסידרא with ואתה קדוש (the unit ordinarily opens with ובא לציון גואל, but at Motzei Shabbos we skip the geulah opening — see Section XVII below).

B) Some Shittos on V'hu Rachum

A small structural point worth flagging: the שיעור noted that on the same theme — extending the Shabbos buffer — some שיטות read the lengthening of והוא רחום at Motzei Shabbos מעריב as serving the same delay-Gehinom function. Most שיטות, however, read the lengthening of והוא רחום more straightforwardly as a means of extending Shabbos kedusha into chol. The serious-business piece — the explicit delay of Gehinom's reactivation — attaches to וִיהִי נֹעַם through קדושה דסידרא.

II. The Tur — The Malach Duma and the Last Shul in the World

The טור[3] gives the picture in named form. There is a מלאך named דוּמָה — appointed by הקדוש ברוך הוא over the נשמות of the deceased. The name has a pasuk-anchor: תהילים קט״ו[4] — לֹא הַמֵּתִים יְהַלְלוּ יָ-הּ וְלֹא כָּל יוֹרְדֵי דוּמָה. רש״י בברכות י״ז ע״א[5] identifies Duma explicitly as the מלאך ממונה על הנשמות של המתים.

The Tur's structural picture: Duma waits through the entire Shabbos. He waits until the last shul in the world — ממתין עד כנסיה אחרונה — completes קדושה דסידרא. Only then does he reactivate גהינום for the reshaim. The shul in Hawaii, the last minyan davening late on Motzei Shabbos in some far time zone, is the one that triggers the global restart.

"Doomed" — A Tentative Linguistic Speculation Rabbi Ephrati shared a chiddush of his own that he had floated to Nathan once and that — by Rabbi Ephrati's account — Nathan had not loved: the English word doomed may derive from the name of the malach Duma. The conceptual fit is striking — Duma is the angel in charge of the deceased; "doomed" describes one whose fate is sealed for descent to that realm. The lexical and chronological provenance is uncertain and Rabbi Ephrati flagged it as speculation rather than established etymology. The שיעור framed it as a "chiddush" worth recording even if not definitively defensible. The Hebrew דומה also connects to silence (וַיִּדֹּם אַהֲרֹן, the silence of the dead, מוטל כאבן דומה); whether the English word descends from this or from the Anglo-Saxon dom ("judgment") is the standard scholarly question, and the שיעור left it open.

III. Hawaii, the Amshinover, and the Boundary Question

Rav Dan in the kehillah pressed a sharp question that opened on the Tur's framing. If global Gehinom reactivation depends on the last shul in the world finishing קדושה דסידרא — what about a Rebbe who is famous for davening Maariv extremely late? The שיעור flagged the אמשינובער רעבע — the Amshinover Rebbe of ארץ ישראל — who is known to make Havdalah extraordinarily late, sometimes hours after standard zeman. Is the Amshinover Rebbe, by extending his own Maariv, holding back the reactivation of Gehinom for the entire world until he finishes?

Rav Yossi pressed the converse: and what about the earliest shul on Friday night? Does the earliest Shabbos-acceptance — early מקבל שבת, early candle-lighting, early davening — establish the start of the universal Gehinom-off period? The שיעור acknowledged the converse question is sharp but did not have a settled answer in the ראשונים for it. (Rabbi Ephrati had noted last week the חפץ חיים's chiddush that an individual Yid's personal Shabbos clock in shamayim begins when he was מקבל שבת — see last week's שיעור — but that frames the individual case rather than the global case.)

IV. The יסוד ושורש העבודה — Personal Minhag, Personal Clock

An alternative framework competes with the Tur. The יסוד ושורש העבודה[6] — ר' אלכסנדר זיסקינד מהוראדנא — reads the same structural reality at the level of the individual. On his reading, for an individual's נשמה, the relevant Motzei Shabbos clock follows his own lifelong personal מנהג of when he davens Maariv on Motzei Shabbos.

The conceptual contrast is sharp. The Tur's picture is global and communal — the last shul in the world triggers a universal reactivation. The Yesod V'shoresh Ha'avoda's picture is individual and biographical — when you davened Maariv every Motzei Shabbos throughout your life is when your personal Gehinom clock turns back on. The Tur came centuries before the Yesod V'shoresh; the two readings have coexisted in the halachic-spiritual landscape without explicit resolution.

Rabbi Ephrati flagged the practical consequence: those who are מקבל שבת early gain backward; those who finish Motzei Shabbos late gain forward; those who do both gain on both ends.

A Practical Halacha — Wife's Candle-Lighting in Early-Shabbos A contemporary practical-halacha question that the שיעור flagged: ר' צבי יעקב שטיין, the local rav of the kehillah, had sent out a recent text/WhatsApp psak on a question that arises naturally from the structural early-Shabbos framework. If a husband is מקבל Shabbos early, when does his wife have to light candles? Rav Stein's framework: it depends on the husband's kavanah. If the husband is davening early for convenience — for scheduling reasons, family logistics, etc. — but is not actually being מקבל Shabbos at that earlier zeman, then his wife may continue to light at the standard later time. But if the husband is actively being מקבל Shabbos at the earlier time — drawing the kedusha of Shabbos forward as Friday morning's הכנה or as early מקבל שבת in shul does — then the wife must light correspondingly early, because she cannot be in a different Shabbos-time from her husband. The structural principle ties into the broader machinery: the personal Shabbos-clock is real, and the household must be on a coordinated clock.

V. Today is the יארצייט of the Tur

Rabbi Ephrati mid-שיעור flagged a striking coincidence. Today — June 28, 2026, י״ב תמוז — is the yahrtzeit of ר' יעקב בן הרא״ש, the Tur (born c. 1269, נפטר ד׳ תמוז ה׳ק׳ in Toledo)[7]. The שיעור took the coincidence as a real תוספת ירחא. "Fifty Yidden are sitting learning Tur on his yahrtzeit. What a זכות." The Tur is the structural author of the entire Friday-night/Motzei-Shabbos liturgical machinery we are walking through — and the kehillah learning his sefer on his yahrtzeit is itself a halachically-meaningful echo of the architecture being learned.

(The exact day of the Tur's petira is, in the standard biographical sources, ד׳ תמוז; the שיעור's "today" reference attaches to ה׳ק׳, with the local minhag and the שיעור day creating the loose attribution. The yahrtzeit-week resonance is real even if the exact date is approximate.)

VI. The Zohar Hakadosh — Three גורם רעה לעצמו

The שיעור pivoted from the structural geography to the practical halacha. The זוהר הקדוש in his הקדמה[8] identifies three categories of people who inadvertently bring bad consequences upon themselves — they do not mean to, but the structural machinery generates the result.

A) Improper Bread Disposal — קשה לעניות

The first: throwing bread in the garbage. Bread — לחם — has structural weight in the halachic-spiritual machinery that other foods do not have. To בזל in bread, even crusts and leftovers, opens a structural risk: קשה לעניות, it brings poverty.

The שיעור walked through practical halacha. Crumbs less than a כזית the עולם is more lenient about, but the מקובלים say one should not be בזל in פרורים either. What about moldy bread? Not ראוי לאכילה — different rules. The practical alternatives Rabbi Ephrati recommended: feed it to birds outside; feed it to fish if you have an aquarium; or — the standard contemporary practice — double-bag it before discarding, the bread placed in two separate bags so that the kisui is structurally complete (analogous to the כיסוי-בתוך-כיסוי required for carrying a Tehillim or Siddur into a bathroom).

And — sharply — hanging bread on top of the garbage can is also a problem. The גמרא פסחים קי״א ע״ב[9] codifies that hanging bread is קשה לעניות. It is itself זילזול. The שיעור brought a contemporary teshuvah from ר' בן ציון אבא שאול[10] in אור לציון: someone asked about kindergarten cubbies where the morah hangs each child's sandwich-bag on a hook — was this a problem? Rav Ben Zion's psak was that since the morah is hanging it, not the owner, the דרך בזיון aspect does not attach in the same way; the issue is specifically when one hangs his own bread. The teshuvah preserves the structural concern while reading the kindergarten case narrowly. A separate practical solution — the mashgiach at Motti's store in Boro Park instructs employees to moisten stale bread before disposal so it is no longer ראוי לאכילה at all.

B) Cursing — קללה לעצמו ולאחרים

The second category: cursing oneself or others. The Zohar's framework is sharp. There is a מלאך ממונה over every word that emerges from a person's mouth, charged with bringing the word to fulfillment. If the word is a curse — לא ינוח ולא ישקוט — the מלאך does not rest until the curse finds application. The structural principle: אל תפתח פה לשטן.

And — critically — this applies to self-curses. Words blurted in anger, in moments of frustration, in throwaway speech — they are not metaphysically inert. The Arizal[11] reads the pasuk לֹא יַחֵל דְּבָרוֹ כְּכֹל הַיֹּצֵא מִפִּיו יַעֲשֶׂה (במדבר ל׳:ג׳) on two levels at once: as a halachic imperative (one must fulfill the נדרים that come out of his mouth) and as a metaphysical descriptive (whatever a Yid says is in fact done). The koach hadibbur is rooted in the נשמה being a חלק אלוק ממעל — divine in origin, and therefore the words it produces are not merely sound but structural acts.

C) מלאכה Before Vehi Noam — The Reshaim's Curse

The third — the one that ties directly to the Motzei Shabbos sugya. The Zohar Hakadosh writes that if a Yid does מלאכה on Motzei Shabbos before reciting וִיהִי נֹעַם, יושב בסתר, and ואתה קדוש — turning on a light, lighting a fire, starting a car, performing any standard weekday act — he signals the end of the Shabbos respite to the structural machinery. The resha'im in גהינום, who had been enjoying their שבת-off, are pushed back into the fire prematurely. And — the precise mechanism — they curse the Yid who triggered the restart.

Rabbi Ephrati pressed the natural objection: they are resha'im in גהינום; why should I care about their curse? The answer is the גמרא בבא קמא צ״ג ע״א[12]. Everyone knows the famous אל תהי ברכת הדיוט קלה בעיניך — do not lightly dismiss the blessing of a simple person. Fewer people know the structural parallel: the same Gemara codifies אל תהי קללת הדיוט קלה בעיניך — do not lightly dismiss the curse of a simple person either. Even the curse of a rasha in גהינום, on this reading, has structural reach. The Yid who triggers the restart prematurely catches the curse. לא עלינו ולא עליכם.

So the structural function of waiting through וִיהִי נֹעַם, יושב בסתר, ואתה קדוש is not only to delay the global reactivation; it is to give the resha'im a buffer of extra time — even a minute or two — so that they do not curse you when they are sent back. The shtickel kedusha you draw down into the week is the same shtickel kedusha that protects you from the consequences of triggering the restart too quickly.

VII. The Chortkover Rebbe and זלמן השותק of Vienna

The שיעור brought a sobering maaseh that anchors the principle that words have structural weight. There was a Yid in Vienna — known to the kehillah as זלמן השותק, "Zalman the Silent" — who came to shul every day his entire life and never spoke to anyone. The kehillah assumed he was an introvert, perhaps shy, perhaps just temperamentally quiet. They named him for the silence.

Near the end of his life, knowing he was about to be נפטר, זלמן rose in the בית מדרש and gave a קלאפ on the bimah. The kehillah was shocked — the silent man speaking. He told them why he had been silent.

As a young man, freshly married, with a temper he had not yet learned to control, he had gotten into a serious fight with his new wife. Rabbi Ephrati noted with a quiet weight the universality of the moment: a couple just married, neither yet skilled in the art of שלום בית, neither yet practiced at being מותר. In the heat of an argument, he had cursed her — "זאלסט ווערן פארברענט אין א פייער" ("you should burn in a fire"). It was thrown off; he did not mean it.

A few weeks later there was a tragedy. His wife died in a fire.

He had come to the טשארטקאווער רבי[13] in shock — "Rebbe, what do I do?" The Chortkover Rebbe's תיקון: "Shut your mouth. Don't talk for the rest of your life. Daven, learn, talk what is necessary — don't shmooze, don't talk." He had kept the tikkun his entire life. He was now telling the kehillah at the end of his life so that they would learn the lesson — "be careful what comes out of your mouth."

VIII. Rav Yitzchak Zilberstein and the Boy — The Rizhyner's Tikkun

The שיעור brought a structurally parallel contemporary maaseh. רב יצחק זילברשטיין[14] — the rav of Ramat Elchanan in Bnei Brak, longtime author of practical-halacha sefarim — was approached by a young boy in deep distress.

The boy explained: he had a friend in his class who was everything the boy was not — wealthy, talented, top of the class, great parents, deeply matzliach. The boy had been consumed with קנאה. He had no shem schooling on how to manage the emotion; he had no good outlet. He had taken to davening — every single day — that the friend's mother should die. The thinking was childish but explicit: if his mother dies, he becomes a יתום, his hatzlacha collapses, the gap between us closes.

After several months of daily tefillos, the friend's mother had been נפטר.

The boy had come to Rav Zilberstein in panic: "Was it me? Was it my tefillos? Do I need a tikkun?" Rav Zilberstein's response was structurally parallel to the Chortkover's: the precedent was real. The Rizhyner Rebbe[15] in such a case had ruled that imposing complete silence as the Chortkover had done with זלמן was a גזירה שאין הציבור יכול לעמוד בה — too heavy for a young person. The Rizhyner's alternative tikkun: every single tefillah for the rest of your life — daven for another Yid. Daven for someone else's refuah. Ask the Eibishter to give a Yid relief. The structural logic: the original act was a tefillah that cursed; the תיקון is to spend the rest of your life in tefillos that bless. The same engine, reversed.

A Sober Note on Zilberstein's Stories Rabbi Ephrati flagged a careful caveat. Not every מעשה preserved in Rav Yitzchak Zilberstein's published sefarim is verbatim accurate — sometimes poskim shape a story around the psak to make it memorable. The שיעור's framing: "this particular story happens to be a true story." The general principle for engaging with Rav Zilberstein's published maaseiyos is to take the psak seriously while holding the narrative-shape lightly. The structural-halachic point about the koach of speech, and about the tikkunim for misdirected speech, is firm regardless of whether any individual story is documented to the letter.

IX. The Function of וִיהִי נֹעַם

We now return to the structural meanings of the three units. וִיהִי נֹעַם serves two related functions.

First, as developed above: it is the buffer that delays Gehinom's reactivation so the resha'im are not pushed back prematurely, sparing the Yid from their curse. Second — independently — it is a tefillah for the success of the week ahead. וִיהִי נֹעַם ה' אֱלֹקֵינוּ עָלֵינוּ וּמַעֲשֵׂה יָדֵינוּ כּוֹנְנָה עָלֵינוּ. We ask הקדוש ברוך הוא, the moment Shabbos is over, as we enter שֵׁשֶׁת יְמֵי הַמַּעֲשֶׂה, that our work — whatever business each Yid is in — should succeed. The tefillah is direct and parallel to the request Moshe Rabbeinu made for the success of the משכן (see Section X below).

X. Moshe Rabbeinu's Brachah at the Mishkan

The pasuk-anchor for וִיהִי נֹעַם is striking. שמות ל״ט פסוק מ״ג — at the completion of the משכן — has וַיְבָרֶךְ אֹתָם מֹשֶׁה. רש״י[16] on the pasuk says that the brachah Moshe gave was exactly the line we say at Motzei Shabbos: וִיהִי נֹעַם ה' אֱלֹקֵינוּ עָלֵינוּ וּמַעֲשֵׂה יָדֵינוּ כּוֹנְנָה עָלֵינוּ. The very brachah that Moshe Rabbeinu used to bless the Yidden's avodah at the completion of the משכן is the brachah we deploy every Motzei Shabbos for the coming week.

And then: the גמרא[17] adds that Moshe continued — after saying וִיהִי נֹעַם, he continued into יושב בסתר עליון (Tehillim 91). Why? Because יושב בסתר drives away the מזיקים from the world. When the משכן was completed, the שכינה descended; the מזיקים were displaced; Moshe Rabbeinu pronounced both pieces of the unit together — first the brachah for hatzlacha, then the protective Shir Shel Pega'im. The Motzei Shabbos liturgy mirrors Moshe's order exactly.

XI. יושב בסתר — שיר של פגעים

The גמרא בשבועות ט״ו ע״ב[18] names יושב בסתר (Tehillim 91) שיר של פגעים — the song against harmful forces. It is recited in three structural contexts: at Motzei Shabbos (because the gates of Gehinom are about to reopen and מזיקים rush out to drag the נשמות of the resha'im back to their suffering); at קריאת שמע על המיטה (because night itself is a time of פגעים — the kehillah will recognize this from their daily nightly Shema); and at levayah (in Flatbush and Boro Park kehillot the practice is to recite יושב בסתר while accompanying the aron — because the moment a נשמה just leaves the body is the moment of maximal exposure to מזיקים, and the unit serves as shemira at the levayah procession).

The שיעור's structural sharpening: יושב בסתר functions wherever the structural seam between worlds creates an opening that the מזיקים can exploit. The unit closes that opening. Motzei Shabbos is one such seam — the gates of Gehinom reopen, mazikim spill out, and the kehillah erects a shemira before continuing into the week.

XII. The Current גלות — A Sober Aside

The שיעור took a moment, mid-sugya, for an explicit and difficult aside. Rabbi Ephrati flagged what every kehillah member was carrying: "Every morning I open my phone and see another beautiful innocent face of a 19- or 20-year-old soldier, חייל in ארץ ישראל, ה' ינקום דמו. Last week five killed on Shabbos, ה' ינקום דמו."

The cumulative weight of the war casualties — multi-year, post-October-7th, ongoing — was named openly. "רבונו של עולם, יומי צרותינו די." The אלמנות, the יתומים, the parents who have lost their loved ones, the mothers and fathers — "Hashem yerachem." The שיעור did not let the structural-halachic discussion run as if the present moment did not exist. The שיעור's framing of the aside: "It's terrible. It's horrible. We're desensitized because October 7th was a big number — but every year there's an עולם ומלואו of new tzaros. ממש we have to cry."

Rabbi Ephrati closed the aside with the structural pivot back into the sugya — "the only thing that holds the world up, that lets the world survive, is two things, says the גמרא:"

XIII. ואתה קדוש — סוטה מ״ט ע״ב and the World's Sustenance

The גמרא בסוטה מ״ט ע״ב[19] states explicitly: in גלות, אֵין לְךָ יוֹם שֶׁאֵין קִלְלָתוֹ מְרֻבָּה מִשֶּׁל חֲבֵרוֹ. Every day in גלות, the curses and tzaros are more numerous than the day before. The world cannot, on its natural metaphysical balance, sustain itself. Only two structural acts hold the world up: קדושה דסידרא when Yidden recite ואתה קדוש יושב תהילות ישראל, and אמן יהא שמיה רבא at קדיש.

The structural reading the ראשונים build on the Sotah Gemara: when one sanctifies הקדוש ברוך הוא's name, the koach of that sanctification dispels מזיקים. קדושה דסידרא at Motzei Shabbos serves the same function as יושב בסתר — different mechanism, same outcome. The pair are bookends of the same structural transition.

XIV. Why We Skip ובא לציון

A structural observation. The standard daily קדושה דסידרא opens with וּבָא לְצִיּוֹן גּוֹאֵל. At Motzei Shabbos we open from ואתה קדוש directly, skipping the opening pasuk. Why?

The משנה ברורה[20] quotes the אבודרהם: אין גאולה בלילה. גאולה does not happen at night. The opening line of ובא לציון גואל speaks the language of גאולה; at night — particularly at the Motzei Shabbos transition, which is the deepest entry into the workweek's night-mode — we do not open with גאולה-language. We start from ואתה קדוש directly. The same principle was discussed in connection with the structure of Maariv earlier in the year.

XV. ויתן לך

The שיעור flagged a small but recurring practical point. The full Motzei Shabbos liturgy includes ויתן לך — the long compilation of pesukim of blessing — codified by the רמ״א[21] in שולחן ערוך. The kehillah in Harborview, like Rabbi Ephrati's own father's shul, has fallen out of saying it together publicly in shul; people increasingly say it at home after Havdalah (the Arizal's own practice, the שיעור noted). At Young Israel-type kehillot the מנהג of saying it all together publicly has been preserved. The שיעור framed this as a beautiful minhag whose recovery is structurally worth considering — "It's a Rama. I don't know what happened. My father also schtings on Motzei Shabbos without saying ויתן לך."

XVI. גוט פון אברהם — The Berditchever's Tefillah

A complementary chassidish minhag: the ברדיטשעווער[22] composed a Motzei Shabbos tefillah in Yiddish — גוט פון אברהם, גוט פון אברהם — that has its own niggun ("בא ליבט דיין פאלק, דיין באליבטע פאלק") and is recited at the same structural moment as ויתן לך. The Rama does not bring it (it post-dates the Rama); it is preserved in the chassidish-mesorah literature. Rabbi Ephrati's mother used to say it every Motzei Shabbos at twelve o'clock; the שיעור flagged it as a moiredike niggun worth knowing about even where the kehillah's own minhag does not include it.

XVII. Why No וִיהִי נֹעַם in a Week Containing Yom Tov?

The שיעור now opened the most substantive practical-halachic dispute of the sugya. When a Yom Tov falls in the middle of the coming week — say, Pesach falls on Tuesday night, with Motzei Shabbos being the start of a non-full work-week — do we still say וִיהִי נֹעַם on that Motzei Shabbos?

A) The Standard Reading — No Full שֵׁשֶׁת יְמֵי הַמַּעֲשֶׂה

The standard reading takes the surface of the prayer at its word. וִיהִי נֹעַם ה' אֱלֹקֵינוּ עָלֵינוּ וּמַעֲשֵׂה יָדֵינוּ כּוֹנְנָה עָלֵינוּ is structurally a tefillah for the full שֵׁשֶׁת יְמֵי הַמַּעֲשֶׂה — a six-day work-week. If the coming week contains a Yom Tov (with its איסור מלאכה), the structural setup is incomplete. So we don't say it. פשוט פשט.

B) The חידוש נפלא of ר' אברהם הדאדי of Tripoli

The שיעור then opened a chiddush that Rabbi Ephrati flagged with explicit delight. ר' אברהם הדאדי[23] was the gadol of Tripolitanian Jewry in the 1800s. Libyan Jewry, the שיעור noted in an aside, is an under-recognized branch of כלל ישראל — a centuries-old community in Tripoli with its own unique minhagim, distinct from generic Sefardim and from תימנים, with no major kehillah in America (no Tripolitanian community in Queens, Flatbush, or the rest of the New York map) but substantial kehillot today in ארץ ישראל following the קיבוץ גלויות. Rav Avraham Adadi wrote — Rabbi Ephrati cited his שו״ת ויקרא אברהם — a striking structural chiddush.

On Rav Avraham Adadi's reading, when Yom Tov falls in the middle of the coming week, הקדוש ברוך הוא does not turn Gehinom back on at all that week. The Yom Tov-off period flows directly out of the Shabbos-off period. There are only two or three weekday-nights between the Motzei Shabbos and the Yom Tov; הקדוש ברוך הוא does not bother reactivating Gehinom for just that short interval. (The שיעור brought a ראיה from the Zohar Hakadosh that Rav Adadi marshals; the precise Zohar citation Rav Adadi works with was not specified in the שיעור.) If Gehinom does not reactivate, the resha'im are not pushed back, the mazikim do not pour out, and the entire structural rationale for וִיהִי נֹעַם / יושב בסתר / ואתה קדוש is absent. Don't say וִיהִי נֹעַם, don't say ואתה קדוש, go straight to עלינו.

Rabbi Ephrati was open about the structural-historical status of the chiddush: "It's a big chiddush; I don't know if other אחרונים מסכים to this, but it makes me very happy." The chiddush is the second-order explanation for the surface halacha — it preserves the practice but supplies a deeper conceptual reason than the standard "no full work-week" answer.

C) The Counter — בן איש חי and Baghdad

The בן איש חי[24] — ר' יוסף חיים of Baghdad — held the structurally opposite position. On בן איש חי's reading, the function of וִיהִי נֹעַם is not primarily Gehinom-delay but kedusha-mamshich: drawing the holiness of Shabbos forward into the coming week. On that reading, the absence of Gehinom-reactivation in a Yom-Tov-mid-week scenario is structurally irrelevant — you still need the kedusha-pull forward, and so you still must say וִיהִי נֹעַם.

And — this is the operational note — the Ben Ish Chai did not just rule from the desk. He walked from shul to shul in Baghdad changing the local minhag. Where the previous Baghdadi practice had been not to say וִיהִי נֹעַם in a Yom-Tov-mid-week scenario, the Ben Ish Chai personally changed the kehillot's practice to say it. The kabbalistic argument — based on the structural integrity of the mamshich-kedusha function — outweighed for him the surface argument from the absence of a full work-week.

D) Mishnah Berurah on Edge Cases — Erev Pesach and Purim

The משנה ברורה handles edge cases at the borderlines:

If Erev Pesach is during the coming week: there is an איסור מלאכה on Erev Pesach from chatzos onward, but Erev Pesach is not technically a full Yom Tov. The שיעור flagged a יעב״ץ[25] teshuvah with a long pilpul both ways. The Mishnah Berurah paskens say וִיהִי נֹעַם — Erev Pesach's איסור מלאכה does not break the full-work-week pattern in the way that a true Yom Tov does.

If Purim falls in the coming week: also say וִיהִי נֹעַם — Purim has no איסור מלאכה at all, so it does not disrupt the work-week structure.

Tisha B'Av Erev: Rabbi Ephrati flagged for next week — "כל ערב תשעה באב מעריב, suddenly we don't say וִיהִי נֹעַם, we say only ואתה קדוש. Why? We'll discuss when we get to Tisha B'Av אם ירצה השם." The structural exception will need its own treatment.

XVIII. Standing for וִיהִי נֹעַם

The משנה ברורה codifies a practical halacha that flows from the Ben Ish Chai's framing. One must stand for the words "וִיהִי נֹעַם ה' אֱלֹקֵינוּ" — because at that moment one is structurally drawing Shabbos kedusha down into the coming week, and that is a moment of kabbalas kedusha requiring standing.

The structural parallel — explicitly drawn by the Mishnah Berurah — is to other moments of kabbalas kedusha that require standing: וּפְרוֹשׂ עָלֵינוּ סֻכַּת שְׁלוֹמֶךָ in מעריב of Friday night; בוֹאִי כַלָּה at the end of לכה דודי in קבלת שבת; מזמור שיר ליום השבת. All four moments share the same structural function: the kehillah is being given (or drawing forward) an increment of Shabbos kedusha, and the appropriate posture is standing. וִיהִי נֹעַם joins the same family. After the opening line, one may sit for יושב בסתר.

XIX. מלוה מלכה — The Fourth Seudah

The same structural-mamshich principle generates the practical encouragement to eat מלוה מלכה. The שיעור pressed this as an underappreciated minhag in contemporary American kehillot. The סעודה רביעית after Shabbos — even just מזונות, a small grain-based snack — is the structural mechanism that brings Shabbos kedusha into the coming week through the same engine as וִיהִי נֹעַם does liturgically.

Rabbi Ephrati framed it directly to a kehillah member (Reb Chezky): "You eat Melave Malka every Motzei Shabbos? You should start. Coming this Motzei Shabbos you should start eating Melave Malka — even if you can't wash, even if it's late, even if there's Shalosh Seudos right before — at least מזונות. The Shabbos kedusha you bring into the מלוה מלכה carries forward into the rest of the week."

XX. יוֹשֵׁב תְּהִלּוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל — Hashem Awaits the Tehillos

A small but lovely structural chiddush from one of the ראשונים. The pasuk וְאַתָּה קָדוֹשׁ יוֹשֵׁב תְּהִלּוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל — Hashem is הקדוש, sitting on the praises of Israel — admits a second reading. יוֹשֵׁב can mean either sitting or awaiting (יוֹשֵׁב וּמְצַפֶּה). One of the ראשונים[26] reads יוֹשֵׁב תְּהִלּוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל as "He awaits the praises of Israel" — הקדוש ברוך הוא is structurally waiting for Klal Yisrael to daven Maariv Motzei Shabbos, and he does not reactivate Gehinom until they do. The pasuk's surface meaning becomes its operational mechanism.

XXI. The Machzor Vitry on Besamim

Rav Dan in the kehillah pressed a sharp connection. If Rav Avraham Adadi is right that Gehinom does not reactivate at all in a Yom-Tov-mid-week scenario, what about Havdalah's בשמים?

The מחזור ויטרי[27] — the same Rashi-school siddur that this kehillah encountered two weeks ago — offers a secondary reason for the standard Havdalah custom of smelling בשמים. The standard reason (from the Gemara in ביצה) is נשמה יתירה: the spices comfort the soul as the extra Shabbos-נשמה departs. The Machzor Vitry's second reason is structural: when Gehinom reactivates at Motzei Shabbos, the burning of the resha'im produces a bad odor — the spiritual world is, in a real sense, briefly stinking — and the בשמים at Havdalah counteract that odor so the Yid is not himself influenced by it.

Rav Dan's question: if Rav Adadi is right and Gehinom does not reactivate that week, the second reason for בשמים does not apply. Should one therefore not smell בשמים at that Havdalah? The שיעור's answer: "לא פלוג" — we do not vary the practice by week, and we rely on the first reason (נשמה יתירה) which still applies even when Gehinom does not reactivate. The double-source for the בשמים custom provides exactly the structural resilience the kehillah needs to maintain the practice consistently.

XXII. The Bach, the Sassover Rebbe, and the Baal Shem Tov

The שיעור closed with a striking maaseh — the Baal Shem Tov's ראיה through the window. The Baal Shem Tov was preparing to leave his home one morning to perform a ברית; he was always punctual and would leave early. On this particular morning, however, he was standing at the window — looking out, not moving. His chassidim asked what he saw.

The Baal Shem Tov said: I see the בח[28]. (Rabbi Ephrati noted a parallel version where the figure is the סאסאווער רעבע — the שיעור flagged that the two versions exist; the structural point is the same.) The Bach was being mevareach — נפטר — and his נשמה was arriving in the Olam Ha'emes. The malachim wanted to bring him directly into Gan Eden. But he refused to enter.

Why? Because the Bach had been the major posek to be מתיר חדש בחוץ לארץ — the heter that allows Yidden in chutz la'aretz to eat grains harvested before Pesach without strict yashan-discipline. Many Yidden across the centuries had relied on the Bach's heter. The Bach knew that those Yidden — if their other zechuyos were not sufficient — would be subject to going through גהינום first for not having been makpid on yashan. So the Bach refused to enter Gan Eden until he had first passed through גהינום himself — to suffer some of the heat for his heter — and to extract every Yid who had relied on his heter and bring them up to Gan Eden with him. Only after he had brought up the last person did he allow himself to enter. The Baal Shem Tov watched the entire process from his window in real time, and only then could he proceed to make the bris.

The שיעור framed this as the chassidish svara that there is — a structural principle that a tzaddik does not enter Gan Eden first but passes through Gehinom to elevate those who relied on him. The maaseh is preserved in the chassidish mesorah and was offered as not claimed as Brisker-style proven halacha but as a real piece of the broader mesorah within which the entire Gehinom-Motzei-Shabbos discussion lives. "רב יצחק יונה, these are legends and traditions that we have by chassidim, אבער דאס איז ניט קיין חסידישע בית מדרש."

The arc of the שיעור in three movements. (i) The full source-canvas for the gates of Gehinom on Motzei Shabbos — Zohar, Medrash Tanchuma, Medrash Rabbah, Mechilta, Tana D'bei Eliyahu all converging — and the Tur's named mechanism: the מלאך דומה waiting for the last shul in the world to finish קדושה דסידרא, with Yesod V'shoresh Ha'avoda's individual-clock reading as the alternative framework. Today is the Tur's yahrtzeit, and the kehillah is learning his sefer. (ii) The Zohar Hakadosh's three goyrem ra l'atzmo — bread, cursing, premature מלאכה — with the structural sharpening that the resha'im's curse at being pushed back into Gehinom catches the Yid who triggers the restart (Bava Kama 93's אל תהי קללת הדיוט קלה). The sobering Salman HaShosek-Vienna maaseh, the Rav Yitzchak Zilberstein / Rizhyner tikkun, the Arizal's reading of ככל היוצא מפיו יעשה. Plus the structural functions of the three units — וִיהִי נֹעַם (Moshe's brachah at the Mishkan, plus hatzlacha-for-the-week), יושב בסתר (Shir Shel Pega'im from Shevuos 15b — also at קריאת שמע על המיטה and at levayah), and ואתה קדוש (Sotah 49b's world-sustaining force). (iii) The Yom-Tov-mid-week machloikes — the standard "no full work-week" pshat against Rav Avraham Adadi of Tripoli's chiddush nifla (Gehinom does not reactivate that week at all) against the Ben Ish Chai's kabbalistic insistence that one must still say וִיהִי נֹעַם for the mamshich-kedusha function (he walked Baghdad shul-by-shul changing the minhag). The Mishnah Berurah's edge-case rulings on Erev Pesach and Purim. Standing for וִיהִי נֹעַם as kabbalas kedusha. מלוה מלכה as the seudah-level mamshich. The Machzor Vitry on Havdalah בשמים. And closing with the Baal Shem Tov / Bach maaseh — the tzaddik who passes through Gehinom first to lift his followers up with him.

The framework is in tension (or in productive complementarity) with the Tur's global-communal reading; the שיעור treated them as coexisting layers rather than as mutually exclusive. The Gemara appears in the context of the broader sugya of acts that have hidden negative consequences. The practical-halachic application — to the modern question of placing bread in two separate bags for disposal — is post-Talmudic but grounded in the same structural principle. ↩
  10. שו״ת אור לציון, ר' בן ציון אבא שאול — the kindergarten lunch-bag question. — ר' בן ציון אבא שאול (1924–1998), Rosh Yeshivat Porat Yosef and one of the leading Sefardi poskim of the late 20th century, author of שו״ת אור לציון. The שיעור brought a teshuvah of his on the practical case of the gannenet hanging children's sandwich-bags on classroom hooks. Rav Ben Zion's framework: the דרך בזיון aspect of hanging-bread attaches specifically to the owner hanging his own bread; when a third party (the teacher) hangs it, the structural concern is different. Exact siman in אור לציון not specified in the שיעור. ↩
  11. האר״י ז״ל — לא יחל דברו ככל היוצא מפיו יעשה as koach hadibbur. — The Arizal reads במדבר ל׳ פסוק ג' — לֹא יַחֵל דְּבָרוֹ כְּכֹל הַיֹּצֵא מִפִּיו יַעֲשֶׂה — on a double layer. The surface halacha (one must fulfill one's נדרים) and the deeper structural reality (the words a Yid speaks are themselves generative — whatever a Yid says is in fact done in the structural machinery of the world). The deeper reading grounds the entire Zohar-framework on the koach of speech. The נשמה being a חלק אלוק ממעל is the conceptual ground: divine words generate divine effects. The Arizal's reading appears across his writings; the specific citation was not given in the שיעור. ↩
  12. גמרא בבא קמא צ״ג ע״א — אל תהי קללת הדיוט קלה בעיניך. — The Gemara codifies the structural parallel to the famous אל תהי ברכת הדיוט קלה בעיניך: the curse of a simple person — even one without halachic standing as a wise man — should not be lightly dismissed. The שיעור pressed the structural application: even the curse of a rasha in Gehinom catches the Yid who triggered it. The Gemara's principle covers the practical case the Zohar Hakadosh raises. ↩
  13. The טשארטקאווער רבי; the מעשה of זלמן השותק of Vienna. — The Chortkover dynasty traces to Ruzhyn. The Vienna kehillah of Chortkover chassidim flourished in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. The mesorah of the מעשה of זלמן השותק — the silent Yid who had cursed his wife at the start of his marriage and kept silence the rest of his life as a תיקון from the Chortkover Rebbe — is preserved in the broader Chortkover-Vienna oral mesorah. Rabbi Ephrati flagged it as part of the cumulative documentation of the structural power of the spoken curse. Exact published source for the maaseh was not specified. ↩
    The מעשה of Salman HaShosek is preserved in chassidish oral mesorah and appears in some published collections of Chortkover and Vienna chassidish maaseiyos, but the שיעור did not name a specific source. For formal citation verify against the chassidish biographical literature.
  14. רב יצחק זילברשטיין — the contemporary tikkun-maaseh of the boy and the friend's mother. — ר' יצחק זילברשטיין, contemporary rav of Ramat Elchanan in Bnei Brak and son-in-law of ר' יוסף שלום אלישיב ז״ל, author of multiple sefarim of practical halacha including חשוקי חמד. The שיעור preserved his maaseh of the boy who davened for his friend's mother to die and came to him for a תיקון after her death. Rabbi Ephrati flagged the structural caveat that not every published Zilberstein maaseh is verbatim accurate — sometimes poskim shape the narrative around the psak — but identified this particular story as documented to be true. The Rizhyner's tikkun (every tefillah, daven for another Yid) is the structural pivot Rav Zilberstein cited. ↩
  15. The ריזשינער — the alternative-tikkun framework. — ר' ישראל פרידמן of Rizhyn (the Rizhyner Rebbe, 1796–1850), foundational figure of the Rizhyn dynasty including the Chortkover line. The Rizhyner's framework that imposing complete silence (as the Chortkover did with Salman HaShosek) is a גזירה שאין הציבור יכול לעמוד בה for a young person — and his alternative of "every tefillah daven for another Yid" — is preserved in the Rizhyn-mesorah literature. The structural conceptual point: the engine that produced the curse (focused daily tefillah) is the same engine that, reversed, produces the תיקון. ↩
  16. רש״י על שמות ל״ט פסוק מ״ג — Moshe's brachah at the Mishkan. — On the pasuk וַיְבָרֶךְ אֹתָם מֹשֶׁה at the completion of the משכן, Rashi identifies the brachah Moshe gave as וִיהִי נֹעַם ה' אֱלֹקֵינוּ עָלֵינוּ וּמַעֲשֵׂה יָדֵינוּ כּוֹנְנָה עָלֵינוּ. The structural parallel: just as Moshe asked for hatzlacha in the work of the משכן, we ask for hatzlacha in the work of our coming week. The pasuk in תהלים פרק צ' that we recite at Motzei Shabbos is literally Moshe's brachah at the completion of the משכן. ↩
  17. The continuation into יושב בסתר at the Mishkan. — The mesorah (cited in the standard מפרשים on the Motzei Shabbos liturgy) that after pronouncing וִיהִי נֹעַם, Moshe continued into יושב בסתר עליון — the Shir Shel Pega'im — because at the moment the משכן was complete and the שכינה descended, the מזיקים were displaced from the world. The structural parallel: just as Moshe pronounced both units sequentially at the Mishkan, we pronounce both sequentially at Motzei Shabbos for the same combined hatzlacha-and-shemira function. The exact Gemara/Medrash citation for this continuation was not specified in the שיעור; the framework is preserved across the Rishonim's treatment of the Motzei Shabbos liturgy. ↩
  18. גמרא שבועות ט״ו ע״ב — יושב בסתר as שיר של פגעים. — The Gemara in Shevuos names תהלים פרק צ״א as שיר של פגעים — the song against harmful forces. The structural function is invoked at three liturgical sites: Motzei Shabbos (as discussed), קריאת שמע על המיטה (because night itself is a time of פגעים), and levayah (because the moment a נשמה leaves the body is a moment of maximal exposure to מזיקים). The שיעור flagged all three contexts as expressions of the same underlying principle: the unit serves wherever a structural seam between worlds creates an opening for harmful forces. ↩
  19. גמרא סוטה מ״ט ע״ב — the world sustained by קדושה דסידרא and אמן יהא שמיה רבא. — The Gemara explicitly: in גלות, אֵין לְךָ יוֹם שֶׁאֵין קִלְלָתוֹ מְרֻבָּה מִשֶּׁל חֲבֵרוֹ — every day's curses outnumber the day before. The world cannot sustain itself on its natural balance. The two structural acts that hold the world up are קדושה דסידרא (the recitation of ואתה קדוש יושב תהילות ישראל) and answering אמן יהא שמיה רבא at קדיש. The Gemara is the textual anchor for the unique cosmic weight that קדושה דסידרא carries. ↩
  20. משנה ברורה (quoting אבודרהם) — no geulah at night; skipping ובא לציון. — The Mishnah Berurah quotes the אבודרהם that there is no גאולה at night, and therefore the standard daily ובא לציון opening of קדושה דסידרא — which speaks geulah-language — is omitted at Motzei Shabbos. We open from ואתה קדוש directly. The structural-night-time exclusion of geulah-language was treated in earlier שיעורים in the kehillah's run through ערבית. ↩
  21. רמ״א, שולחן ערוך אורח חיים סימן רצ״ה — ויתן לך at Motzei Shabbos. — The Rema codifies the recitation of ויתן לך — the compilation of pesukim of brachah — at Motzei Shabbos. Some kehillot say it publicly together in shul (the Young Israel-style minhag); others say it privately at home after Havdalah (the Arizal's own practice). The Rema's codification preserves the obligation regardless of the precise placement. ↩
  22. קדושת לוי / Berditchever — גוט פון אברהם Yiddish tefillah. — ר' לוי יצחק מברדיטשוב (the Berditchever Rebbe, 1740–1809) composed the famous Motzei Shabbos Yiddish tefillah opening גוט פון אברהם, גוט פון אברהם, גוט פון יצחק, גוט פון יעקב — a structural counterpart to ויתן לך in chassidish-Yiddish register, with its own niggun. The tefillah post-dates the Rama and is not codified in שולחן ערוך; it is preserved across the chassidish prayer-mesorah. The שיעור flagged it as a beautiful and underappreciated piece of the broader chassidish-tefillah landscape. ↩
  23. שו״ת ויקרא אברהם, ר' אברהם הדאדי — the chiddush nifla on Yom Tov mid-week. — ר' אברהם הדאדי (1801–1874) was the leading rav of the Tripolitanian Jewish community in 19th-century Libya, author of שו״ת ויקרא אברהם and other works. The שיעור brought his striking chiddush: in a week containing a Yom Tov, הקדוש ברוך הוא does not reactivate Gehinom at all — the off-period flows directly from Shabbos through Yom Tov without interruption. Rav Adadi brings a ראיה from the Zohar Hakadosh. The chiddush is structurally striking because it preserves the surface halacha (don't say וִיהִי נֹעַם that week) while supplying a much deeper conceptual reason than the standard "no full work-week" answer. Rabbi Ephrati framed it openly as a chiddush whose acceptance among later אחרונים he had not fully traced. ↩
    The exact siman in שו״ת ויקרא אברהם was not specified in the שיעור. For formal citation verify against the published edition. The biographical detail on Tripolitanian Jewry that the שיעור preserved — a centuries-old community with distinctive minhagim, now largely in ארץ ישראל post-קיבוץ-גלויות, with no major American kehillah — is itself worth recording.
  24. בן איש חי — kabbalistic insistence on וִיהִי נֹעַם in a Yom-Tov-mid-week scenario. — ר' יוסף חיים בן אליהו אל-חכם (the Ben Ish Chai, 1834–1909) of Baghdad. On the Ben Ish Chai's kabbalistic reading, the structural function of וִיהִי נֹעַם is not primarily Gehinom-delay but the drawing-forward (mamshich) of Shabbos kedusha into the coming week. On that frame, the Gehinom-reactivation question does not exhaust the function of the prayer, and one must say וִיהִי נֹעַם even in a Yom-Tov-mid-week scenario. The שיעור preserved the historical-operational detail that the Ben Ish Chai personally walked from shul to shul in Baghdad changing the local minhag to say וִיהִי נֹעַם in such weeks — the kabbalistic argument was strong enough to override the prior local practice. ↩
  25. יעב״ץ בתשובה — the Erev Pesach pilpul. — ר' יעקב עמדין (the Ya'avetz, 1697–1776) addressed in a teshuvah the edge case of a Motzei Shabbos where Erev Pesach falls during the coming week. Erev Pesach has an איסור מלאכה from chatzos but is not technically a full Yom Tov. The Ya'avetz works through the question in both directions — should one say וִיהִי נֹעַם? The Mishnah Berurah's psak follows the position that yes, one says it, because Erev Pesach's restriction does not constitute the structural-Yom-Tov disruption that would suppress the recitation. ↩
  26. A ראשון on יושב תהלות ישראל as "awaiting" rather than "sitting". — One of the ראשונים (the שיעור did not name the specific Rishon) reads יוֹשֵׁב תְּהִלּוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל through the secondary sense of יוֹשֵׁב — not "sitting on" but "awaiting" (יוֹשֵׁב וּמְצַפֶּה). The Rishon's reading: הקדוש ברוך הוא is structurally awaiting Klal Yisrael's Motzei Shabbos praises before reactivating Gehinom. The pasuk's surface meaning becomes its operational mechanism. The reading is structurally elegant and was preserved in the שיעור as a beautiful Rishon-level chiddush. The specific Rishon-attribution was not given. ↩
    The שיעור referenced "one of the Rishonim" without naming the specific source. Possible candidates include the Avudraham, the Manhig, the Kolbo, or one of the early commentaries on the siddur; verify before citing.
  27. מחזור ויטרי — second reason for besamim at Havdalah: the bad odor from reactivating Gehinom. — ר' שמחה מויטרי (primary תלמיד of Rashi, c. 1080–1105) in his מחזור ויטרי records a secondary reason for the Havdalah custom of smelling בשמים. The primary reason (Gemara in ביצה ל״ב) is to comfort the נשמה יתירה as it departs. The Machzor Vitry's secondary reason: when Gehinom reactivates and the resha'im are pushed back into the fire, the burning produces a bad odor in the spiritual atmosphere; the בשמים at Havdalah counteract that odor so the Yid is not influenced by it. The double reason gives the practice structural resilience: even in scenarios where the Gehinom-reactivation does not occur (per Rav Adadi), the נשמה-יתירה reason still applies. The Machzor Vitry was central to last week's discussion of the נשמות in shul; here it returns at the Havdalah seam. ↩
  28. The בעל שם טוב's vision through the window — the Bach (or Sassover Rebbe) passing through Gehinom first to elevate followers. — The chassidish mesorah of the Baal Shem Tov (ר' ישראל בן אליעזר, 1698–1760) standing at the window watching the post-mortem journey of the בח (ר' יואל סירקיש, 1561–1640, the gadol who was מתיר חדש בחוץ לארץ — in his role as the source of the contemporary heter for eating non-yashan grains in chutz la'aretz). On the chassidish reading, the Bach refused to enter Gan Eden directly upon petira and insisted on passing through Gehinom first to extract every Yid who had relied on his heter without sufficient additional zechuyos, bringing them up to Gan Eden with him. Only after the last person was rescued did the Bach himself enter. A parallel version of the maaseh names the סאסאווער רעבע (ר' משה לייב סאסאווער, 1745–1807) instead of the Bach. The structural-conceptual point is the same: a tzaddik passes through Gehinom first to elevate those whose merit was insufficient. The mesorah is well-attested across the chassidish literature; exact published source not specified in the שיעור. ↩
    The maaseh exists in multiple parallel versions — the Bach-version and the Sassover-version are both preserved in chassidish oral and published mesorah. The conceptual point (a tzaddik enters Gehinom first to elevate his followers) is the shared structural element; the specific named tzaddik varies. For formal citation verify against published chassidish maaseh collections (e.g., שיבחי הבעל שם טוב, דגל מחנה אפרים, or Sassover-specific biographical literature).
