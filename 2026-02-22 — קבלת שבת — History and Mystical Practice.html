Rabbi Ephrati · Sunday Morning שיעור on תפילה

קבלת שבת

From the Fields of צפת to Our Siddur — History, Mysticism, and the Wedding of the שבת
Recorded 22 February 2026 · Reworked from the original שיעור

קבלת שבת as we know it is barely 450 years old. The Talmudic sages had no order of לכו נרננה, no לכה דודי, no procession at the western door. What they had was something far simpler — and in some ways far more vivid: they walked out of the city to greet the שבת in the fields. This שיעור traces how that quiet practice was transformed in 16th-century צפת by the אריז״ל and his circle into the liturgy we open our סדורים to every Friday afternoon, and what each piece of it is meant to do to us.

I. The Talmudic Roots — Going Out to the Fields

Before there was a Kabbalas Shabbos service, there was a practice. The גמרא בשבת קי״ט ע״א[1] records that רבי חנינא would wrap himself in his fine garments on ערב שבת toward sunset and announce, בואו ונצא לקראת שבת המלכה — "Come, let us go out to greet the Shabbos Queen." רבי ינאי would don his Shabbos clothing and call out, בואי כלה, בואי כלה — "Come, bride; come, bride." Two simple, exquisite gestures: the metaphor of queen, the metaphor of bride, and a physical movement of going out beyond one's normal place to meet the day.

No piyut. No psalms. No service. Just one or two words on the lips and a step beyond the threshold. That is the raw material from which everything we now do at קבלת שבת was eventually built.

II. The רמב״ם — Sitting in Honor and Waiting

The רמב״ם בהלכות שבת פרק ל הלכה ב[2] codifies the practice of preparing for שבת at a time when no formal קבלת שבת service yet existed. He describes how a person should arrange his home, immerse, dress in his Shabbos garments, wrap in his tallis, and then sit — בכובד ראש, in a dignified posture — to wait for the arrival of the day. The רמב״ם specifically invokes the Talmudic רבי חנינא and רבי ינאי as the models.

The contrast with our era is jarring. The רמב״ם paints a picture of a yid finished with all his work, bathed and dressed, sitting still in his place — waiting. We arrive at שיעור-time having put the chicken in the warmer two minutes earlier and rushed through traffic. The רמב״ם is not asking us to invent a new prayer; he is asking us to honor the moment with stillness. That stillness is the dignity that the formal קבלת שבת service was, centuries later, designed to recapture.

III. The Revolution of צפת — אריז״ל and His Circle

Everything changes between roughly 1570 and 1572 — a window of perhaps two-and-a-half years in the city of צפת. The אריז״ל, רבי יצחק לוריא, arrives in 1570 and dies in 1572. In that brief span, surrounded by a circle of giants — the רמ״ק (רבי משה קורדובירו), רבי שלמה אלקבץ, רבי חיים ויטאל, and others — he transforms the entire spiritual landscape of עם ישראל. The siddur is not the same after him.

The aftershocks of that two-year window touch nearly every page of our siddur, but in קבלת שבת the imprint is unmistakable. The אריז״ל's תלמידים — most prominently רבי חיים ויטאל — record his practice in works like פרי עץ חיים, שער השבת[3]. There we find the kavanot and the order of mizmorim that would, within a generation, spread across communities from Italy to פולין to ארצות ישראל.

The Two-Year Window Almost everything we associate with the kabbalistic stratum of our siddur — קבלת שבת, much of the kavanot of the אריז״ל, the structuring of לחיים moments — emerges from this single window in צפת. Cities and centuries do not usually produce that kind of transformation that quickly. The fact that קבלת שבת did is itself part of its mystery.

IV. רבי שלמה אלקבץ and לכה דודי

רבי שלמה אלקבץ הלוי (c. 1500–c. 1576) was a refugee — descended from families expelled from Spain in 1492. He moved through Greece and Edirne (Adrianople), where he was a תלמיד of the great kabbalist רבי יוסף טיטצאק[4], before eventually settling in צפת. There he became the brother-in-law of the רמ״ק and a member of the inner circle around the אריז״ל.

Around 1540 he composed לכה דודי[5]. The piyut is built on an acrostic spelling שלמה הלוי. Its imagery — the field of holy apples (חקל תפוחין קדישין), the procession to greet the bride, the appeal at the close, בואי כלה — is not generic poetry. Each phrase is tied to specific kavanot in the אריז״ל's system.

This is what makes לכה דודי remarkable in the אריז״ל's own evaluation. The אריז״ל was famously skeptical of piyut. Most piyutim that predated him, in his view, did not align with the Lurianic kavanos and were not to be recited. The מגן אברהם[6] brings this position. לכה דודי, however, was the rare exception that the אריז״ל not only accepted but championed, because every line — composed before the Lurianic system was fully articulated — somehow already embodied it. The piyut first appeared in print in a siddur in 1584; from there it spread to virtually every nusach of the Jewish world.

The other major work of אלקבץ was מנות הלוי[7], a commentary on מגילת אסתר, written as a פורים מתנה to his future father-in-law before his marriage. To this day, communities study from it on פורים.

V. The Six Mizmorim — Six Days of the Week

The אריז״ל instituted that we precede לכה דודי with six mizmorim from תהלים — פרק צ״ה through פרק צ״ט and פרק כ״ט (לכו נרננה, שירו לה' שיר חדש, ה' מלך תגל הארץ, מזמור שירו לה' שיר חדש, ה' מלך ירגזו עמים, מזמור לדוד הבו לה')[8]. These six correspond to the six weekdays. The thread that runs through them is מלכות ה' — the kingship of הקב״ה — because before we welcome the bride, we must first crown the King.

Not every nusach adopted all six. Some kehillot — particularly Sefardic ones — say only some of them. But the six together form the spiritual structure that the אריז״ל designed: each mizmor "discharges" a weekday, returning the worshiper to clean kingship-of-Hashem before the שבת arrives.

מזמור לדוד (תהלים כ״ט) and the Seven קולות

מזמור לדוד (Tehillim 29) is the climactic mizmor before לכה דודי, and it is structured around the phrase קול ה', which appears seven times. These seven קולות correspond to the seven ברכות of שמונה עשרה of שבת[9]. The custom is therefore to stand for this mizmor and to focus on aligning the seven קולות with what is about to come in tefillah.

VI. The נשמה יתירה — Three Layers

The גמרא בביצה ט״ז ע״א[10] teaches that on שבת a person receives a נשמה יתירה — an "additional soul." But what we receive is not a single thing; it is layered. The classical structure of soul has three primary parts: נפש, רוח, נשמה. The kabbalistic tradition describes the progression of קבלת שבת as the gradual acquisition of all three. The opening mizmorim awaken the נפש of שבת; לכה דודי and the procession draw down the רוח; the entrance into מזמור שיר ליום השבת and the formal evening service crowns the soul with the נשמה of שבת.

Practically: the reason a person feels something different at this moment of the davening — the singing, the dancing, the unguarded joy at לכה דודי — is not sentimentality. It is the נפש of שבת settling in.

VII. The Wedding — Three Times בואי כלה

Why do we say בואי כלה three times at the close of לכה דודי? The first two are obvious from the structure of the piyut. The third — said with the famous turn toward the back of the shul — was the puzzle the שיעור circled.

The answer drawn from the kabbalistic tradition: the three correspond to the three stages of a Jewish wedding[11]. The first בואי כלה parallels the כלה being greeted — the pre-חופה moment when the חתן goes out to meet her. The second parallels the חופה itself, where the union is sanctified. The third parallels the ייחוד — the seclusion after the ceremony, where the marriage is consummated in private union. By that third בואי כלה, we are no longer greeting שבת; we are becoming one with שבת.

This is the שיעור's hashkafic point: the entire procession of קבלת שבת is staged as a wedding, and we are not spectators but the חתן. The שבת is the כלה, the יד וזרוע of הקב״ה chose us as her zivug. רש״י על בראשית פרק ב[12] brings the מדרש: each day of creation had its pair (Sunday with Monday, Tuesday with Wednesday, Thursday with Friday) — only שבת was alone. שבת complained, "You did not give me a בן זוג." The Holy One answered: כנסת ישראל בן זוגך — "כלל ישראל is your partner."

Halachic Hash The רמ״א, אורח חיים רס״ב[13] records the practice of standing and turning to greet the שבת at the final בואי כלה. Some communities do not turn at all; others take a few steps back and forth in a fixed minhag.

VIII. שמור and זכור — Friday Night and Day

The opening line of לכה דודי is built on the famous teaching of ספר הבהיר[14]: שמור וזכור בדיבור אחד — "guard" and "remember" were said in a single Divine utterance at סיני. But within that unity there is a directional quality. שמור — the negative, the work-prohibition — is associated with night, with Friday-night-into-Shabbos. זכור — the positive, the sanctification — is associated with the day. That is why לכה דודי opens specifically שמור וזכור in that order: we are entering the night, and the night is the gate of שמור.

Why night gets שמור and day gets זכור is a קבלה in the deepest sense — the שיעור does not pretend to explain it fully. The pattern, however, is woven through the entire structure of שבת observance: the kiddush we say at night opens with ויכולו and the language of זכור; the kiddush of day is built around language of שמור in the second luchot. The עלייה at קבלת שבת is the night-gate, and שמור opens it.

IX. Adam HaRishon and מזמור שיר ליום השבת

After לכה דודי we say מזמור שיר ליום השבת (תהלים צ״ב). The מדרש בראשית רבה[15] attributes this mizmor to אדם הראשון himself. The narrative thread is striking. The entire saga of אדם — his creation, the eating from the עץ הדעת, the judgment — happened on Friday, in compressed time. As the sun set and שבת approached, הקב״ה's judgment was poised to fall. The שבת itself, the מדרש relates, intervened. שבת stood before הקב״ה and said: "On my day You will not punish your firstborn child."

אדם, saved by the שבת, composed מזמור שיר ליום השבת as a song of gratitude — gratitude not for some abstract thing, but for the שבת that defended him. That is why we sing it in the lull after לכה דודי: the wedding is complete, the כלה has been brought into our domain, and now we sing — as אדם sang — to thank her for what she has just done.

This is also the source for the strong custom of singing and dancing at קבלת שבת. The גמרא בכתובות י״ז ע״א[16] establishes that מצוה לשמח חתן וכלה — there is a mitzvah to bring joy to a חתן and כלה. If קבלת שבת is the wedding, the singing and dancing are not "decorative" — they are the formal rejoicing of the marriage.

X. The קדיש After מזמור שיר — A Halachic Puzzle

After מזמור שיר ליום השבת, we say a קדיש. The question raised in the שיעור: which day does this קדיש belong to? Is it a קדיש of Friday (closing out the weekday tefillah of מנחה / קבלת שבת), or is it a קדיש of שבת (introducing the עברית of שבת)?

The practical question is yahrtzeit: if a person is in the year of אבילות, can he take this קדיש after his standard yahrtzeit period has ended? Or is it already a "Shabbos Kaddish," in which case it would no longer be his?

The שיעור reports a position attributed in the conversation to ר' יודל (a member of the kehillah) that the קדיש belongs to the Friday tefillah, not to שבת — analogous to the קדיש after רוצה ראש השנה. The שיעור suggests this should be more carefully verified in the major poskim of או״ח רס״ב–רס״ז before being relied upon as halachah למעשה. The exact citation should be confirmed in the שולחן ערוך הרב, משנה ברורה, and the קצות השלחן, where the question of the status of this Kaddish is discussed in different formulations.

XI. Practical Halachah — Timing and Mindset

When Does קבלת שבת Take Effect?

שולחן ערוך, אורח חיים רס״א סעיף ב[17] establishes that one can be מקבל שבת — that is, accept the שבת upon oneself — from פלג המנחה onward. The earliest moment that the actual מלאכה-prohibition takes hold is when one says the words committing oneself to שבת — for many, the words בואי כלה שבת מלכתא in the third stanza of לכה דודי. From that moment, no מלאכה may be done.

"Good שבת" — From When?

The שיעור's hashkafic point: the greeting "Good שבת" is itself an act of acceptance — by saying it, you are acknowledging that, for you, it is now שבת. Therefore the greeting cannot be used freely on Friday afternoon before פלג המנחה[18]. From פלג onward, however, one may already use it.

Mental Disconnection

The שולחן ערוך, אורח חיים ש״ו[19] discusses the prohibition of דיבור על עסקי חול on שבת. The שיעור pressed beyond the verbal: the worshiper must enter שבת with the mindset that all weekday concerns are completed. Whether the deal closed or didn't, whether the check cleared or didn't — for the next 25 hours, it is closed. There is a halachic reason and a hashkafic reason: halachically because הרהור on weekday matters disturbs שבת; hashkafically because we are in ייחוד with the שבת המלכה, and one cannot be in ייחוד while still glancing at the inbox.

XII. ספר הבהיר and the Italian Renaissance

An aside from the שיעור — fascinating but properly classified as a historical-cultural note rather than as halachah:

The שיעור raised the influence of ספר הבהיר, one of the foundational kabbalistic texts, on the Italian Renaissance. The line of transmission claimed: the work was translated into Latin in the 15th century by a baptized Jew working under the patronage of the Medici family in Florence, and through this translation it became known to Pico della Mirandola (1463–1494) and to scholars in his circle. The שיעור further claimed that Michelangelo's heavy reliance on Old Testament themes in the Sistine Chapel ceiling reflects this Kabbalistic-Jewish influence, mediated through Pico's circle.

This historical claim — Pico's interest in Kabbalah — is well documented in the academic literature on the Italian Renaissance and is attested in Pico's own Conclusiones (1486), where he argues that Christian Kabbalah confirms Christian doctrine. The further claim about Michelangelo, however, is a matter of art-historical interpretation rather than documented record. The שיעור attributed the connection to a teaching heard from הר' שאול ברמן; the precise source within his shiurim should be located before relying on the connection as established history.

XIII. The 3rd of שבט Falling on Friday

At the very opening of the שיעור, the speaker mentioned a tradition cited by the חיי אדם, in the name of ר' אייזיק הדרשן (16th–early 17th century)[20], that when the third of שבט falls on a Friday — ג' שבט — the winter to follow will be especially severe. The textual hook is a גימטריה on the word בחיגא. This is a popular tradition and was cited as a curiosity, not as a halachic ruling.

The arc from רבי חנינא wrapping his garments and walking into the field, to a yid in our shul standing at בואי כלה with eyes closed, is unbroken — but only because of two-and-a-half years in צפת. Without that window, our קבלת שבת would still be the rambam's silent, dignified waiting. With it, the whole congregation enters into the wedding together. Both are correct. Both are how a yid greets the שבת. The siddur we hold preserves the second; the silence the רמב״ם asks for is the first. The שיעור's quiet plea was: do not lose the silence even while singing.

מראה מקומות

  1. שבת קי״ט ע״א. — The locus classicus for the Talmudic practice of going out to greet שבת. רבי חנינא wraps in his finest garments at sunset on ערב שבת and announces, בואו ונצא לקראת שבת המלכה; רבי ינאי dons his Shabbos garments and calls, בואי כלה, בואי כלה. From these two passages flow both the "queen" metaphor and the "bride" metaphor that structure all subsequent קבלת שבת liturgy. ↩
  2. רמב״ם, הלכות שבת פרק ל הלכה ב. — The רמב״ם describes the proper preparation for שבת: bathing, immersing, dressing in Shabbos garments, wrapping in tallis, and sitting בכובד ראש waiting for the day. He invokes רבי חנינא and רבי ינאי as the models. Significantly, no formal קבלת שבת service appears here — the רמב״ם codifies a posture and a state of mind, not a liturgy. ↩
  3. פרי עץ חיים, שער השבת (מאת ר' חיים ויטאל). — The major repository of the kavanot of the אריז״ל for the entire course of the שבת tefillot, recorded by his foremost תלמיד. The שער השבת is where the structure of the six mizmorim, the kavanot for each phrase of לכה דודי, and the hierarchical drawing-down of the נפש-רוח-נשמה of שבת are laid out. Composed in the late 16th century; printed editions appear in the 17th century onward. ↩
  4. רבי יוסף טיטצאק (Salonika/Adrianople, c. 1465–c. 1545). — A leading Sefardi posek and kabbalist of the post-expulsion generation; teacher of רבי שלמה אלקבץ in his pre-Tzfat years. Cited frequently in the בית יוסף as a halachic authority. His kabbalistic teachings are preserved primarily through students rather than through extensive printed works of his own. ↩
  5. לכה דודי, מאת ר' שלמה הלוי אלקבץ, c. 1540 (first printed in a siddur, Salonika 1584). — The piyut consists of nine stanzas plus a refrain; the first letters of stanzas 1–8 spell שלמה הלוי. Its imagery — the חקל תפוחין (field of holy apples), the שתי לוחות, the ספר התורה, the הדורי coming of the משיח — is built from the language of the זוהר and from kabbalistic sources predating the אריז״ל. It is the only pre-Lurianic piyut accepted into the אריז״ל's nusach without modification. ↩
  6. מגן אברהם, אורח חיים סימן ס״ח. — The מגן אברהם (ר' אברהם אבלי הלוי גומבינר, 17th century) addresses the question of which piyutim are recited and in what manner. He brings the well-known position that the אריז״ל was selective regarding piyutim composed before his time, and notes which were retained in the Lurianic nusach. The מגן אברהם's discussion is the standard halachic reference for the אריז״ל's stance on piyut. ↩
  7. מנות הלוי, מאת ר' שלמה הלוי אלקבץ, ונציה ש״ה (1585). — A commentary on מגילת אסתר composed by אלקבץ as a פורים gift to his future father-in-law. The work weaves halachic, midrashic, and kabbalistic readings of the מגילה. It became one of the standard עדה דרבנן commentaries learned on פורים, particularly in Sefardic communities. ↩
  8. תהלים פרקים צ״ה–צ״ט וכ״ט — the six psalms of קבלת שבת. — The selection and order are the אריז״ל's institution. The six correspond to the six weekdays; the thread that runs through them is מלכות ה' — the kingship of הקב״ה — because before welcoming the bride one must first crown the King. Some Sefardic kehillot omit one or more; the standard nusach in the Lurianic tradition retains all six. ↩
  9. תהלים פרק כ״ט — the seven קולות ה'. — The phrase קול ה' appears seven times in this single mizmor (verses 3, 4 [twice], 5, 7, 8, 9). The kabbalistic reading aligns these seven with the seven ברכות of the שבת עמידה. The custom of standing for מזמור לדוד and of meditating on this correspondence is brought in many Sefardim and Chassidim siddurim with kavanos. ↩
  10. ביצה ט״ז ע״א — נשמה יתירה. — ר' שמעון בן לקיש teaches that on ערב שבת הקב״ה gives the worshiper an additional soul, and at מוצאי שבת that soul departs — and that is why we say בשמים in הבדלה, to revive the now-bereaved נפש. The kabbalistic tradition expands the single "additional soul" into the three-tier structure (נפש, רוח, נשמה) acquired progressively across the שבת tefillot. ↩
  11. תיקוני זוהר ותקון מ״ז; שולחן ערוך אבן העזר סימן ס״א וסימן ס״ב. — The three stages of the Jewish wedding — חופה (canopy / greeting), נישואין (consummating union as a halachic act), and ייחוד (private seclusion completing the marriage) — are codified in the laws of אבן העזר. The kabbalistic mapping of these three stages onto the three repetitions of בואי כלה at the close of לכה דודי appears in תיקוני זוהר and is developed in the kavanos works of the אריז״ל's circle. ↩
  12. רש״י על בראשית פרק ב פסוק ב; בראשית רבה י״א ח. — רש״י cites the מדרש: each day of creation has its zivug — Sunday with Monday, Tuesday with Wednesday, Thursday with Friday — but the שבת had no partner. שבת complained to הקב״ה: לכולם נתת בן זוג ולי לא נתת. הקב״ה answered: כנסת ישראל בן זוגך. This מדרש is the foundation of the wedding metaphor for קבלת שבת. ↩
  13. רמ״א, אורח חיים סימן רס״ב. — The רמ״א (ר' משה איסרליש, 16th-century Krakow) records the Ashkenazic practice of standing and turning to face the entrance of the shul at the final stanza of לכה דודי, in order to physically greet the שבת as it enters. Some kehillot have a custom of taking three small steps as well; the precise minhag varies, but the turn itself is widely codified. ↩
  14. ספר הבהיר, סימנים נ״ה–נ״ז (ed. Margalioth) on שמור וזכור. — ספר הבהיר, one of the earliest extant kabbalistic works (12th–13th century, Provence), establishes the gendered/dimensional pairing: שמור as the feminine, night-aspect of שבת; זכור as the masculine, day-aspect. The teaching that the two were said in a single Divine utterance derives from שבועות כ׳ ע״ב and ראש השנה כ״ז ע״א; ספר הבהיר's contribution is the directional reading. ↩
  15. בראשית רבה כ״ב יג ופרקי דרבי אליעזר פרק י״ט on Adam and מזמור שיר. — The מדרש attributes מזמור שיר ליום השבת (תהלים צ״ב) to אדם הראשון. The narrative: as the sun set on the Friday of his sin, judgment was poised; the שבת interceded with הקב״ה; אדם, saved, composed the mizmor as a song of gratitude. פרקי דרבי אליעזר develops the story; the version with שבת as the literal advocate appears in several midrashic sources and is cited by רש״י in his commentary on תהלים צ״ב. ↩
  16. כתובות י״ז ע״א — מצוה לשמח חתן וכלה. — The גמרא records the obligation to bring joy to a חתן and כלה through song and dance, and tells the famous account of the תנאים — including ר' יהודה בר אילעאי — who would dance before the כלה holding myrtle branches. The mapping of this halachah onto קבלת שבת — that the singing and dancing at לכה דודי is a literal fulfillment of מצוה לשמח חתן וכלה, with שבת as the כלה — is found in the kavanos works of the אריז״ל's circle. ↩
  17. שולחן ערוך, אורח חיים סימן רס״א סעיף ב. — The basic halachah of when one may be מקבל שבת. The earliest moment is פלג המנחה; the latest the absolute beginning of שקיעה. Once one has been מקבל, no מלאכה may be performed. The practical question of which words in קבלת שבת constitute the formal acceptance is treated by the מגן אברהם, the משנה ברורה, and the שולחן ערוך הרב; the dominant view is the words בואי כלה שבת מלכתא in the final stanza of לכה דודי. ↩
  18. שולחן ערוך, אורח חיים סימן רס״א; משנה ברורה ס״ק כ״א. — The status of the greeting "Good שבת" on Friday afternoon is treated by the משנה ברורה in the context of "speaking of שבת before שבת." The substantive point: the greeting has a quasi-ritual quality of קבלה, and the poskim therefore restrict its use until פלג המנחה. The שיעור's sharper formulation — that saying "Good שבת" already constitutes a degree of acceptance — is stronger than the strict letter of the שולחן ערוך and reflects the עומק of the kabbalistic reading. ↩
  19. שולחן ערוך, אורח חיים סימן ש״ו ושו״ע סימן שכ״ז. — The prohibition of דיבור על עסקי חול on שבת. The שולחן ערוך establishes the basic prohibition on speech about business affairs. Subsequent poskim — particularly the משנה ברורה and the שולחן ערוך הרב — extend the discussion to הרהור (mental dwelling on weekday matters), although the formal halachah generally distinguishes between speech (forbidden) and pure thought (permitted but discouraged). The שיעור's mussar point — that one must enter שבת with the mindset that all weekday concerns are completed — draws on the Chassidic and Mussar traditions of שבת preparation. ↩
  20. חיי אדם בשם ר' אייזיק הדרשן on the 3rd of שבט. — The popular tradition that when the third of שבט falls on a Friday — ג' שבט — the winter that follows will be unusually severe, based on a wordplay on בחיגא. ר' אייזיק הדרשן (Isaac Hadarshan, 16th–early 17th century) is mentioned as the source. This is a popular astrological/calendrical curiosity rather than a halachic ruling and was cited in the שיעור as such. ↩
