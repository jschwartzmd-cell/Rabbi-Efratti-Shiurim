Rabbi Ephrati · Erev יום כיפור Drasha

The Cholera Epidemic and רבי ישראל סלנטר's Decision on יום כיפור

Leadership, Halachic Process, and פיקוח נפש in וילנא, תר״ח (1848)
Recorded 28 September 2025 · Reworked from the original שיעור

Before יום טוב, I customarily set aside our regular שיעור on תפילה and turn to a topic connected to the approaching חג. Today we will examine one of the most famous and most disputed episodes in nineteenth-century Lithuanian תורה — רבי ישראל סלנטר's decision on יום כיפור during the cholera epidemic of תר״ח. It is more than a piece of history; for serious students of the ישיבה world it lights up some of the deepest questions about הלכתי process, rabbinic authority, and the boundary between פיקוח נפש and the integrity of communal הלכה.

I. רבי ישראל סלנטר and the Beginning of the מוסר Movement

The גאון Who Became a דרשן

רבי ישראל סלנטר is universally remembered as the founder of the מוסר movement. People sometimes assume that means he was "only" a בעל מוסר — a man of מידות, of צדקות, of unusual sensitivity in בין אדם לחברו. The truth is that his genius extended far beyond all of that. He was a brilliant גאון, exceptional in תלמוד and in למדנות. As a young בחור and אברך he was known first and foremost for his gaonus, not for his piety.

His transformation came through רבי יוסף זונדל מסלנט — an איש מוסר who reshaped his entire approach to עבודת ה׳. From that point on רבי ישראל underwent something close to a paradigm shift. Rather than confine himself to delivering שיעורים inside the ישיבה of וילנא, he turned outward. He was an extraordinary נואם and דרשן, and he began speaking directly to the המונים — the common people.

The Social Revolution in וילנא

To grasp how revolutionary that was you must picture וילנא — ירושלים דליטא — of that era. There you were respected and valued only if you were a למדן and a תלמיד חכם. Wealth mattered, of course; it always has in Jewish communal life. But beyond תורה and money, if you were merely a decent Jew — someone who excelled in בין אדם לחברו, who made others feel valued, an איש ירא שמים — no one particularly noticed you.

רבי ישראל סלנטר wanted to overturn that. He sought to elevate the ideal of being a Jew of בין אדם לחברו, of caring not to embarrass others, not to humiliate. He wanted those values at the very front of Jewish consciousness. People flocked to his דרשות. His impact was so profound that several ישיבות formally adopted his approach, and the מוסר movement was born.

Opposition Inside the ישיבה World

Not a small number of גדולי תורה opposed his methodology. Their argument was that excessive מוסר study would detract from למדנות, from real learning of גמרא. Today the overwhelming majority of ישיבות learn מוסר, with one notable exception: בריסק. In ישיבת בריסק in ירושלים, where I learned, there are still no ספרי מוסר on the shelves. There is no סדר מוסר. בריסק remains, to this day, the only ישיבה in the world that maintains the original opposition.

An Aside Late in life — about a decade before his passing — רבי ישראל סלנטר suffered a stroke and was paralyzed for a time. Many legends surround it. The doctors told him he was thinking too much about learning, and prescribed two hours of non-intellectual activity each day. What does a brilliant mind do for two hours when not learning? He apprenticed with a נגר and became a skilled cabinetmaker, building ארונות with his own hands. Two hours every day he worked his hands, occupied his mind differently — and his health improved.

II. The Sources for the Story

"The Making of a Gadol"

There is a famous and controversial sefer called The Making of a Gadol[1] by רבי נתן קמנצקי זצ״ל, son of הגאון רבי יעקב קמנצקי and brother of רבי שמואל קמנצקי שליט״א. He was a ראש ישיבה in איטרי and an exceptional תלמיד חכם.

When the sefer was first published — about fifteen years ago — many רבנים in the חרדי world reacted very strongly. They placed it in חרם and declared it forbidden to buy. Naturally, I went straight to the store and bought it, like many others. It became a bestseller. The sefer is controversial because it includes details not necessarily meant for public consumption — even small things, like the fact that רבי ישראל סלנטר smoked. On page 1136 there is an extensive discussion of the קידוש incident on יום כיפור.

"מקור ברוך" of רבי ברוך אפשטיין

רבי ברוך אפשטיין[2] — son of the ערוך השלחן and nephew of the נציב — authored several major works: the תורה תמימה, ברוך שאמר, and an autobiography called מקור ברוך, later translated as My Uncle the Netziv. He was both a גאון and תלמיד חכם and a wealthy בנקאי who managed one of the principal banks in וילנא. מקור ברוך contains a wealth of information about the Lithuanian תורה world, about the נציב, about רבי חיים בריסקר, and about his father the ערוך השלחן. He documents this story in detail, and his version is the one most people know.

III. The Cholera Epidemic of תר״ח

The Medical Crisis

In 1848 — תר״ח — a massive cholera epidemic struck Eastern Europe. Cholera epidemics were relatively common in nineteenth-century Poland, Germany, parts of Belarus and Russia. The Hebrew term used was כולרא — חולי רע, "the evil disease." Cholera is a horrifying illness. The victim cannot digest food, vomits continuously, suffers severe diarrhea, becomes rapidly dehydrated, and very quickly reaches a state of פיקוח נפש.

וילנא Under Siege

The epidemic raged through וילנא precisely during the months of אלול and תשרי. וילנא — ירושלים דליטא — was the preeminent center of תורה scholarship in that region, home to countless תלמידי חכמים, רבנים, and מחברי ספרים. The cholera struck right in אלול-תשרי.

The Communal Deliberation

Throughout עשרת ימי תשובה everyone in וילנא spoke of one thing only. All the physicians in the city — Jewish religious doctors, non-religious Jewish doctors, and gentile doctors — declared the same thing in unison: based on the medical knowledge of that era, if a person did not eat or drink for twenty-four to twenty-seven hours, he became significantly more vulnerable to contracting this disease. (The disease, incidentally, came from contaminated water.) If you do not strengthen and bolster your immune system, there is grave concern that you will become vulnerable. And once you contract it, it is a matter of פיקוח נפש. People were falling like flies.

We can all relate — we remember COVID-19 — but this was much worse, and far more people died. In the densely packed Jewish quarter of וילנא, where they lived on top of one another, the disease spread easily. All the doctors said unequivocally: there can be no fasting. Fasting will make you vulnerable.

Every Jew in וילנא — every תלמיד חכם, every חסיד — debated back and forth: Should we eat? Should we not eat? What should we do? Many people did not want to eat. Healthy individuals who had not contracted the disease did not want to eat. I do not believe they understood then that such diseases have an incubation period of two days to a week — that you can already be sick without exhibiting symptoms.

IV. The Decision on יום כיפור

רבי ישראל's Bold Action

On יום כיפור תר״ח רבי ישראל סלנטר rose. He went to the main בית הכנסת of וילנא. Bear in mind: he was then thirty-six or thirty-seven years old. He was a young man, well known in וילנא but very far from the senior position he later assumed. There were דיינים in their eighties present — far more experienced, far more established, far more accepted. He rose on his own initiative, without consulting the senior דיינים of וילנא. He was so passionate about saving lives that he simply acted.

As מקור ברוך records, he stood and declared בשם התורה — using the very formula we say before כל נדרי:

על דעת המקום ועל דעת הקהל, אנו מתירים…

— and announced that everyone must eat and make קידוש on יום כיפור, right now.

The Congregation's Response

When he issued the פסק the people did not move. Picture the scene: fifteen hundred Jews frozen in place, unable to bring themselves to act. Who eats on יום כיפור? The psychological barrier was insurmountable through words alone.

רבי ישראל understood that leadership in such a moment required more than verbal instruction. He ascended the בימה, produced a bottle of wine and a bag of cake from his pocket, and made קידוש on יום כיפור — right there in front of fifteen hundred assembled Jews. Only when they saw their רב eat publicly did the people, one by one, approach the בימה where he had prepared cakes and cookies. A "קידוש club" formed, and each person made קידוש individually.

This was personal leadership of the very highest order. He placed himself at risk — physically, by breaking the fast when perhaps he himself did not need to; spiritually and socially, by acting first, visibly, irreversibly.

V. The Rabbinic Opposition

The רב of וילנא Issues a Rebuke

After יום כיפור, רבי יחזקאל לנדא הכהן — the elderly דיין of וילנא, eighty years old, among the most respected אחרונים of his generation, author of the ספר ביכורים — issued a sharp rebuke. (We must pause to note a tragedy: the Litvish גדולים are insufficiently known today. The ישיבה world preserves their legacy through Ponevezh, Telz, and Brisk, but Lithuania was devastated in the שואה, and many towering figures have been forgotten. רבי יחזקאל was among the very greatest.)

His rebuke to the entire קהל was blunt:

"You all ate on יום כיפור while healthy? What were you doing in shul? If you were sick you should have been home. If you came to shul, you were healthy — and you ate on יום כיפור? חוצפה!"

His fundamental objection was one of הלכתי process: רבי ישראל סלנטר had not consulted the בית דין before issuing such a sweeping היתר. "You cannot simply nullify יום כיפור for an entire קהל based on your own חשש. Whoever has not yet eaten should not eat. If a person feels faint or weak, that individual must consult a רב. Every case is a שאלה unto itself."

The Institutional Picture Try to picture the dynamic. I am thirty-eight years old, לבדיל בין החיים, and if I were to stand up and tell everyone to eat on יום כיפור, and רבי שמואל קמנצקי שליט״א — today's leading גדול הדור, nearly ninety — were to publicly rebuke me, I would become the laughingstock of the city. That was precisely רבי ישראל סלנטר's situation.

VI. The Clash of Two Principles

Two fundamental principles of עבודת ה׳ collided here.

פיקוח נפש supersedes everything. In a cholera epidemic the danger was real and immediate. Fasting could weaken people and make them more susceptible. רבי ישראל saw the emergency and acted decisively to save lives — ונשמרתם מאד לנפשתיכם.[3]

הלכתי process and authority must be preserved. רבי יחזקאל represented the traditional structure — that major decisions affecting the entire ציבור must be brought before the בית דין; that individual שאלות require individual פסקים; that one cannot unilaterally override established הלכה even with the very best intentions.

This was not merely a disagreement about a פסק. It was a defining moment in understanding what rabbinic leadership in crisis looks like.

VII. The Summons to בית דין and the קושיא in גיטין

רבי איצל הכהן מווילנא made a great מחלוקת out of the incident. He summoned רבי ישראל סלנטר to the בית דין after יום כיפור to apologize and to receive punishment. His argument was unsparing:

"What happened, happened because of you. People desecrated יום כיפור because of you. We must punish you. You should not have ruled on your own without first consulting the בית דין. Had you come to us in וילנא and discussed it, perhaps we would have agreed and announced before יום כיפור that everyone should eat. But you did not come."

This was an era when the בית דין wielded tremendous authority. The בית דין still has authority today — but, unfortunately, fewer listen. Then, everyone was obligated to obey.

רבי ישראל סלנטר answered, in the famous version: "Very well. I will choose my own punishment. But first, permit me to ask one question." He turned to the בית דין — men far older and more senior than he — and said:

"You are such great גאונים. I will ask you a קושיא that I guarantee none of you can answer. It is a question on הלכות גיטין. For three decades you have been writing גיטין in a certain manner, and I have a קושיא on the way you write the גט. According to my question, every גט you have written for thirty years is פסול. Do you want to go there? I will ask the question. If you have an answer, I will accept punishment. If not, nothing will happen."

It is well known that he asked an extraordinarily powerful question on a פסק of one of the אחרונים — the same פסק by which we still write גיטין today. He asked such a forceful קושיא that he stunned them. No one had an answer. They said, "We did not realize he was such a great man in הלכה!" — and they understood that he was on a higher level in learning than they, and that they had no right to overturn his ruling.

We do not know what the קושיא was. We are reasonably certain that either he had an answer in mind, or he believed the original פסק had a good תירוץ — they did not in fact invalidate any גיטין; otherwise it would have been a catastrophe of עיגון, an absolute בלאגן. They did not retroactively פסל any גיטין. He merely asked the question, they had no answer, and they said: "We will not punish him." The חסידים, naturally, maintained that the כפידה of the Koidenover רבי was what later caused his stroke.

VIII. רבינו ברוך's Two Halachic Questions

Question 1 — Why Not פחות מכשיעור?

רבינו ברוך asks the obvious הלכתי question that should trouble each of us. There is a סימן in שלחן ערוך — סימן תרי״ח, דין חולה ביום הכפורים[4] — devoted to the laws of a sick person on יום כיפור. (A useful mnemonic: תרי״ח — ראש השנה ויום חמישי — רפואת חולי ישראל.) Every year, the most common שאלה a רב receives in the period before and on יום כיפור is precisely this — because יום כיפור is the only צום מן התורה and the most stringent fast.

The שלחן ערוך is clear. If a חולה must eat, he is obligated to eat. The same אלוקים who commanded fasting on יום כיפור tells you that if you are sick and need to eat, you are commanded to eat. People become anxious about this; it is a challenge to tell someone, "You are doing רצון השם." But it is the greatest עברה to fast when you are forbidden to fast — comparable to חילול יום כיפור itself.

However, the שלחן ערוך adds a crucial qualification. There is a method: one eats פחות פחות מכשיעור — less than the halachic measure, taken in small portions over time. The שיעור is מלא לוגמיו: fill the cheek with liquid until the cheeks puff out, spit it into a cup, divide that amount in half, and drink slightly less than that half. Everything depends on the size of your own cheeks; this is what the גמרא says, this is what the שלחן ערוך says.

How long must one wait between each portion of פחות מכשיעור? There is a great מחלוקת — either two minutes or nine minutes. I always tell people: if you can be stringent, drink slightly less than that half-cheek every nine minutes.

So the question is sharp: this is not a new הלכה. Why in the world would רבי ישראל סלנטר permit indiscriminately — even to people who came to בית הכנסת and looked outwardly healthy — eating as they wished? He should have said, "Gentlemen, this is the שיעור. Everyone must measure." If he held they were all בספק פיקוח נפש, fine — let them eat פחות מכשיעור. But how could he nullify the fast for the entire קהל?

Question 2 — Why קידוש?

And then the second, more astonishing question. The version of the story that appears in מקור ברוך has him rising at the בימה and making קידוש on יום כיפור. קידוש?! Have you ever heard of anyone making קידוש on יום כיפור?

Even a חולה who must eat does not make קידוש. The קצות החושן[5] holds that when יום כיפור falls on שבת and the person is sick, then from the side of שבת he must make קידוש. But on a regular יום כיפור? You might say יעלה ויבוא — perhaps, in some שיטה — but to make a full קידוש? That would be ברכה לבטלה. Something does not add up.

IX. The Eyewitness Version — שמעון שטרויסון

Different versions tell the story very differently. There is a sefer called גדולים פון אונזער צייט ("Great Ones of Our Time")[6] that records the testimony of an actual eyewitness — a Jew named שמעון שטרויסון — who was in that very בית הכנסת in וילנא in תר״ח. (He should not be confused with the famous ראש שטרויסון, שמואל שטרויסון, who also lived in וילנא at that time. This was a different Jew.)

He says: a great mistake. The story was not like that at all. Stories take on a life of their own.

The Brisker Rav's Aside Once a Jew came to the Brisker Rav and told him a story. The Rav responded with his own version. As the man left, the Brisker Rav turned to his children and said, "I guarantee you that by now he has already added details to the story." And so it was — by the time the man stepped out the door he had already embellished it. Stories decorate themselves.

שטרויסון's testimony is this. רבי ישראל סלנטר prepared before יום טוב many small bags — there were no Ziploc bags then, but small bags — each containing a thin piece of cake less than a כביצה, and small cups, each measured. Very little grape juice or tea or whatever was available.

In the middle of תפילה he circulated through the בית הכנסת of וילנא. This is what a גדול does. He forgot about his own תפילה, his own יום הכפורים, his own self-perfection, his own connection with הקדוש ברוך הוא. He worried only about פיקוח נפש. He went from person to person, looked at their faces, and where he saw weakness he immediately said: "Here. Take this. Eat less than the שיעור. Make a ברכה."

There was no public קידוש at the בימה. He simply went person to person and told them to eat without troubling them to seek out a רב. "I am the רב," he in effect said. "I am פוסק that you need to eat — only those who are sick." And he himself did not eat at all.

שלום עלמנורי, in his ספר עליך שמך ישראל[7] on those who passed away on יום כיפור, brings the same version. Indeed, even the משנה ברורה and ביאור הלכה[8] refer to this story in passing — that is how significant it became. He says: "I tell you, this witness שטרויסון had it right. He did not make קידוש. There is no need to make קידוש. He did not nullify the fast for everyone. It was only for those who were sick, or who felt themselves becoming sick."

In this telling, the elderly בית דין's anger reduces to a much narrower complaint: "You should have sent the people to us. You should have sent them to the בית דין. Why did you decide on their behalf, as if you alone were the רב?"

X. The Deepest הלכתי Defense — חסיד שוטה

Why, then, did רבי ישראל סלנטר choose to permit eating, even person-by-person, without first asking the בית דין? Who gave him the right? What was the תקנת שעה?

The Making of a Gadol opens this question up. The author was the son of רבי יעקב קמנצקי. רבי יעקב קמנצקי זצ״ל had a friend, רבי דוד ליבוביץ — a distant grandson of the חפץ חיים (his maternal grandfather was the brother of the חפץ חיים). The well-known רב חנוך ליבוביץ, who founded the Chofetz Chaim ישיבות throughout America, was his son.

רבי דוד ליבוביץ told רבי יעקב קמנצקי: "I heard this story from the חפץ חיים himself." The חפץ חיים was a young boy living in וילנא at the time of the epidemic — there is the famous מחלוקת as to how old the חפץ חיים actually was when he passed away (ninety-four or one hundred and four — a gap of a full decade), but he was certainly there. His own father became ill and died in that very epidemic.

The חפץ חיים told רבי דוד ליבוביץ — who told רבי יעקב קמנצקי — that the incident occurred this way:

When רבי איצל הכהן summoned רבי ישראל סלנטר and said, "You had no right to rule this way without asking the בית דין," רבי ישראל answered (in addition to the matter of the קושיא in גיטין, which may also be true) by invoking a הלכה in שלחן ערוך about a חולה on שבת. The Jerusalem Talmud puts it this way: when you encounter פיקוח נפש, if you run first to the רב to ask before saving the life — הרי זה מגונה. The Beis Yosef explains the ירושלמי[9]: a רב who has not stood up in the בית הכנסת or in the city to declare publicly that in matters of פיקוח נפש one does not ask but acts — that is the failure. It is not the asker's fault that he asked; it is the רב's fault that the message had not been issued.

And the גמרא in מסכת סוטה[10] calls such a person a חסיד שוטה — a "pious fool." A man who runs from a possible פיקוח נפש to ask his רב before saving a life, says the טור[11], is a שופך דמים — he is spilling blood.

In that light, says the חפץ חיים's tradition, רבי ישראל's defense was complete: precisely because the בית דין had not gone shul to shul before יום כיפור and announced unequivocally that fasting was forbidden for anyone who felt himself becoming weak — precisely because they had not done so — he could not afford to wait. Asking would itself have been a form of שופך דמים.

An Incident in שאוועל Such things really happen. There was a city in Lithuania called שאוועל. A Jew once came to the רב in the front row of the shul, five minutes before נעילה ended on יום כיפור. "Rabbi, I feel weak. I must eat." The רב took him by the hand and said, "It is only five minutes, friend. Calm down. Just five minutes. What can five minutes do to you?" The man returned to his seat and collapsed. And this is not the only time something of this nature has occurred.

The Brisker Approach

פיקוח נפש is not a joke. The תורה knows what it is saying when it teaches that one may be as stringent as one wishes about anything else, but when it comes to a ספק של ספק של ספק of life and death, we are extraordinarily lenient. In בריסק the bar was even lower than the שלחן ערוך. רבי חיים בריסקר was even more lenient — a revolutionary position, and one most פוסקים do not accept; on regular fasts in בריסק they ate. The famous Brisker joke — you must know it — captures the attitude exactly:

The Famous Brisker Joke A young man marries into a Brisker family. His first fast day after the wedding, he comes home from the first סדר and sees the kitchen table empty. "Rebbetzin, nu, nu!" "What 'nu, nu'? It is a fast." He says, "Ah! In Brisk we are stringent about פיקוח נפש. We are frummer than the rest of the world. I do not feel well. Let us go to lunch." She leaves the room, comes back, and sets the table — for two. He says, "No!" She says, "But you said in Brisk you eat on a fast." He says, "Yes — me! But I am not going to be frum on your חשבון. You can fast."

XI. The חתם סופר's Earlier Precedent

When we examine the תשובות of the חתם סופר[12] we find a striking parallel. Twelve years earlier, in 1836, the same situation arose in פרעשבורג during a cholera epidemic. The חתם סופר was asked what to do.

His פסק was essentially the same as רבי ישראל סלנטר's — not a blanket היתר for everyone to eat normally, but rather a directive that everyone eat פחות מכשיעור. When ספק פיקוח נפש is established at one hundred percent, there is no שאלה: everyone should eat. But how to eat is the question, and the חתם סופר said פחות מכשיעור.

The reasoning is straightforward. Consider the established הלכה of a pregnant woman on יום כיפור. If she is healthy, but medical opinion holds that without drinking, dehydration could create ספק פיקוח נפש for the ולד in her womb, she must drink — but not a full שיעור at once. She drinks small amounts, every nine minutes or so, so that ספק פיקוח נפש is averted without breaking the fast more than necessary. כל שכן when you yourself may become a ספק פיקוח נפש: eat — but only פחות מכשיעור.

XII. The בין אדם לחברו Dimension — רבי יעקב קמנצקי

רבי יעקב קמנצקי זצ״ל discusses this incident in his ספר אמת ליעקב[13], and reveals a piece of the story rarely emphasized. רבי ישראל סלנטר, he explains, refrained from approaching the בית דין of וילנא not only on הלכתי grounds but on grounds of בין אדם לחברו.

His reasoning was this. If he were to approach the בית דין and ask, "What should we do? There are people who might become sick and potentially die — how shall we proceed?" the very question would itself be a public embarrassment to the בית דין. The בית דין should have taken the initiative before יום כיפור — visited every shul, posted announcements, declared, "The רב says it is אסור to fast if you feel even slightly faint. If you are a חושש — one who suspects himself in danger — it is אסור to fast."

Since they had not done so, רבי ישראל did not want to be מבייש them publicly with the question. Therefore, on יום כיפור itself, seeing no alternative, he rose and made the decision on his own authority — not despite his sensitivity in בין אדם לחברו, but precisely because of it.

XIII. The Later אחרונים

Fifty to one hundred years afterward, the אחרונים discussed this case extensively. They debated the reasoning of those who held the action was אסור and the reasoning of those who held it was מותר.

רבי ברוך בער ליבוביץ זצ״ל — one of the great ראשי ישיבה in Lithuania — delivered an entire שיעור on this very topic in his ישיבה. He sided with those who held that it was אסור, disagreeing with רבי ישראל סלנטר's היתר.

And the story spread far beyond Lithuania. Even in ירושלים it was discussed at length, because the same cholera epidemic later struck ארץ ישראל and the same שאלה arose. The ראשון לציון of that period — רבי חיים דוד חזן — said, "I know that several years ago in Europe there was a רב who stood up in בית הכנסת and permitted everyone to eat. But here I will not do this. How can one do this?" Everyone knew the story; it had become a defining narrative.

XIV. Conclusion — The Consistency of the פסק

When you read the eyewitness version together with the חתם סופר's parallel ruling and רבי יעקב קמנצקי's bein adam l'chavero insight, the picture sharpens. רבי ישראל סלנטר's actual פסק — give the בני אדם small portions, פחות מכשיעור, only to those in danger or possible danger — is exactly what the שלחן ערוך prescribes. Why eat more than the שיעור when it is unnecessary? The fast is broken only when one eats more than the שיעור.

This was not, in the end, a disagreement about the basic פסק of פיקוח נפש. It was a defining moment about what rabbinic leadership in crisis must look like — when bold, unilateral action saves lives, and when, instead, it undermines the very הלכתי authority that sustains כלל ישראל. רבי ישראל סלנטר walked the very narrow line between the two, and walked it with both halachic precision and unusual sensitivity to others.

There is far more to be said on this topic, but this captures the essential הלכתי and hashkafic dimensions of one of the most remarkable chapters in nineteenth-century rabbinic leadership.
