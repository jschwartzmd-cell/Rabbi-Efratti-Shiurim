בָּרוּךְ שֵׁם כְּבוֹד מַלְכוּתוֹ לְעוֹלָם וָעֶד

Baruch Shem K’vod Malchuso L’Olam Va’ed
Hilchos Krias Shema — Chol HaMoed Chanukah
Recorded 29 December 2024 (Asarah B'Teves week — Chanukah) · Reworked 3 May 2026 · Mareh Mekomos upgraded 5 May 2026

A shiur in Hilchos Krias Shema on the second line of the Shema: Baruch Shem K'vod Malchuso L'Olam Va'ed. The Rabbi traces it from its makor in Pesachim 56a, through the Tzelach's heter for saying it aloud, through the machlokes with the Midrash about the malachim, into the Shiltei Giborim's startling psak that one may even skip it, and finally to the Vilna Gaon's vort on the wings of the malachim — concluding with a Chanukah remez.

I. Hakdamah — A Note on Chanukah Vacation

The Rabbi opened with a l'ilui nishmas for the mother of Rav Chaim Adler, whose yahrtzeit falls on Asarah b'Teves; her name was Onyu (a Hungarian nomen). The shiur and seudah were sponsored by the shul in her memory.

The Shelah HaKadosh on Chanukah Bittul

Chanukah is called in the Gemara a yom ha'pagra — a vacation day. But the Shelah HaKadosh,[1] a gevaldige mechaber, writes that yeshivos giving Chanukah bein ha'zmanim have missed the minhag entirely. Originally, after Sukkos, talmidim learned a fresh zman of about two months; Chanukah was the dedicated chazarah period — solidifying the new limudim, not days off. The Shelah warns that yeshivos which close their doors are doing the wrong thing.

The Bach's Chiddush — Chanukah and Hands

The Bach[2] says davka the reason the Yevanim temporarily prevailed over the Chashmonaim was that Klal Yisrael's hands were weakened — they had become shvach in avodah. Chanukah is therefore not a time to be batel from Torah, but to engage in limud more than ever.

II. The Source — Pesachim 56a

We come now to Baruch Shem K'vod Malchuso L'Olam Va'ed — said b'lachash all year, b'kol ram only on Yom Kippur. Where does it come from? Who composed it? It is not a pasuk — there is no such verse in Tanach.

The Mishnah of Yericho

The Mishnah in Pesachim[3] records distinct minhagim of the people of Yericho. Some Chazal protested; others they let stand. One minhag they let stand: "korchin es ha'Shema" — they "wrapped" the Shema together. Korech in modern Hebrew is "karich," a sandwich; the people of Yericho sandwiched Shema directly into v'ahavta, skipping Baruch Shem. Chazal said nothing; therefore — apparently — one need not say it.

The Story of Yaakov on His Deathbed

Why then do we say it? The Gemara answers from Parshas Vayechi:[4] Vayikra Yaakov el banav. Rashi brings: Yaakov Avinu sought to reveal the keitz — the date of the final geulah. Generations of chachamim have written sefarim attempting calculation; the Gemara in Sanhedrin[5] brings the curse "tipach atzman shel mechashvei kitzin" on those who calculate. We don't do this — but Yaakov Avinu did try.

Suddenly — poof — nistalkah heimenu ru'ach ha'kodesh. Yaakov lost his ru'ach ha'kodesh; he could not say it. He was, k'lashon the Rabbi, "like, I forgot — I don't know when." He became frightened: why has HaKadosh Baruch Hu taken ru'ach ha'kodesh from me at this moment? Perhaps the cause lies among my children.

The Tradition of One Bad Son

Yaakov's avos-line had a pattern: Avraham had Yitzchak and Yishmael; Yitzchak had Yaakov and Eisav. One good son, one bad son. Yaakov feared the same had touched his own twelve. He turned to them on his deathbed:

"My grandfather had Yishmael; my father had Eisav. Perhaps among you there is also a Yishmael or an Eisav?"

"Shema Yisrael" — The Original Address

The twelve shevatim answered as one:

שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל, ה' אֱלֹקֵינוּ, ה' אֶחָד.

The Rabbi paused on the pshat: "Shema Yisrael" here is b'pashtus the address to Yisrael — to their tatte, Yaakov Avinu himself. "Listen, our father — behold, Yisrael!" (One does not normally call one's father by name; v'ein kan ha'makom le'ha'arich, the Rabbi noted, but the pshat is unmistakable.) The shevatim declared with full emunah: we are unified, we are echad, there is no Eisav here, no Yishmael. We are tahor.

Mitaso Shleimah

This is why Yaakov alone among the Avos is described as "mitaso sheleimah" — his bed is whole. Reuvein had his ma'aseh; Shimon and Levi had Shechem; each had his test. But on the yichud Hashem, all twelve stood as one.

Yaakov's Response

Hearing the declaration, Yaakov Avinu answered:

בָּרוּךְ שֵׁם כְּבוֹד מַלְכוּתוֹ לְעוֹלָם וָעֶד.

He is the first to utter these words. Effectively, Baruch Shem is Yaakov's amen upon his children's Shema.

III. The Halachic Tension

Now the Gemara is stuck:

  • Yaakov said it — so we should say it (b'machaneh shel shechinah, says Rashi).
  • Moshe Rabbeinu did not write it in the parshas Shema in V'eschanan.[6] Were we to insert Yaakov's line between Shema and V'ahavta, it would be a hefsek — and one may not be mafsik in the middle of Krias Shema.

The eitzah of Chazal: b'lachash. Whisper it. Yes from Yaakov; no from Moshe; the pshara is silence-as-speech.

IV. The Mashal of "Tzikei K'deira"

The Gemara brings — amar Rabi Yitzchak amrei devei Rabi Ami — a mashal:

"To what is this similar? To a bas melech who smelled tzikei k'deira." (Pesachim 56a)

Tzikei k'deira — the burnt edges at the bottom of the pot. The Rabbi's analogy: "When I was a bachur in yeshivah, they served big trays of baked ziti, and there was a long line for the crispy burnt edges. (Years later I learned it's full of carcinogens — but the pshat is clear.)"

The bas melech wants to taste them — but it would be a bizayon for her standing to be seen at it. So she sends a servant: "Bring me some, secretly. Let no one see. I will eat them in my inner chambers." So too with Baruch Shem: it is praise so weighty, so beautiful, that we cannot say it openly — and yet we cannot do without it. So we say it b'lachash, "smuggled in" between Shema and V'ahavta.

V. The Tzelach (Noda B'Yehudah) — A Halachah of Krias Shema, Not of the Words

Who Was the Tzelach?

The Tzelach — acronym of Tziyon L'Nefesh Chayah — is the Noda B'Yehudah, Rabi Yechezkel HaLevi Segal Landau, Av Beis Din of Prague in the eighteenth century. He was, says the Rabbi, perhaps the first author to name a sefer after his mother — her name was Chayah, and he wrote l'zecher nishmas her. (As an aside to the lone bachur in the shiur: "Build a kesher with your mother. One day you'll write a sefer; you'll want her name to be on it.")

The Tzelach's Chiddush on Pesachim 56a

The Tzelach[7] redefines the entire safek: the issue is not that the words Baruch Shem are intrinsically problematic. The issue is hefsek in Krias Shema. Yaakov's words are wonderful, but inserting them between Shema and V'ahavta would be a forbidden interruption. Hence b'lachash — a permitted, near-silent compromise specifically in the Shema.

The Practical Nafka Mina

Therefore, any other place we are obligated or accustomed to say Baruch Shem — there is no issur. One may say it b'kol ram:

  • On Yom Kippur at the end of Ne'ilah: Shema Yisrael once, Baruch Shem three times, Hashem Hu HaElokim seven times — all aloud.
  • On Yom Kippur Katan (erev Rosh Chodesh) — said by certain kehillos; the Rabbi noted Coney Island Avenue and the Aguda say it on erev Shabbos, while many in Flatbush do not.
  • After accidentally making a brachah l'vatalah.
  • Minhag Ashkenaz: after the second brachah on tefillin.

The Klozz Incident

The Tzelach recounted: he showed up at the Bayinaer Kloyz in Prague for Yom Kippur Katan and instructed the tzibbur to say Baruch Shem aloud. The olam erupted: "What are you doing?!" He calmed them down — the rule of b'lachash, he said, is a din b'Krias Shema, not in the words. Outside the Shema the heter is full.

VI. The Midrash — A Different Yesod Entirely

Against the Tzelach stands a Midrash[8] in Parshas V'eschanan that frames the entire inyan differently:

Moshe Rabbeinu ascended to receive the Torah. He heard the malachei ha'shareis saying these words. He liked them — beautiful praise. He said, "I will take this and teach it to my talmidim." He descended from Har Sinai, taught it to Klal Yisrael — and warned: "Do not say it aloud. The malachim will be jealous."

The Rabbi paused: "What does this mean? Malachim have feelings? Malachim get jealous? We say words we don't fully understand. But there is something in Baruch Shem at such a high madreigah — such a sod — that, recited in this olam ha'gashmi, it is encroaching on the territory of the malachim."

Why Yom Kippur Is the Exception

On Yom Kippur we are k'malachim — fasting, not bound to physical needs, dressed in kittel. We are at the malachim's level. Therefore the heter: we may say Baruch Shem aloud once a year.

The Apparent Contradiction

The Midrash approach has the issur of saying it aloud as d'rabbanan miyad ha'malachim — everywhere, all the time, except Yom Kippur. The Tzelach's approach has it only inside Krias Shema. The halachic nafka mina: when we say Baruch Shem outside the Shema (after a brachah l'vatalah, after tefillin, on Yom Kippur Katan) — aloud or whispered? The two mekoros diverge.

VII. Rav Sholom Shvadron's Vort — Where the Mind Is

The Rabbi noted that Rav Sholom Shvadron,[9] the famous Yerushalayimer maggid, asked a striking question on the Midrash:

On Yom Kippur we say Baruch Shem aloud because we are k'malachim — fine. But at Kol Nidrei we are bloated from the seudah ha'mafsekes. We have been guzzling water for the fast. We will be running to the bathroom seven times. That is the moment we are k'malachim?

And yet at Maariv on motzaei Yom Kippur — having gone twenty-six hours without food or water, clean, tahor, having stood in kittel for an entire day — we say Baruch Shem b'lachash. The guf says we should be k'malachim at the end. So why?

The Answer

Rav Shvadron answered: it is not the guf; it is the machshavah.

  • At Kol Nidrei: bloated, but machshavah on teshuvah, on the kemtanah gedolah she'yesh li b'beis ginzi, on the cleansing of the day to come. You are k'malach in your machshavah.
  • At Motzaei Yom Kippur Maariv: physically pure, but machshavah on the marble cake, the orange juice, the coffee. Now you are not k'malach.

Where the head is — that is the measure.

VIII. Rabi Yehoshua Boaz — The Shiltei Giborim

The Italian Posek Almost No One Names

The Bach[10] in Hilchos Krias Shema brings a startling psak in the name of Rabi Yehoshua Boaz, an Italian posek who was niftar in 1555. The Rabbi: "He is one of the most important Yidden in our historiah, and almost nobody knows his name. We don't realize how much he is part and parcel of our daily limud."

His Two Quiet Monumental Contributions

Open any Talmud Bavli. On the side of every page sit:

  • Mesoras HaShas — cross-references to parallel sugyos elsewhere in Shas;
  • Torah Or — the precise tzitzin for every pasuk the Gemara quotes;
  • Ein Mishpat / Ner Mitzvah — references to where the halachah is brought down in the Rambam, Tur, and Shulchan Aruch.

All three were the work of Rabi Yehoshua Boaz. Until him, the Rabbi noted, you simply had to trust that the Gemara's quoted pesukim were accurate; nobody had charted the references. "He was a baki nifla, and he did it for the entire Shas."

The Famous Heter for Sheitlach

By the way, the Rabbi added — though the olam doesn't even know this: Rabi Yehoshua Boaz was the first posek to permit a sheitel. There is a major machlokes in Eretz Yisrael today over whether to overturn the heter; Sefardim generally are stricter. (The Rabbi: "Litvakim originally didn't wear sheitlach at all in the heimishe world; we became much frummer today.") The issur in question is se'ar b'ishah ervah[11] — does another woman's hair count as ervah the way one's own does? The Rema and many acharonim follow Rabi Yehoshua Boaz's heter; the Beis Yosef's position is debated; the Sefardishe olam today is more machmir. "Our wives walk out of our houses every day based on his heter, and most don't even know his name."

His Position on Baruch Shem

The Bach brings him on a startling psak: if you skipped Baruch Shem entirely, it is not the end of the world. He is medayek from the Rambam that the chiyuv is a minhag, not a takanah. Yaakov Avinu said it; we say it b'lachash; but b'di'eved, if it was skipped, you do not need to go back. It is "minhag b'almah" — a custom alone.

IX. The Mekubalim Disagree Sharply

The olam ha'kabbalah, says the Rabbi — fargess fun dem. The Mekubalim hold Baruch Shem is essential to Krias Shema.

The Background — Tanya and Nefesh HaChaim

The Rabbi paused for context. Rav Chaim of Volozhin was a talmid muvhak of the Vilna Gaon; the Baal HaTanya was the head of the chassidim, then under cherem of the Vilna Gaon. The Tanya sets out the chassidic hashkafah; the Nefesh HaChaim was written, in part, as a teshuvah to it. The two sefarim share much but diverge deeply on many nuanses; one must learn both to see the machlokes.

Both Position Baruch Shem at the Center

The Nefesh HaChaim[12] goes on and on about the chashivus of Baruch Shem. It is the link between yichuda ila'ah (the upper unification) and yichuda tata'ah (the lower) — a "package deal" with Krias Shema itself. The Baal HaTanya, in Sha'ar HaYichud V'HaEmunah,[13] says the same: Baruch Shem is part and parcel of Krias Shema. From here the olam asks: how then can the Shiltei Giborim say one may skip it?

One talmid noted: "There is a vort from the Nefesh HaChaim that the brachos before Krias Shema are themselves a hemshech of kabbalas malchus shamayim." The Rabbi: "Exactly."

X. The Halachah — Minhag, Takanah, or Tzivui?

The Bach paskens like Rabi Yehoshua Boaz: Baruch Shem is a minhag, very choshuv but a minhag. The Mishnah Berurah[14] follows. The Rambam[15] says "ein nahagu" — there is no formal tikun; it remained b'minhag. There is a real chiluk in halachah between a minhag and a takanah, even where the lashon of the Gemara uses "tiknu".

The Acharonim's Difficulty

The acharonim raise a sharp kushya on this psak. The poskim hold that if you did say Baruch Shem but without kavanah, you must go back and repeat it. This makes no sense: if the words are skippable l'chatchilah, why should an unintentional recitation oblige chazarah? The Biur Halachah and the Chofetz Chaim do not make this sevarah explicitly, but the teretz the Rabbi suggested: it is like a brachah l'vatalah dynamic — once spoken, the words must be b'kavanah, even though the original obligation is only b'minhag.

XI. Where Exactly Is the Hefsek?

The Shulchan Aruch[16] requires a hefsek between Shema and V'ahavta. But where? Two shitos:

  1. Pause after Shema Yisrael... echad → say Baruch Shem... → pause → continue to V'ahavta.
  2. Say Shema Yisrael... echad together with Baruch Shem... as one unit → pause → continue.

The second shitah reflects the mekubalim's view that Baruch Shem is part of kabbalas ol malchus shamayim; therefore the hefsek belongs after it, not before.

The Ben Ish Chai on Covering the Eyes

Two weeks earlier, the Rabbi had quoted the Ben Ish Chai:[17] when one covers the eyes for Shema, one should keep them covered through Baruch Shem. We don't practice this today (and most Sephardim the Rabbi knows do not either) — suggesting that, in our practice, Baruch Shem is not fully integrated as one piece with the Shema.

XII. The Vilna Gaon — Six Wings or Four

The Gemara in Chagigah

The Gemara in Chagigah[18] raises a stirah on the malachim: one pasuk[19] says they have six wings; another[20] says four. The Gemara's teretz:

כָּאן בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּם — שֵׁשׁ; כָּאן בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּם — אַרְבַּע.
"Here — in the time when the Beis HaMikdash stood — six. Here — in the time when the Beis HaMikdash does not stand — four."

The Arizal — Six Words on Six Wings

The Vilna Gaon[21] says: this is no stam zach. The Arizal[22] taught that on the wings of the malachim are inscribed the six words:

בָּרוּךְ שֵׁם כְּבוֹד מַלְכוּתוֹ לְעוֹלָם וָעֶד.

When the Beis HaMikdash stood, all six were visible. After the churban, the middle two wings — those bearing "k'vod malchuso" — disappeared (or, says one girsah, were folded under, the way a bird tucks its wings). Only four remain visible. "K'vod malchus" is precisely what is now hidden.

"Galei K'vod Malchuscha"

This is what we mean on Yom Tov when we say:

גַּלֵּה כְּבוֹד מַלְכוּתְךָ עָלֵינוּ, וְהוֹפַע!
"Reveal Your k'vod malchus upon us, and appear!"

We are asking for "k'vod malchuscha" — the words on the two folded wings — to be made visible again. The Ben Ish Chai connects this to "k'vod malchuscha yomeiru u'gevurascha yedaberu": first the gevurah is revealed, then the k'vod malchus can be spoken.

What We Are Missing — The Real Kavanah

The Vilna Gaon's vort is not just esoteric: it tells us what we are davening for when we say Baruch Shem. The sefarim write that the kavanah when saying Baruch Shem is that the k'vod malchus of HaKadosh Baruch Hu should be nisgaleh la'kol — exposed and revealed to all. This is the true kavanah.

XIII. The Connection to Chanukah

A Gematria

The Rabbi was quick to disclaim: "I am not a gematria guy." Still — the gematria of Baruch Shem K'vod Malchuso L'Olam Va'ed is 1,358. The connection to Chanukah is precisely k'vod malchuso:

אֵלֶּה בָרֶכֶב וְאֵלֶּה בַסּוּסִים, וַאֲנַחְנוּ בְּשֵׁם ה' אֱלֹקֵינוּ נַזְכִּיר.
"They with the chariot, they with horses — and we with the sheim of Hashem our G-d we will mention."[23]

The Yevanim had rechev, susim, all the koach ha'gashmi of empire. The Chashmonaim had k'vod malchuso alone. We saw k'vod malchuso on Chanukah — and we yearn for it to return.

XIV. Targum Yerushalmi — A Different Version

The Three Targumim

On every Chumash we have Targum Onkelos and (on the side) Targum Yonasan ben Uziel; Targum Yerushalmi is a nusach acher from Chazal, also called the Fragmentary Targum, surviving in pieces. The Rabbi noted a chaver of his who has dedicated his life to comparing the three Targumim — researching ancient libraries for early girsa'os — and produced a five-volume Chumash setting them side by side.

The Targum's Version

The Targum Yerushalmi[24] brings the same midrash from Pesachim 56a with a small but illuminating shinui: all twelve shevatim said Shema Yisrael; and Yaakov answered Baruch Shem. The dynamic is the same; the emphasis on the tefillah as response and counter-response is sharper.

Rav Asher Weiss's Vort

The Rabbi attributed (with the caveat "I think it's from him") to Rav Asher Weiss, author of Minchas Asher[25] — "a popular sefer, a big posek from Eretz Yisrael" — the following vort:

Every Yiddishe neshamah wants to scream Baruch Shem aloud. Yom Kippur is the only time we may. We grow up waiting all year for the Baruch Shem at Ne'ilah. The neshamah's yearning is real — but the halachah binds us to b'lachash.

So where does the excitement go? Says Rav Weiss: into Amen Yehei Shemei Rabbah. The Gemara in Shabbos[26]:

כָּל הָעוֹנֶה אָמֵן יְהֵא שְׁמֵיהּ רַבָּא בְּכָל כֹּחוֹ — קוֹרְעִין לוֹ גְּזַר דִּינוֹ.
"Whoever answers Amen Yehei Shemei Rabbah with all his strength — they tear up his evil decree."

The olam's urge to scream the praise of Hashem's malchus finds its outlet in the loud Amen Yehei Shemei Rabbah. Same energy, channeled through the permitted vessel.

XV. The Practical Halachah — Kavanah

The poskim bring down l'halachah l'ma'aseh: even though the Shiltei Giborim/Bach/Mishnah Berurah hold one need not return for skipping Baruch Shem, once you say it, you must say it b'kavanah. If said without kavanah, repeat it.

The Pashut Kavanah (Aruch HaShulchan)

The Aruch HaShulchan[27] brings the kavanah pashutah:

  • The yichud Hashem declared in Shema Yisrael;
  • Should remain like that — l'olam va'ed;
  • And, in fact, become more revealed: k'vod malchuso exposed for all (per the Vilna Gaon's vort).

The Deep Kavanah

The sefarim — kabbalistic in orientation — bring a far more nuanced kavanah: the yichud Hashem in yichuda ila'ah versus yichuda tata'ah, lengthy discussions of how the recitation effects the upper-unification and the lower-unification. The Rabbi did not enter the details — "a lengthy discussion in the sefarim" — but the pashut kavanah alone, focused upon, is sufficient to satisfy the poskim's requirement.

XVI. Closing

The Rabbi closed: Baruch Shem is not to be mumbled. We are saying — under our breath — what the malachim say upon their wings. We are asking for k'vod malchuso to be nisgaleh. "Be mechaven. Don't just say b'lachash like a murmel. Make sure the yichud Hashem we declare in Shema remains l'olam va'ed — and grows."

A lichtige, freilche Chanukah!

The vort: k'malachim at Kol Nidrei — bloated but with machshavah on teshuvah; not k'malachim at Motzaei Yom Kippur Maariv — physically pure but with machshavah on the marble cake. The malach-state is in the machshavah, not the guf.

  10. Bach, Orach Chaim 61, citing the Shiltei Giborim (Rabi Yehoshua Boaz). — Rabi Yehoshua Boaz (Italy, niftar 1555) — author of the Mesoras HaShas, Torah Or, and Ein Mishpat / Ner Mitzvah printed on every page of standard Talmud Bavli; trailblazer heter for sheitlach. His psak on Baruch Shem: medayek from the Rambam that the chiyuv is minhag rather than takanah; b'di'eved, if it was skipped, no chazarah required. "Minhag b'almah." ↩
  11. Berachos כ״ד ע״א (24a) — "Se'ar b'ishah ervah." — The textual anchor of the issur of a married woman's hair as ervah. The machlokes: does another woman's hair (a sheitel) carry the same status? The Rema and many acharonim follow Rabi Yehoshua Boaz's heter; the Sefardishe olam is more machmir. ↩
  12. Nefesh HaChaim (Rav Chaim of Volozhin), Sha'ar 3, perakim 11–14. — On Baruch Shem, yichuda ila'ah, yichuda tata'ah; the inseparability of Baruch Shem from kabbalas malchus shamayim in Krias Shema. Written, in part, as a teshuvah to the Tanya, with shared and divergent positions on multiple nuanses; one must learn both to see the machlokes. ↩
  13. Tanya, Sha'ar HaYichud V'HaEmunah, perakim 6–7. — The Baal HaTanya on the inseparability of Baruch Shem from Krias Shema; yichuda ila'ah and tata'ah; Baruch Shem as the formal lower-unification that completes the upper-unification of Shema. ↩
  14. Mishnah Berurah, Orach Chaim 61:13 (and parallels). — Following the Bach: Baruch Shem is a minhag; one need not repeat Krias Shema for omitting it; one does repeat Baruch Shem alone if recited without kavanah. The acharonim's teretz for the apparent inconsistency: a brachah l'vatalah-type dynamic — once spoken, the words must be b'kavanah. ↩
  15. Rambam, Hilchos Krias Shema 1:4 (cf. Pirush HaMishnayos). — On the textual practice and the absence of formal tikun; the lashon "v'eich nahagu" — there is no formal takanah; it is a minhag. The chiluk in halachah between a minhag and a takanah drives the Bach's psak. ↩
  16. Shulchan Aruch, Orach Chaim 61:13. — The requirement of hefsek (pause) between Shema and what follows; debate over where the pause belongs — before Baruch Shem or after. The mekubalim's position (Baruch Shem as part of kabbalas ol malchus shamayim) places the hefsek after; the pshat reading places it before. ↩
  17. Ben Ish Chai, Year One, Parshas Va'eschanan, Halachos 12–13. — On covering the eyes during Baruch Shem; Baruch Shem as part of kabbalas ol malchus shamayim. The contemporary practice (most communities, including most Sephardim the Rabbi knows) does not extend the eye-covering through Baruch Shem, suggesting the line is not fully integrated as one piece with the Shema. ↩
  18. Chagigah י״ג ע״ב (13b). — The stirah on the wings of the malachim: six versus four. The Gemara's teretz: b'zman she'beis ha'mikdash kayam — six; eino kayam — four. The Vilna Gaon and Arizal read this through the lens of the six words of Baruch Shem. ↩
  19. Yeshayahu 6:2. — שֵׁשׁ כְּנָפַיִם, שֵׁשׁ כְּנָפַיִם לְאֶחָד: six wings of the serafim. The Beis HaMikdash-era depiction; correlating to the six revealed words of Baruch Shem. ↩
  20. Yechezkel 1:6. — וְאַרְבַּע כְּנָפַיִם לְאַחַת לָהֶם: four wings of the chayos ha'kodesh. The post-churban depiction; correlating to the four wings remaining after the two bearing "k'vod malchuso" were folded. ↩
  21. Vilna Gaon, Aderes Eliyahu on Yeshayahu 6:2; cf. Likutei HaGra al Tehillim. — The Gaon's vort: on the wings of the malachim are inscribed the six words of Baruch Shem. When the Beis HaMikdash stood, all six were visible; after the churban, the middle two — "k'vod malchuso" — disappeared/folded under, leaving four. Galei k'vod malchuscha on Yom Tov is precisely the petition for those two folded wings to be revealed again. ↩
  22. Arizal, Sha'ar HaKavanos, Inyan Krias Shema; Pri Etz Chaim. — Source for the inscription of the six words of Baruch Shem on the six wings of the malachim. Underwrites the Gaon's reading of the Chagigah stirah and the kavanah when reciting Baruch Shem: that the now-hidden k'vod malchuso be nisgaleh la'kol. ↩
  23. Tehillim 20:8. — אֵלֶּה בָרֶכֶב וְאֵלֶּה בַסּוּסִים, וַאֲנַחְנוּ בְּשֵׁם ה' אֱלֹקֵינוּ נַזְכִּיר. The Chanukah connection: the Yevanim with their koach ha'gashmi, the Chashmonaim with the sheim alone — the k'vod malchuso we daven daily for. ↩
  24. Targum Yerushalmi on Devarim 6:4 (the Fragmentary Targum). — Alternative version of the Yaakov-Shevatim story underlying Baruch Shem: all twelve shevatim said Shema Yisrael; Yaakov answered Baruch Shem. Sharper emphasis on the tefillah as response and counter-response. Survives in pieces; one researcher has produced a five-volume comparative Chumash setting all three Targumim side by side. ↩
  25. Rav Asher Weiss, Minchas Asher al haTorah, Parshas V'eschanan. — The vort on the transfer of Baruch Shem energy into Amen Yehei Shemei Rabbah: every Yiddishe neshamah wants to scream Baruch Shem aloud; the halachah binds us to b'lachash all year except Yom Kippur Ne'ilah; the urge finds its outlet in the loud Amen Yehei Shemei Rabbah. (The Rabbi attributed the vort with the caveat "I think it's from him".) ↩
  26. Shabbos קי״ט ע״ב (119b). — כָּל הָעוֹנֶה אָמֵן יְהֵא שְׁמֵיהּ רַבָּא בְּכָל כֹּחוֹ — קוֹרְעִין לוֹ גְּזַר דִּינוֹ. The Talmudic anchor for Rav Asher Weiss's reading: the explicit Gemara licensing — even encouraging — full-strength response on Amen Yehei Shemei Rabbah, which becomes the licensed channel for the Baruch Shem impulse. ↩
  27. Aruch HaShulchan, Orach Chaim 61. — The kavanah pashutah: the yichud Hashem declared in Shema Yisrael should remain l'olam va'ed and become more revealed (per the Vilna Gaon's vort). Sufficient — without the deeper kabbalistic kavanos of yichuda ila'ah/tata'ah — to satisfy the poskim's requirement of kavanah when reciting Baruch Shem. ↩
