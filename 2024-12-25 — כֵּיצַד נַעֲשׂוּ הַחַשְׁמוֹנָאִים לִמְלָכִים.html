כֵּיצַד נַעֲשׂוּ הַחַשְׁמוֹנָאִים לִמְלָכִים?

How Did the Chashmonaim Become Kings?
Shiur of Rabbi Ephrati — Chanukah / Erev Shabbos Mikeitz 5785
Recorded 25 December 2024 · Reworked 3 May 2026 · Mareh Mekomos upgraded 5 May 2026

A Chanukah shiur on the great kushya of malchus Beis Chashmonai: how a family of Kohanim ascended a throne reserved by Yaakov Avinu's blessing for the descendants of Yehudah. The shiur traces the historical arc from Alexander Mokdon through the Misyavnim, Antiochus, Mattisyahu, and the dynasty itself — and presents the foundational machloikes between the Ramban (it was a sin) and Rav Yaakov Kamenetsky in Emes L'Yaakov (it was a hora'as sha'ah).

I. Framing the Question

The Rambam ends his entire Yad HaChazakah with Hilchos Melachim[1] — meaning malchus is not merely "leadership" but a parshah of halachos with its own dinim, gedarim, and prerequisites. Throughout Tanach there were two malchu'os:

  • Malchei Yehudah — descended from David HaMelech.
  • Malchei Yisrael — the breakaway northern kingdom.

Those who learn Maseches Sanhedrin Shabbos afternoon are familiar with the chilukim between the two. But the Chashmonaim are neither. They are Kohanim — Shevet Levi — and the Rambam at the start of Hilchos Chanukah writes that they seized the throne and ruled kol Yisrael for some two centuries. How is that b'derech halachah possible? Yaakov Avinu in Parshas Vayechi[2] said:

לֹא יָסוּר שֵׁבֶט מִיהוּדָה וּמְחֹקֵק מִבֵּין רַגְלָיו, עַד כִּי יָבֹא שִׁילֹה.
"The scepter shall not depart from Yehudah, nor the staff from between his feet, until Shilo arrives."

The malchus in Klal Yisrael is not to depart from Shevet Yehudah; the mechokek — the lawgivers — must come from Yehudah. How then do Kohanim become melachim?

II. Historical Background — The Rise of Alexander Mokdon

The Beginning of Bayis Sheni

Roughly thirty years after the Beis HaMikdash was rebuilt under Persian permission — approximately 320 BCE — an individual rises to power who would, in the Rabbi's words, "change the way things were": Alexander of Macedonia, son of Philip II. The name itself enters the Jewish lexicon as a Yiddishe nomen.

Alexander's Education Under Aristotle

Alexander was a pampered child — son of the ruler of Macedonia. His father hired the finest teachers; chief among them was Aristotle. The two studied together for some eight years, beginning when Alexander was a teenager of thirteen or fourteen. The shidduch of "the great thinker" with "the great doer," b'lashon the Rabbi, set a trajectory in divrei ha'yamim — and especially in Jewish history — that Klal Yisrael would suffer from greatly, until ultimately prevailing in the yom tov of Chanukah.

Aristotle introduced Alexander, while still impressionable, to Greek philosophy, the arts, and the entire Hellenistic ideology. They had debates and disagreements — but the influence was profound and lasting.

Alexander's Conquests

Following his father's assassination — historians debate whether Alexander himself had a hand in it — he ascended the throne at seventeen or eighteen and rapidly conquered South Asia, North Africa, the entire Mediterranean, the Middle East, and Eretz Yisrael, earning the title "Alexander the Great" — Alexander the Conqueror.

The Encounter with Shimon HaTzaddik

The Gemara in Yoma[3] records the famous account: when Alexander reached the border of Eretz Yisrael, Shimon HaTzaddik, who was Kohen Gadol and leader of Klal Yisrael, came out to meet him. Alexander descended from his chariot and bowed. To his shocked soldiers and servants, who could not understand why the conqueror of nations would prostrate before a Jewish sage, Alexander explained: "Whenever I go out to battle, I see the demus — the image — of this very Shimon HaTzaddik before me, blessing me with victory."

The Ten Questions in Maseches Tamid

Alexander was not only a conqueror but a thinker. Talmud Bavli, Maseches Tamid Daf Lamed-Beis[4] records an entire daf of ten philosophical questions he posed to the Chachmei Yisrael — fierce debates on philosophy and hashkafah. The chachmah of Mokdon meets the chachmah of Chazal over a full page of Talmud.

III. The Hellenistic Era and the Beginning of Misyavnim

What is "Hellenism"?

Hellas was the ancient name for Greece itself; Hellenists are those influenced by the Greeks. The Hellenistic era — three centuries within the tekufah of Bayis Sheni — saw a major part of the civilized world fall under Greek empire and, more dangerously, Greek influence: in music, architecture, science, mathematics, philosophy, and ideology. (Architecture was their shtark zach, as the surviving monuments attest.)

"Was It Good for the Jews?"

Despite the warm encounter with Shimon HaTzaddik, the answer is — nisht azoy gut. Alexander's policy was religious autonomy: every conquered nation kept its religion, paid taxes, came under Greek political rule, and was introduced to Greek culture. (Aristotle himself disagreed with this shitah, arguing for a more uniform Hellenization.) On the surface, Alexander was good for the Jews; Klal Yisrael began naming children after him. But the cultural infiltration was relentless.

Gymnasia and the Rise of the Misyavnim

Many Jews were forced — first by pressure, then willingly — to attend gymnasia, schools of Greek philosophy. There they began raising their children with Greek priorities and Greek hashkafos. The era of the Misyavnim — the Hellenized Jews — had begun.

Prushim vs. Tzedukim

The rabbinic Jews — the Prushim — fought to preserve Torah and especially Torah she'b'al peh, which at this time was not yet written down. Tanach alone was on the page; the rest was held by yeshivos and rebbeim orally. Maintaining the mesorah was therefore a matter of survival.

Their opponents — the Tzedukim — were the talmidim of Tzaddok and Beisus,[5] students of the early Tana'im. Alongside them stood the Beisusim. The Tzedukim rejected Torah she'b'al peh; the Prushim defended it.

One Hundred and Ten Years of Coexistence

For approximately 110–115 years after Alexander, Yevanim, Misyavnim, and observant Jews lived in relative peace. Schools and gymnasia existed; many Jews remained shomrei mitzvos. By the time we reach the Chashmonaim, however, a different Greek dynasty has taken over: the Seleucids (Seleukidim). Under their rule, the pressure on Jewish religious life began to intensify.

IV. Klal Yisrael Without Kings

Who was melech during all these centuries? Nobody. Our last king was Tzidkiyahu HaMelech, taken in shalsha'os shel barzel — chains of iron — to Babel at the end of churban Bayis Rishon. After that, the malchus in Klal Yisrael, alas, ended.

Leadership devolved to the Kohanim Gedolim and the Tana'im. This is the early tekufah of the Tana'im: Antignos Ish Socho — note the Greek name — at the head of Maseches Avos. Throughout this era a Sanhedrin of seventy or seventy-one dayanim functioned with a Nasi at its head — but no king.

No Navi → No Melech

The end of nevu'ah coincides with the end of churban Bayis Rishon — Chaggai, Zechariah, and Malachi being the final nevi'im. As the poskim note, normally one needs a navi to memaneh a king. No prophet, no king. (This itself is a davar ha'shanu'i b'machlokes among the Rambam, the Ramban, and others, to which the shiur returns later.)

V. Antiochus Reverses Policy

The Wicked Antiochus IV

Antiochus IV — Antiochus HaRasha, "Antiochus the Wicked," whom the children sing about — was unlike his father, uncle, and grandfather, all of whom had been Greek emperors. He reversed the long-standing policy of religious autonomy. Where prior emperors had said: let them have their Beis HaMikdash, their korbanos, their Sanhedrin, their laws — we will tax and govern them but stay out of their religion — Antiochus declared: I am going to get involved.

Corruption of the Kehunah Gedolah

Before the story of Chanukah, the Kohen Gadol held the highest position in Klal Yisrael: he ran the Beis HaMikdash, the spiritual epicenter of the people. But the Kohanim Gedolim of Bayis Sheni were largely not tzaddikim. Most became deeply misyaven, deeply influenced by the Greeks. They formed political connections with local Greek governors and emperors, and — clearly recorded both in Chazal and in secular history — bribed their way into the Kehunah Gedolah, despite being neither bekiim in halachah nor particularly frum.

The Gemara in Yoma[6] records, regarding the ketores in the Kodesh HaKodashim on Yom Kippur, that many such Kohanim Gedolim were Tzedukim — including the famous case of the nisuch ha'mayim on Sukkos. They wanted the money, the power, the authority — and that's it.

The Tragic Cycle and Civil War

Historians wonder how Kohanim Gedolim could pay so much and fight so fiercely for the office, given that many died in their first Yom Kippur avodah. The answer is that the cycle was constant: civil war was the rule, not the exception, throughout most of the 420 years of Bayis Sheni. There was sibling rivalry among Kohanim Gedolim, milchamos between Prushim and Tzedukim, conflict between shomrei mitzvos and the Misyavnim. For Yidden, two are enough for a good geshmak argument; in those years, the demographic minority of Jews in Eretz Yisrael compared to those in galus Bavel made the in-fighting all the more pronounced.

VI. The Sefarim Chitzonim

Certain books written in this era — the Sefarim Chitzonim, "external books" — sit outside the canon. They were never accepted into our sifrei kodesh, and some appear to be authored by Misyavnim themselves. The Rabbi singled out two:

  • Sefer HaMaccabim I
  • Sefer HaMaccabim II[7]

In some Christian Bibles (Catholic and Orthodox; the Protestants removed them) these are part of the canon as "First and Second Maccabees." We do not learn them as sifrei kodesh; their dates and details are debated by historians. Yet for general background, says the Rabbi, "I am amongst the curious enough" — they suffice.

Megillas Antiochus

A separate work — Megillas Antiochus, also called Megillas Bnei Chashmonai — is printed in many siddurim.[8] Tradition holds it was written by Shammai and Hillel, our heilige Tana'im, transmitted directly from Mattisyahu ben Yochanan Kohen Gadol and his five sons. Rav Saadia Gaon was the first to print it in his siddur; he himself, in Geon Eikvah, attests that the mesorah traces back to Mattisyahu's family. It is, however, a heavily abridged account of the events.

VII. Jason and Menelaus — Civil War in the Kehunah

Jason / Yeshua

The Kohen Gadol in the days approaching Antiochus was Jason — a Greek name. According to Yosef ben Matisyahu (Josephus Flavius),[9] his Hebrew name was Yeshua (יֵשׁוּעַ). We have a separate Yeshua Kohen Gadol (without the heh) earlier in Tanach; in our literature, this later one is called Yeshua or Jason. He had connections with the local Greek governor.

Menelaus's Coup

Jason's brother, Menelaus — also a Jew, also a Kohen — coveted the Kehunah Gedolah for himself. When a rumor spread that Antiochus had been killed in battle in Africa, Menelaus saw his moment, took a thousand soldiers — a thousand fellow Jews — and attacked his brother. Chaos overtook Yerushalayim: who was the legitimate Kohen Gadol? Even the rabbinic Jews who came to the Beis HaMikdash with their korbanos did not know.

VIII. Antiochus's Rage and the Desecration

The rumor was false. Antiochus was alive — and enraged. He rushed back to Eretz Yisrael and dove into local Jewish politics in a way no Greek emperor before him ever had. He decided who would be Kohen Gadol; eventually he simply assumed control of the Beis HaMikdash himself.

The Pig in the Kodesh HaKodashim

The first thing Antiochus did was bring a pig — a davar acher — into our holy temple, slaughter it, and sprinkle its blood in the Kodesh HaKodashim and on the mizbe'ach outside. He forced the Kohanim to consume davar acher; those who refused, he shechted on the spot. He moved through Yerushalayim in a "boiling rage," slaughtering anyone identified as a rabbinic Jew — different accounts give 40,000–80,000 killed, with many more exiled. From this point, the milchamos with the Greeks turned existential.

The Gezeiros

The gezeiros Antiochus enacted included milah, Shabbos, and Rosh Chodesh — three mitzvos the Misyavnim already targeted. The trick with milah: the Hellenists worshiped the human body. They were narcissists; they had zero bushah — none of the bayshanim, rachmanim, gomlei chasadim that the Gemara in Beitzah Daf Lamed-Beis Amud Bes[10] identifies as the three defining simanim of Klal Yisrael. In their gymnasia they made everyone train naked. They could thus see at a glance who had a bris — and shecht them.

The Rambam's Atypical Aside

The Rambam at the start of Hilchos Chanukah[11] does something very unusual for him: he describes the history — not the halachos first, but the events. He does not specify each gezeirah, but he paints the picture of how terrible malchus Yavan was at the time. The Rambam writing divrei ha'yamim at the head of a halachah is itself m'limeid on the chashivus of the events.

IX. Mattisyahu — The Unlikely Hero

The Irony of Yochanan Kohen Gadol

Mattisyahu ben Yochanan Kohen Gadol witnessed the destruction in Yerushalayim. He was a rabbinic Jew, a parush. The most ironic detail: his father, Yochanan Kohen Gadol, was a Tzeduki. The Gemara in Berachos[12] says that for eighty years this man was the most venerated Jew of his generation — and at the end of his life turned Tzeduki. From here the Mishnah in Avos[13] teaches:

אַל תַּאֲמִין בְּעַצְמְךָ עַד יוֹם מוֹתְךָ.
"Do not believe in yourself until the day of your death."

The miracle is that Yochanan the Tzeduki produced Mattisyahu and the five sons we praise as tzaddikim, kedoshim, u'tehorim in davening. Something turned. The Rabbi flagged this as a question to address later.

Modi'in

Mattisyahu fled to Modi'in — still today a place in Eretz Yisrael, near Kfar HaChashmonaim. Antiochus's people had set up altars to Zeus there, and Antiochus decreed public korbanos to Zeus, forcing the local Jews to attend. Mattisyahu approached the altar, drew a sword, and shechted the Misyaven who was bringing the offering — a Jew who simply did not care. He fled with his sons into the forests around Modi'in, where they hid for some time.

The Rebellion in the Forests

There they gathered a small army — me'atim b'yad rabim, as we say in davening. Perhaps a few hundred men. Against the mighty Greek army it was, by every standard of historia, ludicrous. "And that is its beauty," the Rabbi said.

X. The Rebellion and the Falls of Elazar and Yehudah

Strategy

Each son took a band of men to a different region of Eretz Yisrael and fought every local governor under Antiochus.

Elazar and the Elephants

Antiochus — having traveled to India, North Africa, and the East — had returned with armored elephants, the equivalent of modern tanks. Elazar, son of Mattisyahu, was a gibor: he ran between the elephants in one battle, killing them from below the belly with a spear. Megillas Antiochus describes a gory end — from the mass of tzo'ah, the toxic excrement of so many elephants, Elazar drowned. (The Megillah, the Rabbi noted, is b'farbei mei'historia; the pshat is grim.)

The Origin of the Name "Maccabi"

Of Yehudah — the strongest of the brothers — the popular pshat takes Maccabi as an acronym for Mi Kamocha Ba'eilim Hashem. The Rabbi insisted: that is not the simple pshat. Maccabi is an acronym for Mattisyahu Ben Yochanan Kohen — מ.ב.י.כ. It is the family name. (One questioner tried to push for the popular reading; the Rabbi held his ground: "It's nice, it's beautiful, it talks to me — but the simple pshat is the family name.")

Mattisyahu's Charge

When Yehudah fell, his brothers returned to their father with the question: "Yehudah was the strongest. He was the eldest. What do we do now?" Mattisyahu — already an old man — took up his sword:

"I will fight instead of mourning my son's death. Now is the time to fight. A Yid must be proud to fight for the truth."

He ran with his sons to the battlefield. Victory followed victory; the small Judean state outlasted, on the battlefield, one of the mightiest empires of the ancient world.

Antiochus the Fugitive

According to Megillas Antiochus (against which secular historians vehemently protest), as his army was slaughtered one after another, Antiochus boarded a ship and fled. They called him Antiochus HaBoreach, "Antiochus the Fugitive" — a bushah upon his head. He ultimately lost the empire; other Greek emperors took over after him. (Secular historians say he died of disease; the mesorah in our sefarim places the disgrace on his head while alive.)

XI. The Story of Chanukah and the Menorah

The story of Chanukah itself the Rabbi treated briefly. They won the battles — k'mo she'omrim b'davening b'al ha'nissim. They found a pach shemen. They lit the menorah.

Why the Menorah?

Why does the menorah's miracle take center stage? Because they reinstituted avodas ha'mikdash. For a long time no one could enter the Beis HaMikdash — there was a temple to Zeus there; Antiochus had brought korbanos to avodah zarah on our mizbe'ach. Now, finally, they returned to the Beis HaMikdash; they reclaimed it; it was theirs. The nes of the menorah stands in for the deeper inyan: this is chanukas ha'mizbe'ach — the rededication of the mizbe'ach and of the Beis HaMikdash.

The Rabbi flagged a question for another time: why are the Al Ha'Nissim version of the story (battles, victory) and the Gemara's version (the pach shemen) so different? B'ezras Hashem, in a future shiur.

XII. Malchus Beis Chashmonai — The Great Question Returns

The Rambam's Striking Halachah

The Rambam at the beginning of Hilchos Chanukah writes — davar she'eino oseh b'shum halachah acheres, something he does in no other halachah — that the Chashmonaim seized the throne and became kings over kol Yisrael. For centuries we had no real kings — only Sanhedrin, dayanim, Tana'im, chachamim, yeshivos. "Malchus Beis Chashmonai decided: we are going to be kings." Kohanim, who have no business being kings.

The Two-Hundred-Year Dynasty

For roughly the next two centuries of Bayis Sheni, the Chashmonaim ruled. The Rabbi sketched the chain:

  • First — the sons of Mattisyahu: Shimon, and (the Rabbi said) Yochanan.
  • Then — Alexander Yannai (Alexander Janus).
  • Then — Aristobulus.
  • And other descendants — many bearing Greek names.

It began nobly — they were k'doshim; we still call them al yedei kohanecha ha'kedoshim in Al Ha'Nissim. It descended into sibling rivalry in the Jewish royal court, and constant fighting through the rest of Bayis Sheni.

XIII. The Ramban — The Question Is the Answer

The Ramban on Vayechi

The Ramban in Parshas Vayechi[14] asks: How did the Chashmonaim take the throne for themselves?

His answer: The question is the answer. It was wrong. It was a sin. It was a crime. The Ramban — a thousand years after the events — has the koach to say it. Lo yasur shevet mi'Yehudah is a tzivui from Yaakov Avinu; the Chashmonaim, although tzaddikim kedoshim u'tehorim, sat on a throne that did not belong to them.

The Anticipated Question — Shaul HaMelech

One talmid raised: but Shaul HaMelech was the first melech, and he was from Shevet Binyamin! The Rabbi noted: Shaul preceded the establishment of Malchus Beis David. Once David HaMelech's dynasty was selected — Lo yasur shevet mi'Yehudah applied l'doros. (The Rabbi flagged that one could also distinguish hora'as sha'ah here, which becomes the Rambam's view below.)

The Punishment — Hordus

This is, says the Ramban, why these tzaddikim who saved Klal Yisrael, who gave us the nes of Chanukah, who are our heroes — suffered so terribly during their reign. Hordus killed them all. The Gemara at the very beginning of Maseches Bava Basra Daf Gimmel Amud Aleph[15] — which the Rabbi noted had just been finished in Daf Yomi — states this explicitly. Hordus had been a slave of the Chashmonaim; he rebelled and slaughtered them all.

Mikrah? — coincidence? No, says the Ramban. You take what is not yours, you lose what you have. They wanted a throne; it was not enough that they were Kohanim; for that, Hashem caused them to be wiped out.

The Caveat About Shimon

The Ramban adds, almost in three words, a caveat: Shimon, one of the sons of Mattisyahu, was a shtikl Tzeduki — somewhat influenced by the Tzedukim. We praise the brothers as tzaddikim kedoshim; the Ramban hints that there was an additional sin in Shimon's line. The Rishonim who studied this have records of Shimon's connection with the Tzedukim: perhaps not a Tzeduki himself, but influenced — and his descendants therefore deviated.

The Last Chashmona'is

The Gemara records that Hordus killed them all except for one girl, who survived from the Chashmonai dynasty. She climbed to the roof; just before throwing herself down, she cried out:

"From here on — anyone who claims to be a descendant of the Chashmonaim, do not believe him. He must be an eved — for the Chashmonaim are finished."

Hordus, the meshugene, preserved her body in honey for seven years; the Gemara contains a machlokes over what he did with it (v'ein kan ha'makom le'ha'arich).

XIV. The Sanhedrin's Silence — Rav Yaakov Kamenetsky in Emes L'Yaakov

The Real Question

If the Chashmonaim's malchus was a cheit, why did the Sanhedrin remain silent? The Sanhedrin functioned and operated properly throughout this entire era. They were enacting halachos, gezeiros, takanos, siyagim. The entire misnayos of shishah sidrei mishnah were composed during this tekufah — to be compiled by Rabbeinu HaKadosh centuries later, but generated here. A massive halachic body — and not a peep of mecha'ah?

Rav Yaakov Kamenetsky

Rav Yaakov Kamenetsky wrote the sefer Emes L'Yaakov,[16] which the Rabbi called "one of the best sefarim that came out in the past several decades, somewhat underappreciated." He wrote peirushim on Torah, on the Rambam, and on Maseches Avos. He was a talmid of the Slabodka world — solidly of the right-wing yeshivah world — yet was baki in Tanach like few in his generation, and read Jewish history with rare depth.

The Yesod — Bayis Sheni Was Never Meant to Be a Geulah Sheleimah

Once Nevuchadnetzar exiled us to Bavel, says Rav Yaakov, it was over. We came back; we received permission to build a Beis HaMikdash — but only as a tekufah moshikra, an interim period. Chazal understood that the Yiddishe structure of self-rule in Eretz Yisrael would never again be what it had been until Mashiach arrives.

The signs are clear in the Gemara's list of differences between the two Batei Mikdash:

  • No Aron
  • No Keruvim
  • No Hashra'as ha'Shechinah in the same measure

Many of the giluyim of Bayis Rishon simply did not exist in Bayis Sheni.

Therefore — No Plans for a Melech

Chazal at the time were not planning to appoint a king. They knew this was a temporary geulah; that we would yet return to galus until "ki yavo Shilo"; that Mashiach would bring the tikkun ha'shaleim. They feared that establishing a true malchus would cause Klal Yisrael to mistake the moment for the geulah ha'sheleimah — to think it was over, the work was done, like the days of David. So they davka refrained.

Why So Many Gezeiros and Takanos in Bayis Sheni?

Rav Yaakov presses further: why is it specifically in Bayis Sheni that Chazal generated nearly all the gezeiros and takanos? His answer: Chazal knew this tekufah would last 420 years — long, but not forever. They knew Klal Yisrael was on the way out, into a galus that would last 1,000–2,000 years. The Jews would need fortification for what was coming. They needed siyagim, gederim, and gezeiros — protections that are not d'oraisa but d'rabbanan. This is why davka in Bayis Sheni, the Chashmonaim themselves, the Kohanim Gedolim, the Sanhedrin, and the chachamim all multiplied gezeiros and takanos.

The Chachamim's Silence Explained

And so when the Chashmonaim assumed the throne — the chachamim did not protest. They did not even hold there should be a king at this tekufah. They wanted the status quo: Sanhedrin, dayanim, chachamim — and that's it. This was not a time for malchus.

Why then did the Chashmonaim seize it? Because they felt: "Im lo achshav, eimasai?" — if not now, when? If we don't consolidate power, all the battles of the past decade will lose their effect. The unity holding Klal Yisrael together against the Misyavnim will dissolve. Someone must run the malchus and the Beis HaMikdash simultaneously. Therefore, says Rav Yaakov, it was not so terrible a cheit. Perhaps they should at most have declared their reign moradi — temporary, a hora'as sha'ah.

And Zerubavel?

One talmid raised: Zerubavel, in Ezra and Nechemiah, was supposed to be king and was from Beis David. The Rabbi accepted: l'derech ha'kavah, indeed; for a temporary kingdom, a hora'as sha'ah, even another shevet can serve. Lo yasur shevet mi'Yehudah, in this view, is a statement about the malchus l'olam — the eternal kingship — not about every interim arrangement. And that, says Rav Yaakov, is exactly what happened.

XV. The Rambam vs. the Ramban — Tzivui or Havtachah?

The Rambam Praises the Chashmonaim

If you look at the Rambam in Hilchos Chanukah, he praises the Chashmonaim for taking the throne. The lashon: they were chazakim, they were gevurim, and they took the malchus. The kingdom returned to Eretz Yisrael, to Klal Yisrael; we were no longer under Greeks, Babylonians, or Persians. Finally, a king of our own — but only temporarily.

The Underlying Machlokes

Here lies the foundational machlokes:

  • The Ramban — "Lo yasur shevet mi'Yehudah" is a tzivui, a commandment of Yaakov Avinu to his descendants. Take the throne if not from Beis David and you have transgressed the tzivui of your zaken, your grandfather.
  • The Rambam — "Lo yasur" is a havtachah, a promise. For all eternity, Malchus Beis David will rule. But this leaves room for hora'as sha'ah kings from other shevatim.

A Parallel Machlokes — Yishuv Eretz Yisrael

The Rabbi noted a striking parallel machlokes between the same two Rishonim: yishuv Eretz Yisrael.

  • The Ramban: "V'horashtem osah v'yeshavtem bah"[17] — it is a mitzvah.[18]
  • The Rambam: it is not counted among the taryag mitzvos — it is a havtachah.

"You can paskin as you wish," the Rabbi added; many Rishonim hold it is a mitzvah, and today certainly is. "I'm not talking politics — I'm talking halachah." The machlokes recurs across multiple parshiyos: tzivui or havtachah?

XVI. Why Is There No Maseches Chanukah?

A second-tier question — for which time only allowed a b'krov answer: Rabbeinu HaKadosh gave us a maseches for nearly every yom tov — Megillah, Yoma, Rosh HaShanah, Sukkah. Why no Maseches Chanukah?

The Chasam Sofer Legend (Rabbi: "I Don't Like It")

The Chasam Sofer's Sefer Chut Ha'Meshulash[19] — compiled by his grandson, the legend's source — records the explanation. The Rabbi noted: The Chasam Sofer wrote more than nearly any other rav in his generation. This particular thing he did not write down. His grandson testifies that he heard it from his grandfather. I'm not sure he heard it correctly.

The reported statement: Rabbeinu HaKadosh came from Zera Beis David; he was the Nasi (the Reish Galusa being in Bavel, as the Daf Yomi learners had just seen in Sanhedrin[22]). Allegedly, Rabbeinu HaKadosh took it personally — "You Chashmonaim dared take away our throne? I'm not writing a maseches about your yom tov."

"Are you serious?!" said the Rabbi. "Rabbeinu HaKadosh lit the menorah just like you and I light the menorah. How could the Chasam Sofer have said such a thing?"

Other rabbanim who quote this Chasam Sofer reframe it: Rabbeinu HaKadosh did not omit the maseches vindictively but l'sheim shamayim: because of our aveiros, the Chashmonaim were not zocheh to a maseches. Not revenge — but a withholding b'cheshbon.

The Vilna Gaon — The Lost Maseches

A second tradition, one the Rabbi clearly preferred: the Vilna Gaon's son writes in his father's name that there was a Maseches Chanukah, and we lost it.[20] We lost many masechtos through the galus.

Rav Hai Gaon records a startling episode: one day in the yeshivah in Bavel, an old man — zaken v'chacham v'yashish ba'yeshivah — appeared with a long white beard, took out an entire maseches they had never seen before, and read from it. Rav Hai Gaon begged him: "Stay! Let me copy this maseches! We have all shishah sidrei mishnah — but we are missing this one!" The man said, "I must go," and departed. Rav Hai Gaon lamented: I lost that maseches forever.

Other Lost Masechtos

The Gaon said we once had masechtos on many inyanim we now lack. We have a Maseches Mezuzah — what about tefillin? Where is the dedicated maseches? The list of losses, said the Gaon, is the tragedy of galus.

The Sefas Emes — A Chassidic Reading

The Sefas Emes[21] takes the absence of Maseches Chanukah and makes a simchah out of it. The chiddush of the Sefas Emes, and of chassidus in general: Chanukah is entirely Torah she'b'al peh. That is why it remains b'al peh — there is no written tradition because the essence of Chanukah is the unwritten chelek of Torah. The gedolei chassidus elaborate this yesod in many directions.

XVII. Closing

The Rabbi closed: there is more to discuss; b'ezras Hashem next week, more nikudos on this parshah. He wished the tzibbur:

A lichtige, freilche Chanukah! · שבת שלום!

  4. Tamid ל״א ע״ב–ל״ב ע״ב (31b–32b) — eser she'eilos of Alexander Mokdon to the Chachmei HaNegev. — A full daf of philosophical and hashkafic debate between Alexander and the Chachmei Yisrael. Alexander as a thinker, not only a conqueror — and Chachmei HaNegev as the equal opponents in inquiry. ↩

Mishnah, Avos 1:3 — Antignos Ish Socho; the breakaway talmidim Tzaddok and Beisus. — The early tekufah of the Tana'im opens with Antignos Ish Socho, whose Greek name itself signals the era's cultural pressure. Together with the famous incident of the nisuch ha'mayim on Sukkos, demonstrates the depth of Tzeduki infiltration into the Kehunah Gedolah in Bayis Sheni. ↩

The Rambam's atypical historical introduction at the head of a halachah — describing the gezeiros of malchus Yavan, the rebellion, the rededication, and explicitly noting that the Chashmonaim ruled as kings for over 200 years. ↩
  12. Berachos כ״ט ע״א (29a). — Yochanan Kohen Gadol who served eighty years and turned Tzeduki at the end of his life. The most venerated Jew of his generation; the most cautionary tale in Shas for spiritual complacency. ↩
  13. Mishnah, Avos 2:4 — Hillel: אַל תַּאֲמִין בְּעַצְמְךָ עַד יוֹם מוֹתְךָ. — "Do not believe in yourself until the day of your death." The principle Mattisyahu's family inverted — going from Tzeduki father to tzaddikim kedoshim sons. ↩
  14. Ramban on Bereishis 49:10. — The Ramban's critical peirush: the Chashmonaim's assumption of the throne was a cheit, in violation of Yaakov Avinu's tzivui. The Ramban also notes (in three words) that Shimon ben Mattisyahu had a Tzeduki influence — perhaps an additional sin in his line. ↩
  15. Bava Basra ג׳ ע״ב–ד׳ ע״א (3b–4a). — Hordus, formerly an eved of the Chashmonaim, killing all of them; the surviving Chashmona'is who jumped from the rooftop after declaring that any future claimant to Chashmonai ancestry must be presumed an eved; Hordus's preservation of her body in honey for seven years. ↩
  16. Rav Yaakov Kamenetsky, Emes L'Yaakov al ha'Torah, Bereishis 49:10 (also discussions in Emes L'Yaakov on the Rambam and on Maseches Avos). — The yesod that Bayis Sheni was a tekufah moshikra, an interim geulah never meant to be the geulah ha'sheleimah; that Chazal deliberately avoided malchus precisely so that Klal Yisrael would not mistake the moment for the final geulah; that the explosion of gezeiros and takanos in Bayis Sheni was preparatory fortification for the long galus ahead; and that the Chashmonaim's seizure of the throne was a hora'as sha'ah — not a major cheit. ↩

  18. Ramban, Hosafos l'Sefer HaMitzvos, Mitzvas Asei 4 (the Ramban's addition to the Rambam's Sefer HaMitzvos). — The Ramban's addition: yishuv Eretz Yisrael as a mitzvah d'oraisa binding in every generation. The classical structural disagreement with the Rambam runs across multiple parshiyos — tzivui versus havtachah. ↩
  19. Source-type note (oral attribution): Sefer Chut Ha'Meshulash — biographical compilation of the Chasam Sofer assembled by his grandson. The Rabbi expressed open skepticism: the Chasam Sofer himself never wrote this; the grandson's testimony alone is the source. Reframed by some rabbanim as a l'sheim shamayim withholding b'cheshbon rather than personal grievance. Treated here as oral tradition.

  20. Vilna Gaon (cited by his son R' Avraham); cf.
