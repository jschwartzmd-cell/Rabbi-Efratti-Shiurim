Siyum שמונה עשרה: יהיו לרצון, אלוקי נצור, and the מחלוקת of Pressburg

Closing the עמידה — נוסחאות, ראשונים, and the תפילה of מר בריה דרבינא

ר' חיים יהודה בן מאיר ווילינגער ז״ל — survivor of the war and the death march from סיילישע, who left behind a beautiful family. May his memory be a blessing.

After the long break of ימים טובים we return to our regular structure. Last time we finished the ברכה of שים שלום; today we move into what comes next — the close of שמונה עשרה, the נוסח question of עושה השלום versus המברך את עמו ישראל בשלום, and the much later piece of תפילה we call אלוקי נצור.

I. עושה השלום vs. המברך את עמו ישראל בשלום

The נוסח Question

Last time we addressed the נוסח אשכנז version of the closing ברכה. Most of the world says ברוך אתה ה' המברך את עמו ישראל בשלום. נוסח אשכנז, however, says עושה השלום during עשרת ימי תשובה. I forgot to mention one fascinating fact: there is a sefer called חוט המשולש[1] which reveals that in earlier times נוסח אשכנז said עושה השלום the entire year — not only during the ימים נוראים. Today, אשכנזים do it only during עשרת ימי תשובה.

Why Specifically During עשרת ימי תשובה?

We discussed at length why these days are different. The גמטריא of השלום equals סנדלפון — the מלאך who writes and records our כתיבה וחתימה טובה. During עשרת ימי תשובה we want to invoke the רחמים associated with that מלאך. This led us into the whole question of how we are permitted to seem to daven to מלאכים at all; the answer being that when we say מכניסי רחמים we are not actually davening to them.

And there is a beautiful irony: the גמטריא of עושה equals השלום, so even by saying the single word עושה you invoke that same מלאך. Before the מנהג אשכנז narrowed down to say עושה השלום only during the ימים נוראים, there was a genuine מנהג to say it the whole year. It became a controversial topic, and different סידורים printed it different ways.

II. The Sefer חוט המשולש

חוט המשולש is a fascinating sefer that every yid should have at home, especially every תלמיד of the חתם סופר. One of the חתם סופר's grandchildren wrote it as a biography of three generations: his grandfather רבי עקיבא איגר, his father the חתם סופר, and his own father the כתב סופר. (רבי עקיבא איגר was the חתם סופר's father-in-law: the חתם סופר's first wife was childless for many years, and his second marriage was to רבי עקיבא איגר's daughter.) The title is taken from the principle that three consecutive generations of תורה — the גמרא in כתובות calls it חוט המשולש לא ינתק[2] — do not get cut off.

The descendants kept writing, and each named his sefer after his own name:

חתם סופר → כתב סופר → שבט סופר → דעת סופר.

The first one to make עליה moved to ארץ ישראל. The אייניקלך of the חתם סופר still serve as great רבנים today; there are thousands of them.

III. The מחלוקת in פרעסבורג

The Chassidic Revolution

In חוט המשולש there is a long account of a great controversy in פרעסבורג. פרעסבורג was always a unique city — a city of Oberland yidden who davened נוסח אשכנז. But after the בעל שם טוב revealed himself there was an upheaval over the נוסח של תפילה, and the חתם סופר and others adjusted certain things in response.

The חסידים in the 1700s changed davening on two main fronts.

The physical posture of davening. The חרמות against the חסידים — yes, the וילנא גאון and his תלמידים placed the חסידים in חרם — describe how when they davened they looked like absolute lunatics. They jumped up and down, rolled on the floor, called out. We also shuckle when we learn and daven, based on the זוהר הקדוש and other ספרים, but the חסידים took it to another level. The וילנא גאון objected that this turned the decorum of the shul into a circus.

The נוסח התפילה itself. They changed the actual נוסח. The שו״ת literature of the פוסקים of that period is full of sharp קושיות against the חסידים over those changes. למעשה, the חסידים won. The חפץ חיים said toward the end of his life that without the בעל שם טוב and the חסידיש movement, the משכילים would have taken over כלל ישראל. We needed that warmth — even though it came at a price: changes in shul decorum, in נוסח, and the introduction of all kinds of מנהגים based on קבלה that had not been part of our standard Jewish experience. הקדוש ברוך הוא knows what He is doing. The חסידים eventually adopted נוסח האר״י (or נוסח אר״י of חב״ד), incorporating elements based on קבלה into the נוסח התפילה.

The Pressburg Split — "עושה'ניקס" and "המברך'ניקס"

In פרעסבורג the קהל split over precisely this. One camp held to המברך את עמו ישראל בשלום, the other to עושה השלום. The מחלוקת became so acute that one camp was nicknamed the עושה'ניקס and the other the המברך'ניקס.

Picture the irony: the ברכה of שלום itself became the dividing line of two warring camps. We all want שלום — that is what we are asking for. And that is what they fought about.

IV. יהיו לרצון — Before, After, or Both?

What is יהיו לרצון?

At the very end of שמונה עשרה we say a פסוק from תהלים: יהיו לרצון אמרי פי והגיון לבי לפניך. May the words of my mouth — and even more, the unspoken thoughts of my heart — be לרצון, accepted, before You. From the standpoint of דקדוק the verb is open: it could mean "the things I will say in the next few minutes of שמונה עשרה" or "the things I have already said." That ambiguity matters.

The גמרא in ברכות

The גמרא asks: who told us to say יהיו לרצון at the end of שמונה עשרה? Maybe — like ה' שפתי תפתח at the beginning — it should be at the start.[3]

The answer comes through this principle: תפילה in the strict sense is שמונה עשרה. קריאת שמע is not תפילה. We bundle it all together as davening, but when חז״ל say "תפילה" they mean the עמידה. Just as we are not מפסיק between גאולה and תפילה — and ה' שפתי תפתח is not considered a הפסק but rather part and parcel of שמונה עשרה — the same applies to יהיו לרצון. It is part of the עמידה.

Following דוד המלך

And we always follow דוד המלך, especially in שמונה עשרה. Every ברכה of שמונה עשרה is patterned on קפיטלעך תהלים in the order in which דוד המלך embedded his בקשות. After eighteen קפיטלעך תהלים — corresponding to our eighteen ברכות — דוד המלך says יהיו לרצון אמרי פי והגיון לבי לפניך. So obviously it must come at the end.

(Technically it is after the nineteenth perek, not the eighteenth. But פרק א and פרק ב are counted as one in the times of the גמרא — a famous גמרא in ברכות. We have nineteen ברכות too, with ברכת המינים, so the math works out perfectly.)

The מחלוקת הראשונים

Then the question sharpens: do we say יהיו לרצון before אלוקי נצור, after it, or both?

The רשב״א in מסכת ברכות writes: if the גמרא says דוד המלך said it immediately after the eighteen קפיטלעך, and we mimic דוד, then it must be said immediately after שמונה עשרה — before אלוקי נצור. Why? Because אלוקי נצור is not part of שמונה עשרה.[4]

The וילנא גאון raises a powerful objection. If you say יהיו לרצון first, then אלוקי נצור is no longer part of שמונה עשרה — it is as if you have already taken three steps back. So the גר״א says: leave יהיו לרצון for last. Insert אלוקי נצור directly after המברך את עמו ישראל בשלום while you are still standing in שמונה עשרה, and only then say יהיו לרצון.

We adopt the גר״א's מנהג; the רמ״ע מפאנו[5] also says it. The משנה ברורה[6], however, says that what we actually do today is even better: say it both times — once before אלוקי נצור (as part of the עמידה proper, satisfying the רשב״א) and once after (satisfying the גר״א). It is admittedly a bit תרתי דסתרי and does not perfectly hold together, but that is the מנהג.

A Standing Question on מנחה of יום כיפור There is a great question in the אחרונים at מנחה every יום כיפור. Look at any מחזור: do you say על חטא before אלוקי נצור or after? וידוי has to be part of the תפילה — it cannot come after שמונה עשרה. The אחרונים argue exactly what to do; many say to follow whatever is printed in your מחזור, and יש על מי לסמוך either way.

V. Where Did אלוקי נצור Come From?

Did you know אלוקי נצור only appeared later? This whole tefillah that every yid says religiously three times a day, and considers part of davening, was not back in the day. In the time of the גאונים and ראשונים not many people said it. The אבודרהם[7], one of the great מפרשים on תפילה, says it was originally only for יחידים. Today it is in every סידור — but if you research ancient סידורים you will find that not all of them included it, and not all the גאונים said it.

Its source is a גמרא in ברכות[8] about a famous אמורא named מר בריה דרבינא — Mar, the son of Ravina. (This is a different Ravina than the one we know from "רבינא ורב אשי," who came later.)

VI. מר בריה דרבינא — A Man מלומד בנסים

נס הכותל and the Story Behind a Legend

The past year, when we were in וורמייזא with הרב זצ״ל, we visited the בית מדרש of רש״י. The historian Yehuda Gevera — a remarkable historian — brought us to the start of רש״י's בית מדרש and showed us an indentation in the wall. According to legend, when רש״י's mother was carrying him she was walking down a very narrow street where she lived. A knight on horseback came galloping through the alley. She would have been run over — and we would not have רש״י על כל התורה or על כל הש״ס. The legend says הקדוש ברוך הוא made a נס: the wall opened, she pressed herself into it, the knight passed, and ברוך השם, we have רש״י. (The historian was, in fact, mocking it, and proved conclusively that the story never actually happened.)

But where does such a story come from? It comes from the גמרא — not about רש״י, but about מר בריה דרבינא. The גמרא in ברכות דף נ״ד[9] says he was walking in the marketplace of מחוזא — מחוזא was a place in modern-day Iraq, near present-day Mosul — when suddenly a mad camel charged at him. Behind him a hole opened in the wall, and he flew into the safety of the house. For the rest of his life every time he passed by that place he said:

ברוך שעשה לי נס במקום הזה.

That is the ברכה you make on the spot of a personal miracle. The גמרא records other נסים of his as well; he was מלומד בנסים — the expression we use for someone to whom miracles routinely happen. גמרא is not legend. Whatever חז״ל say is אמת לאמיתו.

The Origin of Breaking the Glass under the חופה

מר בריה דרבינא is also the source of the מנהג to break a glass under the חופה. The גמרא in ברכות tells that he was making a חתונה for his son, and everybody was very happy — too happy for his liking. Perhaps he was a Litvak; the שמחה had crossed into הוללות, into wildness. So at his own son's חתונה he stood up, took a vase worth four hundred זוז — it must have been an extraordinary, expensive piece — and smashed it on the floor. Everyone fell silent. The הוללות dissipated. They realized they had gone too far.

From there comes the מנהג that the חתן smashes a glass under the חופה — תוספות[10] says it is זכר לחורבן: אם אשכחך ירושלים תשכח ימיני.[11] May we all be זוכה to break glasses at חופות for our children and grandchildren.

VII. The תפילה of אלוקי נצור

The same גמרא says that מר בריה דרבינא, after his daily שמונה עשרה, decided to add his own private words. That is itself a חידוש. We are not allowed to add to the מטבע שטבעו חכמים — the formula minted by the אנשי כנסת הגדולה. We are talking about the last generation of נביאים, שמעון הצדיק, and figures from centuries before him.

But the גמרא explains: while you may not add new ברכות, you can add personal language at the place we now call אלוקי נצור. מר בריה דרבינא added in his own words: אלוקי נצור לשוני מרע… עד כל מחשבותם — until "all their thoughts." (Our printed נוסח is slightly different from the גמרא's; the גמרא has the language closer to "anyone who wants to harm me." נוסח ספרד keeps that explicit, while other נוסחאות have softened it. But that is what one is supposed to have in כוונה.)

Once you have said יהיו לרצון, however, you have effectively sealed שמונה עשרה — it is as if you have already taken three steps back. From that point on, you may add whatever you wish in your own words.

VIII. The Two Questions Behind the Whole סוגיא

We left off with two questions raised by רבינו ברוך that hover over the closing of שמונה עשרה.

First — why this תפילה? Why did the early generations standardize the personal addition of this particular אמורא — מר בריה דרבינא's אלוקי נצור לשוני מרע — and not the personal תפילה of any of the dozens of other אמוראים the גמרא in ברכות records?

Second — why specifically about לשון הרע? Out of all the תרי״ג מצוות, why do we conclude שמונה עשרה with a request to be saved specifically from לשון הרע? We say ברוך השם, we thank for everything, and then specifically: "please, הקדוש ברוך הוא, save me from לשון הרע today."

The מפרשי תפילה answer that this is itself a סימן — a testimony to how prevalent and dangerous לשון הרע really is. We do not fully appreciate what we are saying. Often, even as we are still taking our three steps back, we are already speaking לשון הרע to the person beside us. The גמרא teaches: do not say "I am a צדיק" — everyone stumbles in לשון הרע, if not actual לשון הרע then at least אבק לשון הרע. A yid davens to הקדוש ברוך הוא: I know I am about to step into the world. I have just spoken to You. Please let the words that come out of my mouth today be holy.

IX. Two Beautiful כוונות in יהיו לרצון

Accepting Even Words Without כוונה

Another way to read יהיו לרצון is straightforward and personal. None of us is מכוון to every single word of שמונה עשרה unless we are holding at a very high level. Saying every word — fine, ברוך השם. But thinking every word? That is hard. We say to הקדוש ברוך הוא: the words that left my mouth, even where I had no כוונה, even if they are empty — accept them. אמרי פי. And the פסוק adds והגיון לבי — the parts where I did have כוונה, accept those too.

The world makes a joke. If a man wants to take a trip he needs a plane ticket and a visa. If he wants to be in Hong Kong, all he has to do is take three steps back, three steps forward, and start שמונה עשרה — his brain is already in Hong Kong. We are simply asking הקדוש ברוך הוא: even if our כוונה wandered, please accept the words. The פסוק אלה ברכב ואלה בסוסים ואנחנו בשם ה' אלוקינו נזכיר — the גוים have רכב and סוסים and military; we have הקדוש ברוך הוא's name.[12]

The רד״ק — Thoughts Beyond Words

The רד״ק[13] on that very פסוק says something beautiful. The meaning is something else: there were always thoughts and כוונות behind the words that could not be put into words. Sometimes you want to daven for something but cannot find the language. We are supposed to use our own words at this point in שמונה עשרה. The פוסקים say: add whatever you want into אלוקי נצור — a שידוך for your child, פרנסה, anything — ask.

But sometimes the thought is in your head and you cannot get it out. We tell הקדוש ברוך הוא: You are יודע מחשבות. You know what I mean even when I cannot say it. Read my מחשבות as if I had said them. That is also a beautiful כוונה a yid should have at this point of davening.

X. When to Lengthen — אלוקי נצור or יהיו לרצון?

Practically speaking, where should one elaborate? The פוסקים say it is better to lengthen after יהיו לרצון, not during שמונה עשרה proper. The reason is technical: if you are מאריך and מאריך in personal בקשות while still in שמונה עשרה, you will miss קדיש, miss קדושה, miss אמן. After יהיו לרצון you may answer קדיש and קדושה, you may say אמן, and you can elaborate as long as you wish. That is exactly how the שלחן ערוך[14] frames it.

XI. The שלה הקדוש and the פסוק of One's Name

Identity by Name

The שלה הקדוש[15] teaches something profound about the connection between a person's name and his נשמה. A person here in this world is identified by his looks, his career, his vocation — and also his name. In עולם האמת a נשמה is identified only by one thing: by its name. There is even a tradition that when a נשמה arrives there for its accounting, some people forget their own name at that crucial moment.

The שלה brings down a סגולה: at the end of שמונה עשרה, recite a פסוק that begins with the first letter of your name and ends with the last letter. If your name is ישראל, find a פסוק beginning with י and ending with ל. (I personally practiced this מנהג in my youth and later left it off, since it does not actually originate with the שלה himself, but many continue the practice and it remains printed in our סידורים.)

Where Does the מנהג Actually Come From?

Its origins are obscure. There is a fascinating phenomenon in תנ״ך study: when learning with פירוש רש״י you sometimes encounter additions in parentheses. Some are authentic רש״י; others are פירוש אחר. Who wrote them?

אפרים זלמן מרגליות of Brody — one of the most prolific authors of the nineteenth century — addressed exactly this in his ספר שם אפרים[16]. In his commentary on רש״י על נ״ך, particularly in שופטים פרק כ', he writes very sharply about how the printers and publishers added material to רש״י without proper attribution, creating confusion that led people to believe רש״י had said things he never said. Despite his protests, those parenthetical additions remain in our printed editions of נ״ך to this day.

The earliest reference to saying the פסוק of one's name appears in ספר מכלול from one of the ראשונים, and in תריסר אסר[17]. But that source does not say to recite it during davening — it simply says that if a person recites the פסוק that begins with his name's first letter and ends with its last, the תורה will protect him from עונש גהינום. There is no instruction to incorporate it into תפילה.

The first appearance of the practice in the סידור is אמסטרדם 1699[18]. (I have actually seen a copy of that סידור.) From that point on the printers began including it universally — why not? Everyone wants an extra זכות against גהינום. רבי יעקב עמדין[19] also brings the מנהג, and after 1699 it appears in virtually every printed סידור.

The זוהר הקדוש[20] cites the broader idea — a person's name is essentially his נשמה, and references פרשת האזינו on this. Therefore when you invoke your name through the פסוק you are invoking your נשמה, and the כח התורה of repeating that פסוק provides spiritual protection.

XII. Names from תנ״ך — נמרוד and ישמעאל

An interesting discussion arose over which biblical names are appropriate. נמרוד is a popular name in ארץ ישראל today, yet נמרוד is an infamous personality in תנ״ך. ישמעאל too is a תנ״ך name, and we still find yidden named ישמעאל. Which biblical personalities are considered acceptable to name a child after, and which are not?

We have to judge yidden לכף זכות. Many people simply do not know the תנ״ך context — they hear a name that sounds biblical and pleasant and use it without realizing the associations. There is even a story of a מוהל who refused to perform a ברית מילה when the parents wanted to name the child נמרוד, precisely because of that figure's reputation in תנ״ך.

XIII. The Name Is the נשמה

In עולם האמת a yid is identified by his נשמה. Here, by looks, by what he does, his career, his vocation, and also by his name — but in עולם האמת, only by his name. That is why I like to ask people their Hebrew name. I do not want to call a yid by his English name; I want to call him by his real name. There were many גדולים who opposed English names altogether. Today we have to use them — but ever notice how all the Hungarian yidden who came after the war changed their names to English names? Even in Hungary they had Hungarian names.

The האריז״ל[21] says that when a father names a son at the ברית מילה, it is רוח הקודש. Why? Because a name is not a label; it is a heavenly assignment of identity. הקדוש ברוך הוא, כביכול, identifies נשמות by name.

דרך אגב — Waking with the Hebrew Name The בני יששכר writes in his מאמרי חודש כסלו, in ספר אגרא דפרקא[22], one of the סברות: when you want to wake someone up — your child, a guest — alarm clocks do not help, lights do not help, water does not help. Say his Jewish name. Because then you are speaking to the נשמה, not the גוף. The נשמה leaves the body during sleep; you are inviting the נשמה back down. Use the Hebrew name.

XIV. שכר ועונש and גהינום

There is a tradition — I am calling it a tradition because we do not know the exact מקור in חז״ל — that when a yid comes to עולם האמת, הקדוש ברוך הוא will judge us, and רשעים will be punished.

שכר ועונש is real. Whether we whitewash it or tell our kids "השם loves you, everything is good" — everything is not so good. There is עונש. You do מצוות, you receive שכר. You do עברות, you receive עונש. Even in 2025 we cannot forget this. תשובה כלפי שמיא is genuine — we just had יום כיפור and we are clean — but a חטא, untreated, gets עונש.

When a person comes up to שמים, the ספרים describe the experience in language that should make any of us tremble. They write of the מלאכי חבלה surrounding the נשמה, of the פטש it receives. It is a נשמה — it is spiritual — so how do you receive a physical blow? The ספרים struggle to give us the imagery, and the imagery is bad news. רבותי, if you have a connection in שמים, be careful when reading those ספרים. You can faint.

This is the deeper backdrop to the closing of our שמונה עשרה. We ask הקדוש ברוך הוא to accept our words even where כוונה fell short, to read our hearts where we could not find the language, to keep our tongues from לשון הרע, and to remember our נשמות by their true Hebrew names.

סיכום

May we be זוכה to daven to הקדוש ברוך הוא and have all our תפילות accepted. May we merit to say יהיו לרצון אמרי פי with proper כוונה — for both the words we say and the thoughts we cannot express. May our names be called in שמים with כבוד, and may we be guarded from לשון הרע and from every עבירה.

מראה מקומות  ·  Mareh Mekomos

  1. חוט המשולש, by a grandchild of the חתם סופר — three-generation biography (רבי עקיבא איגר, חתם סופר, כתב סופר). — The classic biographical compilation by ר' שלמה סופר (1853–1930), grandson of the כתב סופר and great-grandson of the חתם סופר. Title from קהלת ד':י"ב — והחוט המשולש לא במהרה ינתק, applied in ב"מ פ"ה ע"א to three consecutive generations of תורה in one family. The sefer contains the detailed history of the פרעסבורג נוסח controversy and the שיעור's note on the early אשכנז practice of saying עושה השלום the entire year. ↩
  2. קהלת ד':י"ב; בבא מציעא פ"ה ע"א. — החוט המשולש לא במהרה ינתק — the principle, applied in ב"מ פ"ה to a chain of three generations of תלמידי חכמים in one family, that תורה in three successive generations is "not easily severed." (Note: the שיעור attributed the source to כתובות; the principal גמרא location is ב"מ פ"ה ע"א.) ↩
  3. ברכות ד' ע"ב and ברכות ט' ע"ב; ברכות ל"א ע"א. — The גמרא in ברכות establishes that ה' שפתי תפתח (תהלים נ"א:י"ז) is recited at the start of שמונה עשרה as integral to it (no הפסק). The parallel discussion of יהיו לרצון at the close — its source in תהלים י"ט:ט"ו following the eighteen קפיטלעך of תהלים that parallel the eighteen ברכות of the עמידה — is in ברכות ד' ע"ב and is foundational for the placement of יהיו לרצון at the end of שמונה עשרה. ↩
  4. חידושי הרשב"א, ברכות ל"א. — The רשב"א (ר' שלמה בן אברהם בן אדרת, Barcelona, c. 1235–1310) holds that יהיו לרצון should be said immediately after the eighteen ברכות, in parallel with דוד המלך's placement after the eighteen קפיטלעך — and therefore before אלוקי נצור, since אלוקי נצור is post-עמידה. ↩
  5. ביאור הגר"א, או"ח קכ"ב; רמ"ע מפאנו. — The גר"א (ר' אליהו מווילנא, 1720–1797) in his ביאור on the שלחן ערוך argues against the רשב"א: אלוקי נצור belongs inside שמונה עשרה proper (before the three steps back), and יהיו לרצון only after. The רמ"ע מפאנו (ר' מנחם עזריה מפאנו, 1548–1620) makes the same point in his liturgical writings. ↩
  6. משנה ברורה, או"ח קכ"ב, ס"ק ג'. — The משנה ברורה records the universal current מנהג: say יהיו לרצון both before and after אלוקי נצור, satisfying the רשב"א (first recital, attached to the עמידה) and the גר"א/רמ"ע מפאנו (second recital, sealing the תפילה just before the three steps back). The משנה ברורה acknowledges this is somewhat תרתי דסתרי but defends it as the established מנהג. ↩
  7. אבודרהם, on סיום שמונה עשרה. — ר' דוד אבודרהם (Seville, mid-14th century), author of the classic commentary on the סדור. He records that אלוקי נצור was originally an addition recited only by יחידים (individuals as their personal closing תפילה), and only later became universal. Verify exact location in printed אבודרהם on the תפילה of שחרית. ↩
  8. ברכות ט"ז ע"ב – י"ז ע"א — אמוראים' personal תפילות; ברכות ט"ז ע"ב for מר בריה דרבינא's אלוקי נצור. — The גמרא in ברכות ch. 2 (after המשנה of תפילת הדרך) records the personal closing תפילות of various אמוראים: רבי, רבי אלעזר, רב, רבי יוחנן, רב חייא, רב, and others — and famously the תפילה of מר בריה דרבינא, beginning אלוקי, נצור לשוני מרע, which became the standard closing תפילה of every שמונה עשרה. ↩
  9. ברכות נ"ד ע"א-ע"ב. — The משנה: הרואה מקום שנעשה לו נס מברך... ברוך שעשה לי נס במקום הזה. The גמרא (נ"ד ע"ב) brings the story of מר בריה דרבינא in the marketplace of מחוזא — the mad camel and the wall that opened — and notes he was מלומד בנסים. (Note: מחוזא was a Babylonian Jewish center on the Tigris, not "near Mosul"; the shiur's geographic detail should be verified.) ↩
  10. תוספות, ברכות ל"א ע"א, ד"ה אייתי; שבירת הכוס בחופה. — תוספות on the גמרא of מר בריה דרבינא smashing the costly cup at his son's חתונה derives the מנהג of breaking a glass under the חופה as a זכר לחורבן, on the פסוק in תהלים קל"ז: אם אשכחך ירושלים תשכח ימיני. Codified in רמ"א, אבן העזר ס"ה ס"ג. ↩
  11. תהלים קל"ז:ה' — אם אשכחך ירושלים תשכח ימיני. — The classical פסוק from על נהרות בבל recited by the חתן just before breaking the glass: an oath never to forget ירושלים even at the height of personal joy. ↩
  12. תהלים כ':ח' — אלה ברכב ואלה בסוסים, ואנחנו בשם ה' אלוקינו נזכיר. — From the קפיטל recited by the לוים over a קרבן in time of trouble. The contrast between gentile reliance on chariots and horses and Jewish reliance on שם ה'; cited in the שיעור as background to יהיו לרצון's plea that the name we invoke carries our words past wandering כוונה. ↩
  13. רד"ק על תהלים י"ט:ט"ו. — רבי דוד קמחי (Provence, c. 1160–1235) on the פסוק יהיו לרצון אמרי פי והגיון לבי לפניך: דוד המלך asks that even the unspoken הגיון לב — the כוונות and מחשבות behind the words that he could not articulate — be accepted by הקב"ה, יודע מחשבות. The שיעור develops this as a beautiful כוונה for the closing of שמונה עשרה. ↩
  14. שלחן ערוך, אורח חיים סימן קכ"ב; משנה ברורה there. — The שלחן ערוך codifies the order of אלוקי נצור, the personal additions, and יהיו לרצון. The פוסקים direct that personal בקשות and any lengthening should come after יהיו לרצון, not within the עמידה proper, so as not to forfeit responses to קדיש, קדושה, and אמן. ↩
  15. של"ה הקדוש — שני לוחות הברית, מסכת תמיד. — ר' ישעיה הורוויץ (1565–1630), author of שני לוחות הברית. The connection between a person's name and his נשמה, and the סגולה of saying a פסוק at the close of שמונה עשרה that begins with the first letter and ends with the last letter of one's name, is associated with him in popular tradition. The שיעור notes that the סגולה's direct attribution to the של"ה himself is uncertain; verify against the printed של"ה. ↩
  16. שם אפרים, ר' אפרים זלמן מרגליות (1762–1828), Brody. — ר' אפרים זלמן מרגליות of Brody, one of the leading תלמידי חכמים and bibliographers of his era. In שם אפרים he documents the printers' interpolations into פירוש רש"י on נ"ך (especially in שופטים and שמואל) — additions later editions perpetuated. The שיעור invokes him to illustrate how unattributed מנהגים and פירושים migrate into our printed liturgy. ↩
  17. ספר מכלול (רד"ק) and ספר תריסר אסר.
    Note: מכלול by the רד"ק is a grammatical work; the citation given here should be verified against later מקובלים' compilations citing the סגולה of saying a פסוק matching one's name as protection from עונש גהינום. תריסר אסר ("Twelve Bonds") is a less commonly cited title; verify the printed source. The סגולה in its earliest forms appears in קבלי-influenced חיבורים of the late ראשונים period; printed סדורים began including the practice from the late 17th century onward.
    ↩
  18. סידור Amsterdam 1699 — first appearance of the פסוק of one's name in print.
    Note: Late-17th-century Amsterdam סדור printings (the major Jewish printing center of the era) appear to be the earliest mass-distribution liturgical books to insert the פסוק of one's name at the close of שמונה עשרה. After 1699 the practice spread universally.   19. סידור היעב"ץ — ר' יעקב עמדין (1697–1776). — ר' יעקב עמדין, son of the חכם צבי, in his celebrated סידור היעב"ץ (also known as סידור עמודי שמים) records the מנהג of saying a פסוק of one's name and discusses its meaning. His endorsement was central to the practice's universal acceptance in סדורי אשכנז. ↩
  20. זוהר, פרשת האזינו; cf. פרשת תזריע. — The זוהר develops the principle that a person's name is the channel of his נשמה and reflects his spiritual root. The locus classicus is זוהר ח"ב, פרשת תזריע ל"ב ע"ב on naming, and discussions in פרשת האזינו on the soul's identification by name. The סגולה of saying the matching פסוק draws on this conceptual framework. ↩
  21. האריז"ל — שער הגלגולים, on naming and רוח הקודש at ברית מילה. — ר' יצחק לוריא (1534–1572) teaches in his קבלי writings (שער הגלגולים, שער הכוונות) that the name a father gives his son at the ברית מילה is given by רוח הקודש — that הקב"ה places into the father's mouth precisely the name that matches the נשמה's root. Cited widely in later חסידי and קבלי literature. ↩
  22. אגרא דפרקא, בני יששכר (ר' צבי אלימלך מדינוב, 1783–1841), מאמרי חודש כסלו. — ר' צבי אלימלך מדינוב, author of the בני יששכר and אגרא דפרקא, brings the practice that to wake a sleeping person — child or guest — one calls his Hebrew name, since the נשמה ascends during sleep and is summoned back through the spiritual identifier. Verify exact aphorism number in אגרא דפרקא; the source is widely cited in חסידי literature on השכמת הבוקר. ↩
