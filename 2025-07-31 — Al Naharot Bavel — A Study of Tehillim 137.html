על נהרות בבל — A Study of תהלים Chapter 137

Special pre-תשעה באב Shiur of Rabbi Ephrati
Recorded 31 July 2025, three nights before תשעה באב תשפ"ה  ·  Reworked 3 May 2026

A pre-תשעה באב walk through תהלים פרק קל"ז — the פרק we say before בענטשן on weekdays — opened with the מלבי"ם's reading that the יהודים in בבל had everything בגשמיות but maintained the ability to cry; the מדרש's harrowing account of the march to בבל and the willows hung with violins; the אלשיך הקדוש on "the worst part of גלות is when we cannot cry"; and the deeply uncomfortable question about where the מלאכים were during the שואה and on שמחת תורה two years ago. The פרק's harsh closing reframed by the מלבי"ם: not bloodlust, but the recognition of what we will not become.

I. Why This פרק

It is fitting to gather before תשעה באב to enter the consciousness this season requires. We say פרק קל"ז daily before בענטשן on weekdays — the מחבר in סימן א' of שולחן ערוך[9] brings it as הלכה for the mourning of the חורבן; on שבת and יום טוב we say שיר המעלות instead. Reading the פרק in depth, with what חז"ל and the מפרשים have to say, sets the perspective for what we are supposed to feel during תשעה באב and the days surrounding it.

דוד המלך wrote this פרק long before the חורבן בית ראשון. He sees it ברוח הקודש. The image he draws is one we know well — burned into our נשמות across generations, even if our parents and grandparents are the ones who saw it directly. How many paintings of this scene; how many photographs.

II. פסוק א' — "על נהרות בבל שם ישבנו גם בכינו"

על נהרות בבל שם ישבנו גם בכינו, בזכרנו את ציון.
תהלים קל"ז:א'[3]

The Literal Reading

An image of mourners — אבלים — walking the rivers of בבל with sacks on their backs, just expelled from ירושלים, weeping when they remembered ציון.

The מלבי"ם's Reading — Settled and Crying

The מלבי"ם[1] proposes a different reading. Don't think for a moment that in בבל we lacked גשמיות — we had it all. Real estate, business, prosperity, whatever a community needs to survive in גלות. It took time to break into the Babylonian network, but they got in. The מלבי"ם brings פסוקים in ירמיהו[4] describing how, not long after they arrived, יהודים in בבל already had homes, fields, vineyards, businesses.

So "שם ישבנו" on the מלבי"ם's reading does not mean sitting like an אבל on the floor. It means we settled. "עס איז געווען אין ארדנונג" — it was okay; we had what we needed. "גם בכינו" — and we still cried. Despite the comfort, despite the גשמיות, we retained the ability to weep for what we lost.

III. פסוק ב' — "על ערבים בתוכה תלינו כנרותינו"

What does a person take when he is expelled from his homeland? On the eve of the שואה, what did our grandparents take? The most precious, sentimental, valuable thing they owned. דוד המלך says: when כלל ישראל left ירושלים, they took their harps. Their violins.

What the כנורות Mean

The כנורות represent the connection of כלל ישראל to הקדוש ברוך הוא. When יהודים went up to the בית המקדש for the עבודה, the לוים sang שיר וזמרה. That divine music — real music, not the music we have today, holy ניגונים, קדושה in sound — awakened the דבקות with הקדוש ברוך הוא. נבואה in ancient times came through music. The כנורות were a כלי for that connection.

That is what the יהודים took with them.

The Willows — Salix Babylonica

דוד המלך describes hanging the harps on the ערבים by the Euphrates. In botanical taxonomy these trees are Salix Babylonica — the Babylonian willow, named after this very פסוק. In English they are called weeping willows for the drooping branches that look like mourning. כלל ישראל hung their harps on trees that themselves appeared to weep.

The רד"ק — When Tears Prevent Song

The רד"ק[5]: כלל ישראל took the harps in hope of a quiet moment, a moment of מנוחת הנפש in which to take them out and play, to hear those tunes that had stirred דבקות in the בית המקדש. When that moment finally came, they began to play — and could not continue. The memory of ציון, of the glory of the בית המקדש, broke through. They wept and stopped: "We cannot play. The wrong time. We cannot play שיר ה'." They hung the כנורות on the weeping willows: "You belong here now. We are too emotional. You will have your time. But this is a time to cry, not to play שיר ה'."

The מלבי"ם — The Sweetness of בבל

The מלבי"ם's חידוש is sharper. He reads ערבים with a play on the word: ערבים as in willows and ערבים as in sweet things (מלשון ערבים — נעימים).

Because of the sweetness of בבל, we hung our harps.

בבל tempted us with the pleasures of עולם הזה: "Come and join us, sing and dance with us, eat our food, enjoy our wealth, enjoy בבל." Precisely because of that sweetness, "על ערבים בתוכה תלינו כנרותינו". "How can we play music celebrating the גשמיות of בבל? How can we celebrate their culture, their lifestyle, their עולם הזה with our harps?"

The sweetness of עולם הזה — what does it represent? It represents people who hate רוחניות, who hate יידישקייט, who hate יהודים. Why do they hate us? Because we have תורה, we have a connection with הקדוש ברוך הוא; we are the antithesis of celebrating עולם הזה for its own sake. We use עולם הזה for עבודה; עבודה is much higher.

So when בבל calls us to dance to its music, the יהודים of that generation said no. "שם ישבנו" — even though we settled here. "גם בכינו" — we still cry, because we still yearn for קדושה. "תלינו כנרותינו" — we hung the harps. "Sorry, נבוכדנצר. Sorry, בבל. Sorry, אומות העולם. We know what we lost. We may not have it now, but we yearn to reconnect, and the two cannot mix."

IV. פסוק ג' — בבל's Demand

כי שם שאלונו שובינו דברי שיר, ותוללינו שמחה: שירו לנו משיר ציון.

"תוללינו" from the root of "תלל" — those harps we hung: בבל demanded we take them down and play. בבל saw כלל ישראל hanging the harps on the willows and said: "No! רוחניות is your past. You are out of your homeland. The Jewish story of connecting with הקדוש ברוך הוא in the בית המקדש is over. עולם הזה is the present. You are a nation among nations now."

What Was Demanded

The מלבי"ם reads the demand precisely. Every nation has a national anthem — a song boasting of wealth, resources, power, talents. בבל asked: "What is yours?" Ours was שיר ה' — the songs of the בית המקדש, the שיר של יום, קפיטלעך תהלים, the שירה of השראת השכינה.

And בבל wanted something even worse, says the מלבי"ם: "Don't stop singing the שיר ה'. We want to hear it — the way you sang and danced to הקדוש ברוך הוא in the בית המקדש. Take that very song. Just change the words. Cut and paste. Where you sang to הקדוש ברוך הוא, sing to בבל — same intensity, same passion, same enthusiasm. For עולם הזה."

We said no. כלל ישראל at that moment maintained their דיגניטעט. "We are not like the אומות העולם. We will not dance to the tunes of the nations of the world. We will not sing their music." The point cuts deep. It is not narrowly about Jewish or non-Jewish music. It is about taking the שיר ה' and bending it into a celebration of עולם הזה. That, בבל wanted; כלל ישראל would not give.

V. פסוק ד' — The Eternal Question

איך נשיר את שיר ה' על אדמת נכר?

דוד המלך asks: how can you ask a יהודי who experienced קדושה in the בית המקדש, who lived within the השראת השכינה confirming itself daily, who could go to a נביא and ask "what is my תפקיד?" — to sing שיר ה' on the soil of strangers? Impossible. You cannot expect a יהודי who knows what שיר ה' is, what שיר ציון is, to celebrate the גשמיות of בבל with the songs of ציון. The two do not go together.

VI. The מדרש — The Brutality of the March

A יהודי must know this מדרש[7] to know who he is.

The מדרש on the first פסוק: בבל did not let us sit for a moment. They expelled us from ירושלים and made us walk and walk — in chains, hungry, thirsty, under the burning Middle Eastern sun. It was תשעה באב. No food, no drink, metal chains, single-file marches. The Holocaust echo: schnell, schnell, dogs barking. Go. No time to think. No time to have a moment of מנוחת הנפש.

Even the simple human need — to weep, to come to terms emotionally with the tragedy — the בבלים would not allow. After losing so much, a person needs to weep. The first time they let us sit was "על נהרות בבל" — at the Euphrates, after a tremendous distance. "שם ישבנו" — there, finally, after so many days, we could sit. And for the first time we could cry.

The אלשיך — The Worst Aspect of גלות

The אלשיך הקדוש[6] asks: what is the worst part of גלות בבל? What is the worst aspect of חורבן בית המקדש? Not the destruction of the בית המקדש itself. Not the geographic exile.

The worst part is that they did not let us cry.

Not allowing a יהודי to cry is the worst גלות. Perhaps the אלשיך means more than the physical prevention. If we engage too deeply with עולם הזה, if we dull our sensitivity to קדושה — to יידישקייט, to the בית המקדש, to the שכינה הקדושה — if we become like בבל instead of like the יהודים who said no — then we cannot cry. Not because anyone is forcing it, but because desensitization has set in. We know we are supposed to cry. We know there is something to cry about. But there is a numbness; the tears do not come.

The Soldier at the כותל

The famous 1967 story[11] when the Israeli forces reached the כותל. רב גורן proclaimed "הר הבית בידינו! הכותל בידינו!" יהודים rushed from across ירושלים to embrace the stones — to daven, to dance, to weep. Among them stood a soldier from a kibbutz, a secular soldier from a non-religious upbringing, weeping. He stood next to a religious soldier from his platoon — a בחור with כיפה and ציצית — also weeping. The religious soldier asked: "I understand why I am crying. Why are you?"

The secular soldier answered: "Do you know why I am crying? I am crying because I do not know what you are crying about. I do not understand what there is to cry about, and that itself makes me cry."

Only a יהודי could give that answer. The נשמה of a יהודי, even buried under decades of secular life, knows there is something to cry about. The יהודים of בבל retained that ability; many in גלות across our generations have not. The אלשיך's פשט stands.

VII. נבוכדנצר's Cruise and the Pride of the Exiles

The מדרש continues. As the יהודים walked the river, נבוכדנצר was on a cruise ship sailing the Euphrates beside them — שריו וגדוליו with him, כל מיני זמר playing royal music, אוכלים ושותים ומקרקרין (the Hebrew word for partying). The complete contrast: oppressor in luxury; oppressed in chains.

Heads Held High

Watching the יהודים walk into גלות, נבוכדנצר saw something extraordinary. Their heads were held high. Their bearing was dignified, despite being naked, in shackles, having lost everything, carrying a sack of clothing and a worn harp or violin. A יהודי is a יהודי. Even leaving ירושלים with smoke billowing behind them, even with everything stripped, they did not bow their heads.

נבוכדנצר demanded of his servants: "What is going on? How are these יהודים walking into גלות with their heads held high? Take loads of stones and sand and place them on their backs — they shouldn't walk with heads up!" The servants gathered boxes, filled them, placed them on every יהודי. Now they could no longer hold their heads high — bent, hunched, walking like ones truly going into גלות. נבוכדנצר could not tolerate the sight of a יהודי walking into גלות with dignity intact.

הקדוש ברוך הוא's Decision

At that moment, the מדרש teaches, when even the pride of כלל ישראל was being stripped away — when בבל declared "You are no longer a יהודי; that's your past; you are like everybody else" — הקדוש ברוך הוא decided: "דיינו. If this is how My children are treated, return the world to תוהו ובוהו. Let us start again. This is not the world I created."

The מלאכים' Plea

The מלאכים came before הקדוש ברוך הוא: "רבונו של עולם, how can You destroy the world? It is bad enough that You destroyed the בית המקדש; must You now destroy the entire בריאה?"

הקדוש ברוך הוא answered — and the לשון of the מדרש is severe — "תנחומין של הבל תנחמוני. Your consolations are like curses. אל תחישו לנחמני. נחמות from you are toxic right now. You have a problem? You have a complaint? Go down to the world below. Go to those יהודים walking "על נהרות בבל" and take those heavy loads off their backs."

מיד the מלאכים descended and removed the boxes. Eventually נבוכדנצר even restored some clothing to the יהודים. Their dignity, for a moment, was restored — because the מלאכים intervened, after הקדוש ברוך הוא told them to fix the problem themselves rather than to console.

VIII. The Eternal Question

I want to ask a question — a question I know sounds sacrilegious, but I must ask it[2].

I am a יהודי, a grandson of Holocaust survivors, a great-grandson of יהודים who died על קידוש השם. We are part of כלל ישראל; we know what it means to be killed, to be exiled. We saw what those ימ"ש did on שמחת תורה two years ago. This is not foreign to us.

Where were the מלאכים seventy-five, eighty years ago in Auschwitz? When יהודים got off the trains naked, carrying whatever they could salvage from their homes? When children were torn from their mothers and sent to the right and to the left? Where was הקדוש ברוך הוא through every expulsion, every pogrom, every רדיפה across centuries? Where were the מלאכים in כפר עזה, in מושב בארי?

If הקדוש ברוך הוא behaves this way when He sees His children leaving ירושלים for בבל — declaring "דיינו; I will destroy the world; remove the loads from their backs" — where were the מלאכים in all our גלויות since?

I know we are not supposed to ask such questions, but they bother me, and they bother every one of us. The answer, I believe, lies in what the מדרש says next.

IX. The Demand to Sing — and the Jewish Refusal

נבוכדנצר ordered the יהודים, as they sat for the first time at נהרות בבל — hungry, thirsty, exhausted: "Get up and prepare. I am about to begin a feast on this ship. Take your harps from the willows and play music for me."

His own band had כל מיני כלי זמר. It was not enough. He wanted the יהודי's music — to take שיר ה' and uproot it from our hearts, to redirect that intensity toward what בבל represents, toward materialism.

The מדרש: כלל ישראל looked at one another and said, "It is not enough that we destroyed the בית המקדש through our עברות. Now you want us to stand up and play with those very same instruments in front of this midget, in front of this רשע, in front of his עבודה זרה?"

What This Tells Us

Pause. Absorb what the מדרש is teaching. Even hungry, thirsty, with heavy loads on his back, even after losing his רוחניות and his life — the יהודי can still look at the man who did this to him, at the רשע, and say: "I should stand up and play music for this midget?"

That is a יהודי. נבוכדנצר was victorious; he burned and destroyed everything sacred — but now he wants me to play for him? I should play music for this midget? The יהודים understood. They had the sensitivity to recognize that he was a midget in the larger scheme — physically (חז"ל say נבוכדנצר was small), but more fundamentally, in the order of the universe. I should take the שיר ה' and play for someone so small? Play for his עבודה זרה?

The מדרש records — sparing the gory details — that the יהודים saw no way out of the demand: he would force them. So they took their right thumbs, the fingers needed to play strings, and bit them off — they maimed themselves so that they would be unable to play the harps for נבוכדנצר, for his עבודה זרה, for this midget.

X. The משל of the Queen

The מדרש: a משל. There was a queen married to a king, both of royal lineage. One day he said, "My queen, please pour me a cup of wine." She was in a rebellious mood and refused. He expelled her for refusing to pour wine for the king.

She wandered, looking for a home, for someone to marry. Eventually she found someone — but he was a מוכה שחין, a leper covered in boils head to toe, an awful sight. But that was who she found.

One day the מוכה שחין said, "My dear wife, pour me a cup of wine."

What did she answer? The מדרש: "ריקן! You empty-headed fool, you idiot. I did not pour wine for the king when he asked. I made a mistake; I sinned; I should have. But now you want me to pour wine for you, for a מוכה שחין?"

כלל ישראל: "I did not pour wine for הקדוש ברוך הוא. I did not play music for הקדוש ברוך הוא as I should have, and that is why the בית המקדש was destroyed. Now you want me to play music for the אומות העולם, for בבל, for this midget, for this רשע, for this עבודה זרה? I should pour wine for the מוכה שחין? No. I am still a יהודי. I have my pride, my דיגניטעט. We will not sell ourselves to the גוים who want us to dance to their tunes."

That is what the מדרש says. They bit their fingers off. And then they said the next פסוק — not "we will not" but "איך נשיר": "How can we?" Our fingers are gone. We do not have hands anymore. We cannot play. They held up their maimed hands to נבוכדנצר: "We cannot."

XI. נבוכדנצר's Rage and the Secret of גלות

The מדרש: this enraged נבוכדנצר. This is the secret of גלות. He wanted to rip the בית המקדש from our hearts, to uproot רוחניות. The יהודים bit their fingers off rather than play the music of עולם הזה, the music of materialism, the music of the midget.

He was enraged because he understood, in that moment, what a יהודי is. You can take the בית המקדש from him; you can take ארץ ישראל and ירושלים; you can take רוחניות from him — and he is still a יהודי. He still looks at you like a midget. You are nothing.

At that moment נבוכדנצר became so enraged that he slaughtered thousands upon thousands of יהודים before they even reached גלות — killed brutally by נבוכדנצר and his soldiers.

The מדרש reads "ושואלינו שמחה" in light of this — "תלי תלים של הרוגים", piles upon piles of slain. When the יהודים refused, when they bit off their fingers, נבוכדנצר became enraged and killed them. Yet, says the מדרש, this is a מזמור, a שיר of דוד המלך: we had שמחה, we took delight in our restraint — in the fact that we did not give in, we did not sell ourselves to the music of the גוים. When we turned and saw the piles of corpses on the ground, that itself brought שמחה into our hearts — because we still knew we were יהודים, the nation of הקדוש ברוך הוא.

XII. The Answer to Our Question

The moment a יהודי goes out into גלות, he is already reestablishing his connection with the בית המקדש and with הקדוש ברוך הוא. That is a יהודי. The very same יהודים through whose חטא the בית המקדש was destroyed — when they see how נבוכדנצר deals with them, immediately respond: "We are still a יהודי. We will not give you שיר ציון. How could the joy we had singing in the בית המקדש be equated with the songs of עולם הזה? We cannot. Our hands are maimed. The כנורות belong on the weeping willows. That is where they belong."

And the question comes back, with the answer in it:

Are we ready to bite our fingers off rather than dance and sing to the tunes of the אומות העולם?

The יהודים in that generation were ready — and they did. The מלאכים descended for them. If we are not ready to make even a small sacrifice in our עולם הזה for our יידישקייט, then the calculus is different.

XIII. The Essence of גלות

גלות is not just sitting on תשעה באב reciting קינות for a few hours and calling it a day. גלות means the victory of עולם הזה — the triumph of בבל, of materialism, of excessive luxuries, of eating and drinking without restraint. It means living a grub life, the life of a גוי, instead of living as a יהודי is meant to live: in the בית המקדש, with קדושה and טהרה, elevating all materiality toward something transcendent.

The moment a יהודי declares "I no longer enjoy my מקדש; I have no pleasure from it; at least let me enjoy life — eat, drink, be merry; let me enjoy the prosperity that בבל offers" — at that moment that יהודי loses his ability to cry.

The גמרא in בבא בתרא ס' ע"ב

The גמרא[8]: at the time of the חורבן, many יהודים began abstaining from meat and wine. רבי יהושע approached them: "Why?" They answered: "How can we eat meat? How can we drink wine? Meat was just brought as קרבנות in the בית המקדש, drawing us close to הקדוש ברוך הוא; wine was just poured as נסכים on the מזבח, bringing us to דבקות. Now? It has no taste. How can we?"

רבי יהושע: "If so, you should also not eat grain and wheat — used in מנחות. You should not consume oil. You should not eat פירות — used for ביכורים." They had no answer.

The גמרא: it is "גזירה שאין הציבור יכול לעמוד בה" — a decree the ציבור cannot uphold. The Nine Days alone are difficult enough; every five seconds another סיום on Zoom or wherever. We cannot impose universal abstention למעשה. But the sensitivity the מדרש records — "How can I enjoy any עולם הזה when I do not have the בית המקדש?" — that is the spirit of the הלכה, even if not the letter.

XIV. פסוק ה'-ו' — "אם אשכחך ירושלים"

אם אשכחך ירושלים תשכח ימיני, תדבק לשוני לחכי אם לא אזכרכי, אם לא אעלה את ירושלים על ראש שמחתי.

From this פסוק the גמרא derives a foundational principle: at every moment of joy — even the greatest שמחה of our life, the night of a חתונה — we place אפר on the חתן's head.

It is not just a token. There is no real שמחה anymore. We once had real שמחה; perhaps our senses are too dulled to know what we are missing. But at the very least, we should know how a יהודי is supposed to think.

The יהודים of בבל refused meat and wine because the meat in their mouths lacked the "salt of קדושה". There is no real joy in pure materialism — pleasure to the senses, yes; but devoid of meaning. That is why a חתן wears אפר: "In truth, I do not have complete joy. It is not the שלימות of שמחה we had in the days of the בית המקדש."

"תדבק לשוני לחכי" — let my tongue cling to the roof of my mouth if I do not remember ירושלים above my highest joy. You can sing a beautiful song for ירושלים under the חופה; what matters is the מחשבה at that moment — we are missing the real joy. We may be too deep in עולם הזה to truly yearn for it. But we should at least know what we are supposed to yearn for.

By the מלבי"ם's logic the פסוקים fit precisely: the more you enjoy בבל, the more you engage with the music and dancing of the אומות העולם — beckoning "come and join us; you are no longer the nation of הקדוש ברוך הוא; you are like all other nations" — the more you lose the ability to cry, the capacity to yearn for real things.

XV. פסוק ז' — אדום on the Sidelines

זכר ה' לבני אדום את יום ירושלים, האומרים: ערו, ערו, עד היסוד בה!

An odd shift: we are speaking about בבל, the first גלות. אדום is the last גלות, hundreds of years later. Why does דוד המלך fast-forward to אדום?

Two Approaches

  1. The מדרש: during חורבן בית ראשון, the בבלים destroyed — but the אדומים stood on the sidelines, cheering and jeering, urging בבל: "Don't stop! Burn it to the ground! ערו ערו עד היסוד בה!" דוד המלך pleads: "הקדוש ברוך הוא, remember that they laughed and encouraged בבל to make it worse."
  2. An alternate reading: דוד המלך is referring forward to חורבן בית שני, when אדום acted similarly.

The Deeper פשט

By our extended reading: when a יהודי lives a life immersed in עולם הזה, when he sells himself to play the music of the אומות העולם — even though ירושלים still stands desolate, the moment a יהודי says "I don't care; let me enjoy עולם הזה" — at that moment, "ערו ערו עד היסוד בה": the very foundations of the בית המקדש have been uprooted from within him.

And conversely: if a יהודי can preserve his Jewish pride — "I do not have the בית המקדש now, but I know what real joy is, I know what שירת ה' is" — then אדום did not succeed in uprooting the foundations. The יהודים of בבל were זוכה: even though אדום screamed for the יסוד to be ripped out, the יסוד remained deep in their hearts; the גוים could not extract it.

XVI. פסוק ח'-ט' — The Harsh Conclusion

בת בבל השדודה, אשרי שישלם לך את גמולך שגמלת לנו.
אשרי שיאחז ונפץ את עולליך אל הסלע.

The פרק ends with two of the harshest פסוקים in תנ"ך. "Daughter of בבל, the destroyed one, fortunate is the one who repays you for what you did to us. Fortunate is the one who seizes your children and smashes them against the rocks."

What has happened to us? Are we a brutal nation? Do we wish to take Babylonian babies and smash their heads against rocks? Is this the conclusion the פרק wants — a searing image of murder?

The Rebbe never understood it as a child. The מלבי"ם resolves it in one word.

The Meaning of "גמול"

What does גמול mean? When you pay for what was done to you, that is שכר — reward. גמול reflects something deeper: receiving back not only what you deserve, but in the exact manner you perpetrated it.

The way the בבלים made us suffer — the monstrosities, the unspeakable acts — what did they do? They took our babies and smashed them against the rocks. Read מדרש איכה; read what they did to women and children then; read the same record across our generations of שונאי ישראל.

The פסוק is not "we want this to happen to them." The פסוק is: by the way, you should know — the only true justice for what you did, the only justice possible to repay a nation that committed such atrocities, is the very same. גמול. When you bring such animal behavior into the world, eventually it turns back upon you. The פרק does not glorify the smashing; it reports the מציאות.

XVII. The Lesson — What to Read

I think the מלבי"ם understands the harsh ending differently — not so harsh after all.

The next time we face a נסיון, when we struggle and want more עולם הזה, when we are tempted to dance to the tunes of the גוי — the next time we sit with one for a cocktail and he says "come, sit, relax, enjoy. You are an American citizen — maybe a יהודי second. ארץ ישראל is the past; that's over" — at that moment, do this:

Open Perfidy.[10] Have you read Perfidy? Read it every year. If you do not have the patience, watch Schindler's List — actually, perhaps not. Take out a book about the שואה. Take out a book about שמחת תורה two years ago.

Read what they did. Then think twice before you eat from that hand — because that is what they represent. And we represent the exact opposite. We are קדושה, דבקות, הקדוש ברוך הוא's nation. We represent the בית המקדש filled with תורה and עבודה. We carry a portfolio — תורה, עבודה, meaning, עולם הבא, שכר ועונש, גן עדן, גיהנם — an entire world. The בבלים, the גוים, the שונאי ישראל represent the antithesis. They want to wrestle that portfolio out of our hands.

Remember: they smashed our babies against the rocks. Do you want to join the people who smashed your babies against rocks, who vanquished רוחניות, who destroyed the בית המקדש, who could not tolerate the very word "יהודי" because the existence of a יהודי as ambassador of the Divine in this world drove them mad?

Perhaps that is why דוד המלך closes the פרק with that note. The next time you are tempted, remember "בת בבל השדודה" — at the end of days they will be repaid with the exact passion and hatred with which they punished us across גלות. Think about what they did to יהודים. Then ask: do I want to go to that side, or do I want to remain like the יהודי in בבל, gazing at the silent harp on the willow, waiting for the day משיח comes and we play שיר ה' again?

XVIII. Closing

May we be זוכה this תשעה באב — at the very least — to adopt this mindset. We are יהודים. We are higher than עולם הזה. We are עולם הבא'דיקע יהודים. We are ירושלים'דיקע יהודים. We are שיר ה''דיקע יהודים. Before we run to עולם הזה, before we join the גוי, let us think about that.

May הקדוש ברוך הוא bring us the גאולה השלמה במהרה בימינו. אמן.

  3. תהלים פרק קל"ז — primary text of the שיעור. — The full פרק: nine פסוקים attributed to דוד המלך through רוח הקודש, recited daily before ברכת המזון on weekdays per the מנהג codified in שולחן ערוך אורח חיים סימן א'.   4. ספר ירמיהו on the prosperity of יהודים in בבל; מדרש איכה on the atrocities of the חורבן. — ירמיהו's איגרת in פרק כ"ט records his instructions to the early Babylonian exiles: "בנו בתים ושבו, ונטעו גנות ואכלו את פרין... ודרשו את שלום העיר אשר הגליתי אתכם שמה" — build houses, plant gardens, seek the welfare of the city. The פסוקים ground the מלבי"ם's reading that the exile community settled in material comfort. מדרש איכה (a foundational מדרש on מגילת איכה, redacted in ארץ ישראל in the late tannaitic-early amoraic period) records the brutal details of the חורבן — the atrocities, the deportations, the systematic targeting of women and children — that ground the מלבי"ם's reading of "גמול" in the closing פסוקים of פרק קל"ז. ↩
  5. רד"ק on תהלים קל"ז:ב' — emotional inability to play. — רבי דוד קמחי (the רד"ק, 1160–1235, Narbonne) reads "על ערבים בתוכה תלינו כנרותינו" as the structural mark of an emotional impossibility: the exiles took their harps with the hope that there would be moments to play them; when those moments arrived, the memory of ציון broke through and they could not continue. They hung the harps on the willows as a public sign of their inability — and as a deferred hope that the harps would one day be taken down again. The פירוש is the standard פשט; the מלבי"ם's reading on "ערבים" as sweet things is an additional layer. ↩
  6. אלשיך הקדוש on תהלים קל"ז — the worst aspect of גלות is when we cannot cry. — רבי משה אלשיך הקדוש (1508–1593, Tzfas; תלמיד of רבי יוסף קארו and contemporary of the אריז"ל), in his פירוש על תהלים, develops the חידוש that the deepest dimension of גלות is not the geographical or material loss but the loss of the capacity to weep. The בבלים's prevention of weeping during the march — recorded in the מדרש — is the surface manifestation; the deeper reality is the desensitization that creeps in when a יהודי over-engages with עולם הזה and loses the spiritual tenor that allowed his ancestors to weep at the loss of the בית המקדש. The reading frames the entire פרק: "גם בכינו" is presented as the achievement, not the suffering. ↩
  7. מדרש תהלים (שוחר טוב) on פרק קל"ז — the brutal march; נבוכדנצר's cruise; the loads on the יהודים' backs; the מלאכים; the משל of the queen and the מוכה שחין; the bitten fingers. — מדרש תהלים (also known as שוחר טוב, redacted in ארץ ישראל across the early amoraic and gaonic periods) develops on פרק קל"ז a sustained narrative of the גלות march. The brutalities of the march; נבוכדנצר's parallel cruise on the Euphrates with כל מיני זמר and revelry; the loads of stones placed on the exiles' backs to bend their dignity; הקדוש ברוך הוא's threat to return the world to תוהו ובוהו; the מלאכים' plea, met with "תנחומין של הבל תנחמוני", redirected to remove the loads themselves; נבוכדנצר's demand that the יהודים play their harps for him; the משל of the queen who refused her king and ended with the מוכה שחין; the יהודים' biting off their thumbs to render themselves unable to play; נבוכדנצר's rage and the resulting "תלי תלים של הרוגים" — all are recorded in this מדרש. The text is one of the most narratively rich of the מדרשים on חורבן בית ראשון. ↩
  8. גמרא בבא בתרא ס' ע"ב — abstaining from meat and wine after the חורבן; רבי יהושע's response; גזירה שאין הציבור יכול לעמוד בה. — The סוגיא in בבא בתרא records the פרושים of the post-חורבן generation who began abstaining from meat and wine on the grounds that the קרבנות and נסכים had ceased; רבי יהושע's response that consistency would require abstaining from grain (מנחות), oil (חביתי כהן גדול), and fruit (ביכורים) as well — leading to the כלל that we cannot impose on the ציבור stringencies that exceed the תורה's mandated mourning. The סוגיא grounds the הלכה of measured mourning: real gestures of חורבן-consciousness (the תשעה ימים, תשעה באב itself, the broken glass at חופה, the unfinished wall in a Jewish home) within the framework of life that continues. The Rebbe brings the סוגיא as the structural answer to the question of how the sensitivity of the יהודים in בבל can be lived in our generation: not by abstaining from everything, but by carrying the inner posture across all of life. ↩
  9. שולחן ערוך אורח חיים סימן א'; משנה ברורה — mourning the חורבן; מנהג of על נהרות בבל on weekdays and שיר המעלות on שבת and יום טוב. — The opening סימן of שולחן ערוך אורח חיים establishes daily חורבן-consciousness as the foundation of a יהודי's posture; the משנה ברורה there elaborates the מנהגים, including the מנהג to recite פרק קל"ז (על נהרות בבל) before ברכת המזון on weekdays as a structural reminder of the חורבן, with פרק קכ"ו (שיר המעלות) substituted on שבת and יום טוב when explicit mourning practices are suspended. The מנהג grounds the Rebbe's framing of this שיעור: פרק קל"ז is not an academic study but a daily liturgical text whose substance shapes the consciousness of every יהודי. ↩
  10. Perfidy by Ben Hecht (1894–1964; American screenwriter, novelist, journalist, Zionist activist) — published 1961 by Julian Messner, Inc. The work is a sustained polemic against the conduct of Mapai (the dominant Israeli Labor Zionist party) and its leadership during and after the Holocaust, focusing on the Kastner trial and the broader question of Jewish leadership's failures and complicities under conditions of Nazi atrocity. Hecht draws on extensive primary documentation. The book is hated by the Israeli political establishment of its era and remains controversial; the Rebbe brings it as recommended reading not as an endorsement of every claim but as a corrective reading material against the comfortable assimilationism into Western political and cultural narratives. The point: when tempted to identify with the גוי's framing of history, read what actually happened to יהודים when they relied on the goodwill of the world around them.
