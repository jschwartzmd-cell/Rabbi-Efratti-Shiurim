Ashrei: A Gateway to Olam HaBa
Shiur of Rabbi Ephrati — Sunday Morning Series on Tefillah
Recorded 25 August 2024 · Reworked 30 April 2026 · Mareh Mekomos upgraded 5 May 2026

Sponsored by Michael Shema l'iluy nishmas his wife's grandmother, Frumid bas Shloima, on her yahrtzeit. Today is also the yahrtzeit of Rabbeinu Chaim HaLevi Soloveitchik — Reb Chaim Brisker — who revolutionized the landscape of Torah havanah and is the rebbe of every yeshiva bachur.

I. Reb Chaim Brisker and the Rogatchover

A glimpse of the two greatest minds of the last two centuries: Reb Chaim Brisker and the Rogatchover Gaon learned together as chavrusas in Slutsk under Reb Yossel of Slutsk. They were eight or nine years old and had already mastered half of Shas. (The classic line: when one of the gedolei ha'dor tested the young Rogatchover and asked which half he had learned, he replied "whichever half you want.")

Reb Yossel ran a basic shiur for balebatim — a simple Daf Yomi-style class for ordinary working men. The two child iluyim sat in. Lacking derech eretz, they peppered every line of Gemara and every Tosfos with questions, frustrating the rest of the tzibbur. The men complained: "Throw out these two sheigitzlach!" Reb Yossel responded: "I am not throwing out these two shekotzim — they are the two future gedolei ha'dor." And so they became.

II. The Gemara's Promise on Ashrei

The Gemara in Berachos[1] states:

כָּל הָאוֹמֵר תְּהִלָּה לְדָוִד בְּכָל יוֹם שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים — מֻבְטָח לוֹ שֶׁהוּא בֶּן עוֹלָם הַבָּא.
"Whoever recites Tehillah l'David three times a day — he is assured a portion in Olam HaBa."

The Gemara identifies two unique features in this single kapitel:

  1. Aleph-beis acrostic structure (one pasuk per letter, with nun deliberately omitted).
  2. The pasuk "Posei'ach es yadecha u'masbi'a l'chol chai ratzon"[3] — the foundation of emunah in Hashem's daily provision for every living creature.

Other kapitlach contain one or the other; only Ashrei joins both. That combination is what earns the promise.

Chelek vs. Ben Olam HaBa

The Mishnah in Sanhedrin's Perek Cheilek[2] says kol Yisrael yesh lahem cheilek l'olam ha'ba — every Jew has a portion. Yet here the Gemara singles out the Ashrei-sayer with the language ben olam ha'ba — a child of olam ha'ba. The Rishonim distinguish: every Jew has the basic cheilek, but aveiros can erode and even forfeit it. Ben olam ha'ba is a stronger guarantee — a level that withstands the erosions of aveirah. Different "seats," as it were, in the same olam ha'ba.

Kavanah Is Required

The Radak and Rabbeinu Bachye[4] emphasize that the Gemara's promise is not for rote recitation. The promise requires kavanah — actually focusing on the words. When one truly hears the pasuk "Posei'ach es yadecha", one internalizes the reality of Hashem's daily shefa; that internalization is the spiritual armor against the aveiros that would otherwise erode the cheilek.

The Mishnah Berurah[5] rules that if one realizes mid-Ashrei that he said Posei'ach es yadecha without kavanah, he must return to that pasuk and continue from there with proper intent. Even if one realizes only after davening — walking out of shul and reflecting on whether the tefillah was real — the ruling is the same: go back to Posei'ach es yadecha and complete the rest of the mizmor with kavanah.

To aid focus, many Sephardim physically open their hands as they say "Posei'ach es yadecha" — symbolizing the construction of a kli kibul, a vessel ready to receive Hashem's brachos.

III. Why Three Times a Day?

Rashi on the Gemara[6] explains: k'neged shalosh tefillos — the three daily recitations correspond to Shacharis, Minchah, and Maariv.

This raises an obvious puzzle: in practice we recite Ashrei twice in Shacharis (once in Pesukei d'Zimra, once before U'va l'Tziyon) and once in Minchah — but never in Maariv. If the count is per tefillah, why is one missing from Maariv and one extra in Shacharis?

The Rishonim's Answer

In the era of Chazal, Tefillas Maariv was reshus — optional — and many did not daven it. Shuls in their day were typically located outside the city limits, and travel after dark was unsafe. To ensure that no one would forfeit the merit of the third Ashrei, Chazal moved that recitation forward into Shacharis. We have since accepted Maariv as chovah, but the original placement of Ashrei remained.

Once or Three Times?

Rabbi Akiva Eiger in his Gilyon HaShas records an alternative girsa — once a day, not three. He cites the Rosh, who held one Ashrei daily was sufficient. Rav Amram Gaon takes a softer position: Chazal required three not because three are strictly necessary, but to ensure that one is reached. We have paskened like the simple reading of the Gemara and the Zohar HaKadosh, which call for three. Whatever the count, the operative ruling is: do not skip even one, even if one cannot make it to Minchah with a minyan.

IV. The Structure of Ashrei

The Two Introductory Pesukim

The kapitel formally titled in Tehillim is Tehillah l'David (Psalm 145), which begins with "Aromimcha Elokai HaMelech". So why do siddurim begin Ashrei with two prepended pesukim?

The first pasuk — "Ashrei yoshvei veisecha, od y'hallelucha selah" (Tehillim 84:5):[8] The Gemara in Berachos[7] teaches that the Chassidim HaRishonim would "shohin sha'ah achas" — wait one full hour before davening — to focus the mind. The entire institution of Pesukei d'Zimra is the mesorah's adaptation of that practice for Klal Yisrael as a whole: a structured time of preparation before Shemoneh Esrei. It is therefore precisely fitting that Pesukei d'Zimra's central mizmor opens with the pasuk that teaches the requirement of preparation: "Happy are those who sit in Your house" — first sit, settle, focus, then say "od y'hallelucha".

The second pasuk — "Ashrei ha'am she'kachah lo, ashrei ha'am she'Hashem Elokav" (Tehillim 144:15) — is actually the closing pasuk of the previous kapitel. The Rishonim offer a remez: this pasuk contains the word Ashrei twice, and combined with Ashrei yoshvei, that gives us three Ashreis as a remez to the three daily recitations.

(The Maharshal[9] — alone among the Acharonim — held that one should skip "Ashrei ha'am she'kachah lo" and begin only with "Ashrei yoshvei... Tehillah l'David". No printed siddur follows him, but the position is preserved in his name.)

The Missing Nun and the Samech That Answers It

The Gemara teaches that David HaMelech, by ruach ha'kodesh, omitted the letter nun from the aleph-beis sequence of Tehillah l'David. The reason: nun is the letter of nefilah — falling. Specifically, of the pasuk in Amos:[10]

נָפְלָה לֹא־תוֹסִיף קוּם בְּתוּלַת יִשְׂרָאֵל.
"She has fallen and shall rise no more — the maiden of Israel."

David did not want a Jew, while reciting Ashrei, to be confronted with that grim image of permanent fall. The mefarshim note that the pasuk can be re-punctuated — "naflah, lo sosif kum" can also be read across the comma break — but the optical impression of permanent collapse remained, and David HaMelech avoided it.

And immediately after the missing nun, the samech answers it:[11]

סוֹמֵךְ ה' לְכָל הַנֹּפְלִים, וְזוֹקֵף לְכָל הַכְּפוּפִים.
"Hashem upholds all who fall, and raises all who are bowed down."

The very theme of nefilah that nun was avoiding is now reintroduced — but reframed: yes, falls happen; and Hashem catches. The aleph-beis's structural omission becomes a theological statement.

The Concluding Pasuk and a Plea for Olam HaBa

After the closing pasuk of Tehillah l'David (which ends on the letter tav), we add:[12]

וַאֲנַחְנוּ נְבָרֵךְ יָ-הּ מֵעַתָּה וְעַד עוֹלָם הַלְלוּיָ-הּ.
"And we shall bless Hashem from now and forever — Halleluyah."

The Rishonim explain this addition: having just recited the tefillah that earns one ben olam ha'ba, we immediately petition HaKadosh Baruch Hu: let us continue praising You even in olam ha'ba. If we love davening so much that we want it to continue forever, that very desire is itself part of the merit. Every morning we are saying: not only do we want olam ha'ba, we want to keep davening in olam ha'ba.

Thanking Hashem for the Ability to Thank Him

This connects to a deep theme that recurs in our tefillos: not only do we thank HaKadosh Baruch Hu for what He gives us, we thank Him for granting us the capacity to thank Him. Not every person has the presence of mind to recognize his Creator and offer praise. The zechus of tefillah itself is something to be grateful for. The closing pasuk we add to Ashrei is, in part, that recognition.

V. The Halleluyah Connection — Rav Amram Gaon's Chain

One detail of the closing pasuk is the trailing word Halleluyah. Why is it there?

Rav Amram Gaon, in his siddur, explains: every kapitel of Pesukei d'Zimra that follows Ashrei opens with Halleluyah and closes with Halleluyah (the six final kapitlach of Tehillim). The Halleluyah we add at the end of Ashrei creates a shalsheles — a chain — connecting Ashrei seamlessly into the Halleluyos that follow.

The Chassidic Practice of Repetition

Some Hassidic communities — Satmar, Vizhnitz, others — preserve a practice that makes the shalsheles audible: at the end of Ashrei, after "v'ad olam", they say Halleluyah, Halleluyah — the closing Halleluyah of Ashrei immediately joined to the opening Halleluyah of the next kapitel. The Vizhnitz siddur prints them together. The Rebbe noted that during his years as a Rav in Miami, his Satmar chasidish congregants recited it this way.

The Shlomo Kluger Objection

Rav Shlomo Kluger, in his peirush on Orach Chaim,[13] objects to the chained recitation. His reasoning: we have a klal that any pasuk Moshe Rabbeinu did not say, we do not say — meaning, we do not invent new pesukim by stitching parts together. "V'anachnu nevareich Kah me'atah v'ad olam" ends one pasuk; "Halleluyah! Hallelu Kel b'kodsho" begins another. Saying Halleluyah, Halleluyah back-to-back, says the Shlomo Kluger, manufactures a pasuk that does not exist in Tanach.

The Kabbalists — following the Arizal — hold that the shalsheles connection is itself important al pi sod, and they preserve the practice. Nigleh and nistar arrive at different conclusions; communities follow their mesorah.

VI. Repeating Verses to Mark Endings

The Tur's Sephardic Minhag

The Tur[14] records a fascinating minhag from the Spanish communities of his era: at the end of every section of tefillah, the last pasuk was repeated twice. The repetition served as a structural marker — a sign that one had reached the end of a discrete unit. The Spanish community of his day no longer practices this universally, but two vestiges survive even in Nusach Ashkenaz:

  • Kol ha'neshamah tehallel Kah — repeated at the very end of Pesukei d'Zimra (closing pasuk of Tehillim), to mark that Pesukei d'Zimra proper has concluded. (Note: Vayivareich David and Shiras HaYam, though they appear between Baruch She'amar and Yishtabach, are not formally part of Pesukei d'Zimra; the core is the six final kapitlach of Halleluyos.)
  • Hashem yimloch l'olam va'ed — repeated at the end of Shiras HaYam, marking the end of the shirah.

(The Sephardim still preserve the practice in places we no longer do — for example, repeating the closing pasuk of various tefillah sections.)

VII. The Shiras HaYam Question and the Maharil Diskin

The repetition of Hashem yimloch l'olam va'ed at the end of Shiras HaYam generates a striking stira — a contradiction — between our siddur and the Sefer Torah.

The Contradiction

If the repetition signifies that the shirah has ended, then the next pasuk — "Ki va sus Paroh b'rechobo u'b'farashav ba'yam"[15] — is not part of the shirah; it is a narrative epilogue describing why the shirah was sung. Indeed, that is how the Ramban[16] reads it.

However, in the Sefer Torah, the shirah is written in the unique brick-laying format ("ariach al gabei l'veinah") — and Ki va sus Paroh is written in that same format, as part of the shirah. The Rambam in Hilchos Sefer Torah, citing Maseches Sofrim,[17] codifies this. So our siddur and our Sefer Torah reflect two different rulings.

The Story of the Maharil Diskin

This stira is at the center of one of the most dramatic stories of Brisk, recorded in the biography Sar M'Brisk (HaSrof M'Brisk) by Moshe Sheinberger[18] — a biography reviewed for accuracy by the Brisker Rav himself.

When Rav Yehoshua Leib Diskin — the Maharil Diskin — became Rav of Brisk in the 1870s, he was less than thirty years old. Brisk was already known as "Yerushalayim of Lita"; the elder talmidei chachamim of the city did not all welcome the appointment of so young a rav.

One particularly senior talmid chacham — significantly older than the new Rav — came to him with the very stira we just described. He demanded an answer: how can our siddur say one thing and our sifrei Torah say another? The Maharil Diskin explained the machloikis between the Ramban and the Rambam; the siddur follows one view, the Sefer Torah the other.

The man was not satisfied. He decided — on the basis of his own kasha — that he would "fix" every Sefer Torah in Brisk: he would go to each sofer, each shul, and have the sifrei Torah rewritten to remove Ki va sus Paroh from the shirah formatting, to bring the sifrei Torah into alignment with the siddur.

The Maharil Diskin was aghast. "You want to change a mesorah that all of Klal Yisrael has, because of your kasha?" The man was not deterred — partly out of conviction, partly out of chutzpah to undermine the young Rav. He began going from shul to shul; the sofrim, intimidated, did not refuse him. The Maharil Diskin ran after him pleading.

Erev Shabbos at the Mikveh

That very week, Erev Shabbos, this man went to the mikveh. He drowned there. The story is brought down by the Maharil Diskin's own brother in Sar M'Brisk: midah k'neged midah — because he had sought to alter the pasuk describing the water that drowned the Egyptians, water itself overcame him.

The lesson is grave: tampering with the mesorah — even one letter, even with a sincere kasha — is a frightening matter. We have the siddur as our avos received it; we have the Sefer Torah as our avos received it; the apparent contradiction reflects an ancient machloikis, not an error to correct.

An Aside on "Eilav Tishma'un"

The same biography contains another exchange that bears mention. The author had quoted a maspid who, eulogizing the Maharil Diskin, applied to him the pasuk "Eilav tishma'un"[19] — "to him you shall listen" (the verse is from Parshas Shoftim, addressed to the navi). The Brisker Rav, reviewing the manuscript, objected: "That phrase is for a navi; the Maharil Diskin was a great Rav, but not a navi. Take it out."

The author then brought the manuscript to the Satmar Rav (then in Yerushalayim), who had a different judgment. He directed the author to Rabbi Akiva Eiger's comment on Shulchan Aruch Orach Chaim 153, where Rabbi Akiva Eiger himself uses the phrase "v'eilav tishma'un" in reference to the Arizal — specifically when explaining how the minhag of saying Nakdishach together with the shliach tzibur, against the pshat of the Shulchan Aruch, is followed because the Arizal ruled so. The Satmar Rav reasoned: if Rabbi Akiva Eiger could apply the phrase to the Arizal, the maspid could apply it to the Maharil Diskin.

VIII. Closing

Ashrei sits at the heart of Pesukei d'Zimra: a kapitel structured to be memorized, framed by pesukim that teach the very purpose of preparation, opening with the missing nun that turns its absence into a theological promise, closing with a plea to keep praising Hashem in olam ha'ba. Around it cluster the precise structural details of tefillah — the shalsheles of Halleluyahs, the markers of repetition, the contested pasuk at the boundary of the shirah.

And the larger lesson of the Maharil Diskin story stands behind it all: every word of our tefillah, every formatting decision in our siddur and sefer Torah, sits in a fabric of mesorah that we approach with reverence. We may understand or not understand; we do not "fix" — we receive, we transmit, we daven.
