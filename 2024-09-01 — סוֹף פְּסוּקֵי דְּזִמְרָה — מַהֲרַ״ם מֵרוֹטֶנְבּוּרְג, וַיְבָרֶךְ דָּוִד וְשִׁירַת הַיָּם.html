סוֹף פְּסוּקֵי דְּזִמְרָה — מַהֲרַ״ם מֵרוֹטֶנְבּוּרְג, וַיְבָרֶךְ דָּוִד וְשִׁירַת הַיָּם

The End of Pesukei d’Zimra: Maharam of Rothenburg, Vayivarech David, and Shiras HaYam
Shiur of Rabbi Ephrati — Sunday Morning Series on Tefillah
Recorded 1 September 2024 · Reworked 30 April 2026 · Mareh Mekomos upgraded 5 May 2026

Where does Pesukei d'Zimra formally end — and what is everything that comes after it doing in our siddur? The answer winds through the prison cell of the Maharam of Rothenburg, the foundations of the first Beis HaMikdash, the teshuvah movement of Ezra HaSofer, and a remarkable teaching of Avraham ben HaRambam on the imagination.

I. Where Pesukei d'Zimra Truly Ends

The core chiyuv of Pesukei d'Zimra is the six concluding kapitlach of Tehillim — the Halleluyos from kapitel 145 (Ashrei) through 150. The Gemara in Shabbos[1] records Rabbi Yossi's wish:

יְהִי חֶלְקִי מִגּוֹמְרֵי הַלֵּל בְּכָל יוֹם.
"May my lot be with those who complete Hallel every day."

Rashi on the Gemara clarifies that the Hallel referenced here is not the Hallel of Yom Tov, but the Halleluyos at the end of Tehillim. Rabbi Yossi's aspiration shaped our practice: every Jew completes these kapitlach daily, culminating in:

כֹּל הַנְּשָׁמָה תְּהַלֵּל יָ-הּ הַלְלוּ-יָ-הּ.
"Let every soul praise Yah — Halleluyah."

We repeat "Kol ha'neshamah" twice — as we discussed last week — to mark, in the manner of the old Sephardic minhag, the formal close of a section. That is the structural endpoint. Everything that follows — Baruch Hashem L'olam, Vayivarech David, Shiras HaYam — is appended for reasons we will trace.

II. Two Halachic Cautions on Repetition

Before turning to the appended units, two structural halachos bear on what follows.

Don't Manufacture a New Pasuk

The Hassidic minhag of chained Halleluyahs — saying "Halleluyah, Halleluyah" across the boundary of two kapitlach to bridge them — runs into a halachic concern. The klal is: "Kol pasuk d'lo paskei Moshe, anan lo paskinan lei" — any pasuk that Moshe Rabbeinu did not divide, we do not divide on our own. Stitching the closing Halleluyah of one kapitel together with the opening Halleluyah of the next manufactures a pasuk that does not exist. (This is, incidentally, the same reason that Yom HaShishi at Kiddush on Friday night is preceded by a hum or pause — to prevent its becoming part of a manufactured pasuk.) Most poskim caution against the chained recitation; some Hassidic communities preserve the practice based on Kabbalistic grounds.

Don't Repeat the Pasuk in a Way That Implies Shtei Rishuyos

The Mishnah in Berachos[2] rules that one who repeats "Shema, Shema" aloud — implying two domains, two divinities — is silenced. The concern dates to the era of minim and apikorsim who attempted to invade the batei midrash of Chazal. Out of this concern, Chazal were broadly cautious about repetition during tefillah. The Rambam may confine the strict prohibition to Shema alone, but most poskim extend the caution.

The chazzan question. This is why chazzanim are halachically discouraged from repeating words for musical effect. Yossele Rosenblatt, the legendary chazzan, was once invited to lead Yamim Noraim in Belz. The Belzer Rebbe accepted on one condition: he must not repeat any word. Yossele, knowing he could not honestly promise that, declined. Other communities were less stringent and he davened elsewhere — but Belz held the line on that halachah.

III. The Appended Cluster — Baruch Hashem L'olam

After Kol ha'neshamah, our siddur appends a sequence of four pesukim, each beginning "Baruch Hashem":

  • "Baruch Hashem l'olam, amen v'amen"[3]
  • "Baruch Hashem mi'Tziyon, shochen Yerushalayim, Halleluyah"[4]
  • "Baruch Hashem Elokei Yisrael, mei'olam v'ad ha'olam"[5]
  • "U'varuch Sheim k'vodo l'olam, v'yimalei k'vodo es kol ha'aretz, amen v'amen"[6]

These are scattered pesukim from Tehillim, drawn from kapitlach 89, 135, 106, and 72 — collected and joined here for a specific historical reason.

IV. The Maharam of Rothenburg — A Tefillah Born in Captivity

Who He Was

Rabbeinu Meir of Rothenburg — the Maharam, also called Maharam of Rottenberg — was born around 1220 and was niftar around 1283–1284. He is among the most prolific Rishonim: teshuvos, peirushim, kinos — the foundation of much of Ashkenazic halachah. His talmidim include the Rosh (Rabbeinu Asher b. Yechiel), Rabbeinu Yitzchak Mi'Dura, and many of the great Rishonim; entire dynasties of mesorah trace through him.

His own rebbe was Rabbeinu Yechiel of Paris, a talmid of the famous Or Zarua. The Maharam was personally involved in defending the Talmud against the burning in Paris in the 1240s; his kinah "Sha'ali Serufah Ba'eish"[7] — sung on every Tisha B'Av — laments that very burning.

The Aliyah That Was Stopped

Decades later, the Maharam decided "enough is enough". Eretz Yisrael was the goal; he packed his family and began the dangerous journey, intending to lead a renewal of the yishuv in Yerushalayim. Many other Ba'alei HaTosafos were emigrating in those years; the Maharam was a leading figure of that movement.

He had reached Italy when the journey ended. A Jewish apostate (mumar) — a self-hating Jew who had been baptized — informed Emperor Rudolf I of Germany that the Maharam was fleeing and leading other Jews out of the German Empire. Rudolf had decreed that no Jew was permitted to emigrate; he treated the Maharam's departure as an act of revolt. Authorities seized him, brought him back to Germany, and imprisoned him in the Ensisheim Fortress in Alsace.[8] The fortress still stands today, in part.

Seven Years in Prison — and the Tashbatz

The Maharam sat in captivity for seven years. He had no sefarim. He pleaded with the authorities for at least one consolation: that one of his talmidim be permitted to come to him daily, stand outside his window, and write down the chiddushim he transmitted orally. That talmid was Rabbeinu Shimshon ben Tzaddok, who later compiled what he received into the sefer Tashbatz (Tashbetz Kattan)[9] — a major source for Ashkenazic halachah in our siddurim and beyond.

In the Maharam's peirush on Maseches Oholos,[10] on the perek opening "Migdal HaPoreach Ba'avir" (a tower suspended in the air, used in tumah-taharah discussions), the Maharam writes a poignant line of self-description: "I am in a 'migdal poreach ba'avir' — suspended, cut off from sefarim, alone. If you find a Rishon who disagrees with what I am saying, I am mevatel my da'as — I am writing without my books."

The Refusal of Ransom

His talmidim, led by the Rosh, raised an extraordinary sum — twenty thousand francs — to redeem him. The Maharam refused. He wrote: if I am redeemed at this price, the authorities will only see Jewish leaders as a profitable target — they will seize and ransom every rav. He chose to die in jail rather than set the precedent. He died in captivity. Halachically, his ruling — that we do not pay exorbitant ransoms for captives — became foundational, and historians note that after the Maharam, the German authorities never again tried to ransom-extort a Jewish leader. He won the long war from inside his cell.

Fourteen Years Above Ground

Even more painful: after his death, the authorities refused to release his body. For fourteen years his remains lay in the fortress, denied a Jewish burial. Finally, a wealthy Jew named Rabbi Alexander ben Shlomo Wimpen of Frankfurt paid an enormous ransom — on one condition: that he himself be buried beside the Maharam when his time came. The condition was honored. Both lie today in the beis ha'chaim of Vermayza (Worms), side by side.

V. The Maharam's Halachic Innovation

The Practical Problem

The Maharam's era was one of redifus. German authorities routinely entered batei midrash mid-shiur, mid-davening, and demanded compliance, taxes, conversions, or simply created chaos to harass. Refusing to greet a king or local governor could mean death; the halachah[11] permits — even requires — hefsek in tefillah for kavod ha'melech or moreh malchus, and similarly to prevent hefsed meruba (substantial financial loss).

The structural problem: Pesukei d'Zimra is framed by Baruch She'amar at the start and Yishtabach at the end. The two brachos are meant to seal the unit. If a hefsek shatters the middle, what becomes of the framing?

The Solution

The Maharam instituted a brief, dignified sequence — beginning with "Baruch Hashem l'olam, amen v'amen" — to function as a quasi-bracha seal. Before permissibly stopping, one says these pesukim; upon resuming, one says them again. The cluster is not a formal brachah (one cannot invent a new brachah) but a sequence of pesukim beginning with Baruch formulations — sufficient to function as a structural seal in an emergency.

From Emergency to Standard Siddur

The Beis Yosef[12] records the Maharam's practice as the source for our concluding "Baruch Hashem" cluster. What began as an emergency device under medieval persecution entered the siddur as a permanent structural element. Today, the Mishnah Berurah rules that if a Jew does need to interrupt mid-Pesukei d'Zimra for a permitted reason — relieving himself, answering Kaddish or Kedushah, an urgent matter — he should run to the end and recite Baruch Hashem L'olam first, in the spirit of the Maharam's takanah.

Why Sephardim Say It Too

This takanah originated in Ashkenaz, but it became universal. The vector was the Arizal, whose siddur integrated Ashkenazic and Sephardic minhagim. (The Arizal's father was Ashkenazi — surname Luria — and his mother Sephardic; he was orphaned young and raised by his Sephardic grandfather in Egypt.) The Arizal's siddur formed the basis of Nusach Sfard as we have it, and through that channel, the Maharam's takanah entered Sephardic practice as well.

VI. Vayivarech David — David HaMelech's Hodaah

The Source

The pesukim of "Vayivarech David es Hashem l'einei kol ha'kahal" are from Divrei HaYamim Aleph 29.[13] They are not from Tehillim. To understand their meaning, one must know the moment they describe.

The Foundation Waters

David HaMelech yearned to build the Beis HaMikdash. He was not zoche to do so himself — that zechus belonged to Shlomo — but he prepared everything. The Gemara in Sukkah[14] records that when David began digging the foundation, the tehom — the cosmic underground waters — surged up. The threat was world-flooding. David inscribed the Sheim Hashem on a piece of pottery and cast it into the rising waters; the tehom subsided. Disaster averted, and the foundation could be laid.

The Successful Appeal

David then ran the largest fundraising campaign in our history — the national appeal for the materials, gold, silver, copper, cedar, and craftsmen for the Beis HaMikdash. Despite his dream being deferred to his son, he poured himself into preparing. Finally — after years of effort — the campaign closed successfully. Vayivarech David is David's public hodaah at the moment that goal was reached: "Baruch Hashem Elokei Yisrael avinu, mei'olam v'ad olam... lecha Hashem ha'gedulah v'ha'gevurah..."

Why is this in our daily siddur? Because it is the model of tefillah after a tefillah. David's response to a successful appeal — thank Hashem first, before anything else — became the model for ours.

The Minhag of Tzedakah

This is also the source of the minhag to give tzedakah at Vayivarech David. The Arizal (cited by the Mishnah Berurah) gave three prutos at this point. The Shelah HaKadosh[15] explains the depth: in this tefillah we are reliving David's successful campaign for the Beis HaMikdash; we pair the words with the action of giving — so that every morning, in galus, we participate in a daily echo of David's communal mobilization for Mikdash Hashem.

VII. The Composite Text — Divrei HaYamim and Nechemiah

A detail rarely noted: not all of the unit we call "Vayivarech David" is from Divrei HaYamim. The opening section is — but partway through, we shift to a separate pasuk: "Atah hu Hashem l'vadecha; atah asisa es ha'shamayim..."[16] This is from Nechemiah 9, and behind it lies a second story.

Ezra HaSofer's Crisis

Roughly seventy years after the Churban Bayis Rishon, the first aliyah from Bavel began. Ezra HaSofer initially stayed in Bavel — extraordinarily, because his own rebbe was still there; learning from his rebbe took precedence over reaching Eretz Yisrael. When Ezra finally went up, perhaps a hundred and twenty years after the Churban, he discovered a catastrophe.

The Jews who had returned were intermarrying with the surrounding nations — and not the working class only. Kohanim, Levi'im, and sarim (the elite ministers) — the spiritual and political leadership — had taken non-Jewish wives. The numbers preserved in the text are striking: 113 in one count, 127 in another. Without intervention, this would mean the dissolution of Klal Yisrael within a generation.

The Empowerment

Ezra had been appointed by the Persian king as the official Jewish authority — with the king's full backing for his decrees. He wielded that authority. He convened all of Klal Yisrael in Yerushalayim, decreeing that any who failed to appear would have their property and money confiscated. Everyone came.

The Mass Teshuvah

Ezra rose with a Sefer Torah, tore his garments, pulled out his hair, and wept. The tzibbur wept with him. In a single afternoon — the first mass teshuvah movement in our history — those who had intermarried separated from their non-Jewish wives. (The text does not record conversions; it records separations.) The covenant was renewed.

Ezra then commanded the Levi'im to sing. The teshuvah immediately gave birth to shirah. The pesukim they sang are recorded in Nechemiah 9 — and those pesukim are the ones we recite in our siddur every day, embedded inside "Vayivarech David".

Why These Two Episodes Daily?

Together they form twin pillars: David's mobilization for the Mikdash (building) and Ezra's separation from intermarriage (boundary). Every morning we re-attach to both: the impulse to build Hashem's house, and the impulse to maintain its sanctity from corruption. Both are conditions for geulah; we recite them as a daily covenantal renewal.

VIII. The Five Sefarim of Tehillim

Chazal teach[17] that Sefer Tehillim is divided into five sefarim, paralleling the five chumashim of the Torah:

כְּשֵׁם שֶׁנָּתַן מֹשֶׁה חֲמִשָּׁה חוּמְשֵׁי תּוֹרָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, כָּךְ דָּוִד נָתַן חֲמִשָּׁה סְפָרִים בְּתְהִלִּים.
"Just as Moshe gave Israel five Books of Torah, so David gave Israel five Books in Tehillim."

Some siddurim print four additional pesukim after Kol ha'neshamah, supposedly to evoke the closing pasuk of each of the first four sefarim in Tehillim. Since we just said the closing pasuk of Sefer 5 (Kol ha'neshamah), the addition of the prior four end-pesukim would symbolically complete the entire Sefer Tehillim in a single morning.

A Textual Problem

Several Rishonim note an embarrassing anomaly: only two of the four printed pesukim are actually the closing pesukim of their respective sefarim. The other two are similar-sounding pesukim from elsewhere in Tehillim. This appears to reflect a scribal or liturgical confusion in transmission. Some Rishonim say it is genuinely incorrect; others accept the symbolic intent regardless. The very fact that this textual issue persists in our printed siddurim illustrates how layered our tefillah has become — and how careful the mesorah is, even when problems are flagged, not to "fix" what has been received.

IX. Shiras HaYam — Why It Sits Between Pesukei d'Zimra and Yishtabach

The Halachic Problem

Strictly speaking, Shiras HaYam creates a hefsek. Pesukei d'Zimra formally ends at Halleluyah; Yishtabach is the closing brachah. To insert an extended segment from Chumash between them is, on a halachic level, a structural problem.

The Geonim Did Not Say It Daily

If you examine the siddurim of the Geonim — including Rav Amram Gaon and Rav Saadia Gaon[18] — Shiras HaYam does not appear in daily davening. The Geonim wrote explicitly: communities that wish to say it on Shabbos and Yom Tov may do so, but it is not required, and one should not protest those who omit it.

The Two Yeshivos. The Geonic period centered on the two great yeshivos of Sura and Pumbedisa in Iraq, located roughly 80 miles apart along the Euphrates. Sura was the poorer community; Pumbedisa the wealthier. Some Geonim wrote to their children: better go to Sura than to Pumbedisa — better to sleep on the floor and learn Torah than to live in a palace and lose your ruchnius. The more gashmius a place provides, the less ruchnius it tends to sustain — a principle as true today as it was in Bavel.

The Rambam's Position

The Rambam[19] rules that one may say Shiras HaYam as part of one's daily devotions, but after Yishtabach, not before. This avoids the structural hefsek entirely. Practically, no community follows the Rambam on this point.

The Zohar and the Arizal

The Zohar HaKadosh[20] mandates Shiras HaYam as a daily recitation al pi sod. The Arizal systematized that position, ruling that Shiras HaYam is recited daily before Yishtabach for Kabbalistic reasons we do not fully grasp on the nigleh level. After the Arizal, the daily inclusion became universal.

The Daily Mitzvah of Yetzias Mitzrayim

One halachic thread connects the inclusion: there is a daily mitzvah of zechiras yetzias Mitzrayim, fulfilled at kriyas Shema. Kriyas Yam Suf is the climax of yetzias Mitzrayim; reciting Shiras HaYam before Shacharis reinforces that daily zechirah.

X. How to Say Shirah — Avraham ben HaRambam on Imagination

The Mishnah Berurah, citing the Chida[21] and the Arizal, writes that Shiras HaYam recited with proper kavanah is a segulah for parnasah. But what does "kavanah" mean here? Knowing the meaning of every word? Shiras HaYam contains some of the hardest Hebrew in the entire Chumash — even with Rashi, full comprehension requires hours of study.

Rabbeinu Avraham ben HaRambam

The Rambam's son, Rabbeinu Avraham ben HaRambam, was a major talmid chacham in his own right — author of the sefer HaMaspik L'Ovdei Hashem,[22] of teshuvos, and of substantial commentary. Like the Ksav Sofer after the Chasam Sofer, he is overshadowed in popular consciousness by his father — unfairly, since his work deserves attention in its own right.

In HaMaspik L'Ovdei Hashem, Rabbeinu Avraham develops a teaching that is rare in our literature: the imagination as a tool of avodas Hashem. He calls this faculty "koach ha'dimyon" — the power of imagination — and devotes a chapter to its disciplined cultivation.

Practical Applications

Some examples Rabbeinu Avraham gives:

  • When trying to fall asleep at night, instead of letting the mind drift through chatter, visualize the Beis HaMikdash: the Kohanim bringing the korbanos, the splendor of the avodah, the Levi'im singing on the duchan, Klal Yisrael in Yerushalayim in ma'amados.
  • Visualize Mashiach arriving — what it will look like, what it will feel like. This is not demayonus or fantasy; it is avodah that strengthens hope.
  • For Shiras HaYam: imagine yourself standing on the dry seabed, the walls of water towering on both sides, the Egyptians drowning, the gold and silver washing up on the shore, Klal Yisrael exiting toward Har Sinai.

This is the kavanah the Chida and the Arizal were pointing to. The segulah of Shiras HaYam is unlocked not by linguistic mastery but by sanctified visualization. We are commanded daily to relive yetzias Mitzrayim; Shiras HaYam is one of the moments when that reliving is built into the tefillah.

A Modern Application

Rabbeinu Avraham's principle has fresh urgency in our era of constant input. We have lost the discipline of silence — getting into the car and immediately turning on the radio, falling asleep listening to a podcast, never giving the mind a clean moment in which to imagine. The first step in cultivating koach ha'dimyon is simply to create silence: turn off the input, and let the imagination begin its work.

XI. Synthesis

The end of Pesukei d'Zimra is not a single moment; it is a layered closure that records two thousand years of mesorah. The Halleluyos close it formally. The Maharam's Baruch Hashem L'olam seals it against the violence of medieval Germany. Vayivarech David joins it to David HaMelech's appeal for the Mikdash and to Ezra's teshuvah renewal. Shiras HaYam, mandated by the Zohar and integrated by the Arizal, makes the daily tefillah a daily reliving of yetzias Mitzrayim.

And running through it all is the principle Rabbeinu Avraham ben HaRambam articulated: that tefillah is not only the saying of correct words. It is the disciplined work of the imagination — a faculty we have largely forgotten how to use, and which our tefillos were designed to revive.

The shirah of the Levi'im following Ezra's mass teshuvah: the convening of Klal Yisrael in Yerushalayim, the separation from intermarriage among kohanim, levi'im, and sarim, and the immediate explosion of shirah. The second pillar of our daily Vayivarech David. ↩
  17. Midrash Tehillim 1:2; cf. Bereishis Rabbah. — The teaching that Sefer Tehillim is structured into five sefarim paralleling the five chumashim: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁנָּתַן מֹשֶׁה חֲמִשָּׁה חוּמְשֵׁי תּוֹרָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, כָּךְ דָּוִד נָתַן חֲמִשָּׁה סְפָרִים בְּתְהִלִּים. The basis for the minhag of completing the closing pasuk of each of the five sefarim in a single morning tefillah. ↩
  18. Siddur Rav Amram Gaon; Siddur Rav Saadia Gaon. — The two foundational Geonic siddurim. Neither includes Shiras HaYam in the daily tefillah. Both Geonim wrote explicitly that communities wishing to recite it on Shabbos and Yom Tov may do so, but it is not required, and one should not protest those who omit it. ↩
  19. Rambam, Hilchos Tefillah, perek 7 (Seder HaTefillah). — The Rambam's framework: one may say Shiras HaYam daily, but after Yishtabach, never before — to avoid the structural hefsek within the brachah-frame of Pesukei d'Zimra. The minority position; no community follows it in practice today. ↩
  20. Zohar, Parshas Beshalach (general reference; Zohar II in standard editions). — The Zohar HaKadosh's mandate of daily Shiras HaYam al pi sod. The Arizal systematized this position into the universal practice of recitation before Yishtabach, overriding the Rambam's structural concern. ↩
  21. Chida, Moreh Ba'Etzba / Avodas HaKodesh; Mishnah Berurah 51:17. — The teaching that Shiras HaYam recited with kavanah is a segulah for parnasah. Rooted in the Arizal; codified in the Chida; brought to general practice by the Mishnah Berurah. The kavanah in question is unlocked by Rabbeinu Avraham's koach ha'dimyon, not by linguistic mastery. ↩
  22. R' Avraham ben HaRambam, HaMaspik L'Ovdei Hashem (Arabic original; standard Hebrew translations). — The sefer in which Rabbeinu Avraham develops koach ha'dimyon as a tool of avodas Hashem: visualizing the Beis HaMikdash at night, picturing Mashiach, imagining oneself standing on the dry seabed at Kriyas Yam Suf. A teaching nearly unique in our literature; the practical key to the kavanah of Shiras HaYam. ↩
