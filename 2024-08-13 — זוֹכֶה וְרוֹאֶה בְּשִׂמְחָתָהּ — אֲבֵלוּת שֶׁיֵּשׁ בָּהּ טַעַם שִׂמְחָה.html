זוֹכֶה וְרוֹאֶה בְּשִׂמְחָתָהּ — אֲבֵלוּת שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ טַעַם שִׂמְחָה

Zoche V’Roeh B’Simchasa: Mourning That Already Tastes of Joy
Tisha B'Av Shiur of Rabbi Ephrati
Recorded 13 August 2024 (afternoon of Tisha B'Av) · Reworked 30 April 2026 · Mareh Mekomos upgraded 5 May 2026

A shiur for the afternoon of Tisha B'Av — when one is permitted only divrei emunah u'bitachon. The puzzle of why Chazal phrase the reward of mourning Yerushalayim in the present tense — and what that grammar reveals about the inner life of aveilus.

I. The Halachic Frame of the Day

The Gemara in Ta'anis[1] teaches that one who does melachah on Tisha B'Av "will not see in [Yerushalayim's] joy." There are kulos for one in pressing need, but the plain halachah is unambiguous: the day is for aveilus, not for work. The Gemara's positive formulation is the foundational pasuk of our day:

כָּל הַמִּתְאַבֵּל עַל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם — זוֹכֶה וְרוֹאֶה בְּשִׂמְחָתָהּ.
"Whoever mourns Yerushalayim — merits and sees in her joy."

The pesukim of nechamah in Yeshayahu, read in the Sheva D'nechemta — the seven haftaros of consolation[2] — fulfill that promise. Their language overflows with emunah, hope, and the assured restoration of Yerushalayim's grandeur.

Reb Shmelke of Zalachov on Reading Yeshayahu

Rav Shmuel "Shmelke" Rozovsky of Zalachov, mashgiach of Yeshivas Chachmei Lublin under Rav Meir Shapiro,[3] would offer a counsel to bachurim sunk in depression: open Yeshayahu and read the nechamos — the haftaros we recite after Tisha B'Av. The nevuos of consolation, said Reb Shmelke, are saturated with emunah and hope; they lift the neshamah directly, and the rest of life follows. He was making a halachic-spiritual point, not a clinical one — but the prescription has helped many.

II. The Linguistic Puzzle — Present Tense, Not Future

Why does the Gemara say zoche v'roeh — "merits and sees" — in the present tense? It should have said yizkeh v'yireh — will merit and will see. As of this morning, we have not heard the shofar of Mashiach. Yerushalayim is not yet rebuilt. The Beis HaMikdash is not standing. We sat on the floor; we said kinos; we cried. In what sense are we seeing the simchah right now?

This grammatical kashya is asked by all the mefarshim. The classic resolution comes from the Chasam Sofer — Rav Moshe Sofer, Chief Rabbi of Bratislava in the 1800s[4] — and it draws together Bereishis, Maseches Makkos, and the inner mechanics of nechamah.

III. Vayema'en L'Hinachem — The Mechanism of Forgetting

The Chasam Sofer begins with a pasuk in Bereishis we just read in this morning's kinos. The shevatim sell Yosef HaTzaddik, dip his kesones pasim in blood, and bring it to Yaakov Avinu. All the children gather to console him. The pasuk[5] says:

וַיָּקֻמוּ כָל בָּנָיו וְכָל בְּנֹתָיו לְנַחֲמוֹ, וַיְמָאֵן לְהִתְנַחֵם.
"And all his sons and daughters arose to console him, and he refused to be consoled."

The question forces itself: Yiddishkeit has a structured halachic process for grief — shivah, shloshim, yud-beis chodesh. After a year, the formal aveilus ends. We do not believe in endless bereavement. So what does it mean that Yaakov "refused to be consoled"? Was he rejecting the entire halachic system?

The Midrash's Answer

The Midrash, brought by Rashi on the pasuk: "Ein gozrin gezeirah shel shichchah ela al ha'meis" — the divine decree of forgetting applies only to one who has died. HaKadosh Baruch Hu built into human nature a mechanism that allows the wound of bereavement to gradually heal — only when the person is in fact dead. For someone still alive, that mechanism does not engage. There is no halachic nechamah for the living.

Yaakov refused consolation because, somewhere deep within, he sensed that Yosef was still alive. Accepting nechamah would have been a betrayal of that intuition. As the end of Bereishis reveals, his refusal was vindicated.

The Ditch in the Dining Room. An illustration sometimes given at shivah calls: when a parent is niftar, it is as if a deep ditch has been carved into the middle of your dining room. Every time you walk through, you fall into it. After a while, you learn to walk around the ditch. The ditch never leaves; the loss is always there. But the mechanism of shichchah teaches you to walk around it. That is nechamah — and it works only on the dead. For the living, the ditch can never be navigated, only restored.

IV. Application to Tziyon — Why Yerushalayim Is Still "Alive"

The Chasam Sofer applies the principle directly to Yerushalayim. If Klal Yisrael still cries — after seventeen, eighteen, nineteen centuries — over the destruction of Yerushalayim, that cry is not nostalgia. It is testimony. Yerushalayim is not dead to us. The Beis HaMikdash is not a relic. The mechanism of shichchah never engaged because the loss is not, in the deepest sense, final. The destruction is real but interim; the city, in our hearts, is alive and waiting.

And precisely that is the present-tense zoche v'roeh. A Jew who refuses to be consoled is, at this very moment, in living relationship with the future Yerushalayim. The future joy is already trickling backward into the present, in the form of fidelity that will not let go.

The Napoleon Legend

The welt tells a story of Napoleon walking through the streets of Paris (or some other French city), encountering Jews sitting on the floor weeping, and asking why. When told that they were mourning a bayis destroyed seventeen hundred years earlier in Yerushalayim, Napoleon is said to have responded: "A nation that still cries for something destroyed seventeen hundred years ago is guaranteed to get it back." (Whether it was actually Napoleon is doubtful — the same insight appears in the Toras Soferim of the Rema, well before Napoleon's time.[6] But the substance stands.)

V. Rabbi Akiva on Har HaTzofim

The Chasam Sofer's yesod resolves a famous puzzle in Maseches Makkos.[7] Roughly a century after the Churban, four Tannaim — Rabban Gamliel, Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah, Rabbi Yehoshua, and Rabbi Akiva — ascended Har HaTzofim. They tore kriah. Continuing onto Har HaBayis, they saw a fox emerge from where the Kodesh HaKodashim had stood — the most sacred chamber in creation, where only the Kohen Gadol entered, once a year, on Yom Kippur.

The three Tannaim wept. Rabbi Akiva laughed. The Gemara's exchange is famously elliptical:

אָמְרוּ לוֹ: עֲקִיבָא, מִפְּנֵי מָה אַתָּה מְצַחֵק? אָמַר לָהֶם: מִפְּנֵי מָה אַתֶּם בּוֹכִים?
"They said: Akiva, why are you laughing? He said to them: Why are you weeping?"

Rabbi Akiva then explains: the nevuah of destruction (Yerushalayim being plowed like a field) is bound to the nevuah of consolation (Zechariah: "Od yeshvu zekenim u'zekenos b'rechovos Yerushalayim"[8] — old men and old women will yet sit in the streets of Yerushalayim, with children playing). They are tlui zeh ba'zeh — interdependent. If the prophecy of churban was fulfilled in stone and ash, the prophecy of nechamah is equally certain. The Tannaim answered: "Akiva, nichamtanu — Akiva, nichamtanu" — you have consoled us.

Re-reading the Dialogue

On the surface, Rabbi Akiva's "why are you crying?" looks like rhetorical deflection — a Jew answering a question with a question. But the Chasam Sofer's yesod reveals a deeper reading. Rabbi Akiva was not dismissing their grief; he was identifying its source. The reason I am laughing, he was telling them, is precisely because you are crying. Your tears are themselves the proof. If Yerushalayim were dead to you, the mechanism of shichchah would have engaged centuries ago and there would be no tears. Your tears testify that she is alive. Her vitality is the guarantee of her future simchah. And so your weeping is my laughter's source.

VI. The Inner Arc of Tisha B'Av

This yesod illuminates the structure of the day:

  • Morning — deep aveilus; kinos, sitting on the floor, no tefillin.
  • After chatzos — many communities ease certain customs;[10] one may sit on a chair; some Sefardim sweep the house and a few even paint the walls. These are not contradictions to the aveilus; they are the aveilus revealing its inner nechamah.
  • The day is called a moed — a festival.[9] The deepest mourning is bound to assured joy; faithful aveilus contains, hidden inside, the seed of redemption.

VII. Daniel Goldhagen and the German Professor

Daniel Goldhagen — Harvard-trained Jewish professor and son of a Holocaust survivor — wrote the seminal Hitler's Willing Executioners (1996).[11] The book documents in extensive detail how ordinary German citizens — not only Hitler, not only the SS, but rank-and-file soldiers and civilians of 1939–1945 — actively believed that exterminating Klal Yisrael was good for Germany, good for the "Aryan race," good for the Third Reich. He compiles their personal records, their letters, their willingness, their initiative.

At a conference in Berlin, a respected German academic posed Goldhagen a challenging question — phrased politely, but pointed: "With all due respect, why do you Jews not move on from the Holocaust? Eight decades on, books, documentaries, new initiatives in yeshivos. Aren't there other concerns in the world today? Why this obsession?"

Goldhagen's answer was quiet and devastating, and it captures exactly the Chasam Sofer's yesod:

"Thousands of years ago, a home burned down in Yerushalayim. To this day, observant Jews mention that home roughly sixty times daily — through Shacharis, Minchah, Maariv, Birchas HaMazon, the entire siddur. If we have not moved on from a destruction that occurred two millennia ago, do you really expect us to forget an event that happened seventy years ago?"

This is not pathology. It is mesorah. Never again requires never forgotten. A Yid who has come to terms with the absence of Yerushalayim is no longer misabel — and the entire structure of present-tense nechamah collapses.

VIII. Rav Yisrael Meir Lau and the Drashah at Neilah

Rav Yisrael Meir Lau, shlita, former Chief Rabbi of Eretz Yisrael,[12] is among the most powerful darshanim alive. The Rebbe relayed a drashah Rav Lau gave at Neilah in Beit Knesset HaGadol on King George Street in Yerushalayim — an audience filled with paper-yarmulke wearers who had not fasted, who came only for Neilah, who wept openly under the force of his words.

Lulek

Rav Lau was born in 1937 in Piotrkow, Poland. His father, Rav Moshe Chaim Lau Hy"d, was Chief Rabbi of Piotrkow. Rav Meir Shapiro — founder of Daf Yomi and Yeshivas Chachmei Lublin — was a cousin of the Lau family and had himself served as Rav of Piotrkow before relocating to Lublin. Rav Lau's childhood nickname was Lulek.

When the Nazis came, his father was killed almost at once. His mother and his older brother Naftali remained with him. Their father had instructed Naftali: "Take care of Lulek." At the selection — at four or five years old — Lulek's mother shoved him toward Naftali's side. He cried, ran back, clung to her. She pushed him away again, calling out to her older son: "Naftali, take care of Lulek." Then to the child himself: "Goodbye, Lulik. Goodbye, Naftali." Those were the last words his mother ever said to him. He realized only later that her violent shoves had saved his life.

"I Am Older Than You"

By the end of the war, Lulek was in Buchenwald (he had been in Lichtenberg earlier). When the Americans arrived to liberate the camp, a Jewish chaplain in the U.S. Army — Rabbi Herschel Schacter (the father of Rabbi Jacob J. Schacter; not to be confused with Rav Hershel Schachter of YU)[13] — walked among the corpses. Lulek, terrified of the Americans, hid behind a pile of dead bodies. Rabbi Schacter later described what he saw:

"I looked at a pile of Yidden, and between the corpses I saw two eyes. Two eyes burning with fire. I walked closer and realized there was a small Jewish child hiding behind the bodies — a thing impossible at that point in the war, in 1945. I asked him: Wie alt bist du, mein Kind? How old are you, my child?"

The boy answered: "I am older than you."

Rabbi Schacter, fifty-five years old, asked how that could be. The boy explained:

"You laugh and smile like a child. I saw you. I don't laugh anymore. I don't know how to laugh; I don't know how to smile after what I saw. And I don't even know how to cry anymore. Ich bin shoyn oysgeveynt. I have cried out all my tears. So tell me — am I not older than you?"

The Suitcase

An American soldier, seeing the orphaned child had nothing, gave Lulek a small empty suitcase — so that he should feel he owned something in the world. Lulek carried that suitcase from one orphanage to the next, from displaced-persons camp to Eretz Yisrael, into adulthood, into marriage, into his career.

His wife eventually pleaded with him to throw it out — it was old, worn, falling apart. He refused: "At my age, this is all I had. Whenever our children complain about food or want some treat, I want to be able to show them this suitcase and tell them what I had at their age." When they moved into a new apartment in Tel Aviv, he placed the suitcase in the boidem — the attic. Years later, one of his children asked to see it. Rav Lau climbed seventy-five steps to the attic, opened the box where it had been stored — and all that remained were the handles. The suitcase had disintegrated.

The image is itself a mashal. Even the empty vessel of his orphaning had broken down — but the neshamah it had once held continued.

IX. Rav Lau's Drashah — Let the Letters Testify

The Midrash at the opening of Eichah Rabbah[14] describes the moment of the Churban. Avraham, Yitzchak, Yaakov, and Moshe Rabbeinu each plead before HaKadosh Baruch Hu for Klal Yisrael; their pleas are not accepted. The letters of the aleph-beis then come forward as edim — witnesses against Klal Yisrael:

  • Beis — "Bereishis bara Elokim": they did not keep the Torah that begins with me.
  • Gimmel — "Gedilim ta'aseh lecha": they did not wear tzitzis.
  • And so on, letter by letter.

Avraham Avinu rises and silences each letter as it approaches: "Hush — do not testify against my children." Only Rachel Imeinu's plea — invoking how she had given the simanim to her sister Leah out of pure vatranus — finally moves HaKadosh Baruch Hu to promise: "Mini koleich mi'bechi v'eineich mi'dim'ah ki yeish sachar lifulaseich... v'shavu vanim li'gvulam"[15] — withhold your voice from weeping; the children will return to their borders.

Rav Lau's Inversion

Rav Lau then offered an inversion of the midrash, drawn from Parshas Vayelech:[16]

וְעַתָּה כִּתְבוּ לָכֶם אֶת הַשִּׁירָה הַזֹּאת... כִּי לֹא תִשָּׁכַח מִפִּי זַרְעוֹ.
"And now, write for yourselves this Song... for it shall not be forgotten from the mouth of his offspring."

Moshe Rabbeinu writes that the Torah will be a shirah — a Song — that testifies. The simple reading is that the Torah testifies against Klal Yisrael when they violate it. Rav Lau argued otherwise: the Torah testifies for Klal Yisrael. Through the most catastrophic centuries of our history — through the Crusades, through the expulsions, through the Holocaust — Yidden kept the Torah:

  • They tore strips from their shirts to make peshilos (wicks), pressed them into shoe-button bases, smuggled in oil, and lit Chanukah licht in the camps.
  • They acquired flour and water by means hard to fathom and baked matzos in hiding.
  • They put on tefillin in barracks, whispered Kriyas Shema, kept tznius, said tehillim.

"Therefore," said Rav Lau, "I disagree with Avraham Avinu. Let the letters testify. Let them come — aleph, beis, all of them — and testify how Klal Yisrael kept the Torah even in the deepest hester panim. The same tefillin we will put on this afternoon are the same tefillin our fathers wore through the worst of galus. The same Shema we will say is the same one our grandfathers whispered in the camps. That is the testimony I want from the aleph-beis."

The tzibbur in Beit Knesset HaGadol, on Yom Kippur at Neilah, wept openly.

X. Synthesis

What keeps us is not Eretz Yisrael alone, nor a geographic location. What keeps us is Torah and mitzvos. The Torah does not die — and so a nation that holds the Torah does not die. Yerushalayim, the Beis HaMikdash, the Avodah, the Kohanim and Levi'im — these are folded into the Torah-life of the people who refuse to let them go.

To sit on the floor on Tisha B'Av and cry — to refuse nechamah, to insist that what is gone is not really gone — is itself the greatest nechamah. The aveilus is the witness. The witness is the merit. The merit is the present-tense simchah. Zoche v'roeh b'simchasah — at this very moment, on the afternoon of Tisha B'Av, before we have put on tefillin, before Mashiach has come, we are already merit-ing and already seeing.

May we be zoche that the tikkun ha'shaleim of Mashiach and geulah sheleimah arrive bimheirah b'yameinu, amen.
