Guest שיעור · Rabbi Yechiel Spero

Preparing and Leading the פסח Seder

A Practical Guide for Parents — Setting Up for Technical Success on the Most Important Night of the Year
Recorded 18 March 2026 · Reworked from the original שיעור

This week's mid-week שיעור, in advance of פסח, was delivered by Rabbi Yechiel Spero — best known for his books and for his website awesomepesach.com, where he gathers the practical tools, props, and reward systems he has developed over years of leading large סדרים. Below is the reworked version of his talk: practical, hands-on, and pointed toward a single goal — that every yid leave the סדר knowing הקדוש ברוך הוא loves them.

I. הכל בסדר — A Deeper Meaning

We have all heard the expression הכל בסדר — everything is in order, everything will be fine. There is a much deeper meaning. Your entire spiritual year is contained inside ליל הסדר. The night is more than a pleasant family gathering — it is a transcendent spiritual experience. The מקור is the חיד״א in his ראש כל פסח[1]: כל השנה — the entire spiritual year — is found within ליל הסדר.

Therefore the goal is not merely to have a "nice time," but to create a real experience. I prepare my סדר with great care. On the first page of my personal הגדה I have written: relax. You are leading the סדר. This is not your performance. I invest so much time in preparation that when the moment arrives I need to remind myself to breathe. The true purpose: a positive experience with your children, connecting with them on a deep level.

II. Setting Up for Technical Success

Engaging Children Through Ownership

Everyone loves the fruit of his own labor. When a child has built something, he wants to be there to see it on display. The more your children are involved in preparing for פסח, the more they will want to be present at the table to see "their product" come to life. Have them grind the מרור. Prepare the חרוסת. If you do not buy ready-made salt water, let them prepare it and learn exactly how salty it should be. Have them make signs and prepare activities. Their investment in the preparation is the secret to their attention at the table.

Tools and Materials

One of my best purchases is a laminator — about $30. Wonderful for סוכות (you can laminate the children's projects and reuse them year after year — my father ע״ה loved hanging old projects we had made), and invaluable for פסח: it keeps children occupied beforehand and lets the projects survive grape juice spills.

Sticky notes are essential for a particular reason. Today, sadly, children come home from school and forget the מצוה: והגדת לבנך[2] — you shall tell your son. Not "והגיד בנך לך" — your son shall tell you. The מצוה is for the parent to transmit, not for the child to perform. It is wonderful that children know many דברי תורה — but the purpose of the night is not for them to deliver them.

When your child comes home with countless insights, do not crush his spirit. Hand him five sticky notes and say: "You will share five דברי תורה at the seder. The rest we will hear during chol hamoed." Adjust by how many children you have. They will pick their best five, they will be excited, and you will have room to actually transmit something.

III. The Purpose of the Seder — A Personal Message

What is my goal? I want every yid at my seder to leave knowing that הקדוש ברוך הוא loves them.

I have signs to teach this. One says simply FAITH in English. I show it and ask the children: what does this mean? When they answer, "Forward All Issues Toward Hashem," they get a Seder Shekel. (Seder Shekalim are the in-house currency of my seder; we will return to them.)

By the end of the seder, my children should know: everything in life — every struggle they have faced — is because הקדוש ברוך הוא loves them. Tonight you are leaving your personal מצרים. You are being freed from your personal struggle, whatever it is. This is not just beautiful תורה. I have 150 pages of magnificent תורה — but that is not the purpose of the seder. The purpose is to relive[3] the experience of יציאת מצרים. So we use props.

IV. A Personal הגדה

I was once interviewed on a podcast and they asked me — and I have no filter, unfortunately — "What do you say to people who go to hotels?" I said: I tell them not to go. But if you do go to a hotel, you have more time to prepare — because you are not cooking, shopping, or cleaning. You have time to prepare the questions and the messages.

An absolute must: print your own הגדה. I am not telling you not to buy beautiful published הגדות with every conceivable דבר תורה. I am telling you to print one for yourself. The advantage: I open my computer and write into the document — tell this story here. Use this prop. Remind my wife to start warming the chicken soup. Wherever you are holding, you do it now.

Some of you have parents or grandparents who are Holocaust survivors. If you have been blessed and they are alive and present at your seder, we envy you — they can tell you and your children how they left their own מצרים. And where should they tell it? If you remember a story about your parent or grandparent — drop it directly into your personal הגדה. When you go through the seder you will not remember every story on your own; you are running a seder at 10:30 at night. So you sit with a personalized version: notes, stories, an agenda, everything you want. You can find templates for personal הגדות online (my website has one) and add your own notes.

V. Involving Older Children

One of the best ways to engage older children: pool noodles. I dress my older son Amir as a "wicked Egyptian" — a costume from Party City and a pool noodle — and let him walk around the table hitting the younger ones. It sounds wild. It works. The reason: the more the older ones are involved, the more they want to be there. Honestly, they want to hit their younger siblings. Give them roles.

I tell my older children — especially eighteen and up — "Listen, I am bribing you this one time. I am asking you for two activities and one דבר תורה." Know what motivates each child. Bribe them, because that is the מצוה of the night — the מצוה of transmission — and hopefully their hearts will be open to the messages. The younger children feed off the excitement of the older ones. If the older ones lead, the younger ones will follow.

VI. Pre-Measured Bags of מצה and מרור

The night before פסח, my daughter and I sit at the table with a kitchen scale. I do not do this for halachic reasons alone — I do it because it makes the seder run.

We have a list of the שיעורים of מצה for each part of the seder[4] — מוציא מצה, כורך, אפיקומן — and the שיעורים of מרור. We open several boxes of מצה. My daughter weighs; I bag, seal, and label. מוציא מצה. כורך. אפיקומן. מרור. Count the people at your seder, multiply by two, and prepare every bag in advance.

Why? Because מצה is not like slicing חלה — especially when you are trying for a precise minimum שיעור. If the מצה bags and מרור bags are ready in advance, the table flows. (If you object that part of the experience is doing the measuring at the seder itself — fine. Take a small piece from each pre-prepared bag and add to it. But "everyone measures" turns into chaos.)

If this does not work for you, do not do it. For me, my daughter and I spend one hour the night before, and the seder simply flows.

I also open all the wine bottles before the seder. There are wine stoppers on my website. Trying to peel foil and uncork while the children are climbing the walls — it kills the rhythm. Do it beforehand.

And for מצה: I keep two boxes — one labeled "whole" and one labeled "broken." I open several boxes ahead of time and consolidate the whole ones, so before any meal of פסח I am not hunting for whole מצות. (When you buy a box of מצה not all of them are whole.)

Setting the Table

This sounds extreme: do not set the full table. You are not eating until much later. All anyone needs is a plate and a cup. You do not need every fork and every plate from the start. This is not "setting up for technical success" — it is setting up for grape juice on the linens. The forks come out at שולחן עורך.

VII. The Reward System — Seder Shekalim

Prepare a list of prizes — prizes that go beyond what you normally give your children. Distribute them throughout the evening. The children should know that at any moment you might hand out a card. (Cards laminated; on my website.) Trips to Seasons Express, an evening of bowling, whatever it is — they will be on point. This is the night to invest money. Invest in the seder.

Keep Focus Cards

My children have already beaten the system, but here is an excellent system until your kids figure it out. Keep Focus cards. Many laminated cards (templates on my website). My wife and I spend time before פסח writing each child his appropriate cards. One card might say: "Hand in this card at דיינו and receive a Seder Shekel." Another: "Ask a question about חירות and receive three Shekalim." The more they ask — that is the name of the game tonight.

The Hidden Mathematics

Print Seder Shekalim in various denominations — ones, fives, tens, twenties. Never tell the children what each denomination is worth.

Decide in advance how much you will give each child — say, $25. If the child earns 100 Seder Shekalim, each is worth 25 cents. If he earns only 50, each is worth 50 cents. If he earns 200, each is worth 12.5 cents. The child always receives the same total — but he does not know that, so he simply wants to accumulate as many shekalim as possible.

Some children fall in love with the shekalim themselves and prefer keeping them over converting them — that is a complete victory.

And the reward must be immediate: on the first day of חול המועד, hand them their money or take them to buy their present. Immediate gratification reinforces the connection between effort and reward.

VIII. Maximize Questions Over Answers

The goal of the seder is שאלות[5]. Award shekalim more generously for questions than for answers. שאלות לשמה — questions for their own sake.

The Scavenger Hunt

Give each child a card listing items to find throughout the הגדה — עבדים היינו, the first מה נשתנה, the third כוס, the paragraph of והיא שעמדה, other key sections. Prepare envelopes for each child for both nights before יום טוב.

Intentional Mistakes

Deliberately say things incorrectly when retelling the story. "So משה went and hit the ground when it came to דם." The children who catch the error earn shekalim. Suddenly they are listening intently rather than tuning out.

Pre-Selected Questions

Review question lists before פסח (websites devoted to seder preparation have many) and assign specific questions to specific children based on their interests and developmental level. Examples: Is slavery bad or good? Is freedom bad or good? What is good about having structure? What does it mean to have free will? Is hard work good — what has hard work accomplished for you?

Creative Writing

Suggest your children write stories before פסח. What was it like growing up in מצרים as a five-year-old Jewish child? Being a five-year-old Egyptian watching the מכות affect your family and neighbors? Being a לוי watching the slavery? Read these stories at the seder. The children integrate their school learning into a personal narrative.

IX. אמונה Through Relaxation

ליל הסדר is the night of אמונה. When you are relaxed, you demonstrate אמונה. When facing struggles — financial, health, weight, addiction, social, emotional — having faith in הקדוש ברוך הוא brings relaxation. Being relaxed is the ultimate sign of אמונה, and this is what we transmit to our children.

The night commemorates not only our ancestors' redemption but our own. חייב אדם לראות את עצמו כאילו הוא יצא ממצרים[6]. Not merely remember the days of מצרים; relive it. We are leaving our struggles, our personal מצרים, right now.

X. Year-Round Hunting for Props

Preparation for פסח begins throughout the year. Be on the lookout. After Halloween, Walmart and Old Navy clearance often yields costumes that work for משה or for an Egyptian. After Easter, Walmart sells items that can be repurposed — reed baskets work for baby משה in the river. T.J. Maxx and Costco for decorative items. Dave & Buster's for prizes and toys.

XI. Props to Display in the House Before פסח

Starting from the tenth of ניסן, place items around the house each night. When children wake up they discover something new, and excitement builds.

Visual Displays

Posters of מצרים, קריעת ים סוף, and the מדבר. Red painter's tape on the inside of the doorposts (correctly placed inside, where the actual דם was — not outside). Frog decorations placed strategically. A פסח lamb decoration — displayed from the tenth of ניסן.

Physical Props Around the House

A reed basket with a baby doll for משה in the Nile. A brick from Lowe's. Rocks. Chains for slavery. Straw or straws taped to walls (for both the floating basket and brick-making). "For Sale" and "Private Property" signs (for where פרעה went to the river in the morning). Large inflatable animals two or three feet tall — giraffes, elephants — for the מכות. Inflatable musical instruments for leaving מצרים with שירת הים. A large decorative wall covering for two walls. A plastic knife with red coloring for Jewish blood. A bone for the קרבן פסח (or "they worked us to the bone"). A blindfold. Keys for both locking and unlocking — slavery and freedom. The "split cup" from Amazon — two colors that do not mix, representing Egyptians and yidden drinking from the same source.

Two Bowls

Set up two bowls — one labeled עבדות, one labeled חירות (or גאולה). Throughout the seder, whenever anyone mentions slavery or freedom, the children call it out and earn shekalim, which go into the corresponding bowl. They are now on high alert. Divide the totals at the end.

XII. Visual and Tactile Props for the עשר מכות

The מטה of משה Swallowing the Egyptian Staffs

Use a long cardboard tube from wrapping paper and two short paper-towel tubes[7]. Connect the two short tubes with fishing line, leaving about three feet of line between them. Attach about ten feet of line to one end and feed it through the long tube. With practice, you can demonstrate how the מטה of אהרן actually swallowed the other staffs. Write מטה אהרן on the outside.

דם — The First מכה

The מדרש teaches that the מכה of דם penetrated not only the water but the bodies and fruits of the מצרים. Demonstrate viscerally: buy oranges before יום טוב. Have a yid cut one open — orange inside, normal. Have a "מצרי" (a guest playing the role) cut open another orange and reveal that it is a blood orange. (Specialty stores carry them.) The red interior at the moment of cutting drives home the meaning.

One year I scooped out two pineapples and inserted a piece of tin (a 9×13 pan, two to three inches wide) down the middle as a divider. On one side I poured red Jell-O; on the other, clear. The dramatic visual: blood penetrating the fruit while the Jewish side remained pure.

צפרדע — Frogs

I have a large frog from Costco — though I had to buy it during חנוכה and hide it from the children for ten and a half months until פסח.

ברד — Fire and Water Together

The נס of ברד was that fire and ice coexisted without one extinguishing the other. Two visuals work well. Marshmallow swirls — multi-colored marshmallow candies symbolizing different elements blended. Frozen fire — a clear cup with half an inch of water at the bottom; add yellow, orange, and red gummy candies; freeze. When you present it: fire frozen inside water. The children love it.

שחין — Boils

Mega Button candies are perfect representations of boils. Once a year — and remarkably, they never go bad.

חשך — Darkness

This מכה offers a wonderful interactive moment. Hide fake jewelry around the house before the seder. When you reach חשך in the הגדה, announce a "mannequin challenge." Everyone above a certain age (you decide — seven, eight, or nine) must freeze in place completely, experiencing the paralysis the מצרים felt. Meanwhile the younger children — still mobile — run around searching for the hidden jewelry, reenacting how בני ישראל moved freely through מצרים finding where the מצרים had hidden their treasures.

קריעת ים סוף — Splitting the Sea

A simple demonstration that never fails. Take a bowl of water and sprinkle pepper liberally across the surface. Have a child dip a finger — nothing happens; the pepper stays put. Then put a tiny drop of dish soap on your fingertip and touch the center. The pepper instantly splits to the sides, opening a clear path through the middle. Try it tonight; it is remarkable.

Another visual: hold a clear glass at the bottom. Put a small amount of grape juice or wine at the very bottom. When you pour clear water in (just as the מצרים poured water into their cups), the red liquid rises up — and from the child's angle the water itself appears to have turned to blood, because the grape juice at the bottom is hidden by his perspective.

XIII. Making the Concepts Personal

Personal Definitions of עבדות, חירות, מרור

One question I pose every year that generates extraordinary discussion: I go around the table and ask each person, "What is your definition of עבדות — slavery?" The answers vary beautifully by life experience. My daughter might correctly say: "math class." My father-in-law, an accountant, will say: "tax season." I might say: "4:30 PM on a difficult workday." Then I ask, "What is your definition of חירות — freedom?" "June 15, when school ends." "April 16, when tax season is over." Finally, "What is your definition of מרור — bitterness?"

Through this exercise the seder becomes real. Each person is now re-experiencing leaving מצרים personally. Everyone has something in life that represents slavery, something that represents bitterness, and hopefully something that represents freedom. On this night, every yid is leaving his own personal מצרים.

XIV. Physical Reenactment — Leaving מצרים

Bricks of Slavery

The מדרש[8] teaches that the מצרים used Jewish babies as bricks in their buildings — a horrifying reality, taught in an age-appropriate way. Use couch cushions as "bricks." Have your children build a structure. Then take your youngest and lay him across one of the "bricks," with more cushions placed on top. The visceral experience helps them understand the cruelty — and their relief when "freed" makes them appreciate יציאת מצרים.

The Chase Through מצרים

Right after דיינו — when we celebrate leaving מצרים — I have built this into my personal הגדה: I dress as פרעה (a simple crown, perhaps from a fast-food restaurant, plus special clothing) and chase the children out of "מצרים." They run through the hallway, which I call the ים סוף, grabbing candies I have placed along the walls. At the end of the journey I have inflated musical instruments waiting for them (inflated before יום טוב, of course), and we sing שירת הים[9] together with joy and celebration.

XV. Visual Materials — Pictures of the עשר מכות

I print and laminate pictures representing each of the עשר מכות and hang them around the house. I spend real time finding compelling photographs — the hard labor of slavery; a full moon (which always appears on the fifteenth of ניסן); a red river for דם; close-ups of actual lice for כינים; frogs; the jewelry that משה had the option of taking from פרעה's palace; יוסף's coat where it all began. When children wake up they see these images throughout the house, and excitement for פסח builds organically.

Encourage children who are artistic to draw their own pictures of the עשר מכות, and display these around the house too. They take ownership of the chag.

I also found a detailed table runner depicting the עשר מכות. I do not actually leave it on the table during the meal — I hang it on the wall where the children study it. Even when their attention wanders from the formal הגדה reading, they are absorbing the story visually.

XVI. Practical Logistics

Seating

Rather than sitting at the head of the table, consider sitting in the middle. At a table longer than six or eight feet, the children at the ends feel distant. Sitting in the middle makes you equidistant from everyone — no one is in the back of the class.

Wine

Be aware of the שיעורים[10] of מצה, מרור, and wine (minimum approximately 3.3 ounces). It is better to have a full small cup of wine than the majority of a big cup. Choose cup sizes appropriately — not everyone can handle four to six ounces of wine.

XVII. The Deeper Meaning — Painting the Picture

Why "מבית עבדים"?

The פסוק states: אנכי ה' אלקיך אשר הוצאתיך מארץ מצרים מבית עבדים[11]. The question is obvious — why add מבית עבדים? We were enslaved in מצרים for 210 years; everyone knows what we were doing there. Furthermore, this פסוק was said seven weeks after יציאת מצרים. Why the redundancy?

The שפת חיים — Reconnecting to Emotion

The שפת חיים[12] explains: when you go through an emotional experience — sad (a funeral), happy (a child's wedding) — you make קבלות. But what happens? You get used to your new reality and the קבלות fade. How do you maintain the intensity? You paint a picture. Visualize what you were experiencing at that moment — what people were wearing, what the room looked like, the atmosphere. When you reconnect to the picture, you reignite the feelings.

When we left מצרים we knew הקדוש ברוך הוא took us out. But after seven weeks we needed reminding. Therefore the תורה paints a picture — מבית עבדים — to help us reconnect to those feelings. This is the meaning of the מדרש on כל המרבה לספר ביציאת מצרים הרי זה משובח[13]: the more we describe the actual miracles — not gematriyos, not clever דברי תורה, but the actual story of how הקדוש ברוך הוא took us out — the more we reconnect.

XVIII. The Slavery-to-Freedom Exercise

Ask everyone at the seder to bring one item representing either slavery or freedom. The personal investment creates meaningful discussion. Examples: a cell phone — for a child receiving his first phone, it represents freedom; for an adult, honestly, it usually represents slavery. A picture of a boss after thirty years of employment: slavery transformed into freedom. Keys: locking up or unlocking. Chains: the obvious one. One year, this exercise convinced a mother who had insisted on leaving the seder at 10:00 PM to participate fully — she brought a picture of her boss and declared her freedom with the biggest smile.

XIX. Additional Resources and a Word of Caution

My website, awesomepesach.com[14], has all of this and more. I update it constantly with new ideas; everything is free. There is a link to Amazon for items you may want to purchase (posters of מצרים, of the עשר מכות, etc.) — no pressure.

One word of caution from an embarrassing personal experience: I once ordered large posters of what I thought was a majestic image of פרעה and ancient מצרים. When I unrolled them at a speaking engagement in Los Angeles, a friend approached me discreetly: "Yechiel, I cannot display that poster. The woman in it is not dressed appropriately." Please review images carefully before ordering — especially historical depictions — to make sure they are appropriate for your home.

And consider personalizing: take photographs of your own family members dressed with backpacks and walking sticks, as if leaving מצרים. The experience becomes tangible — and personal.

סיכום

Please remember the essential principle: הקדוש ברוך הוא gives your children, on the night of the seder, a special כח to ask questions. And He gives you a corresponding כח to answer with wisdom and depth. If you prepare well — knowing which questions to ask, which messages to convey, which stories of אמונה and triumph from your own life to share — you will succeed. The children's hearts will be open, and your נשמה will be able to transmit your deepest feelings of belief in הקדוש ברוך הוא. These messages will carry your children throughout the year — and throughout their lives. May you have an amazing פסח and a meaningful seder.

  1. ראש כל פסח, חיד"א; cf. שמחת הרגל on הגדה של פסח. — The חיד"א (ר' חיים יוסף דוד אזולאי, 1724–1806) develops the principle that ליל הסדר is the spiritual foundation of the entire year — כל השנה takes its character from the סדר. The חיד"א's commentary on the הגדה appears in שמחת הרגל and in his liturgical writings on ראש כל פסח. The kabbalistic underpinning: ניסן is the ראש of the year for מלכים ולרגלים (ראש השנה ב' ע"א), and ליל הסדר draws down the spiritual flow for the whole year. ↩
  2. שמות י"ג:ח' — "והגדת לבנך ביום ההוא לאמר". — The פסוק from which חז"ל derive the central מצוה of סיפור יציאת מצרים: the parent transmits to the child. The grammatical form is direct: והגדת (you shall tell) — לבנך (to your son). Not the reverse. The פסוק is the textual anchor for the entire pedagogical framework of the סדר; see file 58 fn 10 for the four occurrences in the תורה. ↩
  3. משנה פסחים י':ה'; רמב"ם, הלכות חמץ ומצה פ"ז ה"ו. — "בכל דור ודור חייב אדם לראות את עצמו כאילו הוא יצא ממצרים". The רמב"ם codifies the חיוב as a present-tense reliving rather than a past-tense remembering — "חייב אדם להראות את עצמו" (in his variant לשון), which the חיד"א and others read as a positive מצוה to physically demonstrate one's experience of redemption. This is the textual anchor for all reenactment-based מנהגים. ↩
  4. שיעורים for מצה, מרור, and יין at the סדר — שלחן ערוך, או"ח תע"ב, תע"ה; משנה ברורה ad loc.
    Note: Modern שיעורים follow various calculations (חזון איש, ר' חיים נאה, others). For מצה: מוציא מצה approximately 30 g (one half of a hand-shmurah), כורך approximately 30 g, אפיקומן approximately 30 g (some hold double). For מרור: a כזית of the appropriate species. For יין: minimum רוב רביעית (~75 ml / ~2.5 oz) per cup, with stricter views holding רביעית שלמה (~90–98 ml / ~3.3 oz). Verify against contemporary halachic guides for the specific community's מנהג.
    ↩
  5. פסחים קט"ז ע"א — "כדי שישאלו". — The גמרא in פסחים teaches that the entire structural irregularity of the סדר — dipping twice, leaning, the second cup, the breaking of the matzah — is designed "כדי שישאלו": so that the children will ask. Question-driven engagement is the structural heart of the מצוה of סיפור יציאת מצרים. Rabbi Spero's reward system, scavenger hunts, and intentional mistakes are concrete applications of this חז"ל principle. ↩
  6. משנה פסחים י':ה' — "חייב אדם לראות את עצמו כאילו הוא יצא ממצרים". — The central halachic obligation of the סדר: every yid must see himself as if he personally went out of מצרים. The רמב"ם (הלכות חמץ ומצה פ"ז ה"ו) reads it more strongly: "להראות את עצמו" — to demonstrate, to act it out. The משנה is the textual core of the מצוה the שיעור is teaching how to fulfill experientially. ↩
  7. שמות ז':י'-י"ב — "ויבלע מטה אהרן את מטתם". — The first miracle before פרעה: the staff of אהרן turns into a serpent and swallows the staffs of the Egyptian sorcerers. חז"ל (שבת צ"ז ע"א) develop the theological significance: even after the staff returned to its original form, it still contained the swallowed staffs — a sign of ה''s power that incorporates and absorbs without diminution. Rabbi Spero's tube-and-fishing-line demonstration makes the נס tangible. ↩
  8. מדרש שמות רבה כ"ב:א'; תנא דבי אליהו on פרשת שמות. — The מדרש's graphic depiction of Egyptian cruelty: when Jewish workers fell short of their daily brick quota, Egyptian taskmasters used Jewish infants as substitute bricks to fill the gaps in the buildings. The מדרש is widely cited in pre-פסח educational materials; the שיעור's cushion-bricks reenactment is an age-appropriate way to make the cruelty tangible without inflicting trauma. ↩
  9. שמות ט"ו — שירת הים. — "אז ישיר משה ובני ישראל את השירה הזאת לה'". The שירה sung at the splitting of the sea, included in פסוקי דזמרה daily and central to שביעי של פסח. The שיעור's reenactment with inflated musical instruments and a hallway-as-ים סוף draws on the kabbalistic principle that שירת הים at the סדר reactivates the original moment of redemption. ↩
  10. שלחן ערוך, או"ח תע"ב סעיף ט'; משנה ברורה ad loc. — The שיעור for the four cups is רוב רביעית (a majority of a רביעית) according to most פוסקים; some hold a full רביעית. Modern measures range from approximately 75 ml (~2.5 oz) to 98 ml (~3.3 oz). The משנה ברורה stresses that smaller cups, fully consumed, are halachically preferable to large cups partially consumed. Rabbi Spero's practical advice mirrors this פסק. ↩
  11. שמות כ':ב'; דברים ה':ו' — "אנכי ה' אלקיך אשר הוצאתיך מארץ מצרים מבית עבדים". — The opening of the עשרת הדברות. The phrase מבית עבדים seems redundant — yet ה''s self-introduction at סיני uses precisely this language seven weeks after יציאת מצרים. The שפת חיים (and many מפרשים) read this as a model: emotional experiences fade unless we paint them in vivid detail. The שיעור's pedagogical thesis — make the סדר visceral — is a contemporary application. ↩
  12. שפת חיים, ר' חיים פרידלנדר (משגיח Ponevezh, 1923–1986). — ר' חיים פרידלנדר, משגיח of Ponevezh and one of the great בעלי המוסר of the late 20th century. שפת חיים is a multi-volume collection of his דרשות and מאמרי מוסר, including extensive treatment of פסח and סיפור יציאת מצרים. His teaching that emotional experiences must be re-painted to retain their force is widely cited; שפת חיים על פסח develops the psychological mechanics of סיפור as reactivation rather than mere recall. ↩
  13. הגדה של פסח — "וכל המרבה לספר ביציאת מצרים הרי זה משובח". — The famous formulation in the הגדה, immediately following the four sons. חז"ל's teaching: the more one elaborates on יציאת מצרים, the more praiseworthy. The מדרש goes on to recount the תנאים of בני ברק who spent the entire night in סיפור יציאת מצרים until their תלמידים reminded them it was זמן קר"ש של שחרית. The שיעור reads "elaboration" as concrete description of the miracles, not just intellectual דברי תורה. ↩
