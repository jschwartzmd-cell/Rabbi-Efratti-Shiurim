Rabbi Ephrati · Sunday Morning שיעור on תפילה

עלינו לשבח, אל תירא, and the שש זכירות

From לתכן עולם to the Prague Disputation, and Why We Do Not Say יגדל or אני מאמין
Recorded 1 February 2026 · Reworked from the original שיעור

Two weeks ago we discussed the origins of עלינו לשבח — that יהושע בן נון composed עלינו, while על כן נקוה was authored (in one tradition) by עכן. Today I would like to revisit several points that emerged from further research and from the עולם's questions: the meaning of לתכן עולם, the historical price paid for "להבל וריק," the שלשה פסוקים of אל תירא, the שש זכירות, and the great controversy over saying יגדל and אני מאמין as part of davening.

I. The Meaning of לתקן עולם במלכות שדי

The Modern Misuse

ר' שלמה וולומבסקי raised an important question on the phrase לתקן עולם במלכות שדי. It deserves clarification, especially given how this term is widely misused today. Many Jews invoke תיקון עולם as a slogan for social justice and societal repair. The phrase has become a fixture in Reform and Conservative circles. You will rarely, if ever, hear traditional rabbanim use it that way.

The Original Reading — לתכן with a כ״ף

Some have erroneously attributed this interpretation to the עלינו prayer itself. Research into ancient manuscripts and early סידורים[1] reveals a crucial textual point: the original נוסח is לתכן with a כ״ף, not לתקן with a קו״ף.

The two roots are very different. לתקן with a קו״ף means to repair. לתכן with a כ״ף means to establish, to set in place — like the line in פיוט שבת mussaf לאל אשר שבת: תכן שבת רצה קרבנותיה. The תיכון השבת means establishing it.

The authentic meaning of our phrase, then, is not "to repair the world through social action," but to establish the world under the sovereignty of הקדוש ברוך הוא — a petition that the entire world will one day abandon עבודה זרה and recognize divine sovereignty. Professor Mitchell First of Teaneck has written extensively on this; most ancient manuscripts contain the כ״ף, and Yemenite סידורים still preserve the original reading. In context the meaning fits perfectly: the surrounding language is about kingship and the disappearance of idolatry.

II. The Placement of עלינו — When Not to Say It

The ב״ח[2] explained that the essence of עלינו is to be said before walking out of the בית הכנסת into the wider world. That has halachic implications. The חתם סופר, following the ב״ח, paskens that when מנחה and מעריב are said back-to-back without leaving shul, one should not recite עלינו between the two. The משנה ברורה concurs.

In practice, almost no shul follows this. I have not yet been to a shul that omits עלינו between מנחה and מעריב when they are davened together. The logic is clear: if עלינו is meant to be said upon leaving, and you are not leaving, why say it? Supporting this — on שבת and יום טוב we do not say עלינו after שחרית, because we stay in shul for מוסף. We say it only after מוסף, when we actually walk out. The same principle should apply to a back-to-back מנחה-מעריב.

III. שהם משתחוים להבל וריק — The Censored Line

Now we return to the historically sensitive line in parentheses in many סידורים: שהם משתחוים להבל וריק ומתפללים אל אל לא יושיע.

Christian authorities in the Middle Ages claimed that when yidden recited להבל וריק — and spat — they were directing this contempt specifically at ישו. (Last week we discussed the gematria: וריק equals 316, the same as ישקא.) Long before רבי יעקב קמנצקי's mesorah, the ראשונים already identified this gematria. They clarified that the name being referred to is not the spelling יוד-שין-ואו, which itself should not be pronounced, but the fuller form with an ע at the end — and that what we have in mind is the abbreviation ימח שמו וזכרו.

IV. ספר הנצחון and the Prague Disputation

This brings us to a critical historical episode. The story is told in ספר הנצחון (The Book of Victory)[3], authored by one of the early ראשונים, ר' יום טוב ליפמאן מילהאוזן. He served as רב in פראג in the early fifteenth century. During his tenure a yid named פסח became a מומר, converting to Christianity and adopting the name Peter. Following a pattern we have seen repeatedly — the disputations involving the רמב״ן and רבינו יחיאל מפריז — this apostate informed the Church that yidden disparaged Christianity in their daily liturgy.

In 1401 Peter demanded a public debate in פראג with the גדולי הדור, including ר' יום טוב ליפמאן. His chief evidence was our עלינו prayer, specifically the line שהם משתחוים להבל וריק ומתפללים אל אל לא יושיע. The apostate, already in the 1400s, identified the gematria of וריק as ישקא — six centuries before רבי יעקב קמנצקי.

Eighty Yidden in Prague

This had deadly consequences. In one incident, eighty yidden in פראג were murdered על קידוש השם because of the טענה of להבל וריק. For generations afterward rabbanim instructed their קהילות not to recite the line aloud — recognizing that to do so would only inflame gentile hostility.

The Practice in אשכנז

Early ספרי תפילה of אשכנז reveal the response. The רוקח[4] and other ראשונים write that the מנהג in Germany was to recite the line בלחש, in a whisper, because they were acutely aware that if it were said aloud and overheard by a גוי it would provoke real anger. The caution went so far that many yidden in וורמייזא did not even know that עלינו was supposed to be said after davening — they had never heard it said publicly. The only time it was recited aloud was during the מלכיות of ראש השנה. For long stretches of European Jewish history, עלינו was effectively not said because of these dangers.

The Decree of King Friedrich I (1703)

In 1703 King Friedrich I of Prussia[5] issued a decree that fundamentally shaped our סידורים to modern times. He ordered the controversial words removed from all סידורים, and sent government officials into every shul to monitor whether yidden were saying שהם משתחוים להבל וריק — and whether they were spitting during it. The result: the line was removed from most סידורים for centuries. Only in modern times has it been restored. Today we have no חשש in saying it, because no one is monitoring our prayers as they were then.

The Clever Response of the Singers The יושבי שלו[6] records a fascinating practice. Many קהילות began singing עלינו לשבח לאדון הכל loudly, then jumping straight to ואנחנו כורעים — skipping the controversial line entirely. Why sing it loudly? Specifically so that any gentile spy sent by the king to listen would hear and report back: the yidden sang the regular עלינו, and they did not say the offending line. The choreography itself was a defense — a public performance designed to satisfy the censor.

V. The Practice of Spitting

We discussed last time the מחלוקת ראשונים on the מנהג of spitting. The מהרי״ל[7] practiced it, and the ט״ז ruled it permissible — it does not constitute בזיון בית הכנסת — when one means it as an expression of contempt for עבודה זרה while saying להבל וריק. Most קהילות today have discontinued the practice, and contemporary פוסקים have discouraged it. Chabad חסידים maintain it, discreetly. The reason for the general retreat from spitting and from saying the parenthetical line is exactly what the Prague story illustrates: when our liturgy became subject to scrutiny, communities pulled back to protect their lives.

VI. Modern Restoration of the Original נוסח

Modern סידורים — ArtScroll and many others from the late nineteenth and twentieth centuries — have reincorporated this passage and removed the brackets in many editions. This is a return to the original נוסח, now that we live in times and places where we can daven freely without fear. Today, even when the line still appears in brackets, we should recite it. It is part of the original נוסח — perhaps from יהושע בן נון himself. (One opinion among the ראשונים attributes the composition to יהושע, while רבי יוחנן בן זכאי is the one who instituted reciting it daily — which is why this is so ancient. יהושע, who lived more than a millennium before ישו, obviously had no knowledge of him; the deeper כוונה existed long before it became a pretext for persecution.)

VII. The Crusades — עלינו on the Lips of Martyrs

During the Crusades of 1171, רבי אפרים מבון[8] — a great ראשון whose insights we quote frequently — records something profoundly moving. Yidden in France, when they were taken to be killed by the Crusaders, recited עלינו לשבח before being מוסר נפש על קידוש השם. Why עלינו specifically? Because it is the תפילה נאה that expresses our exclusive belief in הקדוש ברוך הוא and no one else. To declare that aloud, in their final moments, was the perfect way to die for the Jewish faith. Every time we recite עלינו, we are joining the words that were on the lips of those מוסרי נפש.

VIII. Halachic Guidelines for Saying עלינו

When you walk into shul and the קהל is saying עלינו, say עלינו with them. It must be recited standing — that is the מנהג all of כלל ישראל goes back to.

The Heavenly Response

The נוסח of the אחרונים, brought by the משנה ברורה in קונטרס אחרון[9], quotes the ראשונים and מקובלים: when a yid says עלינו here below, the heavenly assembly listens and responds ברוך הוא וברוך שמו. They answer our declaration. This teaches that עלינו is enormously significant. It is not a תפילה for mumbling on the way out the door. It should be said with כוונה. רב האי גאון[10] writes that it should be recited with one's טלית still on — though that is already a חומרא, since most yidden have removed their טלית and תפילין by then. רב האי גאון paskens it should be said באימה ויראה.

IX. עלינו After קידוש לבנה and ברית מילה

עלינו is also recited after קידוש לבנה. The אחרונים explain: קידוש לבנה might appear, to an outside observer, as if yidden are talking and dancing to the moon. To make it absolutely clear that no one — חס ושלום — is worshiping the moon, we immediately declare עלינו לשבח, reaffirming our exclusive devotion to הקדוש ברוך הוא. Similarly, עלינו is recited after a ברית מילה. Perhaps because it is recited before leaving shul after a special occasion. Another suggestion: a ברית, with its elements of "sacrifice," cutting, and blood, could in theory raise misperceptions; we conclude with עלינו to clarify our theology.

X. אל תירא מפחד פתאום — Three פסוקים

What They Mean

After עלינו, some קהילות recite אל תירא מפחד פתאום[11]. Some shuls even sing it beautifully. If you hear the words, they are מרעדיק — powerful: do not fear anything. עצו עצה ותפר[12] — no matter what the United Nations says, the עצה of our enemies will be foiled. Then ועד זקנה אני הוא ועד שיבה אני אסבל[13] — until your old age I am He, and until your gray hair I will carry you. הקדוש ברוך הוא takes upon Himself to support, sustain, and bring ישועה to His people.

The Source — The ט״ז

This practice comes from the ט״ז[14]. The טורי זהב writes — and it is rare for the ט״ז to testify about his own personal סדר תפילה — that he himself used to say these three פסוקים daily. The first is from משלי, the next two from ישעיה. He would recite them every day while the שליח ציבור was saying שים שלום. (This is hard to fathom — one is supposed to listen to חזרת הש״ץ — but that was his מנהג.) Today no one practices this; the printers placed the three פסוקים at the end of davening, after עלינו, where the placement makes thematic sense: עלינו declares that only הקדוש ברוך הוא is our master, and אל תירא reinforces the same. The ט״ז heard that in France yidden said it daily; the practice migrated into the printed סידור at the end of davening. The קיצור של״ה[15] writes that even if you do not understand the words, these פסוקים are very, very significant.

The Story of מרדכי and the Children

The מדרש רבה[16] on מגילת אסתר tells the story behind these פסוקים. During the גזירה of המן and אחשורוש, מרדכי הצדיק went to visit the school children in the cheder. He wanted to know what would be — for though there is no longer נבואה, נבואה is occasionally given to שוטים וקטנים, those whose mental state is diminished and small children. Sometimes a child, in his innocent תמימות, can say something profound. So מרדכי asked them: what did you learn today?

The first boy answered: אל תירא מפחד פתאום — do not fear sudden fear. An excellent פסוק for מרדכי to hear with המן's גזירה in the air. The second answered: עצו עצה ותפר — they may make a plan, but it will be foiled. The third answered: ועד זקנה אני הוא — הקדוש ברוך הוא will carry the Jewish people, even when they do not deserve it. מרדכי said: now that I have heard these three פסוקים from these three children in cheder, I know the ישועה is going to come.

Therefore the ראשונים tell us to say these פסוקים every day. It is a daily commemoration of the ישועה of פורים — which, incidentally, is now thirty days away.

XI. After עלינו — Another קדיש יתום

After עלינו and after אל תירא, we say קדיש יתום. Whenever we have said פסוקים we say a קדיש. Since these three פסוקים have just been recited, another קדיש follows.

XII. The שש זכירות — Six Biblical Remembrances

The List

The שש זכירות[17] are six פסוקים of the תורה that obligate every yid to remember:

  1. מעשה מרים
  2. שבת
  3. יציאת מצרים
  4. מעמד הר סיני
  5. עמלק
  6. The מן

The חיד״א actually counts ten זכירות, adding remembrances such as what בלעם and בלק tried to do. We follow the more common list of six.

The האריז״ל — Mental, Not Verbal

The האריז״ל[18], as quoted by רב חיים ויטאל, writes that one does not need to recite the שש זכירות verbally. זכירה means to remember — and you remember with your heart and mind, not necessarily with your mouth. Four very easy מצוות דאורייתא, fulfilled in a single second of intention each: I remember יציאת מצרים (which you already say in פרשת ציצית at ויאמר), I remember הר סיני, I remember עמלק, and so on. The האריז״ל brings only four of the six, and that is it. According to him, you do not need to say them — you simply need to think them.

The תלמידים — Verbal Strengthens Memory

Other תלמידים of the האריז״ל understood differently. They held that he meant the recitation aloud, which reinforces the memory. When you say something you remember it better. The רמב״ם paskens halacha l'maaseh that one is supposed to learn תורה aloud. The famous גמרא in עירובין דף נ״ג ע״ב[19]: ברוריה, the wife of רבי מאיר, saw one of her husband's תלמידים learning while merely whispering the words of the גמרא — she kicked him. She said, "you are a תלמיד of my husband and you are only whispering? תורה has to be said aloud."

We absorb so much through listening today, but it is worthwhile, every day, to say the דף יומי once aloud to yourself. Words you say register far more deeply than words you only hear. The רמב״ם[20] paskens this directly in הלכות תלמוד תורה. The same principle applies to the שש זכירות: the point is to remember; saying them aloud cements the memory. That is why we have them in the סידור — to recite them, to ensure we are מקיים these מצוות דאורייתא, which are otherwise so easy to lose.

When to Say Them

Some hold that ובחרת בנו in אהבה רבה is when one should be מכוון the שש זכירות without saying them aloud. Our common מנהג, however, is to recite them at the end of davening. Why there? It is convenient — a good moment to be מקיים these biblical obligations before walking out. Nothing inherent ties them to the end of שחרית; you could fulfill them any time of day. But the printers placed them there in the סידור, and it is worthwhile to do it. You are being מקיים מצוות דאורייתא in less than a minute.

XIII. לדוד ה' אורי — A Sabbatean Question

From ראש חודש אלול through שמיני עצרת every yid says לדוד ה' אורי twice a day. But certain חסידישע groups — Sanz, Ropshitz, and Bobov — do not. Why?

The promise associated with לדוד is brought from the האריז״ל: a yid who says it twice a day, every day, from ראש חודש אלול through שמיני עצרת, is מובטח that he will have a good year, that הקדוש ברוך הוא will judge him favorably, and that he will be written in the ספר של צדיקים גמורים. (Other zechuyot are presumed; this is one סגולה.)

The trouble is the source. The practice traces to a ספר printed in the 1700s in Turkey called חמדת ימים[21]. Turkey was a hub of שבתאי צבי followers. Many claim — and there is real evidence — that חמדת ימים was authored, at least in part, by נתן העזתי (Nathan of Gaza), the chief disciple of שבתי צבי. Nathan of Gaza wreaked havoc across Europe, spreading שבתי צבי's claim to be משיח; hundreds of thousands of yidden fell into the cult. Therefore Sanz and Ropshitz — and Bobov, which descends from them — refuse to touch any practice traced to חמדת ימים.

There is a raging debate over the authorship of חמדת ימים. There are clear indications that certain sections were written by Nathan of Gaza. But many הלכות in it come straight from the האריז״ל and could be of perfectly clean origin. The יעב״ץ wrote an entire ספר on the question. The מנחת אלעזר[22] treated חמדת ימים as among the worst things that ever happened to כלל ישראל and wrote a full קונטרס warning against learning from it. His grandfather, the מנחת ברוך, was a great קנאי who wrote a complete ספר on the dangers of חמדת ימים.

This is a fascinating piece of liturgical history — the influence of Sabbatean ideas on our סידורים and our מנהגים runs deeper than people realize. But it is also worth noting: רבינו יונה טייב brings לדוד, and virtually all of כלל ישראל says it apart from a few חסידישע groups. So if רבינו יונה טייב said it, we proceed.

XIV. אני מאמין — Who Wrote It, and Why Some Did Not Say It

Mystery of Authorship

Who wrote the אני מאמין that some yidden say daily? We genuinely do not know. This is striking when you consider that these are the thirteen עיקרי האמונה — the most fundamental statements of belief.

The רמב״ם[23], in his introduction to פרק חלק in his commentary to the משנה, establishes the thirteen עיקרים. The אני מאמין declarations correspond to them. But the רמב״ם himself did not write the אני מאמין texts. The יעב״ץ in his סידור suggests perhaps the רמב״ם wrote them, but no real research substantiates it. In all the writings of the רמב״ם there is no indication that one is supposed to recite them aloud every day.

The מהרש״ל — Sharp Opposition

The מהרש״ל[24] had a תלמיד named רבי משה מתוטוטש (last name מת). This תלמיד testified that his רבי, the מהרש״ל, never said אני מאמין. He was strongly opposed. The מהרש״ל's reasoning: "I know yidden who say אני מאמין and they walk away proud — I know the עיקרים, I believe in them — and now they feel free to be עובר on other parts of תורה and on שבת." Reciting a fixed list of beliefs can become a license to ignore everything not on the list.

The האריז״ל — Compartmentalization

The האריז״ל also said not to say יגדל; it appears he did not approve of saying אני מאמין either. The יעב״ץ explains: even though the רמב״ם wrote thirteen עיקרים, the האריז״ל did not agree to enshrine just these in daily davening, because yidden might think this is it — if you believe these thirteen, you are good to go and can do whatever you want. You cannot take parts of the תורה and call them עיקרים while implying the rest is less essential. That is a real danger.

The Precedent — עשרת הדברות in Daily Davening

חז״ל faced exactly this issue once before. The original תקנה was to say עשרת הדברות every day — see גמרא ברכות דף י״ב ע״א[25]. Every day, part of the תפילה was to recite the עשרת הדברות. But חז״ל removed it, because there were מינים who would say "this is all the תורה — only the עשרת הדברות; everything else is garbage." So the practice was discontinued. (That is the same reason some yidden do not stand specifically for the עשרת הדברות when they are read in the תורה. The רמב״ם in a תשובה writes not to stand only for עשרת הדברות — yidden might think the עשרת הדברות is essential and the rest is incidental, time for shmoozing. What we do today is stand the entire קריאה.) The fundamental concern of the יעב״ץ is the same: do not compartmentalize the תורה into "thirteen עיקרים" while implying the rest is optional.

Our Practice

Nevertheless, we do say אני מאמין. We are not so opposed; the concern is real but not decisive. Belief in ישועה does not require this specific recitation; you can express it other ways. The רמב״ם paskens that anyone who does not believe in even one of these עיקרים is מן המינים — outside the basic Jewish belief system, as he writes in הלכות תשובה[26]. But where does the רמב״ם say one must recite the list verbally every day? In the גמרא, when it says something must be said with the mouth, it can sometimes be discharged with the heart. We say אני מאמין during the week. We do not say it on שבת in most נוסחאות. It is not really a part of שחרית proper, but it has become a common מנהג.

XV. The יגדל Controversy

רב דוד כהן and מסעת כפי

There is a remarkable ספר by רב דוד כהן שליט״א — he is now about ninety-five years old; he should be granted a רפואה שלמה and אריכות ימים ושנים. Formerly a רב in Coney Island, now in Lakewood, he is a tremendous תלמיד חכם who has written many ספרים. Among them is a fascinating multi-volume work on the history of תפילה called מסעת כפי[27], twelve or thirteen volumes spanning the entire historical journey of our סידור. In it, רב דוד כהן addresses the פיוט of יגדל — which sets all the רמב״ם's עיקרים in one rhyming poem: יגדל אלוקים חי…

Many authorities, including the האריז״ל, were opposed to reciting it. The יעב״ץ also omits it from his סידור. רב דוד כהן suggests that יגדל was written by a thirteenth-century Italian Jew named עמנואל הרומי — Emmanuel of Rome — a figure one must know to learn הלכות שבת in the שלחן ערוך properly.

Emmanuel of Rome and Reading Novels on שבת

There is a הלכה in שלחן ערוך, הלכות שבת[28], that one is not permitted to read novels on שבת. Many yidden stumble in this. While reading is permitted in general, novels and works of fiction are forbidden on שבת. The מחבר actually gives an example of what counted as novels in his time. (Today's equivalent would be a Patterson, a Harry Potter, popular fiction. The שלחן ערוך writes ודבר דבר — שלא יהא דיבורך של שבת כדיבורך של חול: your speech on שבת should not be like your weekday speech.)

This is one of the rare places where the שלחן ערוך — usually so dry, so halachic — elaborates at length. He specifically mentions a yid named Emmanuel of Rome who wrote fictional novels. The שלחן ערוך paskens that not only is reading them forbidden on שבת, but one should not read them during the week either, because they are full of חולין — empty content. One should either work or learn תורה, not waste time on such books. (We live in a different culture today and are חכם ונבון, but the principle endures: be careful what you read.)

According to רב דוד כהן's research, this Emmanuel of Rome — the very figure the מחבר criticizes so harshly — is the one who authored יגדל. That is why many אחרונים could not bring themselves to incorporate it into the סידור as part of תפילה. The historical claim is debated; some argue יגדל is not by Emmanuel of Rome. But the question casts a long shadow.

XVI. רב סולוביצ'יק — אני מאמין and the Catholic Catechism

The רמב״ן in פרשת ראה

Another concept comes from רב יוסף דב סולוביצ'יק זצ״ל of Boston and YU. His chief תלמיד, רב הרשל שכטר שליט״א, recorded it in his three-volume work on his רבי, in the volume titled נפש הרב[29]. רב סולוביצ'יק based the entire issue on a רמב״ן in פרשת ראה[30]. The פסוק warns: do not look at the gentile nations and ask, "how do they serve their god?" with the intention of doing the same for ה'. The תורה says לא תעשה כן לה' אלוקיך. The רמב״ן writes that you cannot do anything like the גוים when it comes to עבודת ה'.

The Practical Applications

Rav Soloveitchik drew far-reaching conclusions. Just as churches have stained-glass windows, a shul should not have stained-glass windows. (Many do, of course — travel through Europe and you see them.) Even the fact that the Pope wears a head covering similar to a yarmulke raised a question, in his approach, about white yarmulkes. In churches the clergy wear long black robes; in some pre-war European shuls between the two World Wars, the rabbi and the chazzan began wearing elaborate robes and hats reminiscent of church vestments — that practice continues in some places today, but according to רב סולוביצ'יק it falls under the same רמב״ן principle. In churches the priest faces the congregation when leading prayer; some Reform and Conservative shuls adopted this, with the בעל תפילה facing the קהל — that too, said רב סולוביצ'יק, was learned from the גוים.

The Reason for Not Saying אני מאמין or יגדל

And the final application: in the Catholic Church there is something called the catechism. After their prayers — typically on Sundays — Catholics recite a structured list of dogmas: a sequence of "I believe" statements about what they believe and do not believe. If we recite אני מאמין or יגדל in shul in a similar structured fashion, we would be mimicking the Catholic catechism in form. The content of אני מאמין is, of course, אמת — the רמב״ם requires belief in every one of the עיקרים. But the format and the public sequenced recitation, said רב סולוביצ'יק, is what worried so many אחרונים. Some have suggested a חילוק: saying it quietly to oneself may be acceptable; saying it aloud, as a sequenced public recitation, replicates the catechism in form and is the deeper concern.

סיכום

Every word of עלינו is freighted with history. The phrase לתכן עולם calls for the kingdom of heaven, not the slogans of social activism. The line להבל וריק survived eighty deaths in פראג and a royal decree in Prussia, and is now ours again. אל תירא comes to us from three children in cheder during המן's gezeirah. The שש זכירות are four to six daily מצוות דאורייתא that take seconds. And the closing pieces of davening — אני מאמין, יגדל, לדוד — each carry their own warnings about authorship and form. We daven what our מסורה gives us, with כוונה and with eyes open to where every word came from.
