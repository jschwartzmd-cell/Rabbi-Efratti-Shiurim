מַדּוּעַ אֵין מַסֶּכֶת חֲנוּכָּה?

Why Is There No Maseches Chanukah?
Shiur of Rabbi Ephrati — Erev Zos Chanukah 5785
Recorded 1 January 2025 (Erev Zos Chanukah) · Reworked 3 May 2026 · Mareh Mekomos upgraded 5 May 2026

A continuation of the previous week's Chanukah shiur, addressing heoros from Josh Shapp, Robert Engelbert, Reb Shmueli Bornstein, and others. The Rabbi opens with two thoughts proper to Erev Zos Chanukah — the Bnei Yissaschar's outstretched hand of teshuvah, and the Apter Rav's vort on the secular New Year — and proceeds to the central kushya: why did Rabbeinu HaKadosh not compose a maseches on Chanukah? The Rabbi sketches six teirutzim, building toward an original historical teretz from Rav Reuven Margaliyos on the tumultus Iudaicus and the Trajan-era massacre of Alexandrian Jewry — and concludes with a remez in Bava Kama on Yochanan and his sons.

I. Hakdamah — Erev Zos Chanukah and the Secular New Year

The Bnei Yissaschar — A Last Hand to Receive

The Rabbi opened: today is Erev Zos Chanukah; tonight begins Zos Chanukah proper. The posh'tushe pshat for the name comes from the Krias HaTorah — "zos chanukas ha'mizbe'ach"[1] — but the Bnei Yissaschar,[2] as transmitted by his son the Sameach David, teaches that on Zos Chanukah:

כִּי יְמִינְךָ פְּשׁוּטָה לְקַבֵּל שָׁבִים.
"For Your right hand is outstretched to receive those who return."

The hand of HaKadosh Baruch Hu remains outstretched even now to receive any Jew who has not yet completed his teshuvah from the Yamim Nora'im. The Chassidische sefarim, in the Rabbi's framing, "gave us another couple of months" — past the chasimah, past Hoshana Rabbah — through Zos Chanukah as a final gate.

The Apter Rav on January 1st

The Wilanowitzer (the Velver) quotes in the name of the Apter Rav[3] — the Ohev Yisrael from Apta, one of the great second-generation Chassidische rebbes — that he would say "Happy New Year" on January 1st. When asked: our Rosh HaShanah is in Tishrei, why mark theirs?

His teretz: HaKadosh Baruch Hu compares our Rosh HaShanah to the goyishe new year. What were we doing? Davening. A night of cheshbon ha'nefesh. Last night the goyim — drinking, perhaps the night of the year they drink most. HaKadosh Baruch Hu sees the comparison — His children versus the world — and retroactively all of our tefillos from Rosh HaShanah are accepted. Even tefillos blocked on Rosh HaShanah by mekatregim ascend today on the strength of the contrast.

Rav Yaakov Kamenetsky — A New Year's Resolution

The Rabbi shared a moidi'ach recently received about Rav Yaakov Kamenetsky:[4] he walked into a yeshivah class on January 1st (no vacation) and asked the bachurim, "What is your New Year's resolution?" Puzzlement. "Why do we need one?" Rav Yaakov's answer: "An entire country is having a day of cheshbon ha'nefesh and kabbalas tovah. Should we not? If the country is doing it, let us also accept upon ourselves something good."

The Rabbi added — wryly, from his car radio — that 79% of American resolutions concern weight loss and eating habits. One woman: "I'm taking on reading two books this year." The Rabbi's response: "Two books?! We should accept on ourselves to finish at least two masechtos!"

II. The Question — Why No Maseches Chanukah?

Last week, the Rabbi asked: why did Rabbeinu HaKadosh, who composed a maseches for nearly every yom tov — Megillah, Yoma, Rosh HaShanah, Sukkah — not compose a Maseches Chanukah? Heoros have flooded in from Josh Shapp, Robert Engelbert, and others. The Rabbi will work through several teirutzim in turn.

III. Teretz #1 — The Vilna Gaon: It Was Lost

The Vilna Gaon's son writes in his father's name[5] that there was a Maseches Chanukah, and we lost it — along with masechtos on tefillin and other inyanim.

The Difficulty

The Rabbi raised a kushya: when a maseches exists in Shas, other masechtos typically cross-reference it ("k'di'amrinan b'maseches X"). We never find anywhere in Shas a reference to "b'maseches Chanukah" — making it pretty evident that no such maseches ever existed in textual form.

IV. Teretz #2 — A "Vendetta" Against the Chashmonaim

The Reported Chasam Sofer

Some sefarim attribute (in the name of the Chasam Sofer[6]) the explanation that Rabbeinu HaKadosh, descended from Beis David, took it personally that the Chashmonaim — descendants of Levi — had assumed the malchus: "It is not right to my family, to my lineage. I will not put a maseches in mishnah about their yom tov."

Reb Shmueli Bornstein's Support — Chagigah

Reb Shmueli Bornstein of Lakewood, an outstanding talmid chacham and magid shiur known for his "Reid Bites," sent — through Josh Shapp — a strong re'ayah to this approach. The Gemara at the end of Chagigah[7] records that Rabbeinu HaKadosh instructed his son: Rabi Meir is to be cited as "acherim", Rabi Nasan as "yesh omrim" — never by name. Why? Because Rabi Meir and Rabi Nasan had once attempted to undermine Rabban Gamliel, Rabbeinu HaKadosh's father — meaning they tried to "l'akor", to uproot, the kavod of Malchus Beis David. Anyone who attempts to uproot the kavod of Beis David, says Rabbeinu HaKadosh, is not cited by name. By extension — perhaps the same logic against the Chashmonaim.

The Difficulty

But the question remains: a Maseches Chanukah would contain halachos binding on every Jew every year. Whether Rabbeinu HaKadosh had a personal issue with the Chashmonaim or not, the practical halachos still need transmission. This teretz doesn't fully answer the question.

V. The Sforno's Frame — There Was No Cheit at All

The Rambam Praises the Chashmonaim

The Rabbi paused on the assumption underlying teretz #2: was the Chashmonaim's assumption of the throne actually wrong? The Ramban in last week's shiur said yes — it was a cheit. But the Rambam in Hilchos Chanukah[8] praises the Chashmonaim: they were chazakim u'gevurim; the malchus returned to Klal Yisrael. The Rabbi noted the Rambam here does something he does in no other halachah: he opens Hilchos Chanukah with three full halachos of pure historical narrative. The Rambam is "not a storybook; it's a dry book of laws." Why the history?

The Rabbi's Pshat on the Rambam's History

The Rabbi's teretz rests on the Rambam's famous hakdamah[9]: "I am writing this sefer so a Jew need have nothing else at home but a Tanach and my book." (This hakdamah caused the great opposition to the Rambam — the Geonei Bavel attacked him, claiming he would render limud Mishnah and Gemara obsolete; some forty years after his petirah, his sefarim were burned. For a century the Jewish world split pro-Rambam and anti-Rambam; the Rambam ultimately prevailed.) But for a Jew with only the Tanach and the Mishneh Torah — Tanach contains nothing about Chanukah! Therefore the Rambam needed to give a hakdamah histori'is at the head of the halachos. (The Rabbi compared with Hilchos Megillah, where the Rambam opens with no historical introduction — because Megillas Esther is in Tanach.)

The Sforno on Lo Yasur

The Sforno[10] — Rabi Ovadyah Sforno, late-1400s/early-1500s Italian posek, the early Hebraist who tutored some of the famous German Christian galachim in Tanach — disagrees with the Ramban entirely on "Lo yasur shevet mi'Yehudah."

Lo yasur, says the Sforno, means "do not remove". The issur is on removing the malchus from Shevet Yehudah. Once it has been removed — by the churban, by Tzidkiyahu's exile, by the centuries without a melech at all — there is no issur on a member of another shevet assuming the throne. The Sforno's krenkel against the Ramban: "a big ta'us"; there is no issur for the Chashmonaim. Therefore, the teretz based on Rabbeinu HaKadosh's vendetta loses its premise.

VI. Teretz #3 — The Chasam Sofer: Rabbeinu HaKadosh Wrote Only the Unknown

A surprising teretz attributed to the Chasam Sofer[11]: Rabbeinu HaKadosh's mission in the Mishnah was l'chadeish — to record what is not obvious to the olam.

The Rambam's Yesod in Pirush HaMishnayos

The Rambam, in his Pirush HaMishnayos on Maseches Menachos[12], observes that there is no mishnah anywhere stating that one must say Krias Shema twice a day. (Maseches Berachos opens with the zemanim and tnu'os — but never establishes the chiyuv.) There is no mishnah mandating mezuzah on the door, no mishnah mandating the wrapping of tefillin. Why? Because everybody knew — these were universally practiced. The mishnah records that which the olam does not already know.

Application to Chanukah

Says the Chasam Sofer: Rabbeinu HaKadosh lived perhaps 350 years after the events (the Maccabee revolt is dated to 167 BCE; Rabi Yehudah HaNasi was born the day Rabi Akiva died, ~135 CE; the Mishnah was redacted ~50 years later — about 350 years total). By that time, every Yiddishe household knew the halachos of Chanukah. Rabbeinu HaKadosh simply did not need to write them. Although Chanukah was a mitzvah d'rabbanan, a relatively new mitzvah, its practice was so deeply embedded that no tikkun b'kesav was required.

VII. Teretz #4 — Megillas Taanis Already Existed

There is a sefer in leshon ha'kodesh called Megillas Taanis[13]. It is quoted throughout Talmud Bavli and Talmud Yerushalmi; it is, in fact, the earliest known written work of Torah she'b'al peh, predating the Mishnah.

Megillas Taanis is a calendar of all the days on which one may not be misped and may not fast — the yom tov ka'tan's. Most of its content concerns the victories of the Chashmonaim over the Yevanim; it covers, in considerable detail, the story of Chanukah and its halachos. By the time Rabbeinu HaKadosh sat to compose the Mishnah, the korpus of Chanukah halachah and history was already accessible b'kesav. He did not need to duplicate it.

VIII. Teretz #5 — Rav Reuven Margaliyos: The Political Reason

The Rabbi's preferred new teretz, found in the sefarim of Rav Reuven Margaliyos:[14] a brilliant talmid chacham, historian, scholar of historiah Yehudis and even secular sources — and, by trade, a shopkeeper in Eretz Yisrael. He emigrated from Galicia in 1946 or 1947 and ran a grocery; in his spare time he wrote dozens of sefarim. The Margaliyos HaYam on Maseches Sanhedrin — required reading for Daf Yomi learners on Sanhedrin, the Rabbi insisted — is one of his works, full of even secular mareh mekomos. He also wrote a sefer on the question: what motivated Rabbeinu HaKadosh to write the Mishnah?

The Tumultus Iudaicus

The 350 years between the Chashmonaim and Rabbeinu HaKadosh were called by Roman historians the Tumultus Iudaicus — "the tumult of Judea." Three great revolts:

  • The Great Revolt (66–73 CE) — culminating in the churban Bayis Sheni.
  • The Kitos War / Diaspora Revolt (115–117 CE) — in Egypt, Cyprus, and Cyrene/Libya.
  • The Bar Kochva Revolt (132–135 CE) — in Eretz Yisrael, led by Bar Kochva, whom Rabi Akiva believed was Mashiach; ended in catastrophe after some nine years of guerrilla warfare.

The Sikariim and the Movement Out of Eretz Yisrael

The Sikariim (the term, the Rabbi noted, is borrowed today for the Sikrikim in Yerushalayim — the violent extremist sect) — the ancient biryonim — were the brazen Jewish rebels who threw off Roman rule. After the failure of the Bar Kochva revolt, the survivors moved out of Eretz Yisrael into the diaspora. (Most Jews already lived outside Eretz Yisrael; many had never returned after churban Bayis Rishon.) The largest concentrations — Alexandria, Cyprus, Cyrene/Libya.

Dio Cassius — Our Best Source

For most of this we depend on Dio Cassius,[15] the Roman historian of Greek origin who wrote eighty books of Roman history — a contemporary of Rabbeinu HaKadosh. His descriptions are obviously noteh against the Jews — but his accounts remain invaluable.

The Great Synagogue of Alexandria

The Yerushalmi in Sukkah[16] (and the Bavli) describe the shul in Alexandria, vast and majestic — the Rabbi compared it to the Belzer shul in Yerushalayim. The Gemara:

"Whoever did not see this synagogue has not seen hadar."

Inside: seventy kateidri shel zahav — seventy golden thrones, lining the walls in memory of the seventy zekenim of the Sanhedrin. The shul was so large that the tzibbur could not hear the chazan; the shamash would raise a flag at each amen, and the olam would respond to the flag.

The Massacre — Trajan Caesar

The Judean Sikariim, having failed at home, came to Alexandria, Cyprus, and Libya and incited the local Jewish communities with their nationalistic ru'ach. A great revolt erupted: the Jews massacred — according to Dio Cassius — 120,000 gentiles. The brutality in his telling: spits of iron driven through bodies, gentiles burned alive.

Trajan Caesar was the Roman emperor at the time. The Gemara[17] records the personal trigger: Trajan had a son who was born on Tisha B'Av; the Jews mourned that day. He had a daughter who died on Chanukah; the Jews lit candles. His wife pressed him: "Before you go fight the barbarians, come first to Alexandria. The Jews mourn what we celebrate; they celebrate what we mourn."

The Yeshiva of Alexandria — Parshas Ki Savo

The Gemara in the Yerushalmi: he entered the yeshivah and asked, "What are you learning?" They were learning Parshas Ki Savo:

יִשָּׂא ה' עָלֶיךָ גּוֹי מֵרָחוֹק מִקְצֵה הָאָרֶץ... גּוֹי אֲשֶׁר לֹא תִשְׁמַע לְשׁוֹנוֹ... וְהֵצַר לְךָ בְּכָל שְׁעָרֶיךָ עַד רֶדֶת חוֹמוֹתֶיךָ הַגְּבֹהוֹת וְהַבְּצֻרוֹת.
"Hashem will raise against you a nation from afar... a nation whose tongue you will not understand... and they will besiege you in all your gates until your high and fortified walls fall."[18]

The Rabbi paused for a personal note. On September 11, 2001 — also during Parshas Ki Savo — his rebbe in Tzimim on Ocean Parkway, watching the smoke from the roof of the yeshivah, opened a Chumash and read these pesukim: "I don't understand anything. I just know we were attacked in something that will be in history books forever. Let me read you pesukim from this week's parshah." The "high and fortified walls" — the choi'mos ha'gevohos v'ha'betzuros — represented in his rebbe's reading by the Twin Towers.

To the yeshivah Trajan said: "Here I am. That is me." He killed the entire yeshivah. The Gemara describes the subsequent slaughter — the men, then their wives whom Trajan demanded as spoils and who refused — until all the blood of the men and the blood of their wives mixed together and the sea between Egypt and Cyprus turned red with Jewish blood.

The Gemara closes:

"At that hour, the keren of Klal Yisrael was cut off — and it will not be restored until Mashiach ben David, b'meheirah b'yameinu, amen."

Roman Surveillance of Jewish Texts

The Rabbi added two pieces of supporting evidence for the political motive:

  • The Septuagint — Targum Hashivim: Ptolemy placed seventy sages in separate rooms and they translated the Torah into Greek[19]; the olam studied it. (Eleven uniform shinuyim were inserted by ru'ach ha'kodesh; among them, "v'es ha'arneves" was changed because Ptolemy's mother — from Amalek — was named Arneves.) The Romans inherited and used the Targum.
  • The Two Sardiotin in Bava Kama — the Gemara[20] records that malchus Yavan sent "shnei sardiotin" — high-ranking officials — to copy the laws of Bava Kama from the Chachmei Yisrael. They were monitoring our halachah closely. (The Rabbi: "Reb Telz used to say — Bava Kama, his favorite maseches.")

The Conclusion — Censorship Self-Imposed

This is, says Rav Margaliyos, the teretz. Rabbeinu HaKadosh was very sensitive to the political moment. He knew everything he wrote would be censored, edited, and reviewed by the Romans. Chanukah, by definition, is a national holiday — even today secular Israelis celebrate it as the founding national triumph. The Chashmonaim's victory restored malchus to Klal Yisrael. Rabbeinu HaKadosh would not give the Romans a written tractate documenting this victory. He refrained. Light the menorah, do the halachos — but no maseches celebrating in kesav the revolt of the Maccabees over the Greeks.

Why the Beraisa in Shabbos Was Acceptable

One of the talmidim noted: but the Chanukah sugya does appear in Shabbos 21b. The Rabbi: That is a beraisa — oral tradition that later generations could write down. The tana'im after Rabbeinu HaKadosh were a different generation; the amora'im later still. The Roman concern, by then, was muchlat diminished. It is the Mishnah itself, the foundational document, that Rabbeinu HaKadosh kept clear of Chanukah.

IX. The Sefas Emes — Concealment as the Inner Yesod

One talmid pressed: It is striking that Chanukah is the one yom tov we don't really know exactly what happened — tukosha al fum kesha — what occurred? The Rabbi referred back to last week's discussion of the Sefas Emes:[21] Chanukah is "tarpa balbeis" — everything is hidden; everything is b'soyd; when Mashiach comes it will all be revealed. Politically and spiritually the same yesod: Chazal did not want a political ru'ach taking over Klal Yisrael in galus; the Jews had suffered enough tzaros under Trajan and his successors. The first half of the second century was, for Rabbeinu HaKadosh, a moment to suppress, not to publicize, the national malchus story.

X. B'sha'as HaSakanah — The Window

The halachah of lighting ner Chanukah is to place the menorah at the door, opposite the mezuzah, on reshus ha'rabbim. We light inside, by the window. Why? B'sha'as ha'sakanah — in a time of danger.[22] The Gemara set the dispensation; we have inherited it. There is no sakanah on Harborview South; in Eretz Yisrael today, in many neighborhoods of Yerushalayim, they light outside in glass menorah boxes k'tikuno. But the inheritance comes from a moment when celebrating Chanukah openly would have provoked the goyim who knew exactly what we were celebrating.

XI. Sefer HaMaccabim — The Yiddish Sources of the Acharonim

The Rabbi addressed a heorah from a talmid after last week's shiur: "You are an ultra-orthodox rav; the Gemara says one is not permitted to learn sefarim chitzonim." The Rabbi accepted the mussar — but shared a piece of history.

The Engelman Edition (2018, 2024)

In 2018, the Engelman publishing house in Eretz Yisrael released a major new Hebrew edition of Sefer HaMaccabim I; Sefer HaMaccabim II appeared a few weeks before this shiur (late 2024).[23] It is now in stores in America. The Rabbi recommended buying both: a beautiful edition with maps and pictures, expensively produced, citing every early source that quotes Sefer HaMaccabim.

How Did the Tosfos Yom Tov Quote It?

The original Hebrew of Sefer HaMaccabim was lost; only the Greek translation (preserved in Christian Bibles) survived. Yet the Tosfos Yom Tov, the Aruch HaShulchan, and a long chain of mainstream poskim from 1700s Lita and Galicia onward cite Sefer HaMaccabim by name. How?

The Engelman edition's hakdamah: there was a Yiddish translation of Sefer HaMaccabim circulating in the eighteenth century; almost no copies survive today, but it is the source for those acharonim. "If the Tosfos Yom Tov could quote Sefer HaMaccabim," said the Rabbi, "I am certainly allowed to read it. No ta'anos against me!"

XII. Mattisyahu ben Yochanan — Was His Father the Tzeduki?

Last week the Rabbi had said that Mattisyahu ben Yochanan Kohen Gadol's father — that Yochanan Kohen Gadol who served eighty years and turned Tzeduki at the end (per Berachos 29a[24]) — was the same Yochanan. The Rabbi had quoted the Bnei Yissaschar's kushya: If his father was a Tzeduki, why mention him by patronymic in our tefillos? Why not just "bimei Mattisyahu Kohen Gadol"?

Uri's Heorah

Uri came up after the shiur: "Not the same Yochanan!" The Rabbi acknowledged that historically Uri is probably right. He did the research: the Bnei Yissaschar[25] rejects the suggestion that they are different Yochanans, and concludes that they are the same. He therefore offers several teirutzim for why we mention his name — chief among them, the next remez.

XIII. The Remez in Bava Kama — Yochanan, Chenvani, and Ner Chanukah

Eight Mentions of Chanukah in Mishnah

The Rabbi posed a trivia: how many times is the word Chanukah mentioned in Shas? Some say six, some say eight. He looked: seven times "Chanukah," once "Chashmonai" — totaling eight. The Rabbi noted this corresponds beautifully to the eight days of Chanukah.

The Mishnah of the Camel and the Storekeeper

One of these eight is the mishnah at the end of HaKonis in Bava Kama[26] — the mishnah every cheder child learns in sixth grade. A man drives a camel laden with flax (pishtan) through reshus ha'rabbim. A chenvani — a shopkeeper — has merchandise on his stoop, with a candle burning. The flax catches fire from the candle; the entire store burns. Who pays?

  • If the candle is inside the store — the camel-driver pays. He had no business letting an overflowing camel-load reach into the storefront.
  • If the candle is outside on reshus ha'rabbim — the camel-driver is patur. The shopkeeper had no business with a candle in the public way.
  • If it is ner Chanukah — even outside, the camel-driver pays! Because every Jew knows that on Chanukah candles are placed outside, the camel-driver should have known to steer away.

The Bircas HaShalom's Remez

The Bircas HaShalom[27] — a high-level work by one of the great Chassidische rebbes of around 250 years ago, post-Baal Shem Tov, "not a well-known sefer because it is very gehoiben" — sees here a remez to Yochanan Kohen Gadol's downfall and his sons' redemption.

חנוני = יוחנן — the same letters.

The Yoma Story

When did Yochanan go bad? The Gemara in Yoma[28] on daf nun-gimmel records the first ma'aseh of the Tzedukim: on Yom Kippur, when bringing the ketores into the Kodesh HaKodashim, you must place the fire on the kaf of the ketores inside. The Tzedukim insisted — based on a drash of their own — that the fire should be lit outside, before entering. The first kohen to do this was Yochanan Kohen Gadol at the end of his eighty years; the Tzedukim grew popular off his simchah; the avodah was m'tzeves. The Gemara brings: "chayav" — anyone doing this is liable.

The Tikkun Through Ner Chanukah

But the mishnah in Bava Kama: on ner Chanukah, when the candle is set outside — patur!

The Bircas HaShalom's drash: patur here is more than "exempt from damages." When Yochanan's descendants — the Chashmonaim — were misakein the takanah of placing the light outside to celebrate the miracle of Chanukah, they reversed the very cheit of their grandfather. Yochanan had brought fire outside wrongly, lighting the Tzedukim's ascendancy; his descendants brought light outside rightly, lighting the Rabbanan's takanah. The Tzedukim rejected the koach of Chazal; the Chashmonaim' ner Chanukah champions the koach of gezeiros d'rabbanan.

Therefore — patur. Yochanan is exempted by his descendants' tikkun. And therefore — we are permitted to mention his name in the mishnah and in our tefillos.

XIV. Closing

The Rabbi closed: there is much more to say, but he must run to give another shiur in Brooklyn. The olam sang each other a "L'chaim, a lichtige Zos Chanukah!"

A lichtige, freilche Zos Chanukah!

The Rambam's historical exception is itself a tell on the structural question of why he opens here with history. ↩
  9. Rambam, Hakdamas Mishneh Torah. — "L'fichach kara'ti shem ha'chibur ha'zeh Mishneh Torah... she'adam koreh Torah she'b'ksav techillah, v'achar kach koreh ba'zeh, v'yodea mimenu kol ha'Torah she'b'al peh". The source of the controversy with the Geonim over the Mishneh Torah's koach ha'limud; the Rabbi's pivot for explaining the historical introduction at Hilchos Chanukah — a Jew with only the Tanach and the Mishneh Torah would otherwise know nothing of Chanukah, since it is absent from Tanach. ↩
  10. Sforno (R' Ovadyah Sforno, c. 1475–1550), Bereishis 49:10. — "Lo yasur" as a prohibition on removing the malchus from Shevet Yehudah; once removed (by the churban and Tzidkiyahu's exile), no issur binds another shevet who assumes the throne. A direct krenkel against the Ramban's reading. Removes the premise of teretz #2: there was no cheit to be vindictive about. Sforno was a leading early Hebraist who tutored prominent German Christian Hebraists. ↩
  11. Chasam Sofer, Drashos / Toras Moshe, Parshas Mikeitz / Vayeishev / Chanukah. — On Rabbeinu HaKadosh's methodology of recording only what the olam does not already know. By the time of the redactah of the Mishnah (~350 years after the Maccabee revolt), every Yiddishe household knew the halachos of Chanukah; no tikkun b'kesav was required. ↩
  12. Rambam, Pirush HaMishnayos, Maseches Menachos, perek 4 mishnah 1. — The yesod: there is no mishnah establishing the chiyuv of Krias Shema twice daily, of mezuzah, of tefillin — they were universally practiced and required no tikun b'kesav. The same yesod applied (per the Chasam Sofer) to Chanukah's halachos at the time of the Mishnah's composition. ↩
  13. Megillas Taanis — the earliest written work of Torah she'b'al peh. — Calendar of yamim tovim ka'tan's on which hesped and ta'anis are forbidden; quoted throughout Bavli and Yerushalmi; predates the Mishnah. Majority of content concerns the nitzchonos of the Chashmonaim, including extensive coverage of Chanukah and its halachos. By the time of Rabbeinu HaKadosh, the korpus was already accessible b'kesav; he did not need to duplicate it. ↩
  14. Rav Reuven Margaliyos (1889–1971), especially Yesod HaMishnah V'Arichaso; cf. Margaliyos HaYam on Maseches Sanhedrin. — On the motivations of Rabbeinu HaKadosh in composing the Mishnah and the political-historical context of the tumultus Iudaicus. Rav Margaliyos's day job was running a grocery in Eretz Yisrael after his 1946–47 emigration from Galicia; his sefarim (dozens of them) include mareh mekomos from secular sources where they bear on Jewish history. The Margaliyos HaYam on Sanhedrin is required reading for Daf Yomi learners on the masechta. ↩
  15. Source-type note (Roman historian): Dio Cassius (Cassius Dio Cocceianus, c. 155–235 CE), Roman History, especially Books 68–69. A contemporary of Rabbeinu HaKadosh; eighty books of Roman history. His descriptions of the Kitos War (115–117 CE) and the destruction of the Alexandrian, Cyrenian, and Cypriot Jewish communities — the 120,000-gentile death toll, the brutality on both sides — are obviously noteh against the Jews but remain the principal extant non-Talmudic record. Cited as historical source, not as sifrei kodesh.
    ↩
  16. Yerushalmi Sukkah 5:1; cf. Bavli Sukkah 51b. — The Great Synagogue of Alexandria: "mi she'lo ra'ah... lo ra'ah b'hodah shel Yisrael"; seventy kateidri shel zahav in memory of the Sanhedrin; the shamash's flag-raising for the tzibbur's amen. The Rabbi compared the building's grandeur to the Belzer shul in Yerushalayim. ↩
  17. Yerushalmi Sukkah 5:1; Bavli Sukkah 51b — Trajan's massacre of Alexandrian Jewry. — The personal trigger: Trajan's son born on Tisha B'Av, daughter died on Chanukah; his wife's incitement; the destruction of the Alexandrian community; the slaughter of the men, then their wives; the sea between Egypt and Cyprus turning red with Jewish blood. Closing with "karno shel Yisrael nigdah" — until Mashiach ben David. ↩
  18. Devarim 28:49–52 — tochachah of Parshas Ki Savo. — The foreign nation, the unintelligible language, the high and fortified walls. The parshah the yeshivah of Alexandria was learning when Trajan walked in. Read also by the Rabbi's own rebbe in Tzimim on the roof on September 11, 2001 — also during Parshas Ki Savo. ↩
  19. Megillah ט׳ ע״א (9a). — Translation of the Torah for Ptolemy; the seventy sages in separate rooms; the eleven uniform shinuyim inserted by ru'ach ha'kodesh, including "v'es ha'arneves" in deference to Ptolemy's mother (named Arneves, descended from Amalek). The Romans inherited the Targum, monitoring Klal Yisrael's texts. ↩
  20. Bava Kama ל״ח ע״א (38a). — Malchus Yavan sending "shnei sardiotin" (variant: "shnei istrategin") — high-ranking officials — to copy the laws of Bava Kama from the Chachmei Yisrael. Direct evidence of imperial surveillance of Jewish halachic texts; supports Rav Margaliyos's reading that Rabbeinu HaKadosh's Mishnah would have been similarly scrutinized. ↩
  21. Sefas Emes, Likutim, Chanukah. — Chanukah as tarpah balbeis — concealment as the inner yesod of the yom tov; everything b'soyd; Mashiach revealing all. Politically and spiritually the same vector: Chazal deliberately suppressed the political ru'ach of the national-redemption story in galus. ↩
  22. Shabbos כ״א ע״ב–כ״ב ע״א (21b–22a). — "Ner Chanukah mitzvah l'henicha al pesach beiso mi'bachutz... u'visha'as ha'sakanah, manicha al shulchano, v'dai lo": the original mitzvah to place the menorah at the door, opposite the mezuzah, on reshus ha'rabbim; the heter for indoor lighting b'sha'as ha'sakanah. Inherited from a moment when celebrating Chanukah openly would have provoked the goyim; preserved even in places without contemporary sakanah. ↩
  23. Source-type note (modern critical edition): Engelman publishing house (Eretz Yisrael), Sefer HaMaccabim I (2018), Sefer HaMaccabim II (late 2024). Modern Hebrew critical edition with maps, illustrations, and extensive footnotes citing every early source that quotes Sefer HaMaccabim. The hakdamah documents the eighteenth-century Yiddish translation that supplied the Tosfos Yom Tov, Aruch HaShulchan, and a long chain of Litvish/Galician acharonim who cite the sefer by name despite the loss of the original Hebrew.
    ↩
  24. Berachos כ״ט ע״א (29a). — "Yochanan Kohen Gadol shimeish bi'kehunah gedolah shemonim shanah, u'l'sof na'aseh tzeduki"; from here the mishnah's warning "al ta'amin b'atzmecha ad yom mosecha". The textual basis for the Rabbi's identification of Mattisyahu ben Yochanan's father with the Tzeduki high priest of Berachos 29a. ↩
  25. Bnei Yissaschar, Kuntres on Chanukah. — The kushya of mentioning Yochanan Kohen Gadol by name in the tefillah on Chanukah; rejection of the suggestion that there are two distinct Yochanans; teirutzim based on the tikkun through his descendants — chief among them, the remez in Bava Kama developed in §XIII. ↩
  26. Mishnah, Bava Kama 6:6 (sof perek HaKonis). — The mishnah of the camel laden with flax (pishtan) and the chenvani's candle. Three-way din: candle inside the store — camel-driver pays; candle outside in reshus ha'rabbim — camel-driver patur; ner Chanukah outside — camel-driver pays again, since every Jew knows the candle is there. One of the eight Chanukah-mentions in Shas. ↩
  27. Source-type note (Chassidic, lesser-known): Bircas HaShalom — work attributed to a Chassidische tzaddik of the post-Baal Shem Tov generation (~250 years before the shiur), described by the Rabbi as "very gehoiben" and not widely known. The remez identifying חנוני with יוחנן in the mishnah of Bava Kama; patur as the tikkun of his descendants. Cited in the Rabbi's own research on the Bnei Yissaschar's teirutzim.
    ↩
  28. Yoma י״ט ע״ב and נ״ג ע״א (19b, 53a). — The story of Yochanan Kohen Gadol's avodah with the ketores: the Tzedukim' insistence on lighting the fire before entering the Kodesh HaKodashim; the first cheit from which the Tzedukim's ascendancy grew. The Gemara's "chayav": anyone doing this is liable. The Bircas HaShalom's drash: the Chashmonaim's ner Chanukah tikkun reverses precisely this — light brought outside rightly, championing the koach of Rabbanan. ↩
