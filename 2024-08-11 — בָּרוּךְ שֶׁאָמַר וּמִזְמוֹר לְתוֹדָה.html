Baruch She’amar and Mizmor L’Todah
Shiur of Rabbi Ephrati — Sunday Morning Series on Tefillah
Recorded 11 August 2024 (Zayin Av) · Reworked 30 April 2026 · Mareh Mekomos upgraded 5 May 2026

The strange origin of Baruch She'amar; the halachic implications of its non-Talmudic source; and the daily korban todah we offer through Mizmor L'Todah.

I. The Date — Zayin Av and the Three Days of Mourning

This shiur is delivered on the seventh of Av. In Ashkenazic tradition, the seventh, eighth, and ninth of Av were known as the shloshes yemei aveilah (שלושת ימי אבילה) — the three days of mourning — a play on the shloshes yemei afeilah (שלושת ימי אפילה), the three days of darkness in Mitzrayim.

On Zayin Av, Nevuzaradan — Rav HaTabachim, the chief executioner of Nevuchadnetzar — breached the walls of the Beis HaMikdash. For three full days he and his forces desecrated the sanctuary, "achlu v'kirkeru"[1] — eating, drinking, and reveling, turning the holiest place on earth into a circus. Only on Tisha B'Av itself was the fire ignited, and the Beis HaMikdash burned through the tenth.

Nevuzaradan and the Bubbling Blood

The Gemara[2] records the astonishing aftermath. After the murder of the navi Zechariah, the navi's blood would not stop bubbling on the floor of the Beis HaMikdash. Nevuzaradan tried to silence it; in a desperate, escalating frenzy he slaughtered eighty thousand kohanim upon it. Only then did the blood finally calm. He was so utterly shaken by what he had done that he converted — he became a ger tzedek. The Gemara states this in three short words; the magnitude is hard to fathom. It is among the more enigmatic pieces of Aggadah in Shas, and worth contemplating on Tisha B'Av.

Pogroms of Av

This is also a date that recurs in our martyrology. In the late 1300s, a horrific pogrom struck Seville, Spain — four thousand Jews were murdered al Kiddush Hashem, including the rebbetzin of the Rosh (the Rishon R' Asher b. Yechiel) and the daughter-in-law of the Ba'al HaTurim. We mourn these collectively on Tisha B'Av; the kinos hold that we do not multiply formal days of mourning, but absorb each tragedy into the central aveilus on the Churban.

II. Baruch She'amar — The Heavenly Note

We continue from last week. Baruch She'amar has perhaps the strangest origin story in our entire siddur: a petek min ha'shamayim (פתק מן השמים) — a note that fell from Heaven with its full text. The Or Zarua (the grandson of the Ramban, distinct from the famous Or Zarua of Vienna) records the tradition; the Tur[3] brings it as halacha l'ma'aseh; the Taz reaffirms it. The Anshei Knesses HaGedolah saw the note and were mesaken the brachah as part of Pesukei d'Zimra.

Why We Say Baruch She'amar Standing

Some Rishonim derive the practice of standing for Baruch She'amar from this very origin: because of the chashivus of a tefillah that came directly from Shamayim, the Anshei Knesses HaGedolah instituted that we recite it on our feet. Our minhag follows that ruling to this day.

III. The Halachic Problem — Saying a Brachah Not in Chazal

The Pri Chadash's Sharp Question

The Pri Chadash — Rabbi Chizkiyahu di Silva, late-1600s Chief Rabbi of Yerushalayim, whose peirush on the Tur is printed in the back of standard editions — raises a fundamental difficulty.[4] We have a clear rule: one does not recite a brachah that does not appear in Chazal. Baruch She'amar is not in the Bavli or the Yerushalmi; it is mentioned by name in the Zohar HaKadosh, but as a brachah with Sheim u'Malchus — invoking Hashem's name — it is potentially a brachah l'vatalah.

The Pri Megadim's Resolution

The Pri Megadim resolves it: when the Tur records that the Anshei Knesses HaGedolah received the petek from heaven, that means they had a kabbalah on this brachah predating the Mishnah and Gemara by centuries. The Tannaim and Amoraim may have known it but not formalized it in their texts. We rely on the heavenly origin and the Tur's tradition.

The Saberoyim Alternative — Rabbeinu Shlomo of Worms

Rabbeinu Shlomo MiGermaiza (R' Shlomo of Worms / Vermayza) — a Rishon, contemporary, friend, and chavrusa of Rashi — offers an entirely different attribution in his own siddur:[5] Baruch She'amar was composed by the Saberoyim (סבוראים), the sages of the period between Ravina v'Rav Ashi's sealing of the Gemara and the era of the Geonim — roughly 500 to 700 CE.

Aside on the Saberoyim. This is a poorly-known stretch of our history. After the Gemara was sealed by Ravina and Rav Ashi, and before the Geonim arose in Sura and Pumbedisa, there was a roughly 200-year period — about 189 years — in which Klal Yisrael was led by the Rabbanan Saberoyim. They edited certain passages in Shas (the Ritva at the start of Maseches Kiddushin[6] states that daf 2a–3b are not from the Amoraim but from the Saberoyim). Apart from such passages — and Rabbeinu Shlomo's claim about Baruch She'amar — almost nothing is preserved as their independent legacy. Modern scholarship, including extensive Cairo Geniza research, has filled in some details, but the era remains largely silent.

If Rabbeinu Shlomo is correct, the Pri Chadash's question becomes even sharper — the Saberoyim are post-Talmud; how can they have instituted a brachah? The accepted answer is that they were transmitting an older kabbalah, not creating something new — but this is a real and continuing difficulty in the Acharonim.

Rabbeinu Shlomo's Life

R' Shlomo of Worms is a remarkable historical figure in his own right. The most important Torah center of his era was the Kehillos Shum (קהילות שו״ם) — the three communities of Speyer, Worms, and Mainz (Sh-Vav-Mem: Shpira / Vermayza / Magenza). Rashi himself traveled from Troyes to Worms to learn under Rabbeinu Yaakov bar Yakar, and Rabbeinu Shlomo was Rashi's contemporary and close friend, despite their many disputes on nusach ha'tefillah.

Rabbeinu Shlomo enjoyed a unique relationship with Heinrich IV (later Holy Roman Emperor). He persuaded Heinrich to permit Jews to live in Worms without burdensome additional taxes — Heinrich agreed not out of love for Jews, but because of his own conflict with the Pope, who hated them. He awarded Rabbeinu Shlomo the unprecedented title "Bishop of the Jews." This rare era of friendship ended in 4856 / 1096, when Rabbeinu Shlomo was murdered al Kiddush Hashem in the First Crusade — together with thousands of Yidden in Speyer, Worms, and Mainz — losses commemorated annually in the kinos of Tisha B'Av.

IV. The Halachic Status of Baruch She'amar

Hefsek Between Baruch She'amar and Yishtabach

From Baruch She'amar through Yishtabach, one is forbidden to interrupt with dibbur. The Mishnah Berurah[7] rules that if a person did talk between the two brachos, he must return to the beginning and repeat Baruch She'amar, the pesukim, and Yishtabach. He uses the unusual phrasing "yesh li lishav'ev" — "I am inclined to think" — signaling that he is not entirely certain, but this is the halachah as he sees it. Practically, the recommendation is simply: don't interrupt.

The Magen Avraham's Leniency

Because of the unique non-Talmudic origin of Baruch She'amar, the Magen Avraham[8] rules that this brachah has a different status from ordinary brachos. Specifically, one is permitted to answer amen in the middle of Baruch She'amar — something not allowed during a normal brachah. This leniency extends through the body of Pesukei d'Zimra as well: one may respond to Kaddish, Kedushah, and other devarim shebikdushah, since Pesukei d'Zimra itself is praise of Hashem, and answering further praises is not deemed a contradictory interruption.

The Aruch HaShulchan's Structural Chiddush

The Aruch HaShulchan[9] notes a structural oddity: in every other brachah, one opens with the standard "Baruch Atah Hashem Elokeinu Melech HaOlam..." formula. Baruch She'amar begins instead with "Baruch she'amar v'haya ha'olam..." — and only several lines later does the standard formula appear, embedded mid-brachah: "Baruch Atah Hashem Elokeinu Melech HaOlam, hakeil ha'av harachaman...".

The Aruch HaShulchan's chiddush: the actual brachah begins from Baruch Atah Hashem; everything before that is a haktamah — an introductory preamble — and is not technically part of the brachah itself. The practical implication is debated, but in principle there is more flexibility in the opening lines than in the formal brachah body.

V. The Inner Structure and Symbolism of Baruch She'amar

The Ten Beruchin

The word Baruch appears ten times in Baruch She'amar. The Rishonim connect this to the Asarah Ma'amaros[10] — the ten utterances with which HaKadosh Baruch Hu created the world (Bereishis Rabbah; Pirkei Avos). And indeed, Baruch She'amar is fundamentally a praise of Creation: "Baruch she'amar v'haya ha'olam" — Blessed is He who spoke and the world came into being.

87 Words = Paz

The brachah contains exactly 87 words, the gematria of Paz (פז) — fine gold. The Tur connects this to the pasuk in Shir HaShirim[11] describing HaKadosh Baruch Hu: "Rosho kesem paz" (ראשו כתם פז) — His head is like the finest gold. Baruch She'amar is the crown of pure gold we place on the head of our tefillos.

Baruch Merachem al Ha'aretz, Baruch Merachem al HaBriyos

One of the Baruch phrases is: "Baruch merachem al ha'aretz, Baruch merachem al ha'briyos" — Blessed is He who has compassion on the earth, blessed is He who has compassion on His creatures. The Ben Ish Chai[12] records a memorable illustration: a poor Yid in Baghdad came to a wealthy man's home on a muddy day, dripping water onto the elegant carpets. The owner shouted: "What are you doing to my floor?" The poor man answered, "I see you are 'merachem al ha'aretz' — compassionate to the earth (the floor). Now let me see if you are 'merachem al ha'briyos' — compassionate to the creature." The phrasing of the brachah demands the latter: HaKadosh Baruch Hu's mercy is upon both the world He created and the creatures who dwell in it, and so must ours be.

U'V'Shirei David Avdecha — David's Eternal Plea

The brachah concludes "U'v'shirei David avdecha nehallelcha" — and we praise You with the songs of David, Your servant. A midrash records that David HaMelech petitioned HaKadosh Baruch Hu based on the pasuk "Aguro b'oholcha olamim"[13] — "Let me dwell in Your tents for eternities" (plural). The plural, said David, must mean both this world and the next: not only should my tehillim be recited while I live, but after I have departed, Yidden should still recite my words, so that I "dwell in Your tents" eternally. HaKadosh Baruch Hu agreed. Every morning we are fulfilling that promise.

VI. Hodu, Nusach, and the Yemenite Exception

The dispute over the placement of Hodu — before or after Baruch She'amar — depends entirely on whether Hodu is part of Pesukei d'Zimra or part of Korbanos.

The Rambam does not bring Hodu at all. The Yemenite Jews (Teimanim) follow the Rambam exclusively — even more strictly than other Sephardic communities follow the Shulchan Aruch — and accordingly, they do not say Hodu. The Arizal, building on the Zohar, was the one who pressed for daily recitation of Hodu. The Yemenites do not recognize the Zohar at all.

The Dardaim. A subset of Yemenite Jewry — the Dardaim (דרדעים), from Dor De'ah ("Generation of Knowledge," referring to the Dor HaMidbar) — went further and rejected the entire Zohar outright. They hold that the Rambam did not have the Zohar, and on that basis they discard everything Kabbalistic in our liturgy. Many are concentrated near Rosh HaAyin in Eretz Yisrael. They were excommunicated by the Badatz of Yerushalayim, but remain part of Klal Yisrael.

The Ba'al HaTanya argues, by contrast, that the Rambam certainly knew the Zohar and Kabbalah — but deliberately concealed it, because his generation was steeped in philosophy and unprepared to absorb mystical teachings.

The original takanah, predating both Hodu and Mizmor Shir Chanukas HaBayis, was simply: Baruch She'amar → Halleluyos → Yishtabach. Everything else was added later, which is why Nusach Sefard places Hodu outside that frame — it has nothing to do with Pesukei d'Zimra proper, and belongs with Korbanos.

VII. Mizmor L'Todah — The Daily Korban Todah

Source and Practice

Mizmor L'Todah (Tehillim 100) is recited daily after Baruch She'amar. Rashi in his siddur, and Rabbi Yaakov Emden in his (Siddur Yaavetz),[14] explain that the Levi'im in the Beis HaMikdash would sing precisely this mizmor while a person was offering a korban todah. Today, in the absence of the korbanos, daily recitation of Mizmor L'Todah is our daily korban todah.

The Four Who Must Give Thanks

The Gemara[15] identifies four categories of people obligated to bring a korban todah: Choleh (one who recovered from illness), Yam (one who completed an ocean journey safely), Yesurim (one released from captivity), and Midbar (one who safely traversed a desert). The Tur[16] arranges the four into a siman: "Chayim b'chol ha'chayim yoduchaselo chayim", with the four yuds/initials marking Choleh, Yam, Yesurim, Midbar. Today, the same four recite Birkas HaGomel instead.

The poskim have an extensive machloikis about whether modern air travel obligates birkas hagomel — a daily question for any rav serving an American congregation that travels often to Eretz Yisrael.

Why We Say It Daily — Even Without a Specific Yeshuah

The Yaavetz writes that every single day we are saved from countless dangers — many of which we never see. We do not wait for an "obvious" miracle to thank HaKadosh Baruch Hu; the act of waking up in good health, with one's family well, with a successful journey to work, is itself a daily miracle. Therefore, every morning we recite Mizmor L'Todah in place of an actual korban todah.

Numerical Symbolism

Two structural details:

  • Forty words — corresponding to the forty loaves of bread that accompanied a korban todah (ten each of four kinds: chametz, matzah, chalos, rekikim, and the revuchah oil-cake).
  • Four names of Hashem after the opening pasuk, corresponding to the four categories obligated to give thanks.

When We Omit It

The mizmor is omitted on certain days because the underlying korban todah could not be brought on those days:

  • Shabbos and Yom Tov — no nedavah offerings (voluntary korbanos) are brought on Shabbos.
  • Erev Pesach — the forty loaves include chametz bread, which could not be consumed before z'man achilas chametz ended. To avoid the loaves becoming nosar and pasul, the korban was not brought.
  • Erev Yom Kippur — eating the requisite forty loaves before the fast was simply not feasible.

(There is a separate machloikis about whether Mizmor L'Todah is said on Tisha B'Av; Luach Eretz Yisrael by Rav Tukachinsky and the Hinkis luach address this explicitly.)

VIII. The Forgotten Halachah — Singing Mizmor L'Todah

The Shulchan Aruch[17] rules a striking and almost universally neglected halachah:

צריך לאומרו בנעימה, שכל השירות עתידות ליבטל חוץ ממזמור לתודה.
"One must recite it with a melody, for all songs of praise are destined to be nullified in the future, except for Mizmor L'Todah."

This is brought from the Meiri, the Mahari Abuhav, and others — not a fringe opinion. We are obligated to sing this mizmor, not merely recite it. Yet in nearly every Ashkenazic shul, it is read silently. Sephardic teimanim ("they sing everything") are more careful, but even there the practice is uneven.

The Rebbe relayed an account from his brother-in-law (a published talmid chacham who authored an entire sefer on Mizmor L'Todah, written under the encouragement of Rav Gamliel Rabinovitch). At Shtilei Zeisim, a Sephardic shul in Bnei Brak known for extreme precision in nusach, a ba'al tefillah proposed reinstating the Shulchan Aruch's ruling and singing Mizmor L'Todah with the tzibbur daily. The kahal agreed.

Some months later, this ba'al tefillah was diagnosed with what doctors said was a metastasized growth requiring urgent surgery. He traveled to Tzfas, to the kever of the Beis Yosef — the very posek who codified this forgotten halachah in the Shulchan Aruch — and pleaded: "In the merit of being mechabed your halachah, which almost no one keeps, please grant me a yeshuah." When he returned for the pre-operative scan a week later, the doctors were astounded: the growths were gone. (As with all such accounts — to be received with the appropriate measure of reverence and discernment; what is certain is the underlying halachah.) At minimum, the story is a call to take the Shulchan Aruch's ruling more seriously.
