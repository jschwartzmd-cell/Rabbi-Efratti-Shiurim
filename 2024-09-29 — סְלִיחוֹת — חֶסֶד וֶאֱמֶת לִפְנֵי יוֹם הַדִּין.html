סְלִיחוֹת — חֶסֶד וֶאֱמֶת לִפְנֵי יוֹם הַדִּין

Slichos: Chesed v’Emes Before Yom HaDin
Drashah of Rabbi Ephrati — Motzaei Shabbos, opening of Slichos
Recorded 29 September 2024 (1:46 AM, opening Slichos) · Reworked 30 April 2026 · Mareh Mekomos upgraded 5 May 2026

Spoken on the first night of Slichos — late September 2024, during the war following Simchas Torah Tav-Shin-Peh-Daled, with reservists being called up that very night for the Lebanon ground operation. The drashah opens with a mashal from the Brisker Rav and unfolds into a teaching on chesed, emes, and the koach of moving HaKadosh Baruch Hu from Kisei HaDin to Kisei HaRachamim.

I. The Brisker Rav's Mashal — Three Mindsets at the Border

The Brisker Rav, zecher tzaddik livrachah,[1] used to give a brief drashah in his home minyan every Motzaei Shabbos before the first Slichos. The trembling line in tonight's pizmon — "Klal Yisrael Yidden tremble with fear from the upcoming Yom HaDin" — is, on its face, hard to be mechaven to. The Brisker Rav offered a mashal to make it real:

In alte heim Europe, Jews were often barred from many ordinary trades. Many turned to smuggling across borders for parnassah. Picture a Yid who has secured a major deal: he will smuggle expensive merchandise from Poland into Hungary on a single moonless night. He has hired a balagulah who knows the back roads. The crossing date is set thirty days out.

For thirty days the Yid lives with palpable fear. He understands the stakes: if caught, he loses everything he invested, ends up in jail, and his wife and children have no parnassah. He davens, prepares, plans, double-checks every detail.

The balagulah, by contrast, has lower stakes. If they get caught, he loses his horse, perhaps his wagon, sits in jail a few days, pays a knas, returns to ordinary life. He prepares some — but not with the Yid's intensity.

The horse — the ferd, the sus — has no awareness whatsoever. It will pull the wagon across the border tonight or remain in the stable; it has no preparation, no concept, no fear.

Three Levels of Slichos

The Brisker Rav's application:

  • The aspiration — be like the Yid. Prepare for thirty days. Aleph-Beis-Lamed-Vav-Lamed is built for that: weeping, teshuvah, cheshbon ha'nefesh, hisorerus — the full thirty-day preparation that turns the Yid into a different person on Rosh HaShanah morning.
  • The minimum — at least be like the balagulah. We have only four days of Slichos this year (Ashkenazic communities; Sephardim already had the entire month). Don't let those four days slip. Wake up early, feel the gravity of the days approaching, recognize the seriousness of Yom HaDin.
  • The warning — do not be like the horse. To enter Yom HaDin with no awareness, no teshuvah, no preparation, is a tragedy of staggering proportions. The horse is pulled across the border whether or not it understands what is happening. A Yid is not made to be a horse.

II. The Source of the Yud-Gimmel Middos

The central pizmon of Slichos — and of Aseres Yemei Teshuvah as a whole — is the thirteen middos ha'rachamim: "Hashem, Hashem, Keil rachum v'chanun, erech apayim..." Where does this draw its koach?

The Gemara in Rosh HaShanah

The Gemara in Rosh HaShanah[2] on the pasuk "Vaya'avor Hashem al panav vayikra"[3] says something that the Gemara itself flags as almost unsayable:

אִלְמָלֵא מִקְרָא כָּתוּב, אִי אֶפְשָׁר לְאוֹמְרוֹ. מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנִּתְעַטֵּף הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא כִּשְׁלִיחַ צִבּוּר וְהֶרְאָה לוֹ לְמֹשֶׁה סֵדֶר תְּפִלָּה. אָמַר לוֹ: כָּל זְמַן שֶׁיִּהְיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל חוֹטְאִים, יַעֲשׂוּ לְפָנַי כַּסֵּדֶר הַזֶּה, וַאֲנִי מוֹחֵל לָהֶם.
"Were it not written in the verse, it would be impossible to say. This teaches that HaKadosh Baruch Hu wrapped Himself like a shliach tzibbur and showed Moshe the order of tefillah. He said to him: 'Whenever Israel sins, let them perform before Me this very order, and I will forgive them.'"

From this Gemara comes the universal minhag that the shliach tzibbur for Slichos wears a tallis (even at night, when one normally would not), enacting the image of HaKadosh Baruch Hu as shliach tzibbur.

The Question Every Year Demands

If reciting the thirteen middos is the master key, why have we been saying them year after year — for centuries — and the geulah still has not arrived? Last year was not a good year. Two years ago was not much better. The tzaros persist. Where is the promised "v'ani mochel lahem"?

The answer is in the language of the Gemara: "ya'asu lefanai ka'seder ha'zeh" — let them do, before Me, this order. The Gemara elsewhere[4] articulates the principle:

מָה הוּא רַחוּם — אַף אַתָּה רַחוּם. מָה הוּא חַנּוּן — אַף אַתָּה חַנּוּן.
"As He is merciful, so you must be merciful. As He is gracious, so you must be gracious."

The thirteen middos are not a password. They are an instruction in avodah: walk the path. To say them without doing them — without cultivating the rachamim they describe — is lip service. The she'arei rachamim open precisely when our conduct mirrors the middos, not merely our mouths.

III. Tanna D'Bei Eliyahu — Standing as One

A second source for Slichos appears in Tanna D'Bei Eliyahu[5] — the compilation of teachings transmitted by Eliyahu HaNavi in his daily chavrusa with Rav Anan. There, the framing is different.

David HaMelech says "Hashem b'yom tzarah" — HaKadosh Baruch Hu answers us in a day of distress. He asks: Ribbono shel olam, what should Klal Yisrael do in a future age when there is no Beis HaMikdash, no korbanos, no Avodah, when the people are sunk in galus and hester panim? HaKadosh Baruch Hu answers — and the wording is significant — "Stand before Me b'agudah achas": as one bound bundle. Recite the thirteen middos as a tzibbur — meaning, not merely in the same building, but actually as one organism.

The practical translation: more tzedakah — not just tzedakah b'mamon but also tzedakah b'guf: lifting another Jew's spirit, sharing burdens, nosei b'ol. HaKadosh Baruch Hu opens the gates of rachamim to a people whose individual members are unified — not as separate suppliants, but as one.

IV. Tzedek u'Mishpat, Chesed v'Emes

The opening line of Slichos in many kehillos is the pasuk:[6]

צֶדֶק וּמִשְׁפָּט מְכוֹן כִּסְאֶךָ; חֶסֶד וֶאֱמֶת יְקַדְּמוּ פָנֶיךָ.
"Righteousness and justice are the foundation of Your throne; chesed and emes precede Your face."

The structure: "Tzedek u'mishpat" describes Yom HaDin — when Hashem sits on the Kisei HaDin and exacts justice. "Chesed v'emes yikadmu fanecha" instructs us how to approach that day: precede Your face with chesed and emes. Bring those into the courtroom before judgment is rendered.

The Chasam Sofer's Gematria

The Chasam Sofer[7] notes that chesed + emes = Slichos (with one extra letter, but the connection holds). The work of these days is the work of chesed and emes.

What Is Emes?

Emes means Torah: "Emes k'neh v'al timkor"[8]. Chazal identify the truth one acquires and never sells as Torah itself. Real teshuvah requires returning to the book — embracing what it says to do and not do. The opening step of the parshas teshuvah[9] we read this morning is "v'shamata b'kolo" — to listen to His voice, which lives in Torah Shebiksav and Torah She'baal Peh.

In our grandparents' generation, Slichos-time meant more learning — an extra shiur, an extra shtikel Gemara, an extra mussar sefer, an extra kapitel Tehillim.[10] We are shvachei doros, weakened generations, but a kabbalah on a small concrete piece of emes — one halachah kept with precision, one daily seder added — is within reach.

What Is Chesed?

Chesed means practical action: tzedakah when it is hard, kindness when it is inconvenient, generosity when it costs.

V. Malveh Hashem Chonein Dal — A Daring Application

The Baal HaTanya's Letter

The Alter Rebbe writes in Igros Kodesh, in the famous letter at the back of Sefer HaTanya, that if Jews would only understand the koach of tzedakah — they would not be able to tear themselves away from giving it. The pasuk says "Malveh Hashem chonein dal"[11] — one who shows favor to the poor is lending to Hashem. Tzedakah is not a gift; it is a loan. And HaKadosh Baruch Hu repays His loans.

The Gemara generally rules "sechar mitzvah b'hai alma leikah" — there is no reward for mitzvos in this world; reward is reserved for olam ha'ba. Chazal identify tzedakah as one of the rare exceptions: it benefits another person directly, and HaKadosh Baruch Hu "repays" the loan in this world.

The Leket Yosher's Bold Inference

The Leket Yosher[12] — by Rav Yoyzpa Estraicher, talmid of the Terumas HaDeshen (R' Yisrael Isserlein, who fled Germany during a redifus, settled in Austria, and opened a yeshivah there) — records his rebbe's applications of halachah in profound directions.

The Rambam[13] rules that in a din Torah between a malveh (lender) and a loveh (borrower), if the malveh insists that the case be heard at the Beis Din HaGadol in Yerushalayim, the loveh must travel there — even if it takes days. The principle is "Eved loveh l'ish malveh"[14] — the borrower is, in a sense, a servant to the lender.

The Leket Yosher's chiddush, in his rebbe's name: if our tzedakah renders us, kviyochol, the malveh and HaKadosh Baruch Hu the loveh — then we have the standing of the lender. We can insist that the din Torah against us be conducted at the Beis Din HaGadol shel Rachamim, not at the Kisei HaDin. We can kviyochol compel HaKadosh Baruch Hu to move from the seat of strict justice to the seat of mercy. That is what tzedakah can do on Yom HaDin.

The language is daring; we would not formulate it on our own. But it emerges from a Rishon in his rebbe's name, from the Rambam's halachic structure applied through the pasuk's metaphor. The koach of chesed is, on the Leket Yosher's reading, to relocate the entire din.

VI. Rachmana D'anei Le'aniyei Aneinan

The closing refrain of Slichos — sung in the famous melody — is:

רַחֲמָנָא דְעָנֵי לַעֲנִיֵּי, עֲנֵינָא. רַחֲמָנָא דְעָנֵי לִתְבִירֵי לִבָּא, עֲנֵינָא.
"O Merciful One who answers the poor, answer us. O Merciful One who answers the broken-hearted, answer us."

The mefarshim raise an obvious difficulty: this works as a plea if we are actually poor or actually broken-hearted. But what if we are not? What right do we have to invoke a formula based on a status we don't qualify for?

The Gemara in Megillah

The Gemara in Megillah[15] opens a powerful category: "kafin v'ani v'lo yada" — a person can be hungry, impoverished, in dire need, and not even know it. The condition is paradoxical but real: someone so dulled, so habituated to lack, that he no longer registers the lack itself.

That, in avodas Hashem, is our state. We have lived so long in olam ha'zeh, in galus, in hester panim — we are so spiritually impoverished that we do not feel the impoverishment. We say Rachmana d'anei le'aniyei aneinan, and what we mean is: HaKadosh Baruch Hu, You see what we cannot. You know how poor we truly are. You know how broken-hearted we should be. Answer us as the impoverished and broken-hearted ones we are — even if we ourselves cannot yet feel it.

VII. The Moment We Are In

The shiur closes with the historical moment. Bachurim in Eretz Yisrael are dancing tonight in celebration of partial yeshuos — the elimination of senior Hezbollah leadership in the days preceding. But the geulah is not complete. As the Rebbe spoke, his phone showed a text: "They're calling all the reservists. The Lebanon ground operation is starting tonight." Thousands of Yidden who were home with their families this Shabbos are kissing their wives and children goodbye and heading north. The matzav remains kasheh me'od.

The Rebbe: "Had we known on Erev Simchas Torah Tav-Shin-Peh-Daled what would happen the next day, we would have davened differently. We would have said Slichos differently. We can say that every year — but especially this year. Right after a yemei rachamim followed by such a klap for Klal Yisrael, with the entire year shaped by the war that followed — let us set the record straight this time."

VIII. Practical Kabbalos for the Four Days

A short list of concrete kabbalos that translate the drashah into action over the four days that remain before Rosh HaShanah:

Emes (Torah)

  • Add one fixed seder in Torah daily through Aseres Yemei Teshuvah.
  • Learn one piece of mussar each night before Slichos — Rambam Hilchos Teshuvah, Mesilas Yesharim, Sha'arei Teshuvah.
  • Choose one halachah to keep with new precision — "v'shamata b'kolo" begins with one specific act of listening.

Chesed (Tzedakah and Rachmanus)

  • Commit to a measurable daily tzedakah amount; increase it on Erev Rosh HaShanah.
  • Chesed b'guf: call someone lonely, offer real time to a discouraged chaver, forgive a slight that has been festering.
  • Join a communal fund — for hostage families, for soldiers, for widows and orphans of the war.

Tefillah and the Yud-Gimmel Middos

  • Recite Slichos with kavanah, with the tzibbur, focusing on each middah in turn.
  • After each Slichos, choose one concrete act that reflects one middah of that day (erech apayim → patience; rav chesed → an unexpected kindness).

Achdus (B'Agudah Achas)

  • Make peace with one person before Rosh HaShanah — even one phone call.
  • Daven for another's specific need by name, daily.

IX. Conclusion

Slichos is not a password. It is a path. "Chesed v'emes yikadmu fanecha" means: arrive at Yom HaDin holding both Torah and acts of kindness, embodying — not just reciting — the thirteen middos. Then "v'ani mochel lahem" becomes our reality, both for each individual and for Klal Yisrael.

May we be zoche that HaKadosh Baruch Hu moves from Kisei HaDin to Kisei HaRachamim, that He grants ksivah v'chasimah tovah to Klal Yisrael, that the families holding their breath tonight in Eretz Yisrael see their sons and husbands return safely, and that the geulah sheleimah arrive b'meheirah b'yameinu, amen.
